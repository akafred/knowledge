Test-Driven Development

Writing failing tests before implementation — a feedback discipline that survives and gains importance when agents write the code, because tests instrument the loop.

Also known as: TDD, test-driven development, red-green refactor / TDD with an agent, red-green refactor, T*D, doc-test

Tensions

Is code a durable artifact humans must keep reading, or an ephemeral byproduct regenerated from a spec?
from: What did the participants of the Thoughtworks 'Future of Software Engineering' Europe retreat (mid-2026) conclude about how agentic AI is reshaping software engineering?
  • We've Stopped Arguing About Whether AI Changes Software (Greg Herlein): Code is potentially ephemeral — a disposable byproduct the agent regenerates from the spec on demand; the spec, where a developer's taste now lives, is the thing that matters.
  • Why did Martin Fowler invite 60 engineers to a ski resort? (Ivett Ördög): Code must remain human-legible and maintained; agents behave better as 'engineers' moulding a persistent codebase than as 'compilers' regenerating from scratch.
  • Software Design in the Agentic Age: Place Your Bets (Mathias Verraes): Skeptical of throwaway regeneration; unconvinced code can be discarded and regenerated at will — bets that code largely stays.
Assessment: The retreat's own 'largest divide' (Ördög). It is unresolved inside the room and inside the individual essays: Herlein concedes the deterministic 'code is the better spec' counter is 'hard to wave off', and Ördög reports being ironically pulled toward the dark-factory camp by her own good-code practices. The crux is whether a fuzzy prose spec can ever be the durable source of truth when the stated quality bar is verifiability — a tension none of them resolves.
How much of the build pipeline should run with no human reading the code (the 'dark factory')?
from: What did the participants of the Thoughtworks 'Future of Software Engineering' Europe retreat (mid-2026) conclude about how agentic AI is reshaping software engineering?
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): Toward a largely self-evolving software factory where a feature request is 'magically built' into the system; humans build and operate the factory and intervene only at explicit hand-off points.
  • AI Is Changing Software Engineering. The Fundamentals Still Matter. (Abby Bangser): Deliberately bounded autonomy — keep humans in the loop.
  • Software Design in the Agentic Age: Place Your Bets (Mathias Verraes): Dark factories are only safe for organisations that already have high test coverage, skilled engineers, and a high-quality engineering culture; most should cover their fundamentals first.
Assessment: Not simple optimism vs pessimism but a disagreement about preconditions and hidden costs. Ocytko's is a projection of where tooling is heading; Bangser and Verraes both supply reasons the projection is unsafe by default — learning-loss and missing prerequisites. Bangser has the stronger argument that the loss is structural (you cannot buy back organisational learning), which even an improving platform does not address.
Reversibility as a hedge vs a one-way commitment to deeper autonomy.
from: What did the participants of the Thoughtworks 'Future of Software Engineering' Europe retreat (mid-2026) conclude about how agentic AI is reshaping software engineering?
  • Software Design in the Agentic Age: Place Your Bets (Mathias Verraes): Build systems that can revert from agent generation back to human engineering.
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): Head toward a self-evolving software factory — a progressive, largely one-directional build-out of autonomy and platform capability.
  • We've Stopped Arguing About Whether AI Changes Software (Greg Herlein): Commit early and hard, because adoption is a compounding flywheel and the lead becomes a permanent structural moat.
Assessment: Incompatible risk models. Verraes treats deep AI-dependence as the danger to insure against; Herlein treats not-committing as the existential danger. They cannot both be the rational strategy: reversibility trades some upside for exit options, while flywheel-commitment trades exit options for compounding lead. The tie-breaker is empirical (will access/economics deteriorate?) and neither has evidence — Fowler's 'tokenpocalypse' data leans slightly toward Verraes.
Where is the locus of safety for non-engineers / citizen developers building software?
from: What did the participants of the Thoughtworks 'Future of Software Engineering' Europe retreat (mid-2026) conclude about how agentic AI is reshaping software engineering?
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): In a tightly-constrained self-healing platform — not universal upskilling.
  • What Survived Contact (Sam Ruby): In the director's management skill — a non-engineer steering an agent has made a hire, and the requirement is that they can manage by objective.
  • AI Is Changing Software Engineering. The Fundamentals Still Matter. (Abby Bangser): In API-first, MCP-accessible tooling that supports rather than restricts the new builders.

Ruby and Ocytko are the true opposites — Ruby puts the burden on a human management skill, Ocytko designs the human out by making failure modes disappear in the platform. That both are held by attendees of one retreat is a measure of how unsettled the citizen-developer problem is.
Does low-level code quality matter intrinsically, or only instrumentally (via token economics)?
from: What did the participants of the Thoughtworks 'Future of Software Engineering' Europe retreat (mid-2026) conclude about how agentic AI is reshaping software engineering?
  • Software Design in the Agentic Age: Place Your Bets (Mathias Verraes): Code-level quality is automatable and he is 'unsure it matters much in its own right apart from token cost'; you bet on it via an asymmetric payoff, not intrinsic worth.
  • Why did Martin Fowler invite 60 engineers to a ski resort? (Ivett Ördög): Code quality matters intrinsically because code good for AI and code good for humans are the same thing.
  • Fragments: July 6 (Martin Fowler (Jul 6)): Contested and leaning toward design mattering, but on new grounds — token cost as a proxy for architectural quality.
Assessment: The disagreement is about the grounds of the fundamentals-revival, not just its truth. If quality matters only via token cost (Verraes, and Fowler's proxy), the case for it is contingent on token economics and evaporates if tokens get cheap; if it is intrinsic (Ördög), it survives cheaper tokens. This makes the popular slogan 'quality still matters' quietly unstable — the same conclusion rests on incompatible foundations.

Is the model a swappable, commoditized component (so selection is a cost decision), or is model choice load-bearing (so selection is a capability decision)?
from: When working with AI coding agents, how should you select the model, its reasoning/effort level, and its context size for a given task?
  • GitHub - musistudio/claude-code-router: Use Claude Code as the foundation for coding infrastructure, allowing you to decide how to interact with the model while enjoying updates from Anthropic. (musistudio): Swap camp: the model is fungible config behind Claude Code's durable harness; route each sub-task to the cheapest capable model across many providers and keep the harness.
  • Hugging Face's Clem Delangue: Stop Comparing Engines to Cars (Turing Post (Ksenia Se interviewing Clem Delangue)): Swap camp: open weights are not 'behind' — they only look weaker because harnesses were optimized for proprietary APIs; the gap is a harness artifact.
  • The Pulse: a new trend, smart model routing (The Pragmatic Engineer (Gergely Orosz)): Swap camp: 'which model to use' is shifting from a deliberate engineering decision to a commoditized, automatable one; open models cover ~60% of coding work by token spend.
  • Coding Models Are Doing Too Much (nrehiew.github.io): Load-bearing camp: model choice materially changes output quality — the spread is enormous on the exact behavior that matters for brown-field editing.
  • [MODEL] Claude Code is unusable for complex engineering tasks with the Feb updates #42796 (stellaraccident (via https://github.com/anthropics/)): Load-bearing camp: the specific model and its reasoning allocation are structurally required; degrading the SAME model catastrophically broke a proven workflow.
  • Claude Code: Best practices for agentic coding (Anthropic (Claude Code best practices guide)): In the middle / silent: treats 'Claude' as one undifferentiated model and never addresses model selection at all.
Assessment: Both camps are right about different task strata, and the tie is broken by the routing camp's own artifact. Commoditization holds on the bulk/background tier (musistudio and the Pragmatic Engineer are correct that a cheap or open model suffices for a large share of tokens), but model choice is load-bearing exactly on the hard tier — the 'hardest 40%' the routing camp routes AWAY (the Pragmatic Engineer's own gap notes 60%-of-token-spend is not 60%-of-value). nrehiew's 6.5x over-editing spread and stellaraccident's collapse are measured on precisely that hard tier. The clincher the swap camp does not concede: musistudio's OWN per-model transformers (tooluse, enhancetool, reasoning, deepseek, gemini) prove the harness is NOT model-agnostic — every model needs bespoke request/response rewriting just to work behind Claude Code, so the 'any model' artifact self-refutes. Delangue even concedes the frontier gap himself. Verdict: select cheap-and-swappable for bulk, and treat the model as a load-bearing capability decision for the hardest slice — the two positions are not competitors but a partition of the task distribution.
Context size: should you shrink the problem to fit a small usable window (decompose / reset), or grow and trust a large window?
from: When working with AI coding agents, how should you select the model, its reasoning/effort level, and its context size for a given task?
  • [FULL WORKSHOP] AI Coding For Real Engineers - Matt Pocock, AI Hero (AI Engineer (channel); speaker: Matt Pocock (AI Hero)): Shrink: keep every task inside the ~100k smart zone; clear rather than compact; a 1M window is 'more dumb zone', good for retrieval, not coding.
  • Agent Harness Engineering (Addy Osmani): Shrink: fight context rot with compaction, offloading, and skills; do not let the window fill.
  • Harness design for long-running application development (Anthropic Engineering (long-running harness design)): Shrink (on weaker models): context resets with a handoff artifact beat compaction, because a fresh agent has no anxiety about approaching limits.
  • GitHub - musistudio/claude-code-router: Use Claude Code as the foundation for coding infrastructure, allowing you to decide how to interact with the model while enjoying updates from Anthropic. (musistudio): Grow, but only as a per-task escalation: route requests past ~60k tokens to a dedicated long-context model rather than forcing one window to hold everything.
  • Charlie Hills 🦩's Post (Charlie Hills 🦩): Manage in place: /compact and one-task-per-session when context degrades.
Assessment: The apparent 'grow / trust the big window' position for CODING largely dissolves once retrieval is separated from coding — which is exactly Pocock's move ('good for retrieval, less good for coding') and musistudio's (longContext is a routed escalation, not the coding default). No text argues you should code inside a full 1M window. The only genuine pressure toward trusting a bigger window is the vendor-forward Fable 5 / Opus 4.6 claim that coherence loss is being engineered away — but that is a moving target (convergence 5), not a stable dial, and even the harness paper only relaxed context resets AFTER measuring that the new model no longer needed them. A secondary, live sub-dispute survives: reset vs compact. Pocock, the harness paper and best-practices favor clearing/resetting to a fresh agent; Hills and Osmani lean on compaction. The reset camp has the better argument on today's models — compaction preserves the same anxious agent — but the disagreement is real and unresolved. Verdict: shrink-and-decompose is the default for coding; grow only as an explicit retrieval escalation; prefer reset over compact unless the model version has demonstrably killed context anxiety.
Effort/reasoning: does more thinking make output better, or does more reasoning make it worse (over-engineering / over-editing)?
from: When working with AI coding agents, how should you select the model, its reasoning/effort level, and its context size for a given task?
  • [MODEL] Claude Code is unusable for complex engineering tasks with the Feb updates #42796 (stellaraccident (via https://github.com/anthropics/)): More thinking is better and structurally required: reducing thinking depth caused a catastrophic, measurable quality regression.
  • Prompting Claude Fable 5 (Claude Platform Docs): More reasoning over-produces: higher effort yields unrequested tidying, refactoring, and over-planning that must be actively suppressed.
  • Coding Models Are Doing Too Much (nrehiew.github.io): Reasoning is a double-edged sword: reasoning models over-edit MORE by default, yet respond BEST to explicit constraints and become the most faithful editors.
  • Claude Code: Best practices for agentic coding (Anthropic (Claude Code best practices guide)): Middle: scale the thinking budget to difficulty via the think < think hard < ultrathink ladder.
Assessment: This looks like a head-on collision but is really the corpus resolving a category error, so I keep it as a divergence-under-adjudication rather than a live two-camp fight. The two things bundled into one 'effort' dial are separable: reasoning DEPTH (deliberation and research before acting — what stellaraccident measures as the Read:Edit ratio) versus action SCOPE (how much the model rewrites — what nrehiew measures as over-editing). They are separable but DEFAULT-COUPLED: more reasoning, unconstrained, tends to produce more scope. nrehiew is the empirical bridge — constrained reasoning models are the MOST faithful, proving depth and tight scope combine positively when you address them separately. Fable 5 already acts on this, pairing 'reduce effort' with a distinct anti-over-engineering instruction. The actionable verdict: do NOT cut reasoning depth to fix over-editing (that is stellaraccident's exact grievance and it backfires into shallow, edit-first behavior); keep the depth and add an explicit scope constraint. The failure is treating one knob as if it controls both correctness-depth and edit-scope.
Who selects the model/effort/context — a human deciding per task, or an automatic router — and who is trusted to hold the dial?
from: When working with AI coding agents, how should you select the model, its reasoning/effort level, and its context size for a given task?
  • Charlie Hills 🦩's Post (Charlie Hills 🦩): Human selects: the developer manually assigns Opus to think and Sonnet to build.
  • Claude Code: Best practices for agentic coding (Anthropic (Claude Code best practices guide)): Human selects: the user picks the thinking level (think/ultrathink) and configures the tools per task.
  • The Pulse: a new trend, smart model routing (The Pragmatic Engineer (Gergely Orosz)): Auto-router selects — but beware who owns it: an intelligent router (or Cursor 'Auto') picks the cheapest capable model automatically.
  • GitHub - musistudio/claude-code-router: Use Claude Code as the foundation for coding infrastructure, allowing you to decide how to interact with the model while enjoying updates from Anthropic. (musistudio): Semi-automatic, user-owned rules: config-driven scenario routing plus custom-router JS and subagent pinning.
  • [MODEL] Claude Code is unusable for complex engineering tasks with the Feb updates #42796 (stellaraccident (via https://github.com/anthropics/)): The dial must be transparent and user-controllable: the real harm was the PROVIDER silently rationing the effort dial.
Assessment: The human-vs-auto axis is less about capability than about (a) whether task difficulty can be detected a priori and (b) who bears the principal-agent risk. No router in the corpus actually measures difficulty — musistudio routes by token-count and static scenario, and the Pragmatic Engineer's routers infer 'good enough' without a demonstrated oracle — so 'auto' today means 'static-heuristic', not 'optimal'. Automation is safe when the user owns the policy (musistudio) and dangerous when the router profits from the choice (Cursor Auto) or when the provider adjusts the dial invisibly (stellaraccident). Verdict: keep the human holding the difficulty judgment and the effort dial until routers can both measure adequacy and prove neutral incentives; automate only the mechanical dispatch, over rules you own and can audit.
Is code a durable artifact to be maintained, or an ephemeral byproduct regenerated from a more durable source (spec/objective)?
from: What is the future of software development in the agentic/AI age?
  • We've Stopped Arguing About Whether AI Changes Software (Greg Herlein): Code is (or is becoming) ephemeral — a disposable byproduct the agent regenerates from the spec; the spec is 'the point.'
  • Fragments: February 4 (Martin Fowler (Feb 4)): When coding is free, the specification-plus-tests becomes the durable, primary engineering artifact.
  • Software Design in the Agentic Age: Place Your Bets (Mathias Verraes): Specifications could replace code as the source of truth, but throwaway regeneration is a bet he is unwilling to make; keep code durable and revertible.
  • Why did Martin Fowler invite 60 engineers to a ski resort? (Ivett Ördög): Code endures because the winning agents behave like engineers moulding a maintained codebase, not compilers regenerating from scratch.
  • What Survived Contact (Sam Ruby): The durability question is a category error — what endures is neither the code nor the prose spec but the objective and its oracle.
Assessment: The most-cited open question of the corpus, and genuinely unresolved (Herlein: 'no consensus here. None.'). The 'code is the better spec' counter is strong — code is unambiguous, executable, deterministic, whereas a prose spec is reinterpreted each run, which sits in tension with verifiability being the quality bar. Ruby's move is the most defensible: it dissolves the code-vs-spec fight by locating durability in the evaluation rather than any representation. Verraes and Ördög expose the load-bearing empirical unknown — nobody has data on regenerate-vs-patch cost or on long-term evolvability of agent-maintained code.
Where does the non-delegable human asset now live — the harness/platform, the oracle, the fundamentals, or the developer's understanding?
from: What is the future of software development in the agentic/AI age?
  • Notes from FOSE Europe (Giles Edwards-Alexander): In the harness — the continuously-evolving scaffolding of architecture guidance and rigour around the agents — though it must stay team-distributed, not centralized.
  • What Survived Contact (Sam Ruby): In the oracle — the objective's evaluation is the single thing the person directing the model cannot delegate back to it.
  • Jimmy Nilsson's Post — reflections after the Thoughtworks 'Future of Software Development' retreat (Jimmy Nilsson): In the fundamentals plus a renewed 'Extreme Humanism' — the durable disciplines and the conspicuously human element.
  • AI Is Changing Software Engineering. The Fundamentals Still Matter. (Abby Bangser): In right-sized boundaries and the operational loop — humans must stay in operations because their learning is the irreplaceable asset.
  • Fragments: February 9 (Martin Fowler (Feb 9)): In the developer's model-building and shared understanding — the craft of devising abstractions to reason about the domain.
Assessment: This is the deepest structural disagreement — every author agrees a human residue remains but nominates a different residue, and the choice determines what to invest in and what to teach. The harness-centric (Edwards-Alexander), oracle-centric (Ruby), fundamentals-centric (Nilsson/Bangser), and understanding-centric (Fowler) camps are not merely emphases: they imply different career ladders, different hiring, and different failure modes. Ruby's oracle and Bangser's operational-learning are the most operationally concrete; Nilsson's is the least argued. The absence in the corpus of anyone who holds 'nothing is non-delegable' is itself telling (see unexamined assumptions).

Is preserving human understanding of the system a first-order value, or is comprehension safely offloadable so long as output is verifiable?

  • Fragments: February 4 (Martin Fowler (Feb 4)): Preserve understanding: it is a first-order value, and practices that force explanation defend it.
  • Fragments: February 9 (Martin Fowler (Feb 9)): Preserve understanding and craft: cognitive debt and the loss of model-building are the real costs to guard against.

  • We've Stopped Arguing About Whether AI Changes Software (Greg Herlein): Offloadable: the quality bar is 'is it verifiable enough,' not whether a human read or understood the code.
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): Offloadable for most builders: citizen developers need never read the code if a constrained platform makes failure modes disappear.
Assessment: The optimist-practitioner vs. skeptic-humanist axis. Fowler and Nilsson treat comprehension and craft as intrinsic goods whose loss is a harm even when output is fine; Herlein and Ocytko treat comprehension as instrumental — replaceable by verification and platform guardrails. The humanist camp has the stronger long-run argument (Fowler's cognitive-debt case and Ocytko's own 'who-guards-the-guards' problem both show verification and guardrails are themselves fallible artifacts that someone must understand), but the offloadable camp is describing what is actually happening at scale. The crux is whether verifiability can ever be robust enough to make understanding optional — which Ruby's own Goodhart gap (a passable oracle diverging from true intent) suggests it cannot fully be.
