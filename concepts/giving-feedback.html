Giving Feedback (Performance & Coaching)

The manager-employee practice of delivering, structuring, and inviting feedback — critical and positive — to correct behavior, build skill, and sustain motivation without triggering defensiveness.

Also known as: critical feedback, constructive feedback, feedback delivery, negative feedback, performance feedback, two-way feedback conversation, feedback-seeking culture

Documents (2)

  • Our Favorite Management Tips on Giving Feedback HBR Editors · 2026-05-21 Reader 🖥️
    Feedback works when it separates the person from the behavior and is delivered inside a relationship the recipient already trusts: define what feedback must never do (attack character), structure it around a specific behavior plus its impact plus a next step, invite it proactively rather than only issuing it top-down, calibrate its intensity and timing to the relationship's maturity and the issue's severity, and treat it as a two-way, purpose-conferring conversation rather than a one-way verdict. The eight tips are different tactical answers to one underlying claim: feedback's effectiveness is gated by psychological safety and behavioral specificity, not by the giver's honesty or courage alone.

Open gaps (6)

  • incompleteNo tip in the piece cites primary data, sample sizes, or effect sizes for its central claim, even where a named source is credited (e.g., Levitt et al. for midpoint timing, Wetzler for feedback-seeking culture). The reader is asked to trust the compiled prescription without being shown the evidence that presumably backs the original longer articles.
    Our Favorite Management Tips on Giving Feedback — read next: The full HBR source articles linked inline (Wetzler on feedback-seeking culture; Levitt et al. on early-positivity research; Hirsch on two-way feedback) for the underlying studies and their limitations.
  • incompleteThe eight tips are never reconciled with each other where they could conflict: Tip 3 says withhold critique until midpoint; Tip 1's guardrails and Tip 7's conversational approach imply feedback can and should happen continuously and immediately when behavior needs correcting. A manager following the piece literally has no guidance on which tip governs an urgent, early-project problem.
    Our Favorite Management Tips on Giving Feedback — read next: Literature on feedback frequency and timing (e.g., continuous feedback vs. periodic review models) that would let a reader adjudicate when 'start with positivity, defer critique' should yield to 'address it now.'
  • incompleteNone of the tips address feedback across power or identity asymmetries — feedback from a junior employee to a senior manager, cross-cultural feedback norms, or feedback where the recipient has structurally less power to push back or seek recourse if the giver's guardrails fail. The bias-auditing prescription in Tip 1 is the closest the piece comes, but it addresses giver bias, not the receiver's structural vulnerability.
    Our Favorite Management Tips on Giving Feedback — read next: Research on upward feedback, psychological safety across hierarchy, and cross-cultural management (e.g., Erin Meyer's work on feedback norms across cultures) would fill this.
  • uninformedAs 'tip of the day' content compiled from separate prior articles, none of the eight tips engages with counter-evidence or failure modes of its own prescription — e.g., whether structured feedback templates can themselves feel bureaucratic and insincere, or whether a feedback-seeking culture can produce feedback fatigue or performative asking. The genre (curated positive digest) selects for confident, uncontested advice.
    Our Favorite Management Tips on Giving Feedback — read next: Critiques of over-formalized feedback culture and of performance-review theater in the broader management literature.
  • uninformedCitations are underspecified throughout: the Journal of Organizational Behavior study on manager involvement gives no sample size, effect size, or definition of 'manage from a distance'; the Frontiers in Psychology moonlighting study names a sample size (324) but no effect size or engagement instrument; every leader-behavior percentage (33%, 43%, 53%, 56%, 59%, 90%+) is reported without a confidence interval, base rate, or description of how 'average team' leaders were sampled.
    How to Build a Superteam That Keeps Getting Better — read next: The original Journal of Organizational Behavior and Frontiers in Psychology papers directly; Ron Friedman's book 'Superteams' (Simon & Schuster, 2026), from which this article is adapted, for full survey methodology.
  • illogicalSurvivorship bias in case selection: every illustrative organization (Thunder, Meta, Netflix, Amazon, Spotify, LinkedIn, Microsoft, Airbnb, Adobe, Estée Lauder, HubSpot, Keap, Basecamp, Haberman) is a documented success story for the practice it illustrates. No case of a team that experimented boldly and failed, admitted uncertainty and lost credibility, or had a hands-on leader who tipped into micromanagement is discussed, so each practice's downside risk and failure rate are invisible by construction.
    How to Build a Superteam That Keeps Getting Better — read next: Base-rate and survivorship-bias literature (e.g., Mlodinow, Taleb) applied to management case studies; accounts of experimentation cultures or hands-on leadership that did not pay off.
