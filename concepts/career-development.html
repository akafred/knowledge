Open gaps (36)

  • incompleteBeck's individual-utility analysis ignores bequest motives. The 'billion dollars in 30 years' would still be worth something to most people because it could go to children, spouses, foundations, or causes outside one's lifespan. The essay's hard claim that 30-year money is worth 'literally one dollar in my hand today' less than $1 today only holds if one's utility function is purely selfish and lifetime-bounded.
    Extreme Time Value of Money: Late-stage Career Planning — read next: The bequest-motive literature in economics (Kotlikoff, Modigliani); empirical work on intergenerational transfers; Derek Parfit on personal identity and obligations to future selves and others
  • incompleteThe diagnosis of tech-compensation bias is sharp but the prescription is missing. Beck explicitly says he has no answer. The essay gestures at arbitrage but does not survey actual instruments — secondary equity markets (Forge, EquityZen), tender offers, equity-financing products (e.g., from Quid or ESO Fund), salary-heavy negotiations, advisory-shares structures for senior people, or simply not joining startups. A reader looking for what to do is left to invent the response themselves.
    Extreme Time Value of Money: Late-stage Career Planning — read next: Practical resources on liquidity for private-company equity; 'Secrets of Sand Hill Road' (Kupor) on cap-table mechanics; the literature on optimal compensation design when worker time-horizons differ from firm time-horizons
  • incompleteThe argument frames career value purely through financial discounting, but late-career workers often report that meaning, legacy, mentorship, and intrinsic motivation become more important, not less. A late-career worker may rationally accept a long-dated equity grant because it locks them into a community of practice they enjoy. Beck's frame leaves no room for non-financial utility — which is striking given his own continued public writing and teaching, which are themselves long-dated, non-monetary investments.
    Extreme Time Value of Money: Late-stage Career Planning — read next: Erik Erikson's stage theory (generativity vs. stagnation); Arthur Brooks 'From Strength to Strength' on second-half careers; Robert Atchley's continuity theory of aging
  • incompleteThe 'discount rate becomes infinite past 30 years' formulation papers over the actuarial reality that the curve is not a step function — there is residual probability of being alive and well at year 25, year 28, year 32, and rationality requires integrating over those probabilities, not just truncating. Beck shows the actuarial chart but uses it as illustration rather than as the basis for a more nuanced curve.
    Extreme Time Value of Money: Late-stage Career Planning — read next: Standard actuarial discounting under uncertain lifespan (Yaari 1965 on uncertain lifetime and consumption); literature on how to compute expected present value with a stochastic horizon
  • incompleteThe essay treats the worker as the only party whose discount curve matters. It doesn't ask whether firms could (or should) offer differentiated compensation tracks for workers with shorter horizons — e.g., higher cash and lower equity for senior hires, accelerated vesting on age- or tenure-based triggers, or 'advisor' tracks designed to convert long-dated value to short-dated cash. The 'no answer' framing accepts the current design as given rather than asking who could change it.
    Extreme Time Value of Money: Late-stage Career Planning — read next: Compensation design literature; case studies of firms with explicit late-career tracks (academic emeritus structures, partner-track exits in law firms, advisory-board structures); economic work on multi-horizon labor markets
  • implicit_assumptionThe argument assumes 'enjoying' money is the relevant utility — money for experiences, comfort, options. But for many older workers, financial resources function as security or option value (medical contingency, family backstop, philanthropic latitude) even if they aren't spent. A million dollars unspent at death may have served real utility along the way as a buffer. Beck's 'I'd literally rather have one dollar in my hand today' rhetoric understates the option value of future money.
    Extreme Time Value of Money: Late-stage Career Planning — read next: Behavioral economics on the utility of unspent wealth (precautionary savings literature); Daniel Kahneman on experienced vs. remembered utility; literature on financial security as a psychological good independent of consumption
