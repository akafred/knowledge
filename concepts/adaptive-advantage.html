Adaptive Advantage / Adaptive Capacity

The claim that in fast-moving, capability-frontier-shifting environments, durable competitive advantage shifts from static assets (IP, scale, position) to the organization's or individual's capacity to learn and adapt faster than its environment and competitors.

Also known as: adaptive advantage, adaptive capacity, capacity to adapt, dynamic competitive advantage, no moat, speed of learning as advantage

Open gaps (5)

  • incompleteAll three case studies come from a single team (Claude Code at Anthropic) building a CLI tool with unusually tight dogfooding loops. The argument that experimentation should replace upfront design is not tested against contexts with higher costs of failure (healthcare, infrastructure, finance), longer feedback cycles, or larger teams where coordination costs dominate.
    Presentation: Engineering at AI Speed: Lessons from the First Agentically Accelerated Software Project — read next: Literature on high-reliability organizations (e.g., Normal Accidents by Perrow); experiences of AI adoption in safety-critical domains
  • incompleteThe talk assumes a small, highly skilled team that can make and evaluate architectural decisions rapidly. No discussion of how these practices scale to larger organizations where most engineers are not senior architects.
    Presentation: Engineering at AI Speed: Lessons from the First Agentically Accelerated Software Project — read next: Team Topologies (Skelton & Pais); research on scaling agile and continuous deployment in large organizations
  • uninformedThe SQLite analysis conflates SQLite's limitations with the specific way it was deployed (npm native dependency distribution, Better-SQLite3 binding). SQLite-via-WASM (e.g., sql.js) would have avoided the native dependency problem entirely. The multiprocess locking concern is real but standard WAL mode mitigates it significantly.
    Presentation: Engineering at AI Speed: Lessons from the First Agentically Accelerated Software Project — read next: SQLite documentation on WAL mode; sql.js and other WASM-based SQLite distributions
  • uninformedNo engagement with the Resilience Engineering academic tradition (Hollnagel, Woods, Dekker), which has spent 20+ years arguing resilience is the adaptive capacity of a sociotechnical system — not something that can be 'owned' by a single accountable executive. The article reads as if this body of thought does not exist.
    The Case for Hiring a Chief Resilience Officer — read next: Hollnagel, Woods & Leveson, 'Resilience Engineering: Concepts and Precepts'; David Woods' lectures on graceful extensibility.
  • incompleteThe three-forces/seven-transitions framework is presented as applying to 'virtually every leader' and organization, but every illustrative example in the conversation (the food-products company, generic references to 'very large organizations') is a large, AI-adopting, multi-divisional global corporation. There is no engagement with how the framework applies, or fails to apply, to smaller organizations, non-profits, public-sector bodies, or firms in AI-cautious or resource-constrained settings.
