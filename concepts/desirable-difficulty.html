Desirable Difficulty & Productive Struggle

Robert Bjork's principle that certain effortful obstacles in learning (spacing, interleaving, retrieval, generation) slow acquisition but deepen retention and transfer — the effort is the mechanism, not a flaw — which is why AI's removal of difficulty threatens the learning it appears to assist.

Also known as: desirable difficulties, ønskelige vanskeligheter, productive struggle, generation effect, retrieval practice, spacing effect, effortful learning, the effort is the mechanism

    AI-generated code creates a second, largely invisible kind of debt — cognitive debt, the erosion of shared team understanding of what a system does and why, distinct from ordinary technical debt in the code itself — and it is a genuine measurement blind spot: satisfaction scores, NPS and mentor ratings can hold steady or rise while it accumulates, so organizations must replace satisfaction-based signals with trajectory-based skill measurement and deliberate team-level questions to catch it before it becomes irreversible.
  • Our Favorite Management Tips on Giving Feedback HBR Editors · 2026-05-21 Reader 🖥️
    Feedback works when it separates the person from the behavior and is delivered inside a relationship the recipient already trusts: define what feedback must never do (attack character), structure it around a specific behavior plus its impact plus a next step, invite it proactively rather than only issuing it top-down, calibrate its intensity and timing to the relationship's maturity and the issue's severity, and treat it as a two-way, purpose-conferring conversation rather than a one-way verdict. The eight tips are different tactical answers to one underlying claim: feedback's effectiveness is gated by psychological safety and behavioral specificity, not by the giver's honesty or courage alone.
