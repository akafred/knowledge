AI for Social Good

Vendor blog posts (Google especially) presenting AI deployments in disaster response, education, or other humanitarian domains as straightforwardly beneficial — self-reported efficacy claims with no independent verification, a recurring PR genre rather than independent research.

Also known as: AI for Good, crisis resilience, humanitarian AI, social impact AI

Open gaps (3)

  • incompleteThe post treats 'reach' (alerts appearing in Search, Maps, Android notifications) as equivalent to protection, without acknowledging that Public Alerts, Android Earthquake Alerts, and smartphone-based forecasting all assume Android/smartphone penetration and reliable internet or cellular connectivity — both of which vary enormously across the governments and communities the piece claims to serve.
    How governments and organizations are leveraging Google's AI breakthroughs for crisis resilience — read next: Read on disaster-alert equity gaps and the digital divide in low-connectivity or low-smartphone-penetration regions, especially in the same countries named here (Nigeria, Venezuela).
  • incompleteThe piece names only successes. It presents no false positives, no missed events, no cost figures, and no account of what happens when a forecast is wrong or an alert fails to arrive in time — a familiar pattern across other Google-authored posts in this corpus (see extract-google-educators-ai-learning, which similarly reports only positive outcomes for a Google AI program).
    How governments and organizations are leveraging Google's AI breakthroughs for crisis resilience — read next: Seek failure-case studies or post-mortems of AI-based early-warning systems, and independent error-rate analyses of hurricane/flood AI forecasting versus traditional numerical weather prediction.
  • illogicalThe post cites the WMO/national-agency local-data pilot study's finding ('local data significantly improves forecasts') as an established result, while explicitly noting in the same paragraph that the results are 'to be published in the coming weeks' — i.e., not yet peer-reviewed or public at the time of writing.
    How governments and organizations are leveraging Google's AI breakthroughs for crisis resilience — read next: Check whether the study was subsequently published and whether its published findings match the pre-announced framing.
