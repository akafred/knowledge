Jevons Paradox

When technology makes a resource cheaper, total consumption rises — applied to cognition and labor to argue AI produces more work and more engineers, not fewer.

Also known as: Jevons paradox, Jevons' Paradox, Jevons paradox (Wardley's reading), Jevons paradox (on cognition), demand elasticity, unhobbling

Kent Beck — Still Burning Podcast · 2026-06-03		Jevons paradox (on cognition)Shoup's central forecast device. Original (1850s–1860s, William Stanley Jevons): when coal extraction became 10x cheaper, net spending on coal rose, because at the lower price entirely new uses became economically viable. Shoup's application: cognition is now near-free; therefore tasks that were not economically viable as cognitive work will now be done, and the number of engineers (and jobs) will rise, not fall. He pairs it explicitly with the 1900-agriculture analogy to argue that catastrophic-sounding workforce shifts can be true at the surface and benign-to-positive in long-run aggregate.
