Agentic Loop

The while-loop architecture of AI agents — model plus prompt plus tools iterating until a completion condition is met.

Also known as: agentic loop, agent loop, tool loop, Ralph Loop, Ralph (Wiggum) loop, /goal, agentic workflows, agentic applications, agentic application

Tensions

How much of the build pipeline should run with no human reading the code (the 'dark factory')?
from: What did the participants of the Thoughtworks 'Future of Software Engineering' Europe retreat (mid-2026) conclude about how agentic AI is reshaping software engineering?
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): Toward a largely self-evolving software factory where a feature request is 'magically built' into the system; humans build and operate the factory and intervene only at explicit hand-off points.
  • AI Is Changing Software Engineering. The Fundamentals Still Matter. (Abby Bangser): Deliberately bounded autonomy — keep humans in the loop.
  • Software Design in the Agentic Age: Place Your Bets (Mathias Verraes): Dark factories are only safe for organisations that already have high test coverage, skilled engineers, and a high-quality engineering culture; most should cover their fundamentals first.
Assessment: Not simple optimism vs pessimism but a disagreement about preconditions and hidden costs. Ocytko's is a projection of where tooling is heading; Bangser and Verraes both supply reasons the projection is unsafe by default — learning-loss and missing prerequisites. Bangser has the stronger argument that the loss is structural (you cannot buy back organisational learning), which even an improving platform does not address.
How far can day-2 operations be handed to self-healing / autonomous remediation?
from: What did the participants of the Thoughtworks 'Future of Software Engineering' Europe retreat (mid-2026) conclude about how agentic AI is reshaping software engineering?
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): A self-healing platform foundation should make whole classes of failure 'disappear', with increasingly autonomous background 'guardrail' agents refactoring, adding tests, and protecting invariants.
  • AI Is Changing Software Engineering. The Fundamentals Still Matter. (Abby Bangser): Detection, context, and suggestion are safe wins, but automated remediation must remain an intentional, context-specific decision, not a default end state.
  • What Survived Contact (Sam Ruby): Self-healing has, in practice, only moved halfway: diagnosis is automated while remediation is deliberately withheld from the machine.
Assessment: Ruby's report is descriptive (where practice actually stands) and Ocytko's is aspirational (where platforms could go); the gap between 'remediation is withheld' and 'failure modes disappear' is precisely the distance the optimists are projecting across. Bangser supplies the reason the gap may not close on efficiency grounds alone — the withheld human step is also the learning step.
Should the harness/platform-building function be centralised, or emerge per-team?
from: What did the participants of the Thoughtworks 'Future of Software Engineering' Europe retreat (mid-2026) conclude about how agentic AI is reshaping software engineering?
  • Notes from FOSE Europe (Giles Edwards-Alexander): Dedicated harness-engineering teams are a mistake; the friction of each team building its own harness is generative and creative.
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): A centralised platform / 'software factory' with tight, low-friction constraints should absorb operational concerns and guide both citizen developers and their agents from the first prompt.
Assessment: A precise split about the organisational form of the scaffolding, not its existence: both want shared architecture guidance, but Edwards-Alexander wants the building capability distributed (friction as feature) while Ocytko wants it concentrated in a platform (constraint as feature). Each concedes the other's pole — Edwards-Alexander keeps a central harness; Ocytko warns against over-constraining — which is why this is a live, unresolved tension rather than a clean contradiction.
Reversibility as a hedge vs a one-way commitment to deeper autonomy.
from: What did the participants of the Thoughtworks 'Future of Software Engineering' Europe retreat (mid-2026) conclude about how agentic AI is reshaping software engineering?
  • Software Design in the Agentic Age: Place Your Bets (Mathias Verraes): Build systems that can revert from agent generation back to human engineering.
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): Head toward a self-evolving software factory — a progressive, largely one-directional build-out of autonomy and platform capability.
  • We've Stopped Arguing About Whether AI Changes Software (Greg Herlein): Commit early and hard, because adoption is a compounding flywheel and the lead becomes a permanent structural moat.
Assessment: Incompatible risk models. Verraes treats deep AI-dependence as the danger to insure against; Herlein treats not-committing as the existential danger. They cannot both be the rational strategy: reversibility trades some upside for exit options, while flywheel-commitment trades exit options for compounding lead. The tie-breaker is empirical (will access/economics deteriorate?) and neither has evidence — Fowler's 'tokenpocalypse' data leans slightly toward Verraes.
Where is the locus of safety for non-engineers / citizen developers building software?

  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): In a tightly-constrained self-healing platform — not universal upskilling.
  • What Survived Contact (Sam Ruby): In the director's management skill — a non-engineer steering an agent has made a hire, and the requirement is that they can manage by objective.
  • AI Is Changing Software Engineering. The Fundamentals Still Matter. (Abby Bangser): In API-first, MCP-accessible tooling that supports rather than restricts the new builders.
  • Why did Martin Fowler invite 60 engineers to a ski resort? (Ivett Ördög): It is genuinely unsolved — the tension between democratisation and its risks is an open question.

That both are held by attendees of one retreat is a measure of how unsettled the citizen-developer problem is.
Can harness engineering actually solve the AI code quality problem?
from: What is the right approach to building harnesses around AI coding agents, and where are the limits?
  • Agent Harness Engineering (Osmani): Yes — harness engineering is the primary solution. A decent model with a great harness beats a great model with a bad harness.
  • Harness engineering for coding agent users (Böckeler): Partially — harnesses can solve maintainability and architecture fitness, but the behavioral harness remains fundamentally unsolved.
  • Genie Tarpit (Beck): Unknown — genies naturally produce degraded code and nobody knows how to fix this. Harnesses are listed as one possible remedy among several, without endorsement.
Assessment: This is the central divergence in the literature. Osmani is most optimistic, operating from benchmark evidence that harness changes produce dramatic improvements on coding tasks. Böckeler occupies the middle ground, distinguishing solvable from unsolved harness domains. Beck is most skeptical, questioning whether harnesses address the root cause at all. The disagreement is partly empirical (what do the benchmarks actually measure?) and partly about scope (Osmani focuses on task completion; Beck focuses on long-term code value including flexibility). They may not be contradicting each other so much as talking about different time horizons: harnesses help with immediate task success, but the tarpit emerges over accumulated changes.
What is the right metaphor for the human-AI relationship in coding?
from: What is the right approach to building harnesses around AI coding agents, and where are the limits?
  • Patterns for Reducing Friction in AI-Assisted Development (Garg): AI is a teammate — treat it like a junior pair programmer who needs onboarding, context, and rituals.
  • Harness engineering for coding agent users (Böckeler): AI is a system to be governed — the cybernetic governor model with feedforward and feedback controls.
  • Agent Harness Engineering (Osmani): AI is a runtime to be engineered — the harness is an engineering artifact, not a social relationship.
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Thoughtworks): AI is a new organizational participant — Conway's Law applies, and agent topologies must be designed.
Assessment: These metaphors are not mutually exclusive but they lead to different design decisions. Garg's teammate metaphor emphasizes collaboration rituals and may underplay the need for hard constraints (you don't sandbox a teammate). Böckeler's governor metaphor is the most theoretically rigorous but may overclaim the applicability of control theory to creative work. Osmani's engineering metaphor is the most actionable but may miss the social and organizational dimensions. Thoughtworks' organizational metaphor captures the broadest scope but is the hardest to operationalize.
Will better models make current harness practices obsolete?
from: What is the right approach to building harnesses around AI coding agents, and where are the limits?
  • Agent Harness Engineering (Osmani): Harnesses don't shrink, they move — better models create new failure modes at higher capability levels.
  • Genie Tarpit (Beck): The Bitter Lesson (scale + compute) is listed as one possible solution — implying better models might eventually solve the problem without harnesses.
  • Patterns for Reducing Friction in AI-Assisted Development (Garg): Does not address model improvement — the framework lacks a theory of its own obsolescence.
Assessment: Osmani makes the strongest case for permanence with concrete evidence of scaffolding migration. Beck keeps the door open for the Bitter Lesson hypothesis. The truth likely depends on the type of harness: mechanical safeguards (sandboxes, permission controls) may always be needed, while knowledge-transfer patterns (AGENTS.md, priming docs) may become less necessary as models improve at in-context learning.
Should the harness/platform and its safety be centralized or left distributed across teams?
from: What is the future of software development in the agentic/AI age?
  • Notes from FOSE Europe (Giles Edwards-Alexander): Distributed — dedicated harness-engineering teams are a mistake; per-team friction in building harnesses is creative and generative.
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): Centralized — safety for non-engineers should come from a tightly-constrained, self-healing platform that ships access and data guardrails.
  • AI Is Changing Software Engineering. The Fundamentals Still Matter. (Abby Bangser): Centralized enabling layer — platform, SRE, and ops teams should provide API-first, MCP-accessible tooling that others safely consume.
  • Fragments: February 18 (Martin Fowler (Feb 18)): Centralized golden path — platform teams should provide fast-but-safe 'bullet trains' with security built in by default.
Assessment: A real operating-model split. Edwards-Alexander's distributed stance rests almost entirely on the DevOps-team analogy (his own extraction flags it as asserted-on-intuition, not argued), whereas the platform camp has the stronger concrete case for the citizen-developer safety problem — you cannot distribute production-data guardrails to non-engineers. The tension is partly reconcilable: Ocytko himself concedes a platform tight enough for non-engineers may be too constraining for expert engineers' identity and innovation, which is exactly the freedom Edwards-Alexander is protecting. The unresolved question is whether one platform can be both.
How far should software be automated — toward the fully-autonomous dark/software factory, or held back to bounded autonomy with humans in the loop?
from: What is the future of software development in the agentic/AI age?
  • Why did Martin Fowler invite 60 engineers to a ski resort? (Ivett Ördög): Full automation is genuinely viable in context and she is 'ironically' being pulled toward the dark-factory camp.
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): The aspirational endpoint is a largely self-evolving software factory; humans build and operate the backbone rather than the application.
  • AI Is Changing Software Engineering. The Fundamentals Still Matter. (Abby Bangser): Bounded autonomy — keep humans in the operational loop; automated remediation must stay an intentional decision.
  • What Survived Contact (Sam Ruby): Self-healing has stalled halfway on purpose: diagnosis is automated, remediation withheld from the machine.
  • Software Design in the Agentic Age: Place Your Bets (Mathias Verraes): Dark factories are safe only for orgs that already have high test coverage, skilled engineers, and a quality culture; most should cover fundamentals first and stay revertible.
Assessment: The spectrum runs from Ördög/Ocytko (full automation is arriving and desirable) to Bangser/Ruby/Verraes (deliberately withhold the last step). The decelerationists have the better of the safety argument today — Ruby's 'nobody will cede the blast radius' and Bangser's 'removing humans removes learning' identify concrete, unmet prerequisites — but Ördög's context-dependence (backends, COBOL) shows the boundary is domain-specific, not absolute. The real disagreement is less about capability than about who absorbs the blast radius when an autonomous fix goes wrong.
Is preserving human understanding of the system a first-order value, or is comprehension safely offloadable so long as output is verifiable?
from: What is the future of software development in the agentic/AI age?
  • Fragments: February 4 (Martin Fowler (Feb 4)): Preserve understanding: it is a first-order value, and practices that force explanation defend it.
  • Fragments: February 9 (Martin Fowler (Feb 9)): Preserve understanding and craft: cognitive debt and the loss of model-building are the real costs to guard against.
  • Jimmy Nilsson's Post — reflections after the Thoughtworks 'Future of Software Development' retreat (Jimmy Nilsson): Preserve the human: 'Extreme Humanism' and being conspicuously human are load-bearing anchors.
  • We've Stopped Arguing About Whether AI Changes Software (Greg Herlein): Offloadable: the quality bar is 'is it verifiable enough,' not whether a human read or understood the code.
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): Offloadable for most builders: citizen developers need never read the code if a constrained platform makes failure modes disappear.
Assessment: The optimist-practitioner vs. skeptic-humanist axis. Fowler and Nilsson treat comprehension and craft as intrinsic goods whose loss is a harm even when output is fine; Herlein and Ocytko treat comprehension as instrumental — replaceable by verification and platform guardrails. The humanist camp has the stronger long-run argument (Fowler's cognitive-debt case and Ocytko's own 'who-guards-the-guards' problem both show verification and guardrails are themselves fallible artifacts that someone must understand), but the offloadable camp is describing what is actually happening at scale. The crux is whether verifiability can ever be robust enough to make understanding optional — which Ruby's own Goodhart gap (a passable oracle diverging from true intent) suggests it cannot fully be.
How should newly-empowered citizen developers be made safe — constrained platform, human stewardship, or supported API-first tooling?
from: What is the future of software development in the agentic/AI age?
  • Looking back at the Future of Software Engineering unconference (Bartosz Ocytko): A tightly-constrained, self-healing platform — not universal upskilling — should absorb citizen developers.
  • The "funhouse mirror": How AI reflects the hidden truths of your software pipeline (Rachel Laycock (interviewed by The New Stack)): Senior technologists must act as stewards — teaching the 'why' and building guardrails, not gatekeeping or replacing juniors.
  • AI Is Changing Software Engineering. The Fundamentals Still Matter. (Abby Bangser): Support, don't restrict — provide API-first, MCP-accessible tooling that non-engineers can safely consume.
Assessment: All three agree citizen developers are inevitable and must not be blocked, but the safety mechanism differs — encode it in a constraining platform (Ocytko), transfer it through human relationships (Laycock), or expose it as consumable interfaces (Bangser). Ocytko's platform-first case is strongest on the specific failure mode both he and Ördög name (non-engineers leaking or bypassing production-data access), which stewardship alone cannot prevent at scale. Laycock's stewardship answers a different problem — sustaining the junior-to-senior pipeline — that a platform does not. The positions are complementary in principle but compete for the same finite investment, and none supplies a concrete governance model for vibe-coded shadow IT.
