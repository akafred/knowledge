Intent Debt

The absence of externalized rationale, goals and constraints in artifacts agents can read — the one debt AI cannot pay down because the why must originate with humans.

Also known as: intent debt, Intent debt, externalize (intent), tacit intent, load-bearing rationale, cold start (of an agent session), plausible guess (about intent), externalization, cognitive artifacts

Open gaps (12)

  • incompleteThe transfer of the 'cognitive artifacts' framework from human cognition to LLM systems is asserted but not defended. Human cognitive artifacts augment a general intelligence; LLM externalization compensates for specific architectural limitations (finite context, no persistent state). The analogy may be more misleading than illuminating.
    Externalization in LLM Agents: A Unified Review of Memory, Skills, Protocols and Harness Engineering — read next: Look for critiques of anthropomorphic framings of LLM capabilities; compare with distributed systems or compiler theory as alternative analogies.
  • incompleteThe taxonomy assumes memory, skills, and protocols are 'distinct but coupled' — but the boundaries may be artificial. A skill with embedded memory and protocol-like interfaces blurs all three categories. The abstract does not address how to handle such overlap.
    Externalization in LLM Agents: A Unified Review of Memory, Skills, Protocols and Harness Engineering — read next: Check whether the full paper provides clear criteria for categorizing borderline cases.
  • uninformedThe abstract does not address whether externalization introduces new failure modes (brittleness, composability failures, versioning conflicts) that may offset reliability gains. The framing is almost entirely positive.
    Externalization in LLM Agents: A Unified Review of Memory, Skills, Protocols and Harness Engineering — read next: Look for systems engineering literature on integration risk; check if the full paper's 'open challenges' section addresses this.
  • illogicalThe historical progression 'weights -> context -> harness' is presented as a clean trajectory, but in practice these co-evolve. Larger context windows reduce the need for some externalization; better weights reduce the need for elaborate harnesses. The narrative oversimplifies.
    Externalization in LLM Agents: A Unified Review of Memory, Skills, Protocols and Harness Engineering — read next: Consider whether a co-evolutionary or dialectical framing would be more accurate than a linear progression.
  • incompleteThe recurring Osmani blind spot — third post in a row. Osmani diagnoses a structural problem (intent debt is an artifact-class agents cannot pay down; the cost is now paid per-session-per-agent) and then prescribes individual-developer disciplines. He never engages: (a) organizational incentives that actively reward visible-agent-activity over externalized intent (managers measuring spawn count, throughput pressure that captures any efficiency gain), (b) tooling vendor responsibilities (auto-generated /init flows that produce descriptive AGENTS.md rather than intent-bearing AGENTS.md; UIs that make spawning easy and rationale capture optional), (c) compensation structures that reward shipping over documenting. The individual-discipline frame puts the entire fix-burden on the developer and treats organizational/vendor pressure as background — the same move he made in 'The Orchestration Tax is You' and 'Comprehension Debt'. For Kjetil tracking this series, this is now a load-bearing limitation of Osmani's whole program, not a one-off omission.
    The Intent Debt — read next: Paul Reed 'Ironies of A^2 I^2' (already in this repo) on how orgs capture AI efficiency as throughput pressure. John Cutler on institutional overload and how teams metabolize new capabilities — particularly TBM 411-413 you've been tracking. Donella Meadows' leverage points: rule changes and goal changes outrank parameter changes. Greger Wedel-style 'leadership when the org pressures the opposite direction' on what happens when an individual-practice prescription meets organizational counter-pressure.
  • incompleteThe operational test for detecting intent debt — 'helplessness, not friction' — is phenomenologically sharp but methodologically thin. No metric is proposed. No instrumentation is suggested. No team-level practice (e.g., 'in code review, mark every line nobody can defend; intent debt = count(undefended) / count(reviewed)') is offered. For a practical post, the diagnostic side is underbuilt relative to the prescriptive side — and the prescriptions cannot be targeted without measurement.
    The Intent Debt — read next: DORA-style metrics literature for how engineering practice gets measured; Forsgren/Humble/Kim 'Accelerate' on lead metrics for invisible properties; SPACE framework (GitHub/Microsoft research) for measuring developer experience without distortion; possibly Storey's own follow-up research papers on triple-debt measurement.
  • incompleteOsmani concedes 'you can't write down everything' and resolves it as a load-bearingness triage problem — but never gives a criterion for identifying load-bearing decisions IN THE MOMENT (before you know which ones will matter later). This is the operationally hard part. Empirically, much rationale that turns out to be load-bearing did not look load-bearing at decision time. The 'capture the load-bearing why' guidance therefore risks being well-intentioned but unimplementable, or implementable only by people who already have the seniority to predict consequence — i.e., precisely the people whose departure creates the problem in the first place.
    The Intent Debt — read next: Naur 'Programming as Theory Building' on what kinds of decisions are reconstructible from code and which are not. Polanyi on tacit vs explicit knowledge. Decision-research literature (Annie Duke, your Thinking in Bets reading) on the inability to predict consequence and how that affects pre-commitment to recording rationale.
  • incompleteOsmani treats existing artifact formats (specs, AGENTS.md, ADRs, learnings files) as adequate substrates for externalized intent. But intent has rich structure that current formats are flat against: constraints with priorities, non-negotiables vs preferences, time-bound vs principled choices, contingent vs invariant rationale. He gestures at AGENTS.md being 'the conventions, the 'we don't do it this way because…' — but doesn't interrogate whether prose-form artifacts can actually carry the relational structure between intents that an agent would need to reason about. A fully developed treatment would distinguish intent-as-data (structured, queryable, machine-readable) from intent-as-prose (current state).
    The Intent Debt — read next: Knowledge-representation literature (description logics, semantic web). Architecture-decision-record research on what ADRs actually capture vs miss (Michael Nygard's original posts, plus more recent empirical work). Yourdon & Constantine on structured design and what counts as a 'design rationale' (already in the repo).
  • incompleteThe second vignette — tests encoded previous behavior, not intent — implies a fourth paydown lever Osmani does not develop: intent-specifying tests. Specification-by-example, BDD/Gherkin, property-based testing with named invariants, and contract tests can all carry intent in a form an agent can both READ and execute against. The prescription section moves entirely to prose-form artifacts and misses this. For Kjetil specifically — given Kent Beck's TCR work and TDD lineage — this is the most under-developed cell of Osmani's argument.
    The Intent Debt — read next: Kent Beck's TDD and TCR writing (already in your queue). Specification-by-example literature (Gojko Adzic). Property-based testing's claim to encode intent (Hypothesis docs; QuickCheck papers). David Heinemeier Hansson's writing on tests as documentation. Possibly Andre Arko or Drew Petersen on contract testing as intent capture.
  • incompleteStorey's Triple Debt Model is imported uncritically as the organizing taxonomy. But it is not obviously exhaustive: process debt (the way work flows through the team), dependency debt (third-party choices whose rationale and constraints aren't recorded), data debt (schema and semantic-meaning decisions), and organizational debt (decisions about team structure and ownership) are all candidate fourth (fifth, sixth) categories. The independence claim that does so much work in the post depends on the taxonomy being clean — if intent debt overlaps significantly with one of these others, the agent-paydown asymmetry argument may not generalize cleanly.
    The Intent Debt — read next: Margaret-Anne Storey's actual arXiv paper (the post cites it) to see whether she addresses exhaustiveness; broader software-engineering-research literature on debt classifications (Avgeriou, Kruchten); Tom DeMarco's 'Slack' for a process-debt angle.
  • incompleteOsmani does not engage what happens at the codebase / multi-team scale. Prescriptions are local to a repo (AGENTS.md per repo, ADRs per service, learnings file per session). But the intent that matters most often spans services (an architectural principle), teams (a security posture), and time (a business invariant established long ago). At that scale, prose artifacts in each repo do not compose — they fragment. The post would benefit from an explicit acknowledgement that local-repo paydown does not solve the cross-cutting case, and a gesture at what would.
    The Intent Debt — read next: Vlad Khononov on coupling in distributed systems (already shortlisted in your library); the architectural-decision-management literature (Olaf Zimmermann); Chris Richardson's microservices.io patterns on shared architectural concerns; Wardley mapping for cross-cutting intent.
  • incompleteThe series-level pattern is itself worth naming. Comprehension debt → orchestration tax → intent debt is now a tightly linked triptych, and Osmani treats them as cumulatively framing one diagnosis. But he never names the underlying disease. The candidate name — and the missing post — is something like 'externalization debt' or 'legibility debt': all three debts are downstream of a single failure to make the relevant signal (code, mental model, rationale) readable by something other than the senior engineer's head. The post that names that underlying disease, distinguishes it from the symptom-class, and asks what an externalization-first development culture looks like, has not been written. For Kjetil tracking the series, that is the post to ask Osmani for next.
