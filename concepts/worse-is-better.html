Worse Is Better

Richard Gabriel's observation that simpler "worse" technologies often win adoption over more complete or "correct" ones by prioritizing implementation and interface simplicity and survivability over completeness — invoked to explain why inferior-but-simpler options (JSON, VHS) dominate superior alternatives.

Also known as: worse is better, Worse Is Better, worse-is-better, Richard Gabriel's worse-is-better, New Jersey style vs MIT style, popular despite superior alternatives, simplicity of interface over completeness, VHS versus Betamax

    The AI-model market is flipping from US to Chinese dominance in real production usage, and the decisive driver is per-task cost — decisive precisely because the agentic era multiplies calls per task, so distribution consolidates on the cheapest 'good-enough' models faster than any residual US capability lead can matter.
  • Podcast: Increasing Users' Data Agency: From BlueSky's AT Protocol to the Local-First Software Movement Martin Kleppmann · 2026-07-06 source ↗ Reader 🖥️
    Users gain real agency over their data not by eliminating servers or fully decentralizing but by adopting open, standard protocols that keep data portable and provider-independent — the common principle Kleppmann traces from the unbundling of monolithic databases into modular building blocks, through BlueSky's AT Protocol, to the local-first software movement.
  • Worse is better: JSON versus XML Mark Seemann · 2026-07-06 source ↗ Reader 🖥️
    Despite JSON's popularity, XML is the superior data-interchange format in most respects — because its mature ecosystem of standards (schemas, versioning, querying, streaming, even sum types) delivers real capability that JSON only reinvents piecemeal, while JSON's supposed advantages (informality, size, readability) are illusory, marginal, or equally achievable in XML — making JSON another case of 'worse is better'.

Open gaps (10)

  • incompleteAdoption economics of decentralisation are left open. Kleppmann concedes that real decentralisation depends on funded alternative providers actually existing, but never examines why they would be funded or what sustains a commodity, easily-substitutable provider — the very portability that empowers users also destroys the provider's pricing power. Without a viable business model for the exit option, 'agency via guaranteed switching' risks staying hypothetical, as it largely did for email/XMPP federation.
    Podcast: Increasing Users' Data Agency: From BlueSky's AT Protocol to the Local-First Software Movement — read next: Two-sided platform and commons-funding economics; the decline of federated protocols (XMPP, RSS) despite openness; Cory Doctorow on interoperability mandates as the missing forcing function.
  • incompleteThe incentive against open sync protocols is unaddressed. The argument assumes vendors will adopt open, portable sync (like Git's) even though proprietary lock-in is precisely what makes SaaS valuable. No mechanism — market pressure, regulation, a standards body — is offered that would make an incumbent like Google standardise away its own moat, and the Git example itself shows incumbents keep the non-portable parts (GitHub issues, wikis) closed.
    Podcast: Increasing Users' Data Agency: From BlueSky's AT Protocol to the Local-First Software Movement — read next: EU Digital Markets Act interoperability provisions; the history of why Google Docs never opened its sync; the switching-cost/moat economics already in this corpus (extract-mellbye-wrapper-vs-infrastructure).
  • incompleteEnterprise, regulatory, and security fit of local-first is barely touched. With the primary copy of data spread across many user devices, questions of enterprise governance, GDPR / data-residency, audit trails, device loss or theft, and access control at scale go largely unexamined — Kleppmann names end-to-end encryption and decentralised access control only as work still in progress. The hard organisational tradeoffs of device-held data are not weighed against the user-agency benefits.
    Podcast: Increasing Users' Data Agency: From BlueSky's AT Protocol to the Local-First Software Movement — read next: Data-sovereignty sources in this corpus; EU AI Act / GDPR data-residency obligations; enterprise endpoint-security and MDM literature for the device-loss and access-control problems.
  • incompleteThe hard tradeoffs of CRDTs and local-first are presented as a near-free win. Automerge's costs — metadata/storage overhead, merge semantics that need not match user intent, the loss of a server as an authoritative point for validation and permissions — and Kleppmann's own admissions that peer-to-peer is 'really hard' and that encryption/access control are unfinished, are not balanced against the offline/speed/version-control benefits.
    Podcast: Increasing Users' Data Agency: From BlueSky's AT Protocol to the Local-First Software Movement — read next: Ink & Switch's original local-first essay on the 'seven ideals' and their unmet challenges; CRDT literature on garbage collection, storage overhead, and interleaving anomalies.
  • incompleteWho actually benefits and who bears the cost is not asked. The framing centres user empowerment, but shifting storage, compute, conflict-resolution, and support burdens onto clients and app developers — while removing the server's authoritative control — may not translate into real agency for a median, non-technical user. 'Agency' that is only exercisable by switching providers or self-hosting may be agency mostly for the already-technical.
    Podcast: Increasing Users' Data Agency: From BlueSky's AT Protocol to the Local-First Software Movement — read next: Hirschman's 'Exit, Voice, and Loyalty' on the limits of exit-as-empowerment; empirical uptake of GDPR Article 20 data-portability rights; digital-literacy research on who can actually exercise portability.
  • incompleteThe article asserts that ecosystem maturity/standardization makes XML 'superior' but never defines the criteria of superiority or weighs them against JSON's decisive real-world advantages — network effects, JavaScript-ecosystem gravity, and cognitive simplicity — which are the actual reasons formats win adoption. 'Superiority' is treated as a property of the format in isolation rather than of the format-in-its-ecosystem.
    Worse is better: JSON versus XML — read next: Read Richard Gabriel's original 'The Rise of Worse Is Better' — it argues that the 'worse' option's simplicity of interface is itself the deeper virtue, directly challenging the 'why reinvent the wheel' framing — alongside the network-effects and standards-adoption literature.
  • illogicalThe recurring 'why reinvent the wheel?' treats JSON's re-implementation of XML capabilities as pure waste, but the very fact that the ecosystem chose to reinvent a SIMPLER wheel is evidence that XML's richer wheel was mis-sized for the common case. The argument thus assumes what it needs to prove — that XML's heavier standards are worth their cost — and this directly contradicts the Gabriel 'worse is better' thesis Seemann himself invokes as his framing device (a tension the article never acknowledges).
    Worse is better: JSON versus XML — read next: Engage Gabriel's actual claim that simplicity of the common case can rightly dominate completeness, and read the design-fundamentals argument that unused capability is a cost, not a latent asset.
  • incompleteThe size argument is dismissed too quickly — 'when performance really is a consideration, JSON may be too big, too' waves away rather than engages the large middle band of high-throughput text APIs (aggregate bandwidth, parse cost, streaming) and the schema-driven binary formats that are actually chosen when neither text format fits.
    Worse is better: JSON versus XML — read next: Read the schema-driven binary serialization literature — Protocol Buffers, Apache Avro, Cap'n Proto, MessagePack — to see where the real interchange-format frontier lies beyond the XML-vs-JSON text axis.
  • incompleteThe Hyrum's-law/implicit-contract point is used only against JSON, but it cuts both ways: XML with an XSD still couples consumers to undocumented behaviors, and a schema is not a free lunch — XSD complexity, schema evolution, and versioning cost are never weighed against the benefit claimed.
    Worse is better: JSON versus XML — read next: Read on schema evolution and compatibility (e.g. Avro/Protobuf compatibility rules, and API-versioning practice) to see the ongoing cost side of the schema ledger the article omits.
  • incompleteThe configuration-file case rests almost entirely on JSON's lack of standard comments, yet never mentions YAML, TOML, or HCL — the formats that actually displaced both XML and JSON for configuration — which makes the 'XML for config' recommendation weaker and more dated than presented.
    Worse is better: JSON versus XML — read next: Compare TOML and YAML directly on the same criteria (comments, readability, schema tooling) to see why the config-format question was largely settled without either XML or JSON winning.
