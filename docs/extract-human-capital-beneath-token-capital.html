What is the harder human-capital problem beneath token capital?

Turing Post · email · 2026-06-15 Reader 🖥️

AI Labor DisruptionCoordination CostEnterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessHierarchy & Org ChartsAgencySmall Teams & CompressionTalent DensityToken Capital
Beneath Nadella's visible 'token capital vs. human capital' story lies a harder problem — the human capital big firms manufacture best (senior, politically-fluent ladder-climbers) is exactly the kind that wastes token capital, while the frontier-moving talent that would use it well is what corporate structure selects against — so the scarcest capital in the AI age is neither human nor machine but an organizational structure porous enough to hold uncorporate talent and corporate-scale compute together without flattening either.

practical · Analytical newsletter op-ed — a reaction piece that builds a structural argument on top of Satya Nadella's post about token vs. human capital. — Scoped to the ~927-word opening editorial of Turing Post FOD#156; the rest of the newsletter (news roundup, papers, model releases) is excluded from this analysis, so word_count reflects the editorial, not the ~10k-word email. Practical because it lands on a prescription (build a porous structure), but it is carried by a diagnostic argument about how corporate structure selects talent.

Key terms

  • token capital Token Capital
    Adopted from Nadella: the AI capability a firm builds and owns — its models, agents, traces, evals, workflow memory and internal loops — treated as a compounding proprietary asset (a stock), not merely a spend rate.
  • human capital
    The judgment, taste, relationships, context and pattern recognition of a firm's people — but critically split by the essay into two kinds: ladder-climbing veterans and frontier-moving obsessives, only one of which uses token capital well.
  • porous structure
    A coinage for an organizational form permeable enough to let 'uncorporate' people stay uncorporate while giving them corporate-scale compute, data, distribution and responsibility — neither crushing nor domesticating them.
  • ladder creatures / uncorporate people
    Two opposed talent archetypes: 'ladder creatures' who mastered the internal language of quarterly planning and climbed the org chart, versus 'uncorporate' researchers, quants and builders who 'came in sideways' and are 'allergic to permission.'
  • optimize the org chart, not the loss curve
    A compressed contrast between the veteran who improves his political position (the org chart) and the frontier-mover who improves the model (the loss curve) — the one-line statement of which human capital wastes token capital and which uses it.

Arguments

Nadella is right that token and human capital compound, but he omits the decisive qualifier — which human capital — because the kind big firms manufacture best is precisely the kind that wastes token capital. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The best current models were built by bold, uncorporate people who succeeded in spite of, not because of, their corporate employers.
  • The politically-fluent veteran a big firm manufactures best optimizes the org chart, not the loss curve.
  • The irreverent frontier-mover is the kind a rigid corporate structure selects against and pushes out.
Implicit premises:
  • The value of human capital depends on its type, not just its quantity or seniority — human capital is not a homogeneous, additive stock.
  • A firm's recruiting and promotion machinery is a selection filter that systematically favors one talent type over the other.
  • Token capital only converts to value when wielded by frontier-movers; in the hands of org-chart optimizers it is wasted.
Inferences:
  • If the talent a firm accumulates is the wasteful type and the useful type is filtered out, then 'more human capital' can lower, not raise, the return on token capital.
  • Therefore the visible compounding thesis conceals an inverse risk for exactly the firms that look strongest on the old scoreboard.
The scarcest and most decisive capital in the AI age is an organizational structure porous enough to hold uncorporate talent and corporate-scale compute together without flattening either. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Frontier talent is crushed or domesticated by rigid corporate structure but needs corporate-scale compute, data and distribution to do its best work.
  • Firms with the deepest benches and proudest org charts may be worst positioned because every such asset is also a reason not to move.
  • Firms that keep promoting their best ladder-climbers keep wondering why their token capital runs in circles.
Implicit premises:
  • Neither talent alone nor compute alone is the binding constraint; the binding constraint is the structure that mediates between them.
  • It is possible in principle to design a structure that grants corporate-scale resources without imposing the committees and incentives that sand talent down.
Inferences:
  • If both human and token capital are abundant but only a rare structure can combine them productively, then that structure is the actual scarce resource.
  • Therefore the competitive winner is the firm that becomes porous, not the one that accumulates the most of either capital.

Narratives

  • Satya Nadella is cast as 'the Warrior, who can crush your skull,' who suddenly sits down on a stone and turns into The Thinker, 'at the edge' — staring at the possibility that his greatest asset and his heaviest anchor are the same people.
    → Dramatizes the essay's central claim through the most powerful possible witness: even the leader of the dominant incumbent has recognized the trap, which lends the diagnosis authority and stakes.
  • The frontier-builders are drawn as sideways geniuses: Demis Hassabis as Ender Wiggin 'playing and fighting Google's corporate game,' Jeff Dean who 'like Chuck Norris, just looks at the model until it starts converging,' and Liang Wenfeng who ran a hedge fund before DeepSeek — succeeding in spite of their corporations.
    → Concretizes the abstract 'not ladder creatures' category with named exemplars, making the talent split vivid and the 'in spite of, not because of' claim feel evidenced rather than asserted.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What does Nadella mean by token capital and human capital, and is he right that they compound?
    The essay restates both definitions faithfully and agrees they compound, but argues Nadella is 'too polite, or too strategic' to say WHICH human capital compounds — because the kind big firms manufacture best is the kind that wastes token capital.
  • ✅ Under what conditions can corporate America hold frontier talent without domesticating or exhausting them — or does the age of AI reward a shape that looks less like a company and more like a band, a lab, a small huddle of fanatics with infinite compute behind them?
    The essay answers with the 'porous structure' thesis — a form that grants corporate-scale compute and responsibility while letting uncorporate people stay uncorporate — while leaning toward the band/lab shape as the emerging winner. The answer is named but not operationalized.
  • ⭕ Does this diagnosis apply to the ordinary enterprise that BUYS token capital (a telco, a bank) as much as to the frontier lab that BUILDS it?
    Left unresolved. The essay slides between 'the best models were built by uncorporate people' and 'corporate America / almost every enterprise,' treating frontier labs and token-capital consumers as one problem.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe lab-vs-enterprise conflation. The argument is strongest for frontier labs that BUILD token capital (models, evals, loops require irreverent frontier-movers) and weakest for the telco, bank or typical enterprise that BUYS and absorbs it. A firm that consumes AI rather than pushing the frontier may not need 'permission-averse, loudly-wrong' talent at all — for it the binding constraint is more likely absorption, integration and process legibility than porousness for mavericks. The essay treats the two as one problem and never tests whether its prescription even applies to the majority of the 54% of employment it invokes.
    Read next: Read against the enterprise-AI-adoption literature that separates building vs. buying/absorbing AI capability (e.g. forward-deployed-engineer and readiness models): does a token-capital consumer need frontier-mover porousness, or is its real obstacle organizational absorption, and would importing 'uncorporate' talent even help?
  • incomplete'Porous structure' is named as the answer but never operationalized. The essay supplies no mechanism, org-design pattern, incentive structure, or worked example of what porousness looks like, nor how a firm hands 'corporate-scale compute + responsibility' to uncorporate people without recreating the committees and procurement logic that sand them down. The prescription is a direction, not a design.
    Read next: Read toward concrete structural patterns that could fill the mechanism — small-teams/tiny-core bets, autonomous-team designs, skunkworks and lab-within-a-company structures — and test which actually preserve irreverence at corporate scale.
  • illogicalThe essay's own evidence strains against its thesis. Demis Hassabis built Gemini INSIDE Google, a corporate giant — 'in spite of' it, yes, but still inside it and still winning — which shows rigid incumbents CAN produce frontier results, cutting against the flat claim that structure 'selects frontier talent out.' By naming only the winning obsessives (Hassabis, Dean, Liang Wenfeng) it also survivorship-selects, saying nothing about the uncorporate people who were in fact crushed. So 'structure selects them out' is asserted more strongly than the evidence, which really shows 'structure taxes them, but some still win.'
    Read next: Read comparatively for cases where incumbents DID retain and productively hold frontier talent, to separate the strong claim ('structure filters them out') from the weaker, better-supported one ('structure imposes a heavy tax they sometimes overcome').
