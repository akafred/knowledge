AI is reshaping the developer's relationship to understanding: it can erode comprehension of the systems we build, is wisely used to navigate toward an answer but never trusted for the answer itself, and may make code cheap enough that the specification becomes the durable engineering artifact.

mixed · mixed — A deliberately fragmentary post — the author states he cannot truthfully form the ideas into a coherent narrative — collecting independent observations from a Thoughtworks Open Space event; the software-and-AI fragments (understanding, LLM navigation, spec-only libraries) carry the intellectual core, alongside two off-theme asides on ad-funded AI assistants and civic resistance in Minnesota.

Key terms

  • fragment (fragment format)
    A short post that shares an idea as-is, without working it into the coherent narrative of a full article — the author's chosen vehicle for event thoughts he cannot unify
  • navigate the way to the answer
    Using an LLM to orient, locate, and explain — to guide you toward an answer — as opposed to trusting the LLM's output as the answer itself
  • Very (Very) Large Code Base (VVLCB)
    A codebase so large that no responsible human can fully understand it, making LLM-assisted comprehension a net addition rather than a loss
  • software library without code
    Drew Breunig's idea (relayed here) that a library of the future could ship as only a specification and tests, with implementations generated per language on demand
  • gilded cage (advertising)
    An ad-funded business model that is highly lucrative but binds a company to advertisers who can withdraw in downturns and gain leverage to influence or censor content

Arguments

The right way to use an LLM is as a navigator that guides you toward an answer, not as a source to be trusted for the answer itself. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Fully trusting the answer an LLM gives you is foolishness.
  • An SRE uses the LLM to understand what a codebase he cannot fully grasp is doing and to navigate to its crucial parts.
  • That navigational use is valuable even to someone who could never verify the whole system independently.
Implicit premises:
  • LLMs are unreliable on final correctness but useful for orientation and location.
  • Checking or acting on where the LLM points you is safer than accepting what the LLM concludes.
Inferences:
  • Therefore treat the LLM's output as a route to be followed and verified, not as a verdict to be trusted.
Practices that force you to explain your work help counteract AI-driven loss of understanding, because explaining is how the explainer learns. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • A value of pair programming is that you must regularly explain things to your pair.
  • Explaining is an important part of learning for the person doing the explaining.
  • One of the best ways to learn something is to try to teach it.
Implicit premises:
  • AI reduces the occasions that force a developer to explain or reconstruct their reasoning.
  • Preserving explanation-forcing practices therefore preserves comprehension that AI use would otherwise erode.
Inferences:
  • So keeping practices like pair programming defends developer understanding against the comprehension-loss worry.
If coding becomes free, libraries can ship as specifications and tests only, making the spec the primary artifact. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Breunig built a working timestamp-to-phrase library from a 500-line spec and 500 lines of YAML tests.
  • The same spec generated implementations in seven languages.
  • The experiment was undertaken to explore what software engineering looks like when coding is free.
Implicit premises:
  • Implementations generated from a good-enough spec are interchangeable and effectively disposable.
  • A language-independent spec-plus-tests is more durable than any single generated implementation.
Inferences:
  • Therefore, under free coding, the spec and its tests carry the enduring engineering value.

Narratives

  • An SRE responsible for a Very (Very) Large Code Base is not worried about not understanding LLM-written code, because he already cannot understand the codebase he owns; he values that the LLM helps him understand what the code is doing and navigate to the crucial parts.
    → Grounds the LLM-as-navigator thesis and reframes the comprehension-loss worry as least pressing where comprehension was already impossible
  • Drew Breunig built a library that converts timestamps into phrases like '3 hours ago' from a 500-line markdown spec and 500 lines of YAML tests, generating implementations in seven languages.
    → A tangible thought experiment making the 'code is free' / spec-only-library future concrete
  • An ex-Googler explains that advertising was a 'gilded cage' for Google — very lucrative but tying the company to advertisers who pull out in downturns and gain power to influence content.
    → Illustrates the structural risk of ad-funded business models, applied to the prospect of advertising inside LLM chats
  • Minnesotans resist masked paramilitaries peacefully — their principal weapons blowing whistles and filming videos — demonstrating neighborliness and social cohesion.
    → A moral-political aside praising civic resistance; off-theme relative to the software fragments

Questions addressed

  • ⭕ Does using AI erode developers' understanding of the systems they build?
    Acknowledged as a real worry; two partial mitigations are offered anecdotally — explanation-forcing practices like pair programming, and using the LLM to comprehend code you could not grasp anyway.
  • ✅ How should a developer relate to an LLM's output?
    As a navigator to be followed toward an answer, not an oracle to be trusted for the answer.
  • ⭕ What does software engineering look like when coding is free?
    Explored, not answered — a spec-only library is presented as a tangible thought experiment suggesting the spec-plus-tests becomes the durable artifact.
  • ✅ What are the risks of advertising-funded AI assistants?
    Conflicts of interest (kickback-driven recommendations) and advertiser capture over content, framed as a lucrative but confining 'gilded cage'.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe comprehension-loss worry is raised and then softened by the SRE anecdote, but the piece never distinguishes genuine understanding from borrowed or illusory understanding: the SRE 'understands what the code is doing' via the LLM, yet whether that is real comprehension or offloaded confidence is not examined.
    Read next: Read on cognitive/comprehension debt and the illusion of explanatory depth — when LLM-mediated navigation deepens versus substitutes for real understanding.
  • incompleteThe 'navigate the way to the answer, don't trust the answer' aphorism gives no criterion for separating the route from the destination — the navigation output is itself a fallible LLM answer that can point you at the wrong part of the code, so the guidance risks being circular in practice.
    Read next: Look at verification patterns, feedback loops, and evals for the navigational (not just the final) outputs of an LLM.
  • incompleteThe spec-only library is demonstrated on a trivial, cleanly specifiable domain (timestamp-to-phrase formatting); the piece does not address whether spec-plus-tests scale to domains with emergent, under-specifiable, or performance-critical behavior, where the spec may grow as complex as the code it replaces.
    Read next: Read on the limits of spec-driven development and where specifications stop being cheaper than implementations.
  • uninformedDiscussing AI-driven loss of understanding, the fragment does not engage the existing vocabulary of comprehension/cognitive debt or cognitive surrender that names precisely this failure mode and its accrual over time.
    Read next: Bring the comprehension-debt and cognitive-surrender literature to bear on the pair-programming and LLM-navigation fragments.
