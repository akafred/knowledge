Generative AI in the Real World: Aishwarya Naresh Reganti on Making AI Work in Production

Ben Lorica and Aishwarya Naresh Reganti · podcast · 2026-04-16 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Agent HarnessAgent-Native DesignDecision-Making PracticesEnterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessEvalsForward-Deployed EngineerHuman-in-the-LoopAI Workflow Patterns
Shipping generative AI in production requires inverting the traditional software effort profile — spending most resources on post-deployment calibration, data understanding, cross-functional collaboration, and workflow-level design rather than on initial build — because the natural-language interface opens an unbounded error surface that only continuous human-in-the-loop refinement can tame.

practical · mixed — primarily expository with argumentative elements — Conversational interview between a podcast host (Ben Lorica) and a practitioner-consultant (Aishwarya Reganti). Reganti supplies most substantive claims from direct enterprise consulting experience. Lorica steers topics and occasionally offers contrasting framings. The conversation is structured around recurring practitioner pain points rather than a single linear argument.

Propositions

  • explicitAI product development inverts the traditional software effort split: 20% building, 80% calibration.
  • explicitThe natural-language interface of AI products opens a much larger surface area for errors than deterministic UI workflows.
  • explicitThe single most important thing anyone building AI products can do is take the time to look at their data.
  • explicit⚡ disputed & corroboratedYou cannot automate the understanding of your own data by having an LLM evaluate itself — manual human effort is required to set up agents properly.
    supports ←AI Companies Are Hiring More · p10 — Ford rehiring 350 veteran engineers to fix and retrain its AI tools is concrete evidence that human expertise cannot be automated out of AI setup.
    contradicts ←Kaggle is making AI benchmark creation effortless · p8 — Kaggle assumes AI agents can rigorously author evals, including self-evaluation; Reganti insists LLM self-evaluation cannot replace manual human effort.
  • explicitTraditional software engineers excel at design thinking, scalable architecture, and treating the model as an API with proper security and system design around it.
  • explicitThe term 'evals' is overloaded and hyped to the point of confusion, conflating model-level benchmarks with product-level evaluation processes.
  • explicitEvaluations must be actionable — if you cannot derive a concrete improvement action from an eval result, it is not useful.
  • explicitWhen building AI products, you should sequence optimization as: low effort first (establish feasibility), then performance, then cost and latency.
  • explicitPredictable reliability (knowing which 10% you get right) is more valuable than higher but unpredictable accuracy.
  • explicitIf a task can be solved with a workflow (with human audit points), do not make it autonomous.
  • explicitHuman judges should be used first for evaluation; transition to LLM judges only after rubrics can be codified from sufficient human-annotated examples with reasoning.
    refinesHarness design for long-running application development · p4 — Reganti conditions criteria-based LLM grading: only after rubrics are codified from sufficient human-annotated examples should LLM judges replace human judges.
  • explicit70-80% of LevelUp Labs' enterprise engagements are productivity and ops focused, not customer-facing, primarily because of trust and reliability concerns.
  • explicitcorroboratedFrontier models are converging on capability; competitive advantage now lies in the harness and application layer rather than model selection.
    supports ←The new GPT-5.6 family: Luna, Terra, Sol · p6 — Hands-on parity between GPT-5.6 Sol and Claude Fable 5 on complex coding is direct evidence that frontier models are converging on capability, per p13.
  • explicitcorroboratedModel personality differences (not capability differences) are the real barrier to model portability, and these will not converge because vendors treat them as competitive differentiators.
    supports ←DeepSeek v4, and the end of the OpenAI/Microsoft AGI clause · p12 — OpenAI's own don't-migrate-prompts guidance for GPT-5.5 is direct evidence that model personality differences block portability.
  • explicitEarly-career people who are 'agent-native' have a genuine hiring advantage because they naturally delegate to and orchestrate AI agents in ways experienced workers do not.
  • implicitThe valued professional skill is shifting from execution to design and orchestration, because execution is increasingly handled by agents.
  • explicitCompanies that fail to involve governance and compliance stakeholders early in AI projects end up scrapping entire projects.
  • explicitCross-functional collaboration must be tighter and more continuous for AI products than for traditional software — the feedback loop must be shared across engineering, SMEs, and testing.

Arguments

AI product development requires fundamentally different effort allocation than traditional software (the 80-20 flip)
Explicit premises:
  • AI products use natural-language interfaces, which allow users to interact in unbounded ways (P2)
  • User behavior changes over time, as seen with ChatGPT from 2022 to present
  • Models themselves change, requiring realignment
Implicit premises:
  • Traditional software's deterministic interfaces constrain user behavior, limiting the error surface
  • The cost of post-deployment iteration in AI is inherently higher because errors are harder to anticipate

Gaps

  • incompleteThe 80-20 flip is asserted from consulting experience but not quantified. No data is offered on what 'calibration' costs in practice relative to build — the ratio could vary enormously by domain, and the 80-20 framing may be more rhetorical than empirical.
  • incompleteThe conversation assumes enterprises have the organizational capacity to run tight cross-functional feedback loops, but does not address the structural barriers (incentives, tooling, culture) that prevent most organizations from achieving this.
    closed byYour AI strategy has a trust problem, not a tooling problem — Names the structural barriers the gap said were unaddressed: titles as permission structures, expensive-because-slow decision loops, and leadership distrust are why most orgs cannot run the tight cross-functional feedback loops AI products require (p1-p3, p11).
  • uninformedThe legal and compliance discussion is acknowledged as outside Aishwarya's expertise. Given that governance failures can kill finished projects (as her own anecdote shows), the treatment is surprisingly thin — no frameworks, regulations, or concrete practices are discussed.
  • illogicalAishwarya argues that model personality differences will not converge because vendors treat them as competitive differentiators, but also argues that model capabilities are converging rapidly. The distinction between 'capability' and 'personality' is never defined rigorously — prompt sensitivity, output style, and error patterns could plausibly converge as capabilities do.
  • incompleteThe claim that early-career 'agent-native' workers have a hiring advantage is supported only by Aishwarya's personal hiring experience at a small consultancy. Whether this generalizes to larger enterprises with different incentive structures is unexamined.
  • incompleteThe optimization pyramid (effort -> performance -> cost -> latency) is offered as a sequential framework but no guidance is given on when to stop optimizing performance and move to cost/latency, or how to handle cases where the two conflict fundamentally.

Closes gaps elsewhere

  • Ashutosh Singh's Post · g4 — Describes a collaborative eval-dataset creation process — human annotation with reasoning codified into rubrics, inside a continuous cross-functional feedback loop spanning engineering, SMEs, and testing — rather than PMs authoring golden examples alone.
