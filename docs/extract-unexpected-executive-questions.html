When an Executive Asks You an Unexpected Question

Melody Wilding · rss · 2026-05-05 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Managing Up & InfluencePrompting as Communication
When an executive asks you an unexpected question, you should diagnose which of three underlying needs — reassurance, guidance, or action — is driving the question, and respond to that need rather than providing exhaustive factual answers.

practical · argumentative — A practical text arguing for a specific communication strategy when questioned by executives. The entire piece is in service of 'you should do X differently' — reframe how you respond to executive questions by diagnosing the underlying need rather than dumping information.

Key terms

  • Reassurance Managing Up & Influence
    An executive's need to confirm that a situation is under control and there will be no surprises — the question 'Is this under control?' expressed indirectly through specific factual questions
  • Guidance Prompting as Communication
    An executive's need for help forming a mental model of a situation — the question 'How should I be thinking about this?' — where they want your interpretation, not raw data
  • Action
    An executive's need to understand what they personally must do — the question 'What do I need to do?' — where they are assessing their own responsibility and required next steps

  • explicitProviding exhaustive factual answers to executive questions is counterproductive even when the facts are correctconf: high
  • explicitCorrectly diagnosing the underlying need and responding to it makes you appear more influential and effectiveconf: high

Arguments

You should diagnose the underlying need before answering an executive's unexpected question strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Executives ask questions that have a hidden underlying need (reassurance, guidance, or action)
  • Answering the literal question with exhaustive data fails to satisfy the exec, who keeps digging
  • Matching your response to the underlying need produces better outcomes
Implicit premises:
  • Executive communication is systematically indirect — they ask about specifics when they want something broader
  • The cost of misdiagnosis (answering the wrong need) is lower than the cost of defaulting to data-dumping
  • There is enough signal in the context to reliably diagnose which of the three needs applies
Inferences:
  • Organizations that trained all presenters in this framework would see shorter, more productive executive meetings
  • If executives are systematically indirect, the same framework might apply to all upward communication, not just unexpected questions

Narratives

  • A professional presents a project roadmap to leadership. Halfway through, the business unit head asks about a legacy system migration and then a December pricing experiment. The presenter provides detailed data each time, but the exec keeps asking more questions.
    → Demonstrates that competent, well-prepared professionals can still fail by answering the literal question rather than the underlying need. The fact that the presenter has the data and delivers it correctly, yet the exec is unsatisfied, is the key dramatic tension that motivates the framework.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why do executives keep asking follow-up questions even when you give them accurate, detailed answers?
    Because the literal question is not the real question — they are seeking reassurance, guidance, or action, and data alone does not satisfy any of these.
  • ✅ How should you respond when put on the spot by an executive?
    Diagnose which of the three underlying needs is at play, then respond with the corresponding template (lead with takeaway, recommend an approach, or specify actions/timelines).
  • ⭕ How do you reliably distinguish between the three needs in real time?

Gaps
