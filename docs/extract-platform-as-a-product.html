Platform as a Product: Delivering Value While Balancing Competing Priorities

Ben Linders · rss · 2026-04-16 source 🖥️

Platform EngineeringTechnical Debt
Internal software platforms must be treated as living products -- with product mindset, clear ownership, two-sided contribution models, and continuous investment -- or they will decay into the same centralised bottlenecks organisations adopted DevOps to escape.

practical · expository — This is a conference talk summary with Q&A, written by a journalist. It exposes Abby Bangser's arguments for treating platforms as products but does not develop original arguments itself. The form is expository reporting of another person's argumentative content.

Key terms

  • platform as a product Platform Engineering
    Treating an internal software platform with the same disciplines applied to customer-facing products: roadmaps, developer feedback loops, investment in quality and usability, clear ownership, and active adoption management
  • platform decay Platform Engineering
    A gradual buildup of ad-hoc tools, undocumented processes, manual steps, and inconsistent patterns that erode a platform's value over time -- distinct from deliberate technical debt because it accumulates unconsciously
  • two-sided market Platform Engineering
    A platform that must serve both consumers (teams using platform capabilities) and producers (teams contributing capabilities); scalability requires decentralising extension to producers, not just optimising consumer experience
  • walking skeleton
    A minimal end-to-end slice of platform functionality that exercises the full system from development through production deployment, used to validate assumptions and expose gaps early

Arguments

Platform teams must adopt a product mindset rather than treating platforms as pure infrastructure. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • People use platforms to achieve outcomes, not because they enjoy interfaces or APIs
  • Software development success requires more than engineering excellence -- it needs design, product thinking, and attention to security, scale, and cost
  • Teams that focus too narrowly on writing code miss the broader needs of users and stakeholders
  • Internal platforms have stricter ROI expectations and must earn adoption
Implicit premises:
  • Internal users have alternatives (build their own, use shadow IT, work around the platform) so adoption cannot be mandated effectively
  • The disciplines of product management (user research, prioritisation, roadmapping) are transferable to internal platform contexts
Inferences:
  • Therefore, platform teams need to treat capability, usability, and outcomes as first-class concerns
  • Therefore, product management practices must be applied to internal platforms
Platforms must decentralise extension to avoid recreating centralised IT bottlenecks. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Organisations introduced platform teams to move away from centralised IT models
  • Platform teams often architect for consumer experience but overlook producer experience
  • Platforms are two-sided markets that need contribution from multiple teams to scale
Implicit premises:
  • A single team cannot build and maintain all capabilities an organisation needs at the pace the organisation requires
  • The value of decentralisation in DevOps was real and should be preserved, not just relabelled
Inferences:
  • Therefore, platforms must actively enable contribution from multiple teams via stable APIs and clear contracts
  • Therefore, ignoring the producer side recreates the exact centralisation problem DevOps was meant to solve
Platform decay is a distinctive and dangerous failure mode for internal platforms. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Technical debt in platforms appears as ad-hoc tools, undocumented processes, manual steps, and inconsistent patterns
  • Internal platforms lack immediate revenue signals that would force debt repayment
  • As decay sets in, maintenance costs rise and adoption slows
  • Teams start working around the platform rather than with it
Implicit premises:
  • Working-around behavior is self-reinforcing: as more teams bypass the platform, the platform gets less investment, which causes more teams to bypass it
  • Internal platforms face a vicious cycle that customer-facing products avoid because revenue loss triggers intervention earlier
Inferences:
  • Therefore, internal platforms require deliberate, continuous investment in quality to counter the absence of natural market signals

Narratives

  • Platform teams that were meant to replace centralised IT end up recreating the same bottlenecks, slow processes, and unclear ownership.
    → Illustrates the irony of platform adoption without product thinking; makes the abstract claim concrete by invoking a widely recognised organisational pattern
  • The garden metaphor: platforms need ongoing care, planting new capabilities while pruning features that no longer provide value.
    → Reframes platforms from projects (with start/end dates) to living systems requiring continuous cultivation; implicitly argues against the 'build it and they will come' approach
  • The walking skeleton: building a minimal end-to-end slice that exercises the full system from development to production deployment.
    → Provides a concrete tactical entry point that makes the abstract product-mindset advice actionable; validates assumptions early and creates fast feedback

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How should organisations approach building and maintaining internal software platforms?
    Treat them as products with product mindset, clear ownership, roadmaps, developer feedback loops, and continuous investment in quality and usability.
  • ✅ Why do platform teams often recreate the centralised bottlenecks they were meant to eliminate?
    Because they focus on consumer experience but neglect the producer experience, failing to recognise platforms as two-sided markets that must decentralise extension.
  • ✅ Why are internal platforms especially prone to technical debt accumulation?
    They lack the immediate revenue signals of customer-facing products, so debt can grow unpaid for long periods without triggering intervention.
  • ⭕ How do you get started with platform-as-a-product in practice?
    The walking skeleton approach is offered as a starting tactic, but the broader organisational transformation (product managers for platforms, contribution governance, feedback mechanisms) is not detailed.
  • ⭕ When should an organisation NOT build an internal platform?
    Not addressed. The article assumes platform building is the right decision and focuses on how to do it well.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe two-sided market framing is the article's most interesting structural insight, but it receives almost no concrete elaboration. What does a good producer experience look like? How do you design contribution models? What governance prevents contributed capabilities from degrading quality? The article names the concept but doesn't operationalise it.
    Read next: Evan Bottcher's work on platform thinking, or Gregor Hohpe's writing on platform architecture governance, would provide the missing operational detail.
  • incompleteThe claim that well-managed platforms 'become less expensive over time' is stated as a general truth but not supported with evidence or even a clear mechanism. This is a strong empirical claim that could easily be false for many organisations (e.g., scope creep, expanding user base, evolving cloud landscape).
    Read next: Case studies from organisations like Spotify, Zalando, or Mercado Libre that have published on platform economics would either support or challenge this claim.
  • incompleteThe article conflates the journalist's framing (Ben Linders) with the speaker's claims (Abby Bangser). It is unclear which ideas are Bangser's original contributions and which are the journalist's interpretation or standard platform engineering wisdom. This makes it hard to assess the novelty of the argument.
    Read next: The original GOTO Copenhagen talk would provide direct access to Bangser's arguments without the journalistic filter.
  • incompleteThe AI section in the Q&A feels bolted on and doesn't connect to the core platform-as-product argument. It asserts that platforms should be 'API-first and event-driven' to support AI but doesn't explain how AI changes the product-mindset argument or what specific platform capabilities AI requires beyond standard good practices.
    Read next: Thoughtworks' or Humanitec's writing on AI-augmented developer platforms would provide more substantive treatment of this intersection.
