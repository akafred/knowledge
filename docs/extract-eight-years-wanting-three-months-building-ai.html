Eight years of wanting, three months of building with AI

Lalit Maganti · article · 2025-03-01 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

AI as AmplifierCode QualityCoding AgentsContext Rot & Context LimitsMental Models & UnderstandingSoftware Design FundamentalsTaste & JudgmentVibe Coding
AI coding agents are a genuine force multiplier for implementation and last-mile polish, and can unlock projects that would otherwise die from the intersection of difficulty and tedium, but they are a dangerous substitute for design, architecture, and taste -- the developer must retain ownership of the soul of the software or risk building a codebase they cannot reason about.

practical · reflective essay / experience report — A practitioner's systematic post-mortem on building a real software project (syntaqlite, SQLite devtools) using AI coding agents over three months, structured as honest assessment rather than advocacy or dismissal.

Key terms

  • vibe-coding Vibe Coding
    Delegating nearly all design and implementation to AI while acting as a semi-technical manager, without maintaining deep ownership of the codebase.
  • obvious code Code Quality
    Code where the specification can be fully expressed as 'write a function with this behaviour and parameters' -- implementation that has a locally checkable right answer.
  • taste Taste & Judgment
    The human capacity to judge API design, architecture, and user experience -- qualities with no objectively checkable answer. Determines whether refactoring is merely rearranging or genuinely improving.
  • standard dialect Code Quality
    The conventional, idiomatic way of writing code in a given language -- predictable, readable, unsurprising. AI defaults to this.
  • context pollution Context Rot & Context Limits
    The problem that design decisions from one part of a codebase implicitly constrain other parts, and determining relevance requires exactly the institutional knowledge you are trying to externalize.
  • force multiplier AI as Amplifier
    AI amplifies the developer's existing capability for implementation but does not substitute for missing capability in design or judgment.

Arguments

AI is an incredible force multiplier for implementation but a dangerous substitute for design. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • AI is better than the author at writing obvious, well-scoped code.
  • AI struggles with tasks that have no objectively checkable answer.
  • The vibe-coding month produced functional but unmaintainable code that had to be discarded.
  • AI has no sense of history, taste, or how a human will feel using an API.
Implicit premises:
  • Software quality depends on both correct implementation and good design.
  • Design quality cannot be verified by automated tests or compilation.
  • The ratio of design-dependent to implementation-dependent work increases as a project matures.
Inferences:
  • Developers should use AI heavily for implementation while retaining full ownership of design.
  • The developer's role shifts from writing code to making design decisions, reviewing output, and maintaining taste.
AI effectiveness varies predictably along two axes: developer expertise and task verifiability. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • When the author understood a domain deeply, AI output could be reviewed and iterated on quickly.
  • When the author could describe but didn't yet know a domain, AI was useful but required more care.
  • When the author didn't know what he wanted, AI led him down dead ends.
  • Even with deep expertise, AI struggled on tasks with no objectively checkable answer (e.g., API design).
Implicit premises:
  • The ability to evaluate AI output is a prerequisite for productive use.
  • Evaluation requires either domain knowledge or an objective correctness criterion (ideally both).
Inferences:
  • Knowing where you are on these axes at any moment is the core skill of working with AI effectively.
Continuous refactoring is essential when using AI to generate code at scale. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • AI generates code at industrial scale.
  • The vibe-coding month showed that without refactoring, the codebase becomes impossible to reason about.
  • AI itself is good at executing refactoring when given direction.
Implicit premises:
  • Code entropy increases faster when generation speed exceeds comprehension speed.
  • Refactoring requires a human to identify the right abstractions.
Inferences:
  • The developer's workflow should alternate between generation and refactoring.
  • Taste determines whether refactoring is productive or merely cosmetic.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Can AI coding agents enable projects that would otherwise never be built?
    Yes -- syntaqlite is direct evidence. The project was deferred for eight years and completed in three months with AI.
  • ✅ What is the right way to use AI for a serious software project?
    Retain ownership of design; use AI as 'autocomplete on steroids' within a tight process of opinionated design, thorough review, eager refactoring, and automated scaffolding to check AI output.
  • ✅ What are the psychological costs of using AI coding tools?
    Addictive slot-machine dynamics, tiredness feedback loops, loss of mental model, deferred design decisions, false confidence from generated tests.
  • ✅ When does AI help vs hurt in software development?
    Two-axis framework: AI helps most when the developer has high expertise and the task has objectively checkable answers; it hurts most when expertise is low and answers are subjective.
  • ⭕ Will AI fundamentally change the role of the software developer?
    Partially addressed -- the author implies the role shifts toward design, review, and taste, but does not explore long-term implications.

Gaps

  • unstated assumptionThe author assumes that the inability to externalize institutional knowledge and design context is a fundamental limitation of AI, not merely a tooling limitation. This conflates current AI capabilities with inherent constraints. Future models with persistent memory, codebase-scale context, and temporal reasoning could potentially bridge this gap.
    Read next: Read work on long-context models, AI memory architectures, and research on whether design taste can be formalized or learned (e.g., Christopher Alexander's pattern language as a possible bridge).
  • selection biasThe author is an exceptionally skilled systems programmer (C++, Android performance, SQLite internals). His experience with AI may not generalize to developers with less expertise, where the expertise-x-verifiability framework would place nearly all work in the low-expertise quadrant. The article does not address how junior developers should calibrate their AI use.
    Read next: Look for studies on AI-assisted development among developers of varying skill levels; compare with Maganti's framework.

  • omissionThe article does not discuss the economics of AI-assisted development beyond the $200/month Claude Max subscription. The 250 hours of personal time invested is mentioned but not analyzed in terms of opportunity cost or compared to hiring/contracting alternatives.
    Read next: Compare with analyses of AI coding tool ROI; consider the total cost including cognitive overhead and the discarded vibe-coding month.
  • unexamined tensionThe author claims 'AI didn't just make the same project faster -- it changed what the project was' (adding editor extensions, docs, WASM playground). But he also claims the core value comes from the parser architecture, which AI was harmful for. This raises the question: did AI expand the project's scope in the right direction, or did the cheap last-mile features dilute focus on the hard core?
    Read next: Consider the 80/20 principle in open source: does breadth of features or depth of core reliability drive adoption?
  • implicit premiseThe expertise-x-verifiability framework assumes that 'taste' and design judgment are inherently human and non-formalizable. This is stated as obvious but is actually a deep philosophical claim about the nature of aesthetic and design knowledge. If taste could be partially formalized (as design systems and API design guidelines attempt), the framework's boundaries would shift.
    Read next: Joshua Bloch's 'How to Design a Good API and Why It Matters'; API design guideline literature; whether LLMs can learn design heuristics from large corpora of well-designed APIs.
