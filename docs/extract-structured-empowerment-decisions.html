How Fast-Growing Companies Can Make Better Decisions

Tatiana Sandino · rss · 2026-05-07 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Decision-Making PracticesLearning Culture & SlackTeam Autonomy
Fast-growing companies that frame decision-making as a binary choice between centralization and decentralization fall into predictable failure modes; a third path — structured empowerment, which combines curated decision options with outcome-based accountability — lets organizations scale while preserving both consistency and local adaptability.

practical · argumentative — The article argues for a specific organizational design approach — 'structured empowerment' — as a third path between centralization and decentralization. It is practical because it prescribes concrete actions (curated option menus, key-result accountability, stress tests) and illustrates them with case studies.

Key terms

  • Structured empowerment Team Autonomy
    A decision-making approach that gives employees curated options (input and process) to choose from, combined with accountability for key results rather than process compliance
  • Curated options (input options / process options) Decision-Making Practices
    Input options are pre-configured resources and materials (e.g., product assortments, promotional bundles); process options are modularized tasks or routines employees choose from to sequence their work
  • Focal employees
    Employees with unique local knowledge about customer needs whose decisions meaningfully affect whether customers are served effectively and profitably — e.g., store managers, nurses, trainers
  • Bermuda Triangle of Management
    The dangerous zone where a fast-growing venture is too large for informal control but not yet able to survive rigid bureaucracy, coined by D. Daryl Wyckoff
  • Five-year stress test
    A diagnostic workshop where a multidisciplinary group of 10-12 people (including focal employees) surfaces risks of doubling down on the company's predominant decision-making approach over five years
  • Double-loop learning Learning Culture & Slack
    A two-level learning system: first loop where employees refine daily decisions through peer/advisor discussion and result tracking; second loop where they provide feedback for system-wide improvement

Narratives

  • CrossFit: decentralization's limits — 15,000 affiliates with 60 HQ employees, no playbooks or oversight; explosive growth but brand dilution and thousands of closures
    → Cautionary tale illustrating that radical decentralization carries predictable costs (P2)
  • China Lodging Group: centralization's limits — top-down standardization stripped hotel managers of pricing and adaptation authority, losing ground to nimbler competitors
    → Cautionary tale showing that centralized control ignores frontline knowledge (P3)
  • OXXO: structured empowerment in retail — moved from pure decentralization to curated planogram and schedule options, tripling stores and doubling per-store profitability in a decade
    → Primary success story demonstrating structured empowerment at scale (P4)
  • School of Rock: structured empowerment in education — The Method App (100 copyright-compliant shows + instructional methods) solved quality, talent, and legal risks while growing from 240 to 430+ schools
    → Franchise case demonstrating curated input and process options (P4)
  • IKEA: rescuing a centralized model — preserved core concept while adding curated product-assortment options for local markets, achieving global consistency with local relevance
    → Shows structured empowerment can rescue over-centralized organizations (P4)
  • Dutch Bros: purpose-driven empowerment — 'Dutch Love' purpose and values anchor structured empowerment so employee discretion stays aligned with brand identity
    → Illustrates how purpose anchors empowering culture (P8)

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why do fast-growing companies lose control of decision-making, and when does it happen?
    At predictable headcount thresholds (50, 80, 150) along four fault lines: alignment, complexity, financial discipline, oversight
  • ✅ What are the failure modes of pure centralization and pure decentralization?
    Decentralization: brand dilution, coordination failure, uneven quality. Centralization: lost frontline knowledge, reduced responsiveness, talent attrition
  • ✅ What is structured empowerment, and how does it differ from simply setting boundaries on empowered employees?
    Curated options + outcome accountability; boundaries only say what employees cannot do, while structured empowerment guides what they should do
  • ✅ How should companies design curated option menus (input and process options)?
    Limit to ~6-7 options; derive from high-performer practices; involve focal employees in menu design; separate input options (resources/materials) from process options (task sequences)
  • ✅ How should companies measure the performance of empowered employees?
    Through key outcome results reflecting customer value proposition and financial targets — not process compliance checklists
  • ✅ What organizational conditions are necessary for structured empowerment to work?
    Formal double-loop learning systems and an empowering culture (purpose, adaptability, candor/psychological safety)
  • ✅ How can a company diagnose where structured empowerment is needed?
    The five-year stress test: a workshop with 10-12 participants including focal employees that surfaces risks of doubling down on the current approach
  • ⭕ Does structured empowerment generalize beyond retail, service, and franchise contexts?
  • ⭕ How does structured empowerment handle genuinely novel or unbounded decision spaces where no pre-curated menu can capture relevant options?

Gaps

  • evidentiaryAll supporting evidence comes from retail, service, hospitality, and franchise contexts; the author acknowledges seeing 'similar principles' in other sectors but provides no cases outside these domains, leaving the framework's generalizability to knowledge-work, tech, or manufacturing undemonstrated.
  • methodologicalThe case studies are observational and retrospective; no controlled comparison or counterfactual analysis is presented. OXXO's tripling of stores and doubling of profitability is attributed to structured empowerment, but confounding factors (market growth, competitor dynamics, macroeconomic conditions) are not addressed.
  • conceptualThe boundary between 'structured empowerment' and well-designed delegation with guardrails is under-theorized. Many mature organizations already provide decision playbooks and measure outcomes; the article does not clearly distinguish where existing management practice ends and structured empowerment begins.
  • scopeThe framework assumes decisions can be modularized into a small set of curated options. It does not address domains where the decision space is genuinely novel, ambiguous, or unbounded — situations where no pre-curated menu could capture the relevant options.
    closed byTBM 421: Minimally Viable Consistency (Part 3) — Provides strategies for decision spaces that resist curated menus: flexible consistency (shared purpose/protocol, not shared form) and legible variety (making structurally different local work legible) cover novel or unbounded domains where no pre-curated option set fits.
  • implicit_premiseThe article assumes that organizational knowledge about 'what works' can be reliably captured, codified, and distributed as curated options. This presupposes that tacit knowledge can be made explicit and that best practices are transferable across contexts — an assumption that the knowledge management literature has long debated.
