12) Greger Teigre Wedel: Når sjefen har svaret, tier resten

When the boss already has the answer (and shows it), other voices fall silent — so a hands-on technical leader's most important discipline is choosing, situationally, when to suppress their own answer and create space for the team to find one.

practical · mixed — Conversational interview that is mostly practical (how to lead, how to scale a product org, how to adopt AI) but threaded with theoretical observations about expertise, authority, and organizational dynamics. The title proposition — 'når sjefen har svaret, tier resten' — is a prescriptive claim about leader behavior, not a descriptive theory.

Key terms

  • å være leder vs. å drive med ledelse Informal Leadership
    Wedel's distinction: being a 'leder' is the formal role with personnel responsibility; 'drive med ledelse' is the activity of moving a group toward an outcome, which one can do without title or authority. He values the second and is ambivalent about the first.
  • når sjefen har svaret, tier resten Leader Restraint
    An operational claim: as soon as a leader signals they already know the answer (because they are technically hands-on, or because they speak too explicitly and detailed), alternative voices fall silent. 'Tier' carries the connotation of voluntary withdrawal, not being silenced by force.
  • ta plass / la andre få plass Leader Restraint
    A learned leadership skill: taking space when you have passion and conviction, vs. consciously holding back to let team members occupy the conversation. Wedel reports being coached to 'tone down' early in his career and now sees it as a dynamic to manage in both directions.
  • lederhatten / brette opp armene Hands-On Leadership & the Pendulum
    Two roles the hands-on technical leader can occupy: 'taking off the leader hat and rolling up the sleeves' to enter whiteboarding/maker mode. Wedel argues this works only if it is made explicit and contained — otherwise the leader role bleeds in and silences the team.
  • produktorganisasjon
    A product org is one where the value sold is technology-driven and where engineers can deeply engage with an identifiable external customer. If you have to twist reality to name a customer, you don't have a product org — you have IT-supporting-business-processes, which requires a different operating model.
  • first principles
    Wedel is mildly sceptical of the term as a buzzword but uses it to mean: the underlying logic that explains why five different methods all work — without which adopting any of them becomes 'theater' and 'incantations'.
  • horisontal (alle må være mer horisontale)
    In the AI section: with AI as a tool, everyone must broaden across roles — designers commit code, backend engineers prototype frontends, PMs make prototypes — without claiming equal mastery. AI raises the floor on adjacent skills.
  • verktøykassa / repertoar
    Wedel describes leadership growth as enlarging a toolbox of postures (coach, decider, hands-on, whiteboarder, director) and getting better at choosing which one to use in which context. Not better formulas; more options.
  • guardrails (DORA-style) Guardrails
    Pre-existing engineering disciplines — tests, continuous delivery, code review — which AI does not replace but in fact requires more of, because AI amplifies whatever practice is in place (good or bad).

Arguments

A hands-on technical leader must consciously suppress their answer in most team conversations. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Being hands-on gives the leader credibility and judgment.
  • When the leader speaks in detail and with conviction, alternative voices fall silent.
  • The leader is additionally 'the boss', which amplifies the silencing effect.
  • Useful team output depends on alternative voices being heard.
Implicit premises:
  • Team members can sense whether the leader already has an answer, even if the leader does not state it.
  • Norwegian (or at least this team's) cultural default is to defer to expressed authority rather than challenge it.
  • The cost of silenced dissent is greater than the cost of a slower or worse leader-supplied answer.
Inferences:
  • Most of the leader's daily skill is what they choose NOT to say.
  • Hiring technically expert leaders without training them in this discipline systematically degrades team contribution.
Mode-switching between 'leader' and 'maker' must be explicit to work. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • When a leader rolls up their sleeves implicitly, the leader role bleeds in and silences others.
  • An explicit announcement ('lederhatten av, whiteboarding-modus') creates a temporary protected space.
Implicit premises:
  • Team members track signals of role, not just content of contribution.
  • Hierarchy is a default that requires active suspension, not absence.
Inferences:
  • Teams that lack a shared vocabulary for mode-switching cannot benefit from a technical leader's hands-on contribution without paying the silence cost.
  • Slack/whiteboard sessions need ritualised openings, not just informal ones.
Leadership growth is repertoire-expansion, not formula-discovery. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Early-career belief: there is a right way to lead, find it.
  • Lived experience: thousands of micro-decisions, each context-specific.
  • Effective leaders carry multiple postures (coach, decider, hands-on, director) and choose dynamically.
Implicit premises:
  • Context is irreducible — no formula can substitute for live judgment.
  • Leadership training that teaches one posture (e.g. only coaching) systematically underequips its students.
Inferences:
  • Over-coaching is a failure mode of leaders who learned the 'hold back' lesson too well.
  • Senior leadership effectiveness is measured by posture-selection accuracy, not by any single posture executed well.
AI does not change the fundamental engineering disciplines; it amplifies whichever ones are in place. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • DORA-style guardrails (tests, CD, code review) determine whether AI output improves or degrades code.
  • Earlier generational shifts (assembler → high-level languages) followed the same amplification pattern.
  • Practitioners with strong fundamentals report a productivity boost; those without get faster decay.
Implicit premises:
  • The value of AI tooling is mediated by the surrounding socio-technical system.
  • Organisations cannot 'buy out' of fundamentals by adopting AI.
Inferences:
  • Organisations weak on continuous delivery should defer AI rollout until guardrails exist.
  • AI procurement decisions are downstream of engineering-discipline maturity, not upstream.
Adopting frameworks without understanding their underlying principle is 'theater'. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Five methods for the same problem often share one underlying principle, interpreted five ways.
  • Without the principle, practitioners cannot adapt the method to their context.
Implicit premises:
  • Method-as-ritual is actively harmful (it consumes effort and creates false confidence), not merely neutral.
  • Org-design problems cross many functions; without shared principle, functions interpret the method differently and conflict ensues.
Inferences:
  • Investment in 'principle-articulation' inside an organisation pays off more than investment in method-rollout.
  • Wedel's six-principle blog series is itself a worked example of this claim.

Narratives

  • Cisco principal engineer: orchestrating 40+ architects without personnel authority. None reported to him; none would have accepted being commanded. He learned a non-coercive style that he now considers more durable than positional authority.
    → Supports the proposition that having authority makes leaders blind, and that the absence of authority is itself a developmental forcing function.
  • Early-career coaching to 'tone down' his intensity in Cisco's multi-cultural, multi-timezone setting. He worked at being less intense, then later discovered he had over-corrected and needed to bring intensity back forward in specific situations.
    → Illustrates leadership-as-repertoire: there is no static answer to 'how intense should I be' — it varies by context, and the leader must keep adjusting.
  • Flutter/Dart side project: built a 'first app' Flutter template during beta release, shipped it to both app stores, and it became a widely-used reference starter for the ecosystem (four-digit GitHub stars).
    → Demonstrates Wedel's claim that learning-for-its-own-sake is his motivation and his way of staying credible at the leadership level — but also shows what happens when a leader's side project becomes a public artifact.
  • Cognite + Aker: a core success factor was that Aker portfolio companies opened their workplaces to Cognite engineers, giving rare access to chemical engineers, offshore operators, process engineers. This depth of access is what made software engineers actually able to model the user reality.
    → Supports the proposition that bridging the engineer-user gap is the hardest problem in industrial software and only happens 'in pockets' driven by structural commitment (here, ownership and access).
  • Frustration of operational engineers (AKBP, Aker BP) at having developers 'in sandals' wander through asking questions while they are on tight project deadlines — and the analogy to NAV technologists trying to understand caseworker / jurist work for vedtak-systems.
    → Concrete instance of the structural difficulty in connecting builders with domain users — the time and culture asymmetries are not minor.
  • Omny Security AI hackathon: Wedel set a deliberate challenge — designers should commit code, backend engineers should prototype frontend, PMs should make prototypes. Not because their output goes into production but because it elevates the inputs of those closest to the customer.
    → Supports the claim that AI levels horizontally: the value is not that everyone becomes equally skilled but that the team's collective inputs are sharper.
  • Advice to his own children (21, 18, 11): be excellent at the basics (math, physics, chemistry, reading, reasoning, communication, group work), and be an experimenter with AI.
    → Personal data point on what he believes is durable vs. ephemeral — fundamentals plus experimentation, not specific technologies.
  • Hosts (Truls / Audun) recall meeting Wedel for the first time and being struck by how he managed to remain technically engaged (coding) while leading at scale — they cite him as the reason they recall the role being possible.
    → Frames the conversation: the hosts treat Wedel as an instance of the rare hands-on technical leader, which is why the silence problem matters here.
  • Jensen Huang / NVIDIA mental model: the organisation as a computer, with processors, sub-processors, GPUs each doing their piece. Wedel cites it approvingly.
    → Used to ground his bachelor-in-psychology-with-group-dynamics-focus view: roles in an organisation are like components in a system, and the leader's design job is to shape interactions rather than command.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What does an experienced technical leader actually do differently from a beginner?
    They hold a larger repertoire of postures and make sharper situational choices about which to deploy — including when to suppress their own answer.
  • ✅ What makes a technically hands-on leader an asset vs. a liability to their team?
    The asset is judgment and credibility. The liability is the silencing effect when the leader's answer enters the room. The discipline is explicit mode-switching and selective withholding.
  • ✅ What is a product organisation, really?
    One whose value is technology-driven AND that can identify and deeply engage an external customer. Anything else is IT-supporting-business-processes and needs a different operating model.
  • ✅ How does AI-assisted development change engineering organisations?
    It levels horizontally and amplifies existing practice. DORA-style guardrails become more important. The first-order effect is broadening, not replacement.
  • ⭕ How do juniors develop senior judgment when AI flattens the early production curve?
    Wedel explicitly names this as unsolved.
  • ⭕ What identity does a senior engineer hold when AI absorbs much of their daily craft?
    Wedel names 'identity problem' as an emerging concern but does not resolve it.
  • ⭕ Is the silence effect culturally Norwegian, or universal?
    Not addressed — Wedel treats the silencing effect as a working assumption.
  • ✅ When IS having (and using) the answer the right move?
    Partially — Wedel names the over-coaching failure mode and the moment for cutting through ('det er tre gode alternativer her, men vi tar den'). The conditions are not fully specified.

Gaps

  • incompleteCultural specificity is unexamined: Wedel never tests whether 'when the boss has the answer, the rest stay silent' depends on Norwegian (or Nordic, or hierarchical) defaults. In flatter or more confrontational cultures, the chilling effect may be weaker — or it may show up differently (silent disagreement vs. silent compliance).
    Read next: Cutler TBM 423 on undiscussables (which treats power-asymmetry-creating-silence as general, not culturally specific) and Edmondson on psychological safety across cultures. Also Hofstede / Trompenaars on power distance dimensions in Nordic vs. other workplaces.
  • incompleteDecision criteria for posture-switching are underspecified. Wedel says leaders should choose among coaching, deciding, hands-on, whiteboarding, director postures situationally — but he names only one explicit trigger (the over-coaching failure mode that calls for cutting through). When to take which posture is left as 'judgment'.
    Read next: Kent Beck on tidy-first vs. mess-first decision rules; Heifetz on adaptive vs. technical work; Cutler's TBM 418 on campfires/trails/quests as posture-selection frame.
  • incompleteThe interaction between explicit mode-switching ('lederhatten av') and team-level psychological safety is asserted but not unpacked. Saying 'we're in whiteboarding mode now' presumably only works if the team trusts that the leader will actually honour the signal — which is itself a function of trust built elsewhere.
    Read next: Edmondson on psychological safety as accumulated track-record; Schein on rituals and their preconditions; Pocock's 'alignment is not AFK' point about why alignment work must be live, not formal.
  • incompleteThe junior-development problem is named but not theorised. Wedel says we don't know how to grow juniors when AI flattens the early production curve. He gestures at 'fundamentals matter more' but doesn't say how organisations should change apprenticeship.
    Read next: Recent writing on AI and the apprenticeship pipeline (Pragmatic Engineer FDE piece, Addy Osmani on orchestration tax, Garg on reducing friction in AI development).
  • incompleteAI-as-amplifier is asserted in both directions (good practice → better; bad practice → worse) but the magnitude of asymmetry is not addressed. If AI amplifies bad practice faster than good, the policy implication ('defer AI until guardrails exist') may be stronger than Wedel states.
    Read next: DORA 2025 detailed findings; the Dave Farley YouTube series Wedel mentions; any empirical work on AI adoption velocity vs. defect rates.
  • incompleteThe six product-org first principles are alluded to but not enumerated in the conversation. Only the first (deep user/customer connection) is discussed.
    Read next: stuff.greger.io blog series on product organisation first principles.
