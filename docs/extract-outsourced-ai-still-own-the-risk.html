You Outsourced the AI—but You Still Own the Risk

Harvard Business Review · article · 2026-07-09 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Agent Governance (Internal)AI Dependence RiskAI Regulation & Governance (External)Enterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessLLM LimitationsMCP (Model Context Protocol)Moats & Defensibility
Because legal and reputational accountability for AI harm falls on the enterprise closest to the end user regardless of who built or trained the model, firms deploying third-party AI must proactively govern four under-managed exposures — upstream model opacity, customization liability, vendor lock-in, and regulatory fragmentation — through contractual transparency, customization governance, engineered portability, and a common standards-based (NIST AI RMF / ISO 42001) compliance foundation.

practical · HBR management article — a diagnostic-plus-prescriptive governance essay (four named 'traps', each with a 'What to do') — Practical: it prescribes governance action for executives deploying third-party AI, not a theory of AI. Written by Harvard Kennedy School senior fellows (Paulo Carvão et al.) focused on AI governance and policy. It reasons from a pattern of real lawsuits/enforcement to a set of managerial remedies; its claims are asserted and evidenced by case citations rather than defended analytically.

Key terms

  • the deployer (vs the provider) / the middle layer
    The enterprise that integrates third-party AI into its workflows and puts it in front of employees or customers — the middle of a three-layer AI supply chain — which is where 'most operational decisions are made' and 'increasingly where liability falls'; the legal classification (deployer or provider) determines where compliance responsibility sits.
  • upstream opacity / the upstream black box
    The deployer's structural lack of visibility into how a third-party model was trained, how it changes and how it fails — aggravated because AI is probabilistic, context-sensitive and drifts, and vendors' model cards/accuracy figures are built for research communities, not enterprise compliance.
  • customization liability / the customization boomerang
    The principle that adapting a vendor model with your own data (fine-tuning, retrieval, white-labeling) shifts legal liability toward you — the 'default assumption should be that customization shifts liability toward you' — because it makes you look more like a provider than a mere deployer under unsettled doctrine.
  • AI lock-in / the illusion of the 'off' switch
    The inability to switch AI providers because the model's behavior is woven into application logic — 'not simply stored in a portable data layer' — making deployments Jenga-like (the model a load-bearing piece); more acute than classic ERP/cloud lock-in and unmitigated by any interoperability/portability standard.
  • portability
    The deployer's deliberately engineered ability to migrate a deployment to a different provider within a defined window — achieved through modular architecture, vendor-neutral data/retrieval/eval infrastructure, exportable formats, and contractual export/change-notice/termination rights; 'a matter of architecture and contract', NOT delivered by a protocol like MCP (which standardizes only the connection layer).
  • regulatory fragmentation / the fragmentation fallacy
    The same AI system being simultaneously governed by divergent regimes (high-risk under the EU AI Act, auditable under NYC local law, unaddressed elsewhere), and the mistake of answering each new law with a separate reactive compliance track rather than a common foundation.
  • common governance foundation (NIST AI RMF / ISO/IEC 42001)
    A single, standards-anchored risk-management program — identify high-risk systems, test, document, monitor, assign accountability — adopted as the baseline onto which each applicable regulation is mapped ('what does this add to our existing program?'); the standards structure risk across the lifecycle but are 'not a substitute for legal compliance'.
  • existing doctrines
    Pre-AI legal tools — wiretapping statutes, anti-discrimination law, consumer protection, the Fair Credit Reporting Act, product-liability and agency theory — that courts and regulators are already using to reach AI harms, so 'framing AI risk primarily through the lens of AI regulation is looking at the right landscape with the wrong map.'

Arguments

Enterprises deploying third-party AI must proactively govern four exposures — upstream opacity, customization liability, vendor lock-in, and regulatory fragmentation — through contractual transparency, customization governance, engineered portability, and a common standards-based compliance foundation. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Accountability for AI harm falls on the enterprise closest to the end user, even for models it did not build (Peloton chatbot suit allowed to proceed).
  • Regulators and courts already reach AI harms through existing doctrines, so no new AI law is needed to create deployer liability (EEOC v. iTutorGroup employer; DOJ IT-firm settlement; Cigna/UnitedHealth class actions; sanctioned law firms).
  • Customization shifts liability toward the deployer under unsettled theory (Workday reached as employers' agent despite contractual disclaimers; Eightfold/FCRA).
  • Enterprise AI deployments are non-modular (Jenga) and there is no portability standard or regulatory floor.
  • Divergent, uncoordinated regimes (EU AI Act, NYC law) make reactive per-law compliance fragile and expensive.
  • Regulators credit demonstrable risk-and-compliance programs and punish their absence (Rite Aid FTC ban).
Implicit premises:
  • Liability tracks proximity to the end user, not causation within the stack — so the deployer, not the upstream lab, is the party who must manage the risk.
  • Portability is achievable enough via architecture and contract to be a genuine hedge (in tension with the piece's own Jenga metaphor and MCP concession).
  • A common standards-based governance foundation actually reduces legal exposure and satisfies multiple regimes at once (assumes the regimes' shared controls are the load-bearing ones).
  • The deploying enterprise has the contractual leverage and governance capacity to negotiate non-standard terms and build vendor-neutral architecture.
Inferences:
  • If liability lands on the proximate deployer regardless of who built the model, then the deployer — not the vendor — must own the risk-management work.
  • If existing law already reaches these harms, waiting for AI-specific regulation before acting leaves the enterprise exposed now.
  • If most regimes share the same controls, one standards-anchored foundation mapped to each law dominates a separate program per law.
  • Therefore the rational posture is to build a common, demonstrable governance program and preserve optionality (portability, clear ownership) ahead of the cases.

Narratives

  • Visitors to Peloton's website used a familiar third-party 'chat' widget; per a class-action complaint the vendor recorded, stored and reused those conversations to improve its ML models. Peloton neither built nor trained the system, yet a California federal judge allowed a wiretapping claim against Peloton to proceed (the parties later jointly dismissed on undisclosed terms).
    → The framing bookend of the whole piece — opens and closes it — dramatizing the thesis that the deployer owns the risk of AI it did not build, and turning an abstract accountability claim into a concrete courtroom exposure.
  • A cluster of enforcement/liability cases where the deployer, not the AI provider, was pursued: the EEOC sued iTutorGroup (the employer) over an age-discriminating screening tool; the DOJ settled with an IT firm whose AI-generated job ads unlawfully excluded U.S. workers; Cigna and UnitedHealth face class actions over third-party AI in claims decisions; law firms were sanctioned for filings containing AI hallucinations.
    → Establishes the accountability-falls-on-the-deployer claim as a pattern rather than a one-off, across employment, advertising, healthcare and legal practice — evidence that regulators/courts look first to the deploying organization.
  • A federal court let a discrimination claim against Workday proceed on the theory that it acted as an 'agent' of the employers using its hiring tools — despite contract language assigning hiring-decision responsibility to those employers; separately, Eightfold-style claims raise the risk that AI hiring scores count as 'consumer reports', pulling employers into FCRA obligations they never anticipated.
    → Illustrates the customization/agency mechanism by which liability crosses the vendor–deployer boundary in unpredictable ways, showing that contracts alone do not fix where responsibility lands.
  • Rite Aid was banned by the FTC from using AI facial-recognition technology after deploying it without meaningful testing, risk assessment or oversight.
    → The negative exemplar for Trap 4's remedy — shows the concrete cost of deploying AI without a demonstrable governance program, motivating the NIST/ISO baseline and 'show, not just say'.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ When an enterprise deploys AI it did not build, who bears the legal and reputational liability if that AI harms someone?
    The deploying enterprise — the middle layer of the AI supply chain, closest to the affected person — does, via existing doctrines (wiretapping, anti-discrimination, consumer protection, FCRA, product-liability, agency), regardless of who built or trained the model.
  • ✅ Which AI risks are enterprises systematically failing to manage?
    Four: upstream model opacity, customization liability, vendor lock-in, and regulatory fragmentation — 'most organizations are not managing these deliberately.'
  • ✅ What should a leader actually do to reduce exposure?
    Build transparency into AI contracts; govern customization as a legal (not engineering) decision; architect and contract for portability; and anchor compliance in a common NIST AI RMF / ISO 42001 foundation with explicit written ownership and demonstrable human override.
  • ⭕ Given the article's own 'Jenga' description of AI lock-in, is the prescribed portability actually achievable — and at what cost?
    Left open. The piece prescribes 'architect for modularity' and 'run a periodic portability exercise' while simultaneously conceding deployments are Jenga-like and that MCP leaves orchestration/prompts/evals/governance to rebuild; it never reconciles the prescription with its own account of how deep the lock-in runs, nor prices the hedge.

Gaps

  • incompletePortability realism vs. the article's own lock-in. Trap 3 both diagnoses AI lock-in as Jenga-deep (the model woven into application logic; MCP standardizes only the connection layer) and prescribes 'design for portability' as the hedge — without showing portability is achievable enough to matter, what fraction of the stack actually migrates, or what the hedge costs. The load-bearing 'portability is a real hedge' premise is asserted against the section's own caveats.
    Read next: Switching-cost / moat analyses specific to AI stacks; total-cost-of-ownership of vendor-neutral architecture; how much of orchestration/prompts/evals actually survives a provider swap in practice.
  • incompleteOpen-weight / self-hosted AI is never distinguished from closed-API AI, yet it changes every exposure. Running open-weight models on infrastructure you control can dissolve upstream opacity (you can inspect and pin the weights), collapse lock-in (the weights are portable), and shift the deployer/provider line — but the piece treats 'third-party AI' as a monolithic closed-vendor relationship and never asks whether self-hosting is itself a structural remedy to Traps 1 and 3.
    Read next: Open-weight adoption and the engine-vs-car (harness) question; self-hosting economics and governance; data-sovereignty / on-prem deployment as a lock-in and opacity mitigation.

This article's p10 (Keus/GreenPT) explicitly answers it: open-weight models let a company self-host, switch, audit, and fork without rebuilding infrastructure — though the answer here is only a single interested party's testimony, not a systematic analysis, so the gap is addressed rather than fully closed with evidence.
  • incompleteSMB-vs-large-enterprise asymmetry is unaddressed. Every remedy — negotiate non-standard contracts and enforceable custom indemnities, run a per-project legal deployer/provider classification, build vendor-neutral architecture, adopt and maintain a NIST/ISO program — presupposes negotiating leverage against frontier vendors and a standing legal/compliance capacity that large enterprises have and smaller deployers do not. The piece prescribes to an implicit large-enterprise reader without saying who can actually execute this.
    Read next: Procurement leverage of small vs. large AI buyers; whether standards-based governance is feasible below enterprise scale; managed/off-the-shelf governance as an alternative to build-your-own.
  • incompleteInsourcing is never weighed as the deeper remedy. The article accepts outsourced AI as the default and manages its risk downstream, but never considers building and owning the capability (or the data/eval/governance layer) as an alternative that would dissolve several exposures at once — opacity, customization-liability ambiguity and lock-in all shrink when the enterprise, not a vendor, owns the model or the surrounding stack.
    Read next: Insourcing vs. buy-and-govern trade-offs; owned AI capability (token capital) as a hedge; when repatriating capability beats contracting around a vendor's risk.
