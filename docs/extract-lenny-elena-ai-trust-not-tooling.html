Your AI strategy has a trust problem, not a tooling problem

Elena Verna (cross-posted by Lenny Rachitsky) · email · 2026-06-05 source 🖥️

Agentic AIAI Confidence TheaterEnterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessHierarchy & Org ChartsAgencyHuman as Orchestrator of AgentsTeam AutonomyTrust
AI tooling produces no velocity unless companies first dismantle the title-gated access, decision-bottlenecking, and middle-management telephone-game that signal distrust of employees — the real unlock is employee agency, not AI agents.

practical · argumentative — Elena is arguing for an action: rewire your org so employees have agency, not just AI tools. The piece moves from diagnosis (command-and-control kills velocity) to prescription (prototype a flat, high-autonomy unit) to a warning (employees must also choose to bear accountability).

Key terms

  • Agency Agency
    An employee's authorized capacity to act on context — having both the information needed to make a call AND the permission to make it without escalation
  • Agents Agentic AI
    AI systems that 'wait to be told what to do' — useful for distributing context but structurally incapable of making the calls
  • Context
    The information substrate required to make good decisions — currently hoarded behind manager titles and Slack channels
  • Parented agent Human as Orchestrator of Agents
    An internal AI agent assigned a human 'parent' — the person with deepest context in that domain — who is responsible for keeping the agent accurate and current
  • Status theater AI Confidence Theater
    Title-and-hierarchy signaling that exists to allocate permission and audibility rather than to coordinate work
  • Organizational muscle memory
    Trained-in habit of waiting for permission; persists even after permissions are formally lifted
  • Trust-based growth / trust-based teams Trust
    External customer trust is downstream of internal employee trust; you cannot 'build in public' or 'react quickly to users' if you don't let employees speak and decide

Arguments

Pushing AI tooling into a command-and-control org will not increase velocity. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • AI requires context to produce good output
  • Employees in command-and-control orgs are denied context (title-gated access, boss-only decisions)
  • Without context, even the best tools produce bad or no decisions
Implicit premises:
  • The bottleneck on output is decision velocity, not execution capacity
  • AI tools enhance the marginal worker, not the marginal decision-maker
Inferences:
  • AI productivity studies that show null effects in large enterprises may be measuring trust deficits, not tool deficits
  • AI rollouts should be paired with permission-system audits
Middle management is structurally redundant in a high-context, high-agency org. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Middle management primarily moves information and decisions up/down the chain
  • Cross-functional unblocking diminishes when teams are fewer and context is centralized
  • Career coaching can be democratized to non-manager roles
Implicit premises:
  • The coordination problems middle management currently solves are artifacts of the distrust structure, not intrinsic to scale
  • Information centralization (a paradox given the agency thesis) is feasible without re-creating gatekeeping
Inferences:
  • If 'centralize context, distribute decisions' is feasible, the operational question becomes: what mechanism distributes context at scale without recreating titles?
Leaders should prototype a flat pod with new people rather than retrofit existing teams. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Whole-org rewrites fail
  • Existing employees have organizational muscle memory of waiting for permission
  • High-agency operators exist and can be hired in
Implicit premises:
  • Muscle memory is sticky enough that retraining is slower than rehiring
  • A pilot pod can demonstrate results that justify broader change
Inferences:
  • Existing managers are unlikely to be the right pilot members — this is a quiet displacement argument
  • 'Innovation theater' risk: the pilot becomes a sandbox that never expands
Autonomy is a contract employees must explicitly opt into, not a gift leaders give. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Autonomy comes with accountability for failure
  • Many employees have not experienced this and may not want it
  • Compensation for accountability is the historical justification for leadership pay
Implicit premises:
  • Current comp structures will need to compensate ICs for accountability they newly carry
  • Some employees will rationally decline the upgrade
Inferences:
  • Pay equity discussions follow: if ICs carry decision accountability, the manager premium has to be re-justified or redistributed
  • Performance management systems calibrated for 'did you do what you were told' will mis-evaluate high-agency ICs

Narratives

  • When you get a manager promotion, you suddenly get added to a separate Slack channel with information you previously had no access to.
    → Shows — rather than argues — that titles function as permission structures. Makes the abstract claim concrete with a felt-recognition example any tech reader has lived.
  • Anthropic's visible structure is 'member of technical staff' or 'member of non-technical staff.'
    → Existence proof that a high-status, high-velocity AI-native org can run without title hierarchy — implicitly suggests title flattening is causally connected to AI-native velocity.
  • At Lovable, each internal AI agent has a human 'parent' responsible for keeping its context accurate.
    → Operational instantiation of the 'distribute context, don't distribute decision authority to AI' principle — shows agents as plumbing for human agency rather than substitutes for it.
  • Telephone-game image: middle management moving decisions and info 'up and down, down and up, over and over.'
    → Reframes middle management's primary function as low-value translation work — preparing the reader to accept that the role's structural need is shrinking.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why are AI transformation initiatives failing to produce the productivity gains companies expect?
    Because the bottleneck is permission, not capability — orgs deny employees the context and authority that would let new tools translate into velocity.
  • ✅ Will AI agents replace employees?
    No — agents lack agency; they amplify context distribution but cannot make the calls that high-agency humans make.
  • ⭕ How should a leader actually begin the shift?
    Hedged answer: prototype with a flat pod of high-agency people, possibly new hires. Elena explicitly disclaims certainty: 'I'm not sure how you switch it.'
  • ✅ What is the operational test for whether your org actually trusts its employees?
    Implicit test embedded in the piece: do junior employees have access to the same Slack channels and decision context as managers, and can they reverse a call same-day without escalation? If no, you don't trust them.
  • ⭕ How do you distribute context at scale without recreating the gatekeeping you just removed?
    Gestures at 'parented agents' as the mechanism but doesn't develop how this scales across a 1000+ person org.
  • ⭕ If middle management is redundant, what happens to existing managers and to the comp ladder that depends on management titles?
    Not addressed.
  • ⭕ How is decision accountability allocated and compensated in a flat-agency org?
    Acknowledges the tension ('that's why leaders get paid more: accountability') but doesn't propose a new comp or evaluation model.

Gaps

  • incompleteTreats trust as a leadership-behavior problem (leaders should give trust, employees should accept accountability) rather than an org-design or contract/incentive problem. There is no mechanism design — no discussion of decision rights documents, RACI alternatives, equity/comp restructuring, escalation policies, or measurement systems that would make agency stick when individual managers regress to control. Pattern-matches to the HBR Managers AI Productivity Boom piece: name a structural problem, prescribe individual remedies.
    Read next: Reinertsen, 'The Principles of Product Development Flow' (decision rights and queue economics); Hamel & Zanini, 'Humanocracy' (post-bureaucratic org design with explicit mechanisms); Laloux, 'Reinventing Organizations' (advice process as a contract for distributed decisions); Spotify's matrix history and post-mortems; works on internal markets for decision rights.
  • incompleteThe Anthropic and Lovable examples are AI-native startups with hiring filters, capital, and mission selection that pre-select for high-agency operators. The piece does not address whether the model travels to a 5,000-person incumbent with regulatory exposure, union representation, or legacy systems.
    Read next: Cutler 'AI Won't Save Your Kingdoms' (incumbents losing to challengers with different structures); case studies on Haier RenDanHeYi; Morning Star's self-management; ING's agile reorg post-mortems.
    closed byWhat is the harder human-capital problem beneath token capital? — That extraction's open gap: the Anthropic/Lovable high-agency examples are AI-native startups that pre-select high-agency operators, and it doesn't address whether the model travels to a large incumbent. This essay supplies the structural obstacle — incumbents structurally select FOR org-chart optimizers and AGAINST high-agency frontier talent, so the model does NOT travel cleanly; the incumbent must first become 'porous' enough to hold uncorporate people with corporate-scale compute. (Partial closure: it names the obstacle without giving the mechanism to overcome it.)
  • incompleteThe accountability-and-comp argument is named but not operationalized. If ICs newly carry decision accountability, what is the manager premium for? She gestures at the tension and moves on. Without a comp/perf-review redesign, the agency upgrade is unilateral risk transfer.
    Read next: Buurtzorg's flat-pay model; literature on profit-sharing and gainsharing; recent work on equity-as-accountability-comp in startups.
  • illogicalThere is a quiet tension between 'centralize information' (named as a benefit reducing the need for middle managers to coordinate) and 'distribute decisions' (the agency thesis). Centralized information typically implies central gatekeeping; distributed decisions imply distributed access. The piece doesn't say what mechanism resolves the tension at scale — 'parented agents' is gestured at but not specified.
    Read next: Conway's Law literature; works on platform engineering as context distribution; Team Topologies (Skelton & Pais).
  • incompleteThe 'high-agency people' framing treats agency as a hireable trait rather than a structural product. This shifts responsibility back to selection rather than to the system. It also has uncomfortable selection-effect implications: who gets sorted out when 'high-agency' becomes the dominant hiring filter?
    Read next: Edmondson 'The Fearless Organization' (psychological safety as the structural enabler of voice/agency); critiques of meritocratic narratives in tech hiring.
  • incompleteNo engagement with the failure modes of the prescription: pilot pods that become innovation theater; flat pods that recreate informal hierarchies; high-velocity decision-making that produces irreversible decisions before reflection (e.g. customer trust violations). The closing trust-based-growth move actually heightens this risk — fast public action with low oversight.
    Read next: Failure-mode analyses of holacracy at Zappos; post-mortems on flat orgs (Valve, GitHub pre-2014); Karen Ho 'Liquidated' on what happens when decision velocity outruns institutional memory.
