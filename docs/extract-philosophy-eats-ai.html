Philosophy Eats AI: What Leaders Should Know

MIT Sloan Management Review · video · 2025-05-14 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Because generative AI is built from and operates through philosophical constructs (purposes, categories, knowledge), the executives who win with AI will be those who deliberately map and reconcile their organization's teleology, ontology, and epistemology — not those who optimize ethics, metrics, or models in isolation.

practical · mixed — A conversational interview that develops a normative thesis about leadership and AI. It is theoretical in deploying a teleology/ontology/epistemology triad as analytical scaffolding, but practical in its prescription that executives must do 'responsibility mapping' and clarify purpose, categories, and knowledge before AI deployment. Echoes Drucker's 'culture eats strategy' as a positioning move — philosophy is named as the operative meta-layer above AI.

Key terms

  • teleology
    The purpose(s) an AI system, organization, or initiative is designed to serve. Schrage and Kiron treat teleology as designable and contested — purposes can conflict (as in Gemini's diversity vs. historical accuracy), and clarifying purpose is the executive's first philosophical task.
  • ontology Organizational Legibility
    How an organization labels, categorizes, and constitutes the things in its world — e.g., what counts as 'loyalty', 'customer experience', 'partnership'. AI training depends on these categorizations being explicit and coherent.
  • epistemology
    The nature and source of the knowledge that informs categorization and purpose inside an enterprise — what counts as knowing, and what evidence justifies an AI-driven decision.
  • teleological confusion
    Kiron's coinage (endorsed by Schrage) for the failure mode in which an AI system is optimizing for multiple purposes that conflict — e.g., diversity mandates fighting historical accuracy mandates — without the designers having reconciled them. The Gemini-on-WWII case is the paradigm.
  • bounded patterns Pattern-Completion Generation (Not Reasoning)
    The pattern-matching counterpart to Simon's 'bounded rationality': because GenAI operates on similarity and pattern rather than logic or reason, the relevant constraint is no longer rationality bounds but pattern bounds — and what should bound patterns is purpose.
  • responsibility mapping
    The current practice of cataloguing the ethical and responsible elements of an AI deployment. Schrage argues this is over-indexed and should be expanded into teleology/ontology/epistemology mapping.
  • learn to prompt / prompt to learn Prompting as Communication
    A reflexive cycle in which the way humans learn to prompt models informs how those prompts then train (or fine-tune the use of) the models — so prompting becomes a co-evolutionary learning practice for human and machine.
  • agentic AI Agentic AI
    Schrage distinguishes agents that merely 'perform tasks' from agents that have genuine agency — making choices, evaluations, and trades. The shift toward agency raises the stakes of teleological clarity.
  • philosophy eats AI
    A deliberate echo of Marc Andreessen ('software eats the world') and Peter Drucker ('culture eats strategy'). The claim is that, as software was eaten by AI, AI is in turn eaten by philosophy — meaning that purpose, categorization, and knowledge become the operative meta-layer determining which AI deployments create value.

Arguments

Philosophy, not technology or ethics, is the binding constraint on AI value creation. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • GPT and its components (training, learning, education) are philosophical constructs.
  • Generative AI makes meaning through pattern similarity, which is itself a philosophical account of meaning.
  • Bad outcomes (Gemini) trace to teleological confusion, not technical failure.
  • Good outcomes (Starbucks loyalty, Amazon customer experience) require ontological clarity about what is being optimized.
Implicit premises:
  • Technical capability is now broadly available and therefore non-differentiating.
  • Ethical AI work has been over-indexed and is insufficient on its own.
  • Organizations that cannot articulate purpose cannot align AI to purpose.
Inferences:
  • Competitive advantage in AI shifts to the layer where purpose, categorization, and knowledge are decided — the philosophical layer.
Google Gemini's historical-image failure is best diagnosed as teleological confusion rather than a technical or ethical bug. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The model had two competing purposes: diversity/inclusion and historical accuracy.
  • These purposes were genuinely in conflict on prompts about WWII and the Founding Fathers.
  • The team privileged the diversity purpose without reconciling it with the historical-accuracy purpose.
Implicit premises:
  • Designers did not (or chose not to) make the trade-off explicit before deployment.
  • Ontological assumptions about 'what diversity should look like' were embedded in the model without being interrogated.
  • Indifference and lack of commitment to philosophical clarity are themselves philosophical postures.
Inferences:
  • Pre-deployment teleology mapping would have surfaced and resolved the conflict, or at least made the trade-off auditable.
Executives should expand 'responsibility mapping' into teleology/ontology/epistemology mapping as the central practical AI exercise. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Organizations already invest in mapping ethical/responsible AI components.
  • Ethical mapping alone leaves teleology, ontology, and epistemology un-articulated.
  • Conflicts between purposes, categories, and knowledge claims cause downstream AI failures.
  • This work is 'inexpensive' — it requires thought, not new infrastructure.
Implicit premises:
  • Executives can be trained or guided through this mapping without becoming professional philosophers.
  • Documenting these layers makes them reviewable, contestable, and improvable.
  • Mapping early is cheaper than rectifying after public failures (Gemini-style).
Inferences:
  • This becomes a standard pre-deployment artifact alongside risk registers and ethics reviews.
The human-AI learning loop must be deliberately balanced: a 'learn to prompt / prompt to learn' virtuous cycle. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • How humans learn to prompt informs how prompts are then used to train models.
  • If humans learn slower than models, the human capital portfolio is wrong.
  • If humans learn faster than models, ML ops is deficient.
Implicit premises:
  • Learning rate is observable and manageable for both humans and models.
  • Symmetric or coupled learning produces compounding returns that asymmetric learning does not.
Inferences:
  • Human-capital strategy and ML ops are joint variables, not separable functions.
AI will sort leaders into those who think philosophically and those who reduce everything to share price. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • GenAI is a battleground for competing philosophies of value creation.
  • Rigorous philosophical thinking is hard discipline; shortcut optimization is easy.
  • Cognitive 'shortcuts' (Ozempic) and disciplined practice (Weight Watchers) produce different long-term outcomes.
Implicit premises:
  • AI lowers the cost of intellectual shortcuts and therefore widens the temptation gap.
  • Compounding advantages accrue to disciplined philosophical thinkers because their AI deployments are more coherent and harder to copy.
Inferences:
  • Competitive divergence between firms will be philosophical before it is technological.

Narratives

  • Google Gemini, in an early instantiation, generated historically inaccurate images of WWII figures and America's Founding Fathers — inserting diversity that did not exist. Schrage and Kiron diagnose this as competing and unreconciled purposes (diversity-and-inclusion vs. historical accuracy), exacerbated by an embedded ontology of what diversity 'should look like'. Schrage adds that the issue was less unthought-through than uncared-about until publicity hit.
    → Anchors 'teleological confusion' with a concrete, recent, widely-known failure. Establishes that the cost of un-mapped purposes is reputational as well as epistemic.
  • Starbucks and Amazon as positive examples — interrogating what 'loyalty' really means. Loyalty as repeat-business is a superficial ontology; loyalty as advocacy/championing/defending is a richer ontology that maps to trackable behaviors (sharing, evangelism, defense).
    → Demonstrates ontological clarity in production. Shows that mapping purpose and categories pays off in measurable, business-relevant behaviors.
  • Ozempic vs. Weight Watchers — asked whether AI lets less-rigorous thinkers become more rigorous, Schrage analogizes: would you take the drug, or follow the diet? Some leaders will use AI to skip the discipline; others will use it to deepen it.
    → Closes the conversation with a stark either/or, framing AI as a sorting mechanism for leadership philosophies rather than an automatic uplift.
  • The Drucker echo — 'philosophy eats AI' deliberately recalls 'culture eats strategy' (and Andreessen's 'software eats the world'). The chain is: software eats world → AI eats software → philosophy eats AI.
    → Positions the article's central claim within a recognized lineage of management aphorisms, claiming the same level of strategic primacy.
  • Herb Simon's bounded rationality as the analogical anchor for 'bounded patterns'. Kiron and Schrage propose feeding this analogy into ChatGPT itself to explore what should bound patterns; Kiron answers 'purpose'.
    → Models the very reflexivity the conversation advocates — using AI to think about how AI should be bounded.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What 'eats' AI in the X-eats-Y progression?
    Philosophy — because AI's constitutive components are philosophical constructs, the meta-layer that determines AI value is philosophical, not technical or ethical.
  • ✅ What is the analogue of bounded rationality for pattern-based AI?
    Bounded patterns, with purpose (teleology) as the bounding principle.
  • ✅ What is the right diagnosis of the Gemini historical-image failure?
    Teleological confusion — unreconciled competing purposes, exacerbated by indifference until publicity forced a response.
  • ✅ What is the most actionable, inexpensive thing executives can do with this philosophy framing?
    Map the teleology, ontology, and epistemology of their AI initiatives — extending current responsibility mapping.
  • ⭕ Does AI make less-rigorous thinkers more rigorous?
    Unsettled — Schrage frames it as a leadership choice (Ozempic vs. Weight Watchers) rather than an automatic effect of the tool.
  • ✅ How should organizations balance the automate-vs-augment trade-off?
    Use philosophical clarity (purpose, knowledge) to discipline which tasks become mindless and where human-in-the-loop adds leadership value.
  • ✅ How should the human-AI learning relationship be designed?
    A 'learn-to-prompt/prompt-to-learn' virtuous cycle in which human and model learning rates are co-managed.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe conversation calls for executives to do teleology/ontology/epistemology mapping but offers no concrete method, template, or worked example of how to do it. Starbucks and Amazon are gestured at but not decomposed into a reproducible exercise.
    Read next: The companion MIT SMR article ('Philosophy Eats AI') likely supplies more; also relevant: Cynefin frameworks, OKR/teleology-mapping methodologies, and ontology-engineering literature from knowledge-graph practice.
  • incompleteThe 'indifference, not ignorance' diagnosis of the Gemini case is asserted but not evidenced. It is rhetorically convenient (it lets philosophy do explanatory work) but the alternative hypothesis — that the team genuinely failed to anticipate the conflict — is dismissed rather than refuted.
    Read next: Reporting on Google's internal Gemini launch decisions; postmortems of similar AI launch failures.
  • incompleteSchrage's claim that simulated reason 'may be better than the real thing' for many use cases is dropped without elaboration. Which use cases? On what evidence? This is a load-bearing claim about epistemology that is not defended.
    Read next: Daniel Kahneman / Gary Klein on heuristics vs. analytical reasoning; recent benchmarks on LLM reasoning vs. expert human reasoning.
  • uninformedNo engagement with critiques that 'philosophy eats AI' is itself a marketing framing — a management aphorism designed to sell consulting and articles, rather than a tested empirical claim. The thesis is defended by examples chosen to fit it.
    Read next: Critical management studies on the 'X eats Y' aphorism genre; the history of how Drucker's 'culture eats strategy' was actually used and misused.
  • incompleteThe learn-to-prompt / prompt-to-learn cycle treats human and model learning rates as observable and comparable. But no operational definition is offered for either learning rate, and there is no discussion of how an executive would measure whether 'humans are learning faster than the models'.
    Read next: Organizational learning literature (Argyris, Senge); ML evaluation/eval-set methodology; recent work on continuous evaluation in production AI systems.
  • uninformedThe conversation foregrounds teleology/ontology/epistemology but is silent on axiology (theory of value) — even though the entire argument is about value creation. The choice of three of the four classical branches is unexplained.
    Read next: Standard introductions to philosophy that include axiology; literature on value-sensitive design in HCI.
  • incompleteSchrage's hint that pattern-based meaning-making is a 'different philosophical insight' is not developed. Is this a continental/post-structuralist claim about meaning-as-similarity? A pragmatist one? The philosophical lineage of the new account is left implicit.
    Read next: Wittgenstein on family resemblance; connectionism vs. symbolic AI debates; recent philosophy-of-mind work on LLM cognition.
