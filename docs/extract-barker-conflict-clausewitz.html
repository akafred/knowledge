How To Handle Conflict: 7 Secrets From History's Master Strategist

Eric Barker · rss · 2026-05-25 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Leadership Under Pressure
To stop conflict from running your life, treat every interpersonal fight the way Clausewitz told generals to treat war — by subordinating it to a concrete purpose, accepting incomplete information, hitting the real center of gravity, preferring patient defense, planning for the other side's agency, prizing morale over material, and stopping the moment the cost of pressing on exceeds the gain.

practical · expository — Self-help / practical essay. Each section converts a Clausewitz idea into a behavioral prescription for interpersonal conflict. Form is expository with light argumentation; the post is held together by didactic structure (seven labeled principles + a 'Sum Up') and humor rather than by a chain of argument that could be challenged premise-by-premise.

Key terms

  • Objective
    A concrete, single, achievable outcome (apology, solution, behavior change) that the conflict is in service of — explicitly NOT an emotional state in the other person you cannot cause.
  • Center of gravity Leadership Under Pressure
    The single underlying thing the other party actually cares about (respect, control, feeling appreciated) — the hinge that, if addressed, dissolves the surface fight.
  • Defense
    Active patience: holding position, conserving strength, counterpunching when leverage is decisive — NOT passivity or avoidance.
  • Friction / 'fog'
    The permanent, unresolvable shortfall of information and the way reality deviates from plans once two adapting parties interact. Treated as a condition to be navigated, not eliminated.
  • Overreach
    Continuing to press an advantage after the strategic objective has been won, typically to extract emotional satisfaction; defined as more dangerous than direct defeat because it disguises itself as justice.
  • Conflict 'taking command' / 'using you'
    The dynamic in which the fight, once underway, rewrites your goals so that your behavior is now serving the conflict rather than the purpose you began with.
  • The enemy gets a vote Leadership Under Pressure
    Treats the counterparty as an autonomous agent with their own plans; any strategy that assumes a passive counterparty is therefore not a strategy.

Arguments

You should always define a concrete objective before engaging in conflict and recheck it during the fight. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Conflict develops a life of its own once begun.
  • Without an objective, success cannot be judged.
  • Multiple coequal goals cannot be pursued simultaneously.
Implicit premises:
  • You have meaningful agency over whether and how to engage.
  • The conflict has a recognizable 'before' in which deliberation is possible.
  • Your goals are knowable to you, prior to engagement.
Inferences:
  • If you cannot state your objective in one sentence, you should not engage yet.
  • Some conflicts should be declined on the grounds that no proportionate objective exists.
Defense beats offense in everyday conflict.
Explicit premises:
  • Holding ground is cheaper than taking it.
  • Defenders know the terrain and have local support.
  • Attackers must move first and sustain pressure.
  • Attackers often move first out of desperation.
Implicit premises:
  • Interpersonal 'terrain' (status, relationships, narratives) confers the same kind of advantage as military terrain.
  • Time is on the defender's side — i.e., the dispute will not deteriorate while you wait.
  • There is a status quo worth defending — which presupposes the defender is the party with the better current position.
Inferences:
  • If you are the underdog, the framework actually recommends declining to fight rather than fighting cleverly.
  • If the dispute is about an injustice in the status quo, 'defense > offense' systematically favors the party benefiting from it.
Stop pressing once you have won. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Each additional 'shove' eventually costs more than it gains.
  • Overreach creates new resistance instantly.
  • Overreach disguises itself as justice and is therefore hard to detect from the inside.
Implicit premises:
  • There is a recognizable moment of 'having won' that both parties can locate.
  • Your emotional payoff and your strategic interest can be separated in real time.
Inferences:
  • A useful operational rule would be a precommitment: end the exchange at the first concession, not the last.
  • Disputes that lack a clear win condition (chronic resentment, status, identity) may not be amenable to this principle at all.
Plan with the counterparty's agency in mind, recursively. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The other side has plans of their own.
  • Real strategy assumes resistance.
  • At least two recursion steps are needed.
Implicit premises:
  • The other side is roughly as rational and goal-directed as you are.
  • Their utility function is legible enough to model.
  • Strategic interaction in interpersonal disputes is sufficiently game-like to reward recursive reasoning.
Inferences:
  • Where the counterparty is not goal-rational (grief, addiction, ideology, psychosis), the framework breaks down.
  • This argument quietly recasts intimate disputes as strategic games, which is itself a normative choice.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How do I stop ordinary conflicts (work, family, partner) from spiraling and consuming me?
    By importing seven Clausewitzian habits — define objective, accept uncertainty, target center of gravity, prefer defense, model the counterparty, attend to morale, and stop when you've won.
  • ✅ When is more information actually helpful, and when is it procrastination disguised as diligence?
    Treat incomplete information as the permanent condition; the real question is whether you can decide, not whether you have enough data.
  • ✅ Why do arguments 'go sideways' when I was supposedly winning?
    Because you overreached past the strategic win in pursuit of an emotional one; the parting shot manufactures a new fight.
  • ⭕ Which kinds of conflict is this framework actually suited to?
  • ⭕ What does it mean for the counterparty to be 'the enemy' in domains (intimacy, parenting, grief) where the adversarial frame is itself the problem?
  • ⭕ Does 'defense > offense' systematically advantage the party benefiting from the status quo, and if so, when is that ethically defensible?
  • ⭕ How much of this is actually Clausewitz, and how much is generic conflict advice given a strategist's veneer?

Gaps

  • incompleteThe piece never delimits the analogy. Clausewitz wrote about conflict between coherent state actors with continuous policy interests, capable of mobilizing collective will, and existing in a Westphalian system; everyday disputes are dyadic, intimate, often involve unequal power, and frequently lack a coherent 'policy' on either side. The transfer is asserted in the intro and then never re-examined. This is the central category-jump risk: the seven principles inherit their authority from a domain whose structural features mostly do not apply.
    Read next: Compare against Annie Duke (decisions under uncertainty for individuals — 'Thinking in Bets'), Roger Fisher & William Ury ('Getting to Yes' — interest-based negotiation, an explicit alternative to the adversarial frame), Deborah Tannen on argument culture, and Donald Kagan's 'On the Origins of War' for a check on how state-level conflict actually scales.
  • incompleteWhat is genuinely from Clausewitz here vs. Barker's own framing is unclear. 'Subordinate to purpose,' 'fog and friction,' 'center of gravity,' 'defense as the stronger form,' 'the enemy gets a vote' (this last is more US military doctrine than Clausewitz), and 'culminating point' (Section 7) all have textual warrant in 'On War.' But the operationalizations ('ask: do you want an apology?') are Barker's; readers are left unable to separate Clausewitz from self-help.
    Read next: Read Book I of 'On War' directly, especially chapters on the nature of war, friction, and the political object; cross-reference with Beatrice Heuser's 'Reading Clausewitz' or Hew Strachan's 'Clausewitz's On War: A Biography.'
  • incompleteThe 'seven secrets' don't form a tightly coupled theory; they are seven valuable but largely independent prescriptions sharing a tone. Several overlap (Section 1 ≈ Section 7 on goal drift; Section 5 ≈ Section 3 on the counterparty), while no principle covers ethics, power asymmetry, conflict termination by repair, or pre-conflict prevention. Treating the list as a complete strategy would leave several common conflict types (chronic, structural, power-asymmetric, identity-based) outside the frame.
    Read next: John Paul Lederach 'The Little Book of Conflict Transformation' for a non-strategic frame; Marshall Rosenberg 'Nonviolent Communication' for a frame where 'objective' is replaced by 'shared need'; Carol Tavris & Elliot Aronson 'Mistakes Were Made (but Not by Me)' on the cognitive dynamics of overreach.
  • incompleteSections 2 and 5 invoke ideas (decide under uncertainty; model the other side's agency) that have a much more rigorous treatment in decision theory and game theory. Barker's framing stays at the level of aphorism. For a reader already inside the decisions-under-uncertainty literature (Annie Duke, Kahneman), this is the thinnest layer.
    Read next: Annie Duke 'Thinking in Bets' and 'How to Decide'; Schelling 'The Strategy of Conflict'; Axelrod 'The Evolution of Cooperation' for what 'the enemy gets a vote' actually looks like once formalized.
  • illogicalThe 'defense > offense' principle is stated as a near-universal rule, but the supporting reasons (terrain, supply lines, ability to wait) are domain-specific to military conflict and at least partly contested even there. In interpersonal life, the defender often has the time-on-side advantage precisely when they hold the structural power; recommending defense without specifying that condition can read as 'powerful parties should sit tight and wait the other side out.' Barker doesn't flag this.
    Read next: Cf. Stephen Biddle 'Military Power' on the empirical fragility of the defense-dominance claim; Ury 'Getting Past No' on how to engage when you are the disadvantaged party.
