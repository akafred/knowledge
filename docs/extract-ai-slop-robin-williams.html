The best response to AI slop, infinite advice, and online noise is from Robin Williams

Jay Acunzo · article · 2026-06-18 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

AI SlopCognitive SurrenderAgencyLLM LimitationsTaste & JudgmentWriting as Thinking
AI slop, infinite online advice and content noise are all the same thing — secondhand information, knowing *about* — whereas the durable, non-commoditizable value of any creator's work comes only from firsthand lived experience synthesized through a distinctive personal perspective, so the response to the AI era is to draw on your own life rather than let the noise convince you your experience doesn't matter.

Practical-dominant but mixed: the payload is an exhortation (draw on your own life; keep creating; don't surrender to the noise), resting on a theoretical claim about what is the case (knowing about a thing is categorically different from living it; art, unlike science, is non-reproducible). · A narrative / close-reading essay: a fictional film scene (Good Will Hunting) and, then, the acting behind it are used as extended analogy and load-bearing evidence to carry a second-person motivational argument. Rhetorical, escalating in concreteness ('let's make it concrete'), and it ends by re-performing the scene's lines addressed to the reader. — The Good Will Hunting bench scene is not decoration; it is the essay's central exhibit and the essay mimics its structure, closing with a rewrite of Sean's 'your move, chief' speech aimed at the reader. Watch the scope: Acunzo's native habitat is creators/marketers, but he explicitly universalizes ('it's all art... I don't care if you write blog posts for a Fortune 500') — a move that collides with his own art-vs-science distinction and is the seam where the argument thins into a truism.

Key terms

  • AI slop / noise / infinite advice (collapsed into one category)
    Not three problems but one: secondhand information — endless voices and tools selling you 'secrets' and 'magic', producing lifeless output ('a script the creator forgot to act') whose words are correct but un-lived.
  • Firsthand / lived experience ('little life moments', your own 'LLM')
    The actual living of war, love, loss, failure — the internal well an artist draws on, 'sensed more than consciously considered'; Williams was 're-living, not remembering.' Coinage: AI has large language models, people have 'little life moments.'
  • Knowing vs living (expertise vs wisdom / theory vs experience / information vs experience)
    'The difference between expertise and wisdom, between theory and experience, between knowing and living.' AI (and Will) 'knows'; it 'does not feel nor experience. Because it does not live.'
  • Art vs science
    Science is the search for external truth and is reproducible (Scientist #2 would find the same thing). Art is the internal synthesis of truth into meaning and is non-reproducible (no two artists, given any resources, land on the same creation).
  • Reading the room vs reading the internet
    AI 'has read the internet' but 'can't read the room' — it lacks the embodied, contextual, felt sensing that comes only from living.
  • 'A script the creator forgot to act'
    Online work whose words are all present and technically correct but lifeless, because the creator did not bring their own life to them — the phenomenology of slop.

Arguments

The durable, irreplaceable value of your work in the AI era comes from drawing on your own firsthand lived experience, so keep creating from your life rather than surrendering to the noise that says it doesn't matter.
Explicit premises:
  • Knowing about a thing is categorically different from living it (p2).
  • AI knows but does not feel, experience or live, and cannot read the room (p3).
  • Distinctive work comes from the creator drawing on their own lived experience (p4).
  • Art is non-reproducible because it is the synthesis of a unique life into meaning (p5).
Implicit premises:
  • Lived experience is transmissible into work others can access (p9) — otherwise the prescription is empty.
  • The uniqueness/scarcity of lived experience equals market value (p10/p11).
  • Firsthand vs secondhand is a clean binary (p12).
  • The reader in fact possesses, and can reach, a well of distinctive lived experience worth transmitting.
Inferences:
  • He gives no method for converting experience into distinctive work: the 'how' is asserted ('draw on your own LLMs') while the mechanism is declared inaccessible ('sensed more than consciously considered', the artist 'probably couldn't' explain it) — so the advice is inspiring but unactionable.
  • Because he never separates 'firsthand experience' from 'distinctive voice', the real operative variable may be distinctiveness of perspective — which could be achievable without novel firsthand experience, and which AI-augmented work might also carry.
  • AI can also prompt, augment or occasion genuine firsthand experience, which would blunt the binary he relies on — an inference the framing forecloses.
(Self-undermining) The essay rejects secondhand advice and slop and tells you to trust your firsthand experience — while being itself secondhand advice, built on a secondhand fictional artifact, arguing against secondhand material.
Explicit premises:
  • Secondhand information (slop, noise, advice) is the problem (p1).
  • Firsthand lived experience is the answer (p2, p4).
Implicit premises:
  • Secondhand material re-synthesized through a distinctive personal perspective (his bench, his wedding photos, his framing of the scene) becomes firsthand-valuable rather than slop (p9, p10).
  • Therefore the operative distinction is not firsthand-vs-secondhand but distinctive-voice-vs-voiceless.
Inferences:
  • This relocates the entire thesis: the genuine claim is 'distinctiveness of perspective matters', not 'firsthand experience matters' — and the two can come apart, but he never makes the substitution explicit.
  • If distinctive-voice transmission converts secondhand into firsthand, then the reader who absorbs his transmitted meaning is holding secondhand experience — so the antidote reproduces the condition it opposes, unless 'transmission' is doing unexamined work.

Narratives

  • The Good Will Hunting park-bench scene: Sean (Robin Williams) tells Will (Matt Damon) he can quote every art book but has never smelled the Sistine Chapel, held his dying best friend's head, or known real loss — 'you presume to know everything about me because you saw a painting of mine.'
    → The primary exhibit and emotional proof that knowing-about is not having-lived; Will is explicitly recast as 'the human equivalent of ChatGPT', converting the scene into the AI-vs-human thesis. Its lines are re-performed in the coda, addressed to the reader.
  • Acunzo's own wedding photos, taken at the exact bench visible in the scene ('if you pause the movie at just the right moment...').
    → A small firsthand detail that performs the thesis: he layers his lived experience onto a borrowed artifact — quietly enacting (and unwittingly exemplifying) the secondhand-to-firsthand 'conversion' his whole argument silently depends on.
  • Robin Williams the actor, not Sean the character: any actor was handed the same script, but none would have performed it 'like that'; the performance drew on his own life — an imagined childhood scuffed knee, a lost love — 're-living, not remembering.'
    → The load-bearing bridge from 'experience matters for living' to 'experience is the source of distinctive work' — the analogy that transfers the claim onto the reader's own output.
  • The eye-rolling vignette: an artist protests 'AI has never fallen in love... failed then persisted', and people roll their eyes; Acunzo concedes it 'feels squishy and easy to dismiss', like a gnat on a park bench, then tries to make it concrete.
    → Pre-empts the reader's (and a rigorous skeptic's) suspicion that the claim is soft, signaling self-awareness that it risks being a truism — while not actually resolving the softness.
  • The science counter-example: 'scientific discovery would happen regardless of who does it' — Scientist #2 would find the same truth Scientist #1 did.
    → The contrast case that grounds art's non-reproducibility — but also an explicit admission that some work is a reproducible search for external truth, i.e. NOT art, in tension with his later 'it's all art.'

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What makes human work still matter in an era of AI slop and infinite content?
    Firsthand lived experience filtered through a distinctive personal perspective, which AI — knowing but not living — cannot supply.
  • ✅ Why can't AI replace the creator?
    AI knows but does not feel, experience or live; genuine work is the internal synthesis of a unique life into meaning, and is therefore non-reproducible.
  • ⭕ How does one actually convert lived experience into distinctive work?
    Left open and half-acknowledged: he says 'draw on your own LLMs' and 'how YOU see the world', but also that the process is 'sensed more than consciously considered' and even the artist 'probably couldn't' explain it — so no method is offered.
  • ⭕ If only firsthand experience counts, isn't this essay — and everything the reader learns from it — itself secondhand?
    Never raised. The escape hatch (a distinctive voice converts secondhand into firsthand) is used throughout but never examined, so the recursion goes unaddressed.
  • ⭕ Is firsthand-vs-secondhand a binary or a spectrum, and can AI prompt or augment genuine firsthand experience?
    Treated as a hard binary; the possibility that AI occasions real experience, or that all human work recombines secondhand inputs, is not considered.

Gaps

  • incompleteNo mechanism for how lived experience becomes transmissible work. The load-bearing premise — that experience filtered through a distinctive voice 'turns into meaning others can access', converting secondhand to firsthand — is asserted but never examined, and the recursion it creates (the reader receiving his transmitted meaning is then holding secondhand experience) is never addressed. This is the essay's central unexamined move and the point where an anti-hype reader will feel the truism.

Frame as infrastructure for getting lived experience *through* into distinctive, transmissible work, not as motivation.
  • incompleteThe essay is secondhand advice, built on a secondhand fictional artifact, arguing against secondhand material; it survives only by silently reclassifying 'secondhand-through-a-distinctive-voice' as firsthand. That substitution relocates the real claim from 'firsthand experience matters' to 'distinctiveness of perspective matters' — two things that can come apart (and that AI-augmented work might also carry) — but the move is never made explicit or defended.
    Read next: Read on performative / self-referential contradiction and test whether distinctiveness of voice, not novel firsthand experience, is the actual operative variable. Cross with eating-my-own-dog-food (human-originated seed vs prompt-generated framework) and franco-what-we-lose-when-ai-writes (voice as evidence of a Self) to see whether 'voice' can be specified independently of 'lived experience.'
  • incompleteFirsthand vs secondhand is treated as a hard binary, but all human work recombines absorbed (secondhand) inputs — everyone's 'little life moments' are themselves saturated with others' content — and AI can also prompt, augment or occasion genuine firsthand experience. The essay admits no continuum and no role for AI as a spur to real experience rather than a substitute for it.
    Read next: Read dawkins-claude-delusion (which flags exactly this sharp mimicry/genuine-experience binary as a weakness) and the taste article; probe where on the continuum 'your own LLM' actually sits, and where AI functions as a prompt for firsthand experience rather than a replacement of it.
  • incomplete'It's all art' universalizes the firsthand-experience premium to every kind of knowledge work, colliding with the essay's own art-vs-science distinction (some work is a reproducible search for external truth — explicitly NOT art). The piece never marks where the claim stops applying, leaving an inspiring but unfalsifiable exhortation — a live risk given the creator/marketing register in which it is written.
    Read next: Read Adler on separating a practical exhortation from a testable claim, and ask which categories of work the firsthand-experience premium genuinely governs versus where reproducible correctness — not distinctive voice — is what the audience needs (cf. the taste article's correctness-vs-quality axis, where 'correct' and 'good' come apart in the opposite direction).

Closes gaps elsewhere

It is a criterion, not an engineering method, but it directly answers the 'what makes it irreplaceable' question the other piece leaves open.
