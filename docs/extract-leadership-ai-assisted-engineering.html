Presentation: Leadership in AI-Assisted Engineering

Justin Reock · rss · 2026-04-20 Reader 🖥️

Code Was Never the BottleneckDORA & Engineering MetricsEnterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessMeasuring AI ImpactPsychological Safety
Engineering leaders who frame AI as a throughput multiplier rather than a cost-cutting tool — and back that framing with clear policies, learning time, psychological safety, oppositional measurement, and SDLC-wide integration — are the ones whose organizations see positive ROI from AI adoption, while top-down utilization mandates reliably backfire.

practical · mixed — Conference presentation transcript combining empirical data (DX/DORA research) with prescriptive leadership guidance. Argumentative in structure but delivered as a practitioner talk with case studies and Q&A. Ultimately argues for specific leadership actions.

Key terms

  • throughput Measuring AI Impact
    Total value delivered by the engineering organization to customers; the constraint that matters most per Theory of Constraints. Reock explicitly contrasts this with 'cost' as the wrong optimization target.
  • oppositional metrics Measuring AI Impact
    Metrics deliberately placed in tension with each other (e.g., speed vs. quality) so that gaming one metric surfaces degradation in another. Inherited from the SPACE framework's insistence on multi-dimensional measurement.
  • change confidence DORA & Engineering Metrics
    A qualitative DX metric measuring how confident an engineer feels that a change will not break production, scored via top-box Likert
  • Core 4 DORA & Engineering Metrics
    DX's distilled measurement framework combining DORA, SPACE, and DevEx into four core metrics. Positioned as the foundation that AI metrics should layer onto, not replace.
  • temperature
    The randomness parameter in LLM token selection; Reock frames it as a compliance lever leaders can use to control output determinism
  • time to 10th PR Measuring AI Impact
    Onboarding metric: calendar time from a new engineer joining to their 10th accepted, merged pull request. DX reports AI-assisted organizations halve this.

Arguments

Leaders should frame AI as throughput augmentation, not cost reduction strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Per-company data shows enormous variance — the same tools produce wildly different outcomes
  • Organizations with clear policies, learning time, and psychological safety see positive results
  • Theory of Constraints says throughput, not cost, is the job of the machine of business
  • Zapier is hiring more because per-engineer ROI increases
Implicit premises:
  • Engineers who fear replacement will not adopt AI effectively, creating a self-fulfilling prophecy of poor ROI
  • The labor market for engineers remains competitive enough that fear-driven attrition is a real cost
  • Throughput gains from AI are large enough to justify increased hiring budgets
Inferences:
  • Organizations that frame AI as cost-cutting will lose their best engineers and see negative AI ROI, creating a death spiral
  • The 'throughput vs. cost' framing is itself a signal of organizational health independent of AI
Single-metric AI mandates (e.g., 100% utilization) are counterproductive strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure
  • The Cobra Effect demonstrates incentivization backfiring catastrophically
  • Engineers will game utilization metrics (e.g., updating README files)
  • SPACE framework established that productivity requires oppositional multi-dimensional measurement
Implicit premises:
  • Leaders who mandate single metrics are typically motivated by appearing decisive rather than by genuine understanding of AI impact
  • The political incentive to show AI adoption often overrides the analytical incentive to measure impact honestly
Inferences:
  • Any AI KPI dashboard with only utilization metrics is actively misleading leadership
AI should be applied across the SDLC, not just to code generation strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Engineers get only 5-6 hours/week of coding time in scaled organizations
  • AI saves ~3.4 hours/week but interruptions, meetings, and context switching consume far more
  • Theory of Constraints: an hour saved on a non-bottleneck is worthless
  • Morgan Stanley saves 300,000 hours/year on reverse engineering, not code generation
  • Spotify's incident management agent handles 90% of incidents
Implicit premises:
  • The industry's fixation on code generation is partly driven by vendor marketing, since code-gen is the most visible and demo-friendly AI capability
  • Identifying the actual bottleneck requires the kind of measurement infrastructure most organizations lack
Inferences:
  • Organizations that only deploy AI for code completion are leaving most of the potential value on the table
AI will not replace human engineers in the foreseeable future strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • SWE-bench top performer at 44% autonomous task completion
  • Improvement trajectory flattening (34% → 44% over 18 months)
  • 95% of AI pilots fail (MIT NANDA study)
  • Underlying algorithms haven't fundamentally changed since the 1950s
Implicit premises:
  • Benchmark performance on isolated tasks is a reasonable proxy for real-world engineering capability
  • The current rate of improvement will not suddenly accelerate due to architectural breakthroughs
Inferences:
  • Leaders using AI replacement as a cost-cutting strategy are making plans based on capabilities that don't exist and may never exist
Measurement of AI impact requires a mixed-method, multi-dimensional, longitudinal approach strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Aggregate averages hide per-company variance
  • Qualitative and quantitative metrics tell different stories (the doctor metaphor)
  • Survey data is unreliable without 90%+ participation, good engineering, and Goodhart-awareness
  • Metrics without improvement intent are pointless
Implicit premises:
  • Most organizations lack the analytical sophistication to implement this kind of measurement
  • DX (the speaker's company) is well-positioned to sell this capability — this is partly a sales argument
Inferences:
  • The industry will remain confused about AI ROI for years because few organizations will invest in rigorous measurement

Narratives

  • The Cobra Effect: an emperor incentivized cobra kills, people farmed cobras instead, and when the program was cancelled, released cobras made the problem worse
    → Supports the argument against single-metric AI mandates and Goodhart's Law — incentivizing utilization metrics will produce gaming that makes the underlying problem worse
  • Google's Project Aristotle found that psychological safety, not talent or resources, was the top predictor of team performance
    → Supports the proposition that psychological safety matters most and that leaders must proactively address AI displacement fears
  • Zapier has more bots than humans, reduced standups from 5x to 2x/week, cut onboarding from 30 minutes to 2 weeks, and is hiring more than ever
    → Supports the throughput-over-cost framing — the most AI-forward company is hiring more, not fewer, engineers
  • Morgan Stanley's DevGen.AI agent reverse-engineers legacy COBOL/Perl specs, saving 300,000 hours/year
    → Supports applying AI to non-code-generation bottlenecks in the SDLC
  • Spotify 2.0: SRE incident agent provides context and runbook data in Slack within the first critical minutes, handling 90% of incidents
    → Supports SDLC-wide integration — AI applied to incident response rather than code generation
  • The METR study showed 19% productivity decrease despite engineers feeling more productive
    → Supports the proposition that perception and reality diverge and that measurement is essential — you cannot trust subjective assessment of AI productivity
  • The doctor analogy: 'I don't feel well' vs. 'your vitals are fine' — you need both qualitative and quantitative data
    → Supports mixed-method measurement — system metrics without developer context are insufficient

Questions addressed

  • ⭕ What is the actual ROI of AI in engineering organizations?
    Modest positive on average, but enormous per-company variance. The answer depends on leadership practices, not the tools.
  • ✅ How should leaders measure AI impact?
    The DX AI Measurement Framework: oppositional metrics across utilization, impact, and cost dimensions, using mixed methods (telemetry, experience sampling, surveys) longitudinally
  • ✅ Will AI replace engineers?
    No, not now and possibly never. SWE-bench at 44%, trajectory flattening, 95% pilot failure rate. Frame as augmentation.
  • ✅ How should leaders address engineer fear of AI displacement?
    Proactive transparency, frame as throughput not cost-cutting, share data on AI limitations, invest in learning
  • ✅ Where should AI be applied beyond code generation?
    Across the SDLC: incident management, code review, PRD generation, legacy reverse engineering, onboarding
  • ⭕ What organizational factors differentiate AI winners from losers?
    Clear policies, learning time, psychological safety are identified, but the causal mechanism is not deeply explored — correlation from DORA data

Gaps

  • incompleteThe presentation treats 'leadership practices' as the causal variable explaining per-company variance, but does not control for confounders like codebase quality, technical debt, team size, or domain complexity. Companies that already have good DevEx practices may simply be better at everything, including AI adoption.
    Read next: Research on organizational readiness factors for technology adoption; look at DX's own AI Impact Report for more controlled analysis
  • incompleteThe argument against AI replacing engineers relies heavily on current SWE-bench scores and a claim about algorithms not changing since the 1950s. This understates the pace of agentic and scaffolding innovations (MCP, RAG, multi-agent systems) that improve effective capability without changing underlying model architecture.
    Read next: Anthropic, OpenAI, and DeepMind research on agent scaffolding; François Chollet's critique of benchmark-as-capability proxies
  • incompleteNo discussion of how AI changes the nature of engineering work itself — what skills become more/less valuable, how code review practices should adapt to AI-generated code, or whether existing DevEx frameworks designed for human-only teams remain valid when agents are team members.
    Read next: Kent Beck's work on AI-assisted development; Addy Osmani's Agent Skills framework; research on human-AI teaming from Microsoft Research
    closed byThe Orchestration Tax is You — Answers how AI changes the nature of engineering work: architecture/system-design around the attention bottleneck becomes the urgent skill, and review practices must adapt via batching, offloading machine-verifiable checks, and fleet sizing (p9, p11, p12, p19).
  • incompleteDX is both the research organization and the vendor selling the measurement platform. The talk acknowledges DX's role but does not address how this commercial interest might shape which metrics are recommended or how 'success' is defined.
    Read next: Independent developer productivity research (e.g., DORA team at Google, academic DevEx studies not commercially affiliated)
  • uninformedThe claim that 'underlying algorithms haven't changed since the 1950s' is misleading. While perceptrons date to the 1950s, transformers (2017), RLHF, constitutional AI, chain-of-thought, and scaling laws represent genuine algorithmic innovations. The framing conflates 'neural networks exist since the 1950s' with 'nothing has changed.'
    Read next: Vaswani et al. 'Attention Is All You Need' (2017); Kaplan et al. scaling laws papers; survey of post-transformer architectural innovations
