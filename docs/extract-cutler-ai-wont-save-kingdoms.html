AI Won't Save the Kingdoms We Built

John Cutler · rss · 2026-05-25 source 🖥️

AI as AmplifierContext EngineeringCoordination CostHierarchy & Org ChartsMultiplayer vs Single-Player AI
AI will only dissolve the organizational fiefdoms of the ZIRP era if leaders are honest that those fiefdoms were structural responses to coordination, autonomy, and accountability problems — otherwise AI will simply reproduce the same coordination burden in a new, async, agent-mediated shape.

practical · argumentative — Cutler argues for organizational action — he is telling leaders what they must do (make hard structural choices) and what they must not do (use AI to paper over the political economy of fiefdoms). It is argumentative prose with diagnostic and prescriptive registers, addressed to leaders who built or inherited the GM-fiefdom model.

Key terms

  • Fiefdom Coordination Cost
    A semi-autonomous sub-organization — typically a GM-led 'product' or area — with its own budget, team, and P&L-ish accountability, deliberately walled off from cross-org coordination.
  • GM mindset / GM-fiefdom model Coordination Cost
    An organizational design pattern in which a leader owns the outcome of a business area (like a general manager), not just the work, on the assumption that this produces faster, more focused decisions.
  • ZIRP wave / ZIRP era Coordination Cost
    The zero-interest-rate-policy era of cheap capital that funded organizational expansion: more layers, more heads-of-things, more 'products' even when they were not really products end-to-end.
  • Geometric problem Coordination Cost
    The combinatorial explosion of coordination paths as headcount grows — implicitly anchored on Dunbar's number — which made hierarchical sub-orgs feel inevitable.
  • Coordination burden / coordination tax Coordination Cost
    The work of discovering dependencies, negotiating exceptions, routing around politics, and absorbing ambiguity — pushed downward to the front lines when senior leaders refuse to prioritize across fiefdoms.
  • Context transfer Context Engineering
    Moving information, summaries, retrievals, and pattern-matching across an organization — what AI is genuinely good at.
  • Shared understanding Multiplayer vs Single-Player AI
    Joint commitment about who decides, what tradeoffs matter, and which conflicts must be resolved — produced by negotiation among people, not by information delivery.
  • Coordination theater Coordination Cost
    The appearance of working together produced by agents, copious documentation, and persuasive deflectors — without the friction of actually deciding together.
  • Async abundance Coordination Cost
    The flood of agent-generated artifacts, messages, and decision proxies that overwhelms collaborators who still try to decide together.
  • Operating model (coherent vs. incoherent)
    The combination of ownership, decision rights, and prioritization that determines whether AI amplifies clarity or amplifies dysfunction.

Arguments

AI alone will not dissolve fiefdoms. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Fiefdoms were sustained by political economy: misaligned incentives, contested ownership, and senior leaders unwilling to prioritize tradeoffs.
  • AI improves context transfer but does not change decision rights, incentives, ownership, or slack.
  • Context transfer is not the same as shared understanding.
Implicit premises:
  • The binding constraint on cross-fiefdom coordination is political/incentive, not informational.
  • Information friction was a downstream symptom of structural design, not the root cause.
Inferences:
  • Any AI-driven 'flattening' that does not also restructure decision rights and incentives will reproduce fiefdom dynamics within the AI layer (e.g., teams hoarding agents, agents enforcing local optima).
  • Vendors and leaders who pitch AI as a coordination solution are likely to be the same actors who benefit from leaving incentives unchanged.
AI can make the fiefdom problem worse in a new shape (coordination theater / async abundance). strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • AI amplifies existing habits and tunings.
  • Solo-tuned operators now have new tactics to avoid deciding together: agents, persuasive deflectors, copious async docs.
  • Collaborators who want to decide together are overwhelmed handling the async overflow.
Implicit premises:
  • Decisional load is a fixed-sum problem — if avoiders push it onto collaborators, total throughput drops.
  • 'Working alone' is a stable personal tuning that AI accentuates rather than reshapes.
Inferences:
  • Org metrics that count artifacts (docs produced, messages sent, agent runs) will overstate progress while real decision velocity falls.
  • The next bottleneck for engineering organizations is decision-rights design, not LLM capability.
The 'real opportunity' of AI requires first making hard structural choices: end-to-end ownership, matched decision rights, prioritization, and right-sized autonomous teams. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • AI is an amplifier, not a substitute.
  • A coherent operating model already requires these structural choices.
Implicit premises:
  • Leaders have the authority and option to make these choices but have been deferring them.
  • The 'hard choices' framing assumes the obstacle is courage/will rather than genuine structural pressure (budget cycles, regulatory accountability, headcount politics, capital allocation logic).
Inferences:
  • If structural pressures (not just will) are what produced fiefdoms, then AI plus exhortation will not change behavior; only changing the underlying budget/accountability machinery will.
  • The essay's prescription may be necessary but insufficient — it does not specify how to change the conditions that produce unwilling senior leaders in the first place.

Narratives

  • The ZIRP-era growth-by-adding-products pattern: leaders grow by adding more 'products,' more layers, more sub-orgs, more heads of things, with 'product' becoming a container for any sufficiently large bundle of work.
    → Supports the proposition that fiefdom expansion was incentive-driven (career growth, capital availability) rather than design-driven, and that calling something a 'product' was a labeling move that obscured the lack of end-to-end coherence.
  • The downstream sufferer: the person closest to the work who discovers dependencies, negotiates exceptions, routes around politics, and eventually resigns themselves to functioning the best they can.
    → Carries the moral weight of the argument — locates the cost of unresolved structure in a specific human position. Implicitly an appeal to leaders that this cost is invisible to them.
  • Agents talking to agents: solo-tuned operators sending copious documentation and pulling together persuasive deflectors via AI, shutting down conversations.
    → Concretely instantiates 'coordination theater' — shows what AI-accentuated fiefdom-preservation looks like in daily practice.
  • The overloaded collaborator: people who see value in collaboration desperate to be thoughtful while drowning in async overflow.
    → Establishes the asymmetric cost — the burden of async abundance lands on the collaborative, not the avoiders, recreating the front-line absorption pattern from the fiefdom era.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why are the same leaders who built fiefdoms now reaching for AI to escape them?
    Because AI promises the benefits of dissolution (smaller teams, flatter orgs, faster decisions) without forcing them to confront the political-economic choices they have been deferring.
  • ✅ Can AI actually dissolve fiefdoms?
    Only if leaders first make the structural choices the fiefdom model deferred; AI is an amplifier, not a substitute for organizational design.
  • ✅ What goes wrong when AI is applied to a fiefdom org without those structural choices?
    Coordination theater — the appearance of working together via agents and async artifacts, while the decisional burden migrates to collaborators on the front lines.
  • ⭕ Why have senior leaders been unwilling to prioritize across fiefdoms?
    Cutler asserts the unwillingness and the self-interest of fief-leaders but does not theorize the conditions that produce it (budget cycles, capital markets, headcount accounting, career incentives).
  • ⭕ What organizational design replaces the fiefdom model?
    Gestured at — 'clearer end-to-end ownership, decision rights that match responsibility, smaller teams with enough autonomy' — but not specified in any operational detail. Left for the reader.
  • ⭕ How do we tell coordination theater from real coordination, in metrics or practice?
    Not addressed. Cutler names the failure mode but offers no diagnostic test.

Gaps

  • incompleteCutler treats fiefdoms as cultural-political artifacts produced by unwilling leaders and self-interested fief-heads. He does not engage with the genuine structural pressures that may force fiefdom-like structures: capital-allocation cycles, headcount accounting, regulatory accountability, the way investors and boards read org charts, the difficulty of cross-fiefdom budgeting in a quarterly cadence. If those structural pressures are real, the 'hard choices' framing is too voluntarist.
    Read next: Read management-accounting and capital-allocation critiques (e.g., Beyond Budgeting Round Table — Bjarte Bogsnes; Robert Simons on accountability systems; Niels Pflaeging on cell-structure designs). Also: Henry Mintzberg on configurations as responses to environmental contingency, not pathology.
  • incompleteThe essay names 'coordination theater' but offers no test to distinguish it from real coordination in flight. Without a diagnostic, the concept risks becoming a rhetorical move rather than an operational lens.
    Read next: Pair with Cutler's own prior work on operating systems / TBM 412 (institutional overload), and with explicit decision-rights frameworks (RAPID, DACI) that surface where decisions actually get made vs. where they are documented.
  • incompleteCutler asserts AI cannot create slack, clarify decision rights, neutralize incentives, or resolve contested ownership. But he doesn't examine the inverse possibility: that AI changes the unit economics of coordination (cheaper synthesis, cheaper proposals) in ways that might shift the political calculus over time. The argument is statically true but may be dynamically incomplete.
    Read next: Kent Beck on TCR and small-step economics; recent writing on AI's effect on transaction costs in firm boundaries (Coase / Williamson revisited in AI era). Also Ethan Mollick on agent capability inflection points.
  • incompleteThe 'people with a bias toward working alone' characterization is psychological, not structural. It does not interrogate why an organization rewards or selects for that bias — which is itself a fiefdom-era artifact (individual P&L-style accountability, individual performance ratings, etc.).
    Read next: Allen Holub on accountability culture; Edmondson on psychological safety; Cutler's own earlier writing on directly-responsible-individuals and accountability culture.
  • incompleteThe essay addresses leaders and the front lines but is silent on the middle layer — the fief-leaders themselves as actors who must decide whether to dissolve their own kingdoms. Their incentive structure is sketched (self-interest) but not engaged as a design problem.
    Read next: Schein on organizational culture and middle-management as load-bearing; classic literature on 'turkey voting for Thanksgiving' problems in restructurings.
