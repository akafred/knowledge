16) Inga Strümke om hvor AI er på vei, den outsourcede problemløsningen og datamanifolden

Truls Jørgensen og Audun Fauchald Strand — Heis Podcast · podcast · 2026-05-04 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Cognitive SurrenderData SovereigntyExplainable AIInsourcing & In-House CapabilityLLM LimitationsMythosNeurosymbolic AIPublic-Sector Digitalization
Norway's understanding of AI has been hijacked by the chatbot — both as the dominant interface and as the dominant mental model — and this collapses our maturation path: we substitute buying foreign LLM products for the slower, harder work of in-house machine-learning, problem framing, and explainability, even though the technical theory (data manifolds, approximation, neurosymbolic combinations) and the geopolitics both demand that we develop our own capability rather than outsource our problem-solving.

theoretical · mixed — Predominantly theoretical (Strümke arguing for a particular understanding of where AI is and what it can/cannot do), but laced with practical/normative claims about Norwegian public-sector adoption, the chatbot-as-interface trap, and what KI Norge should do. The single tightest theoretical anchor is the data-manifold framing of why neural-net approximations may converge with reality for practical purposes but never coincide with it. The practical edge is a critique of 'outsourced problem-solving' and the Norwegian risk-aversion that fuels it.

Key terms

  • datamanifold (the data manifold)
    Strümke (2026-05-04, ~36min): all data points that exist in reality form a 'teppe' (carpet/blanket) — possibly with holes — constrained by physical and logical rules; we move around the manifold via interventions; ML models, when trained on samples from it, build an internal approximation of that manifold. The critical property: the model's internal manifold is discontinuous (full of holes), so it cannot smoothly traverse reality, even if it approximates it well on sampled points.
  • outsourcet problemløsning Insourcing & In-House Capability
    Strümke: if you cannot build the system that solves your problem yourself, then using it does not mean 'you use AI' — it means you have outsourced problem-solving to the vendor of that system. She proposes this as a regulatory/narrative reframing for KI Norge: organizations should be required to say 'we outsourced to vendor X' rather than 'we use AI'.
  • modningsreise (maturation journey) Insourcing & In-House Capability
    Strümke: the slow organizational/national journey toward genuine AI capability — proper IT infrastructure, data control, legal clarity, problem specification, in-house developers — which she claims ChatGPT has 'spent bein på oss' (tripped us up) by replacing it with 'buy Copilot licenses and force employees to use it 10x/day'.
  • forbrukerteknologi (consumer technology)
    Strümke: the moment a technology becomes something end-users buy and use, mass adoption explodes — and so does the false belief that 'using it' equals 'understanding it' (her examples: laptops, mobile phones, cameras, and now chatbots).
  • nevrosymbolsk AI (neurosymbolic AI) Neurosymbolic AI
    Strümke: the combination of hardcoded deterministic rules and probabilistic neural models; she frames it as an open academic debate (do we need neurosymbolic, or are neural nets enough?), and observes that Claude Code's use of regex for the literal string 'continue' instead of routing to the language model is an implicit production-level vote for neurosymbolic.
  • forklarbar AI (XAI, explainable AI) Explainable AI
    Strümke: the research field (her own) concerned with rendering neural-net behavior meaningful to humans; she notes Google Scholar publications since 2017 have grown exponentially, so the Norwegian public sector's assumption that explainability is unattainable is out of date.
  • universelle approksimasjonsbevis (universal approximation theorems)
    Strümke: the theoretical result that, given sufficient width/depth and data, a neural network can approximate any function — used as the 'for' side of the argument that nets can in principle match reality's manifold.
  • AI-imperativet (the AI imperative)
    Strümke: the cultural/organizational pressure that 'we must use AI to become more efficient', which she frames as the wrong type of motivation — efficiency-via-tokens treated as the goal rather than as one tool among many.
  • udetekterbare bakdører (undetectable backdoors) / one-shot detection LLM Limitations
    Strümke: a planted behavior in a neural network's parameter space that triggers on specific input, embedded so that detection requires solving a hypothesis test against an unknown distribution; her research bets on whether these are provably undetectable or whether a one-shot method exists.
  • Project Glasswing / Mythos Mythos
    Strümke: Anthropic's most-capable vulnerability-finding model (Mythos) released only to vetted partners (Project Glasswing); she argues this is theater, because she and Riegler showed that a swarm of five small 1.2B-parameter models on a MacBook can already find similar vulnerabilities — so Anthropic's restraint is neither a security mechanism nor sustainable.
  • deterministisk vs probabilistisk Neurosymbolic AI
    Strümke: the stack from transistors → bits → assembly → high-level language has always been deterministic; the language-model layer on top is probabilistic — which is architecturally novel and risky as a translation layer.

Arguments

Norway should stop calling its current AI consumption 'AI adoption' and start naming it 'outsourced problem-solving'. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • If you cannot build the system, you cannot meaningfully be said to 'use AI' in the engineering sense.
  • Calling vendor consumption 'AI adoption' obscures the loss of capability and signals false maturity.
  • Narrative reframing (via KI Norge mandate) is a lever politicians actually have.
Implicit premises:
  • Capability sovereignty is a goal worth optimizing for (not maximal short-term productivity).
  • Most current public-sector 'AI strategies' would not survive this re-labeling test.
  • Language about capability shapes which budgets get approved and which org structures get built.
Inferences:
  • Procurement and reporting frameworks should distinguish 'built' from 'consumed' AI capability.
  • KPI dashboards that count 'Copilot seats activated' are measuring outsourcing, not adoption.
A neural network can asymptotically approach reality's data manifold but can never reach it; for most practical purposes the difference will become undetectable, and that is the danger. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The manifold inside a neural net is discontinuous and full of holes.
  • Universal approximation theorems prove that, with sufficient depth/data, nets can approximate any function.
  • Language data may not encode enough of reality (e.g. acceleration of a falling cup, programming-language semantics where humans lack intuition) for language alone to bridge the gap.
  • We have no access to either manifold — neither reality's nor the model's — so verification is impossible in the general case.
Implicit premises:
  • Practical indistinguishability is not the same as identity, and conflating them is a moral/intellectual hazard.
  • Testing-as-empirical-validation has worked historically only because the space being tested was finite; for language-mediated reality it is not.
  • Asymptotic convergence is only meaningful while we keep spending the electricity, which Strümke implies will continue 'til Google goes broke'.
Inferences:
  • The 'all models are wrong, some are useful' framing must be re-asserted now, while the distinction is still perceptible, because future generations may lose the felt difference.
  • Verification methods that assume manifolds can be compared (intuitive checks like 'does it know the cup falls?') do not generalize to programming-language semantics or other domains where humans lack intuition.
Forcing junior developers to use agents from day one will produce a generation that cannot judge when to use them. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Judgment about when to delegate to an agent requires being able to do the task yourself.
  • Learning is physically slow and painful — neural connections must form through use.
  • Tacit knowledge of code ('magefølelse') only develops through hands-on exposure.
  • Humans are 'trege' (slow) and 'late' (lazy) — without forcing functions, the easy delegation will win.
Implicit premises:
  • The set of judgment calls about agents will grow, not shrink, so the faculty being eroded becomes more important over time.
  • There is no efficient retrofit for tacit knowledge once an organization has skipped it.
  • The 'in 30 years we'll see' framing is an excuse not to act now.
Inferences:
  • Organizational policies that mandate AI use across the board may be eroding the very capacity that lets the organization assess AI tools.
  • Strümke's own NAV experiment — teaching three junior developers to use AI-assisted dev as an enabler — is a counter-program worth watching.
Anthropic's Project Glasswing is theater, not security; vendor-controlled responsible-release is structurally insufficient. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Strümke and Riegler showed that a swarm of five 1.2B-parameter models running on a MacBook can find vulnerabilities comparable to those Mythos found (e.g. cross-file logic, red herrings).
  • Anthropic is a competitor in a market and must monetize; restraint is bounded by financial pressure.
  • Historical precedent (OpenAI's GPT-2 'too dangerous to release' walk-back) shows vendor restraint dissolves once monetization opportunity arrives.
  • There will be a successor to Mythos, and one to that successor; the threat surface is not closed by withholding one model.
Implicit premises:
  • Defensive infrastructure must be public-research-driven, not vendor-policy-driven.
  • Norwegian researchers' inability to mobilize fast enough (forskningsråd cycle) is itself a national-security vulnerability.
  • 'Responsible AI' marketing can mask the absence of actual responsibility.
Inferences:
  • Norway should not rely on Anthropic's restraint as part of any defense posture.
  • The KI Norge mandate should include emergency coordination authority for short-cycle academic mobilization.
Risk-aversion is a worse risk than the risks it is trying to avoid. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Norwegian organizations refuse to interpret data-protection law charitably and default to inaction.
  • Inaction leads to mass purchase of foreign tokens (Copilot licenses).
  • Foreign-token dependence carries strategic, economic, and geopolitical risks that compound over decades.
  • Classical ML (regression/classification) is deterministic, explainable, cheap, and well-suited to the actual public-sector problems.
Implicit premises:
  • Bounded risk-taking in piloted cases is qualitatively different from systemic gambling.
  • The state can absorb risk that individual agencies cannot.
  • Nikolai Tangen's forced resignation is symptomatic of a culture that punishes the prominent and self-reinforces 'cover your ass'.
Inferences:
  • The Norwegian instinct to be 'fasilitering, ikke forced' is a category error when the problem requires concrete case selection and capital deployment.
  • Annual measurement of in-house ML systems built in Norway could function as the missing forcing function.

Narratives

  • Strümke recoiling when gym-goers approach her mid-set to monologue about how they use ChatGPT — and the consolation that public visibility has at least stopped men from explaining to her what she already knows.
    → Establishes the chatbot-as-cultural-event phenomenon at street level, and her embodied skepticism toward the 'everyone now thinks they understand AI' dynamic.
  • AlphaGo defeating Lee Sedol in 2016 as Strümke's personal 'now the future starts' moment — and her surprise that the wider world waited for a chatbot in 2022.
    → Reframes what 'where AI is going' means by relocating the technical breakthrough to a domain (reinforcement learning) that never became consumer technology.
  • Google's data centers using only 3% of input electricity on CPU because ML-optimized airflow and cooling — pre-chatbot AI that nobody noticed.
    → Counter-evidence to the 'AI = chatbot' equation; supports the 'AI is much more than chatbot, but try giving an example' aside.
  • The Claude Code source-code regex for the literal lowercase 'continue' — instead of routing the keystroke through the LLM — which Strümke posted on Instagram and was misread as 'doesn't understand regex uses less power than LLMs'.
    → Concrete proof-by-example that the frontier has already chosen neurosymbolic AI, and a barbed comment on how naturally-language framings prevent people from seeing the technical irony.
  • The jazz student who must play one song for the entire first year before being allowed to move on — and only then discovers the principles generalize to all songs.
    → Analogy for 'depth-first' learning the host Audun describes for junior developers; supports the inference that mastery requires depth before breadth even (or especially) in an AI-assisted era.
  • Strümke buying one of the first XPS gamer laptops as an NTNU student because she 'didn't want to learn garbage collection' — and bracing for the boys at Programvareverkstedet to throw her out.
    → Personal reminder that the 'this abstraction makes you worse' anxiety has been raised at every layer of the stack from manual memory management onward — and that being right about that history does not entail the current case is benign.
  • Inga writing a book shortly after ChatGPT launched and refusing to let 'a wretched language model into my work' — but happily letting it draft sections of reports where the product is the goal, not the expression of her thinking.
    → Personal demonstration of the modal distinction she argues developers must learn to make between high-load-bearing and low-load-bearing artifacts.
  • Standing at the table with cups during the recording, illustrating the data manifold: she can pick a cup up; she cannot make it hover; if she lets go it falls at 9.1 m/s² — and chatbots correctly predict that, which is why we have intuition that their language manifold tracks reality for everyday physics, even though we lack any such intuition for programming languages.
    → The narrative core of the entire 'datamanifold' frame — visualized in lieu of a whiteboard.
  • Mikael Riegler at SimulaMet building a swarm-of-five-tiny-models scaffold on a MacBook that found vulnerabilities comparable to those Anthropic's Mythos found.
    → Carries the entire 'Glasswing is theater' argument and the broader claim that capability is no longer gated by vendor frontier access.
  • Strümke's two PhD students working in opposite directions: one proving undetectable backdoors are undetectable; the other developing a one-shot detection method 'he is just as smart, one year younger'.
    → Personal stake / cliffhanger — and a structural illustration that AI security is still settling foundational mathematical questions, not just engineering ones.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Where is AI actually heading?
    Toward language-modeled chatbots and agents (the two are converging) as the dominant interface, because of natural-language affinity — but the deeper trajectory is whether neural-net manifolds can approximate reality well enough to feel indistinguishable from it, which is an open research question.
  • ⭕ Will LLMs become 'good enough' to write all kinds of software, or are there fundamental limits?
    Strümke translates the question into the data-manifold framing: whether the language sub-manifold (training data) can carry enough information to represent the full target programming-and-system manifold is genuinely open. Humans lack intuition for programming-language semantics in the way they have intuition for falling cups, so verification methods are weaker here.
  • ✅ What should KI Norge actually do?
    Enforce a narrative distinction between 'AI use' and 'outsourced problem-solving'; pick concrete cases (sykefravær) and have the state absorb risk that individual agencies refuse; reduce the over-reliance on facilitation/conferences; coordinate research faster than the Forskningsråd cycle. Strümke admits she doesn't fully understand KI Norge's current mandate.
  • ⭕ How does Norway escape Big Tech dependency without isolationism?
    Strümke names it as 'grisevanskelig' and 'et politisk spørsmål'; we cannot pause, cannot match US datacenter scale, but Norway is the richest country in the world and if we cannot do it, no one can. The honest answer is: we don't have one yet.
  • ✅ Is the felt erosion of programmer skill under AI assistance just the same fear we had about garbage collection?
    Strümke acknowledges yes (historical pattern recurs) but argues 'something is not historically new does not mean it is not dangerous' — we cannot see 30 years forward. The discussion ends in genuine open empirical uncertainty, with the host Audun's optimistic note (more programmers, lower barrier) sitting against Strümke's pessimism about tacit-knowledge erosion.
  • ✅ Is Anthropic's Glasswing a meaningful security mechanism?
    No — Strümke and Riegler showed that a swarm of small open models can already find similar vulnerabilities. Vendor restraint is theatrical and unsustainable.
  • ⭕ Can undetectable backdoors in neural nets be planted?
    Open — two of Strümke's PhD students are working on opposite hypotheses. She admits she believes both, which is logically impossible, and is excited to see which proves correct.
  • ✅ Is XAI mature enough to support real public-sector ML deployment in Norway?
    Yes — the field has grown exponentially since 2017; the Datatilsynet 2021 stance on the NAV sykefravær case is empirically out of date.
