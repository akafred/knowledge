TBM 415: Demand Mix, Discovery, and AI as a (Dys)function Multiplier

John Cutler · email · 2026-04-09 source 🖥️

AI as AmplifierCode Was Never the BottleneckProduct Discovery & Experimentation
How teams should operate is determined by the mix of demand they face and how they shape that demand — and AI will not fix your funnel but will reveal and amplify whatever version of it you are already running, accelerating both functional and dysfunctional patterns.

theoretical · expository — Theoretical: argues for understanding demand mix as the primary lens for organizational analysis, rather than prescribing specific actions. Expository in form: builds a conceptual framework (demand funnel, mix, mandate levels) and then applies it to AI. The AI section is the payoff, but the framework comes first. Classic Cutler: the diagrams carry as much argument as the text.

Key terms

  • demand mix Product Discovery & Experimentation
    The composition of what enters a team's work funnel — where it came from, who is involved, and what type of work it is (support tickets, internal requests, production fires, strategic goals). The mix determines what practices are appropriate. Two teams with identical processes but different demand mixes will have completely different outcomes.
  • demand shaping Product Discovery & Experimentation
    The activities a team and organization use to actively influence what enters the funnel — discovery, experimentation, back-pressure, selective intake, strategic alignment. Distinct from prioritization (which happens after demand arrives). Shaping happens upstream; prioritization happens downstream.
  • (dys)function multiplier AI as Amplifier
    AI amplifies whatever organizational patterns already exist — if demand is well-shaped and teams are learning, AI accelerates learning; if demand is chaotic and teams are negotiating, AI accelerates chaos. The parenthetical signals that the same technology produces opposite effects depending on context.
  • the funnel Product Discovery & Experimentation
    Not a sales funnel. The flow of all inputs (ideas, requests, fires, goals) through filtering, shaping, and execution toward change. Cutler's key move: the funnel can end at 'feeding a team' (bad) or at 'meaningful change for customers' (good). Where you think the funnel ends determines everything.
  • mandate levels
    A vertical spectrum from 'build exactly this' (task execution) to 'generate long-term business outcomes' (strategic autonomy). Different parts of the organization operate at different altitudes simultaneously. The left side (prescriptive) looks simple but pushes complexity downstream; the right side (empowered) looks messy but engages complexity directly.
  • premature convergence Product Discovery & Experimentation
    Making decisions about what to build too early in the process, before sufficient learning has occurred. Driven by the mental model that engineering is the bottleneck and work must be 'de-risked' before reaching it. AI accelerates this pattern if the mental model doesn't change.

Arguments

AI applied to the engineering-as-bottleneck mental model will accelerate organizational dysfunction rather than resolve it strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The dominant mental model treats engineering as the constraint and organizes around feeding it
  • This model produces premature convergence, performative certainty, large batches of pre-shaped work, and effort shifted from understanding customers to managing internal dynamics
  • AI makes it faster to generate ideas, write PRDs, prototype, and ship
  • If the mental model stays the same, more of everything enters the funnel faster
Implicit premises:
  • The bottleneck was never actually engineering capacity — it was the organization's ability to learn and make coherent decisions
  • Speed of execution without speed of learning is not just wasteful but actively harmful — it creates more sunk-cost attachment to bad ideas
  • The people who advocate for AI adoption in organizations are overwhelmingly operating within the engineering-as-bottleneck mental model
Inferences:
  • Organizations that adopt AI most eagerly (those who feel most constrained by engineering capacity) may be the ones most damaged by it — because their mental model ensures AI accelerates their dysfunction
  • The Turing Post 'Unsexy Truth' framework (legibility as prerequisite for AI) maps directly to Cutler's argument: you can't shape demand coherently if you can't describe your own workflows. Legibility is the infrastructure for demand shaping
  • The vibe coding debate is a surface manifestation of this deeper question: vibe coding is the engineering-as-bottleneck model applied to the individual — 'I can code faster' without 'I can learn faster'
Practices that look dysfunctional in one demand context can be perfectly rational in another — there are no universal best practices strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • A feature factory with controlled intake can produce predictable, trusted delivery
  • An 'empowered' organization without demand coherence produces chaos
  • The practices (Scrum, dual-track, continuous discovery) are less important than the demand mix they're applied to
Implicit premises:
  • The agile/product community has been too prescriptive about practices without acknowledging demand context — 'empowered teams' has become dogma disconnected from conditions
  • Calling something a 'feature factory' is typically pejorative, but Cutler is deliberately rehabilitating the concept when the demand context warrants it
Inferences:
  • Organizational advice that doesn't start with 'what is your demand mix?' is likely wrong for at least half of its audience
  • Consulting frameworks that prescribe 'empowered teams' without first analyzing demand mix are selling a solution to a problem they haven't diagnosed

Narratives

  • The efficient feature factory PM: runs a Scrum shop, calls it an 'efficient feature factory,' went from untrusted to loved by stakeholders through controlled intake, back-pressure, and predictability. 'I wish it was different, but that is what we do here.'
    → Rehabilitates the feature factory concept by showing it can be a rational, effective response to a specific demand context. Implicitly challenges the product community's reflexive disdain for feature factories.
  • The 'empowered' PM from a company whose leaders appear on popular product podcasts: 'Honestly, it is a shit show. Empowered here means we are basically empowered to negotiate with dozens of teams across massively competing priorities.'
    → Devastating counter-narrative: the organization that looks right on paper (empowered, product-led) is actually worse than the feature factory, because the demand mix is chaotic and there's no coherent shaping. The detail about leaders on podcasts is a pointed critique of the gap between public narrative and internal reality.
  • AI-powered teams going around the company 'basically commanding other people to help them' while infrastructure teams beg for headcount
    → Illustrates how AI adoption within the engineering-as-bottleneck model creates new internal power dynamics without changing the underlying dysfunction. The AI teams get status; the foundation teams get deprioritized.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How should teams operate / what practices should they use?
    It depends entirely on the demand mix and how it's shaped — there are no one-size-fits-all approaches. Start by understanding your demand context.
  • ✅ What does AI change about product development?
    AI amplifies whatever model you're running. If engineering-as-bottleneck: more dysfunction, faster. If learning-oriented: more learning, faster.
  • ✅ Why do 'empowered' product organizations often feel worse than feature factories?
    Because 'empowered' without coherent demand shaping means empowered to negotiate chaos. The demand mix, not the org model, determines outcomes.
  • ⭕ How do you actually shift from the engineering-as-bottleneck model to the learning model?
  • ⭕ What happens to the teams stuck in the engineering-as-bottleneck model when AI-accelerated competitors using the learning model outpace them?
