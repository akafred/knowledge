Key terms

  • subsidy era AI Unit Economics
    The period when AI companies offered flat-rate or below-cost pricing to acquire users, absorbing the true compute costs
  • demand crunch AI Unit Economics
    A situation where demand for AI compute tokens far exceeds supply, driving up prices and forcing rationing
  • agent harness Agent Harness
    The surrounding product infrastructure (UI, orchestration, monitoring, context management) in which an AI model operates — distinct from the model itself
  • growing up Industrialization of AI
    The transition from experimental/subsidized/unregulated AI to economically disciplined, strategically governed, and productionally mature AI
  • licensing regime AI Regulation & Governance (External)
    De facto government control over which companies can access frontier AI models, even without formal legislation — exercised through informal pressure and compute-access gatekeeping

Arguments

AI has transitioned from startup curiosity to critical global economic infrastructure strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • GPU rental prices up 40% in six months
  • Top two labs generate ~$60B aggregate annual revenue
  • All tiers of labs are sold out of tokens
  • Cloud providers posting record AI-driven growth (AWS +28%, Azure +40%, GCP +63%)
  • Big tech on track to spend $750B on AI in 2026
Implicit premises:
  • Revenue and spending growth at this scale cannot be dismissed as speculative
  • When something is sold out and prices are rising, it has crossed from discretionary to essential
Inferences:
  • If all tokens are being sold and demand is a 'vertical wall,' the constraint on AI impact is now supply-side, not demand-side — the bubble narrative is definitively wrong
Usage-based pricing will replace flat-rate pricing across AI products strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • GitHub Copilot switching to usage-based billing
  • A quick chat and a multi-hour autonomous session shouldn't cost the same
  • Nadella says per-user businesses become per-user plus usage businesses
Implicit premises:
  • The cost structure of AI inference makes flat-rate pricing inevitably unprofitable at scale
  • The subsidy era was a deliberate user-acquisition strategy, not a sustainable model
Inferences:
  • Usage-based pricing will create a cost-consciousness about AI usage that could slow adoption among casual users even as power users spend more
The US government is constructing a de facto licensing regime for frontier AI strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • White House opposed Anthropic expanding Mythos access to 70 companies
  • Cited national security and compute constraints
Implicit premises:
  • The government treats frontier AI capability as analogous to defense-relevant technology
  • Informal power is just as constraining as formal regulation when applied to companies dependent on government contracts
Inferences:
  • This precedent means future frontier models will face government access negotiations before commercial rollout, creating a two-tier market
OpenAI's single-interface Codex strategy is superior to Anthropic's split Claude Code / Cowork strategy strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • Codex tailors itself by work type (finance, marketing, etc.) within one interface
  • Knowledge workers will want to be more technical
Implicit premises:
  • Technical capability is a spectrum, not a binary
  • Simplified tools limit users rather than empowering them
  • The direction of user evolution is toward more technical sophistication, not less

Narratives

  • The GPT goblin incident — GPT-5.1 onwards developed a quirk of mentioning goblins due to a 'nerdy personality' RL artifact that cascaded through model-on-model training
    → Supports the proposition that stacked model training creates emergent, hard-to-predict behaviors with alignment implications. Makes the abstract concern concrete and memorable.
  • The hobbyist PC to Apple II Plus analogy — agent tooling evolving from hand-wired DIY (OpenClaw in January) to integrated products (Codex, Cursor SDK)
    → Supports the claim that the product layer is industrializing, not just the models. Frames current moment as an inflection point in usability.
  • The Anthropic Mythos policy whiplash — White House went from reinstating Anthropic to opposing its model expansion within a single week
    → Illustrates the proposition that government AI policy is improvised and unstable, making the 'informal licensing regime' characterization vivid.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Is the AI investment boom a bubble?
    No — demand exceeds supply, revenue is real and growing, and pricing is shifting to reflect true costs. The bubble narrative is refuted by the data.
  • ✅ What happens to AI pricing as the subsidy era ends?
    Usage-based billing replaces flat-rate, led by GitHub Copilot and Microsoft's broader strategy.
  • ⭕ How will governments regulate frontier AI?
    Emerging informal licensing regime via compute-access gatekeeping, but the form is improvised and unstable.
  • ⭕ Will AI create a permanent underclass?
    Explicitly deferred — author argues Silicon Valley builders are not the right people to answer this and that economists should lead the analysis.
  • ⭕ What are the second-order effects of usage-based pricing on AI adoption patterns?

Gaps

  • incompleteThe piece argues the subsidy era is ending but never addresses what happens to the vast user base that was acquired through subsidized pricing. Will usage-based billing cause churn, consolidation around cheaper models, or a bifurcation between power users and casual users? The demand-is-infinite claim and the end-of-subsidies claim are in tension — if prices rise substantially, demand may not be as infinite as claimed.
    Read next: Benedict Evans or Ben Thompson on AI pricing elasticity; economic analysis of SaaS-to-usage transitions in cloud computing history
  • incompleteThe claim that knowledge workers will choose to become more technical (favoring Codex over Cowork) is asserted without evidence. Historical precedent suggests the opposite — most technology adoption succeeds by lowering the technical bar, not raising it.
    Read next: Clayton Christensen on 'good enough' technology; history of no-code/low-code adoption
  • uninformedThe piece treats the AI industry's 'growing up' entirely from a US-centric perspective. No mention of Chinese AI labs, EU regulation, or non-Western demand patterns, despite framing AI as 'critical global economic infrastructure.'
    Read next: Matt Sheehan on China's AI ecosystem; EU AI Act implementation updates
  • incompleteThe goblin incident is treated as a curiosity with alignment implications, but the piece doesn't explore the depth of those implications — what does it mean for safety training specifically when RL quirks cascade through model stacks?
