AI Enthusiasts Are in a Race Against Time, AI Skeptics Are in a Race Against Entropy

Charity Majors · rss · 2026-07-11 Reader 🖥️

AI as AmplifierAI BacklashAI Confidence TheaterCognitive DebtFeedback LoopsMeasuring AI ImpactPsychological SafetyTechnical DebtTrustVibe Coding
The AI enthusiast-versus-skeptic split inside high-performing engineering orgs is not one side being right and the other wrong but two groups each responding to a real existential threat (missed windows vs. systems decaying into slop) that they never see the other's side of, and the cure is to rebuild a shared reality by telling the whole story and then treat AI adoption as an engineering problem rather than a rhetorical war.

practical · Persuasive practical essay (leadership argument) — a book of rules for how to act, not a theory of how things are. — A prescriptive, diagnosis-then-prescription essay by Honeycomb's CTO aimed at engineering leaders and staff+ engineers navigating the AI transition. It argues from a single composite cautionary tale plus one exemplar (Fin/Intercom) rather than systematic evidence, and is deliberately warm, profane and metaphor-heavy. Its aim is behavioral change — to reconnect two feuding camps — not to establish a proposition about the world.

Key terms

  • race against time
    The enthusiast's existential threat: teams that sit out AI while competitors lean in hard could be out of business before the dust settles — the window, not the technology, is the risk.
  • race against entropy
    The skeptic's existential threat: shipping code faster than anyone can read it degrades reliability, evaporates institutional knowledge and leaves systems nobody understands. Majors never uses the word 'entropy' in the body — she describes it ('reliability degrades... products burbling into incoherence... systems melting into slop'); the title carries the term.
  • no natural feedback loop
    The structural diagnosis: the wins and the costs of AI acceleration accrue to two different groups, so the person who reaps the win never feels the downstream cost and cause is disconnected from effect.
  • tell the whole story
    The first remedy: whoever celebrates a win must also surface its downstream costs (and whoever surfaces costs must include what was achieved), in the same forum where the win was announced.
  • AI is an amplifier
    Borrowed from Nathen Harvey / the 2025 DORA report: AI magnifies the strengths of high-performing organizations and the dysfunctions of struggling ones — so it cannot be a shortcut around engineering discipline.
  • moral authority of ownership
    The standing to decide and to push back is earned by owning the consequences: whoever is left holding the bag should get final say over what goes in it, so engineers who run the code must be extremely involved in defining what they agree to support.
  • credible authority (on AI)
    For a critique to land, the critic must be a genuine expert in the thing being critiqued; arguing against the new ways purely from a position steeped in the old ways forfeits standing.
  • Scylla and Charybdis / don't sink the boat
    The two symmetric failure modes leaders must steer between — the Scylla of missed windows and the Charybdis of systems melting into slop; the leader's job #1 is not to sink the boat while threading between them.

Arguments

The enthusiast/skeptic conflict is a solvable coordination failure, not a war to be won, because both camps are correctly perceiving real threats that the other camp structurally cannot see. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Enthusiasts see real, discontinuous capability leaps and a real risk of missing the window.
  • Skeptics see real reliability decay, evaporating knowledge and unsupportable systems.
  • The wins and the costs accrue to two different groups of people.
  • There is therefore no natural feedback loop connecting cause (the win) with effect (the downstream cost).
Implicit premises:
  • Enthusiasts' load-bearing assumption: the current AI leap is durable and directionally correct, so speed of adoption is the binding risk.
  • Skeptics' load-bearing assumption: trust, comprehension and reliability are slow-built assets whose loss is not recoverable at AI speed, so unread code is a net withdrawal.
  • Both are reasoning correctly from partial, non-overlapping evidence — so the disagreement is an information problem, not a values or competence problem.
Inferences:
  • If both sets of evidence are real but each camp sees only its own, then the divergence is caused by the missing feedback loop, not by one camp being irrational.
  • Therefore restoring the loop (shared reality) dissolves the apparent contradiction and makes joint problem-solving possible.
AI cannot substitute for engineering discipline, so discipline must be invested in more, not less, as AI adoption accelerates. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The DORA 2025 finding that AI is an amplifier of existing organizational strengths and dysfunctions.
  • Fin's real discontinuous leap was produced by pre-existing discipline, fast feedback loops and a measurement culture — not by AI being magic.
  • AI will not solve for a lack of discipline, tooling gaps, or disconnected management.
Implicit premises:
  • The prerequisites AI amplifies (fast feedback loops, guardrails, observability, a measurement culture) are exactly the things that make unread AI output safe to ship.
  • Because AI amplifies dysfunction as readily as function, an undisciplined team that leans in accelerates its own decay.
Inferences:
  • If AI multiplies whatever is already there, then the return on AI is bounded by the quality of the underlying engineering system.
  • Therefore the rational response to faster code generation is to strengthen the feedback and control systems around it.
The people best positioned to make AI adoption safe are precisely the skeptics, but only if they are trusted and credible — so leaders must both listen to them and skeptics must build genuine AI expertise. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Skeptics are the people who metabolize and operationalize change so customers don't leave and employees don't quit.
  • They can only participate constructively when they trust they will be listened to and taken seriously.
  • Standing to push back is earned by owning consequences and by being a credible authority on the topic (AI).
Implicit premises:
  • Ownership and credibility are separable: owning the bag gives you the right to be heard, but expertise in AI is what makes the critique land.
  • A skeptic who refuses to learn AI cedes the very standing needed to shape how it is used.
Inferences:
  • If the safety of AI adoption depends on the skeptics, and their participation depends on trust plus credibility, then investing in both is a precondition for safe adoption.

Narratives

  • A conference presenter tells an easy-breezy story of astonishing vibe-coding wins (year-long rewrites done in weeks, backlogs cleared) to a room of enthusiasts who eat it up uncritically — while colleagues at the same company describe those projects as a horror show, with teams still grinding through cleanup months later. Majors flags it as a deliberate composite ('I am subtweeting MANY TALKS').
    → Opening exhibit: dramatizes the selective success story (confidence theater) and the invisible-downstream-cost asymmetry that the whole essay diagnoses.
  • The Fin (formerly Intercom) engineering org: CTO Darragh Curran challenged R&D to double productivity in a year ('2x'); nine months later they reported 3x'ing output (merged PRs / R&D headcount) with mixed but real results — defect backlog more than halved, >2x product changes, 39% faster idea-to-shipped, code quality improving after 18 months of decline, downtime down 35%. Footnotes concede special conditions: a decade of in-house AI expertise and a near-death experience that 'cleared the deck.'
    → Majors's 'north star of good' — her existence proof answering 'is any of it real?', while modeling the honesty (PRs are imperfect; special conditions) she prescribes.
  • A caricatured exchange: an enthusiast demands shipping without code review ('Company X is doing it... Why do you hate the future?') and the skeptic explodes ('you want me to just auto-accept this shit?... your mother had a face like a donkey'). Both are partly right; the productive version replaces the insults with 'What would it take for you to feel comfortable shipping code to production without reading it?'
    → Dramatizes the difference between rhetorical conversation-stoppers and the engineering reframe that is the essay's second prescription.
  • An exasperated engineer tells Majors: 'I don't WANT to be an AI hater. I studied AI in school! ... I feel like I'm getting backed into a corner where I have to be a hater because I'm the only one left who gives a shit about reality! Is any of it real?'
    → Humanizes the skeptic's trap and motivates Majors's decision to 'show her work' with the Fin example.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Is any of the AI productivity story real, or is it all hype?
    Partly real: the Fin case is a genuine discontinuous leap, but it was gated on pre-existing engineering discipline and a measurement culture — it is not magic, and PR-count is an admittedly imperfect proxy.
  • ✅ Why have enthusiasts and skeptics stopped talking, and why does the divide keep widening?
    Because the wins and costs accrue to different people so there is no natural feedback loop, and because the asymmetry of where wins vs. costs surface is structural rather than malicious.
  • ✅ How should teams settle concrete disputes like 'ship without code review'?
    Reframe them as engineering problems — ask 'what would it take?' and put the prerequisites (evals, tests, guardrails, observability, blast-radius reduction) on the roadmap — rather than as epistemological debates.
  • ✅ Who has the standing to push back on how AI gets used?
    Those who own and operate the consequences AND who are credible authorities on AI itself; ownership grants the right to be heard, expertise makes the critique land.
  • ✅ Is AI a shortcut around engineering discipline?
    No — AI is an amplifier of existing strengths and dysfunctions, so engineering discipline becomes more vital, not less.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe essay explicitly scopes itself to relatively high-performing teams that already have engineering discipline, fast feedback loops and a measurement culture, and excludes disconnected management, low-discipline/low-trust teams, tiny startups and behemoths. But that is most organizations. The whole 'apply engineering rigor / what would it take?' prescription presupposes the very prerequisites the excluded majority lack, and Majors offers no path for how a team without Fin-level foundations bootstraps them under competitive pressure.
    Read next: Read enterprise-AI readiness ladders and the 'velocity multiplier becomes a debt accelerator' work for teams without foundations: how low-discipline orgs sequence guardrails, CI/CD, testing and observability before leaning into AI.
  • incompleteThe core remedy — 'tell the whole story,' 'invite feedback,' 'close the loop,' 'make it easy' — is a behavioral/individual-virtue prescription, yet Majors herself names the structural incentives working against it (wins are trumpeted publicly, costs griped privately; real power dynamics make honest cost-disclosure risky). Beyond 'surface it in the same forum,' she gives no incentive or mechanism-design fix that would make disclosing your own downstream costs structurally rewarded rather than merely encouraged.
    Read next: Read commons-governance and mechanism/costly-signaling work (Ostrom on shared resources; signaling under cheap talk) and psychological-safety instrumentation: how to make cost-disclosure a low-cost, high-status default rather than an act of individual courage.
  • incompleteThe 'north star of good' rests on a single, self-selected success case (Fin) whose own footnotes concede special conditions — a decade of in-house AI expertise and a near-death experience that 'cleared the deck' — and whose headline metric (merged PRs / R&D headcount) she admits is imperfect. How representative or reproducible the 3x result is for teams lacking those conditions is asserted ('well within reach for the rest of us') more than demonstrated.
    Read next: Read controlled/longitudinal AI-productivity studies and the measuring-AI-impact literature on selection bias in success case studies and on outcome-anchored, oppositional metrics beyond PR counts.
  • unstated-assumptionThe framing quietly grants the enthusiast premise as more-likely-true — that the field is 'directionally moving toward software factories and AI-validated diffs' and that the capability leap is durable — which makes the 'race against time' threat as real as the 'race against entropy' one. The essay never entertains that the enthusiast direction could itself be partly hype or bubble, which would deflate the missed-window threat and unbalance the two-sided symmetry it depends on.
    Read next: Read the AI-bubble and frontier-lab-hype debates to test whether the 'missed window' existential threat is as well-evidenced as the reliability-decay one, or whether the symmetry is partly assumed.
