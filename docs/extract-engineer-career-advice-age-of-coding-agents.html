Key terms

  • taste Taste & Judgment
    Earned pattern-matching — the ability to tell good from great, and more basically to tell whether output is good at all — built through deliberate reps ('study good vs. great'), not innate; the faculty whose loss, not bad code itself, is the real risk of AI.
  • the ungradeable part
    The dimensions of engineering work that have no answer key an agent could check against — choosing what matters, judging honestly whether you've got it, and answering for it; the part of a career that resists automation precisely because it cannot be graded. (Also the original post's slug.)
  • keep your alpha ahead of the models
    Maintain a margin of judgment/skill that exceeds what the models can do, treating your edge as a depreciating asset that must be actively renewed as models improve.
  • autonomy is a per-task switch, not a rank
    How much an agent is allowed to run unsupervised is calibrated task by task (scope, definition of done, trust, verification), not a fixed status granted once and for all.
  • the last mile
    The final 20-30% agents don't hand you — gnarly edge cases, architecture, polish — where difficulty and value now concentrate once first drafts are free.

Arguments

Ambitious engineers should invest in the ungradeable meta-skills — selecting problems, taste, directing agents, finishing the last mile, and owning outcomes — because those are what stay scarce and valuable once agents make solving cheap.
Explicit premises:
  • When an agent can solve almost anything given an answer key, solving becomes cheap and abundant.
  • What stays scarce is the judgment to decide which problems to solve and whether the agent actually got it right.
  • Agents hand you only the first 70-80%; framing the problem and finishing the last mile remain on the human.
  • AI cannot be held accountable when prod breaks, so a human must own the outcome.
Implicit premises:
  • Judgment and taste cannot themselves be automated within the relevant time horizon — the durability the piece assumes but never defends.
  • The value of solving falls faster than the value of the meta-skills rises, so reallocating effort toward the meta-skills is the winning move.
  • These meta-skills are genuinely learnable and renewable by an individual engineer rather than fixed talent.
Inferences:
  • If solving is commoditized while framing, judgment, finishing, and ownership are not, an engineer's marginal value migrates to the latter.
  • Therefore effort should shift from being the fastest solver to being the best selector, judge, finisher, and owner.
Cultivating taste — earned judgment about quality — is an engineer's most durable investment in the agent era.
Explicit premises:
  • The real risk of AI is not that it writes bad code but that engineers lose the ability to tell good code from bad.
  • Taste is pattern-matching that has to be earned through reps (studying good vs. great).
  • Models keep improving, so an engineer must keep their 'alpha' — their judgment edge — ahead of the models.
Implicit premises:
  • Human taste and judgment will not themselves be automated soon — the durability the piece assumes but never argues for.
  • Taste requires human reps on the actual work, yet agents now do the very reps (the first 70-80%) that historically built that taste — a bootstrapping tension the piece leaves unaddressed: you need reps to earn taste, but agent-reliance removes the reps.
  • An individual can reliably 'study good vs. great' and convert exposure into transferable judgment.
Inferences:
  • If losing the ability to tell is the core risk and taste is renewable through reps, then deliberately practising judgment is the rational hedge against model improvement.

Narratives

  • A one-line imagined vignette of failure: '"The AI did it" is not a defense when prod breaks. Your name is on the change.'
    → Dramatizes the accountability move by staging the moment of production failure and denying the engineer the exculpation of blaming the tool — turning an abstract principle into a scene with personal stakes.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How should an ambitious engineer build a durable career as coding agents commoditize problem-solving?
    Invest in five ungradeable meta-skills — selecting and framing problems, cultivating taste, directing agents, finishing the last mile, and owning outcomes — rather than competing on raw solving speed.
  • ✅ What stays scarce and valuable when agents can solve almost anything?
    Judgment: choosing which problems are worth solving, framing them precisely, telling good from great, and answering for the result.
  • ⭕ How do you actually earn or rebuild taste when agents are doing the reps that used to build it?
    Unanswered. The piece insists taste is 'earned through reps' and via 'study good vs. great' but never reconciles that with its own advice to delegate the doing (the first 70-80%) to agents.
