What's in a Name: The Moth Podcast

The Moth · podcast · 2026-04-10 Reader 🖥️

Social Connection & Relatedness
Names are not mere labels but carriers of identity, belonging, and meaning — they can wound when they tie us to painful histories, but they can also reconnect us to places and communities where we belong, and they can take on lives entirely independent of their creators' intentions.

practical · narrative/mixed — two personal stories with host commentary framing a thematic question — The Moth is a live storytelling format. Propositions and arguments are embedded in personal narratives rather than stated analytically. The episode is curated around a theme (names) with the host providing connective tissue and her own brief reflection.

Key terms

    A joke term invented by Ryan Estrada in a sleep-deprived comic, defined as wearing completely ordinary clothes and using slang only after it appears in a sitcom — later taken seriously by the fashion industry

Arguments

A surname can be a source of belonging even when the family it came from is lost. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Elise's relationship with her father deteriorated and she ceased contact
  • The name McInerney caused her pain as a reminder of that lost connection
  • In Ireland, the name McInerney was everywhere — on pubs, shopfronts, churches, cemeteries
Implicit premises:
  • Identity and belonging are not solely determined by immediate family relationships
  • Geographic and ancestral roots offer an alternative source of the belonging that names represent
Inferences:
  • The meaning of a name is not fixed by its most painful association; it can be recontextualized through new experiences
  • Travel and encounter with one's heritage can heal the wound of family estrangement
Naming something gives it a reality and power that transcends the namer's intent. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Estrada invented 'normcore' as a throwaway joke in a sleep-deprived comic
  • Someone added it to Urban Dictionary without his knowledge
  • A trend forecasting firm picked it up and declared it a real trend
  • It was nominated for Oxford English Dictionary word of the year
Implicit premises:
  • Once a name enters circulation, its meaning is determined by its receivers, not its creator
  • Cultural systems (media, fashion, dictionaries) amplify and legitimize names regardless of their origin
Inferences:
  • The act of naming is inherently generative — it creates something that can evolve beyond its creator
  • The ironic became sincere and then ironic again (the word 'normcore' became normcore enough to be normcore)
People grow into their names over time rather than being defined by them from the start. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Jody was furious about her hyphenated name as a child
  • She dropped the 'Ann' when she got older
  • She later came to appreciate it after encountering hyphenated names across the Caribbean and the American South
Implicit premises:
  • Identity is not static but develops through experience and cultural encounter
  • A name that feels burdensome in one context may feel like belonging in another
Inferences:
  • The relationship between a person and their name is an evolving negotiation, not a fixed assignment

Narratives

  • Elise McInerney's story: She has always hated her difficult-to-pronounce surname. After her parents' divorce and estrangement from her father, the name becomes actively painful — a link to a family she no longer belongs to. To cope with her father's 60th birthday celebration (from which she is self-exiled), she books a trip to Ireland. There, her name is pronounced effortlessly. In the village of Six Mile Bridge, she discovers her name on pubs and shopfronts, and feels an unexpected sense of belonging — her name connects her to a place in the world even if her immediate family is lost.
    → Demonstrates that names carry layered meaning (phonetic, familial, ancestral, geographic) and that the meaning of a name can be transformed through encounter with its deeper roots. Implicitly argues that belonging is found in unexpected places when you seek it.
  • Ryan Estrada's story: A self-described unfashionable cartoonist, Estrada once made 100 comics in a month under a punishing deadline. One comic jokingly invented fake fashion trends, with 'normcore' as the absurd punchline. Unknown to him, the term migrated to Urban Dictionary, was picked up by a trend forecasting firm, covered by fashion media, and eventually nominated as Oxford's runner-up word of the year. He discovers all this on his birthday when strangers start messaging him. The word appears on billboards, in TV shows, and in a Play-Doh commercial. The bullies who called him unfashionable were right — but his unfashionableness inadvertently made him a style icon.
    → Demonstrates that names and labels, once released, take on autonomous cultural lives. Provides comic counterpoint to Elise's earnest story — where her name was inherited and deeply personal, his was invented carelessly and became culturally significant. Both illustrate that naming shapes reality in uncontrollable ways.
  • Jody Powell's brief interlude about her hyphenated name: Jody-Ann Powell, originally annoyed by the name, dropped the 'Ann,' then gradually reclaimed it after encountering hyphenated names across Caribbean and Southern U.S. culture.
    → Bridges the two main stories and models the host's own vulnerability. Adds a third variation on the theme: a name neither inherited-and-painful (Elise) nor accidentally-created (Ryan), but one gradually grown into.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What is in a name — what meaning and identity does a name carry beyond its function as a label?
    Names carry stories, lineage, belonging, and emotional weight. They connect us to family, place, and culture in ways that evolve over time.
  • ✅ Can a name that causes pain be reclaimed or recontextualized?
    Yes — Elise found that her surname, painful in the context of family estrangement, became a source of belonging when encountered in its ancestral Irish context. Jody reclaimed her hyphenated name after initially rejecting it.
  • ✅ Does the creator of a name control its meaning?
    No — Ryan Estrada's experience shows that once a name enters cultural circulation, its meaning is determined by its receivers and the systems that amplify it, not by the original namer.
  • ⭕ How does naming shape the essence of the thing named?
    Partially addressed — the episode shows naming creating cultural reality (normcore) and emotional reality (McInerney), but the deeper philosophical question is gestured at rather than resolved.
