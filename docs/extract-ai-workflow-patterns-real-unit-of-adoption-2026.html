#5: AI Workflow Patterns: The Real Unit of AI Adoption in 2026

Turing Post (Will Schenk & Ksenia Se) · email · 2026-05-09 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Enterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessHuman-in-the-LoopOrganizational LegibilityAI Workflow Patterns
The real unit of AI adoption is the workflow — a repeating sequence of decisions and actions with human judgment points — and production AI systems decompose into seven primitives (Watch, Validate, Classify, Enrich, Generate, Execute, Elicit) that compose into eight recurring patterns, making the organization's informal decision processes legible and automatable.

practical · expository/taxonomic — Part 5 of 'The Org Age of AI' series. Defines a taxonomy of AI workflow primitives and patterns derived from ~30 production systems at TheFocus.AI. Partially paywalled — the free portion covers the thesis, the seven primitives, and the eight pattern names with brief descriptions; detailed discussion of each pattern is behind the paywall.

Key terms

  • workflow AI Workflow Patterns
    A repeating sequence of decisions and actions that turns an input into an output, with points along the way where a human exercises judgment — distinguished from a pipeline (no judgment) or ad-hoc processes
  • judgment point Human-in-the-Loop
    A point in a workflow where a human exercises judgment; the defining feature that distinguishes a workflow from a pipeline
  • primitive AI Workflow Patterns
    One of seven atomic actions an agent can perform in a single step: Watch, Validate, Classify, Enrich, Generate, Execute, Elicit
  • intelligence on tap AI Workflow Patterns
    The availability of LLMs to address gray-area, informal, and under-specified parts of workflows that previously required human absorption of complexity
  • L0-to-L2 problem Organizational Legibility
    Making the organization legible to itself — discovering and documenting the informal workflows that exist but have never been written down (references Article #2 in the series)

Propositions

  • explicitAI adoption conversations keep happening at the wrong unit — people debate models, agents, frameworks, and use cases, but the actionable unit is the workflow
  • explicitA workflow is distinguished from a pipeline by the presence of human judgment points; remove the judgment and you have plumbing
  • explicitMost organizations have dozens of undocumented workflows in every department, because the humans running them absorbed the complexity years ago
  • explicitLLMs make it possible to address the informal, under-specified parts of organizational processes that were previously too loosely defined for automation
  • explicitEverything an agent does in a single step reduces to exactly seven primitives: Watch, Validate, Classify, Enrich, Generate, Execute, Elicit
  • explicitEight workflow patterns recur across all production AI systems: Triage, Investigation, Draft & Review, Approval, Monitoring, Elicitation, Sync, Curation
  • explicitThe interesting question is always which judgment points an agent can handle, which still need a human, and how the human knows when to step in
  • implicitThings that used to have to be extremely tightly fitted can now be loosely coupled

Arguments

The workflow is the right unit of AI adoption
Implicit premises:
  • Effective adoption requires a unit of analysis that is concrete enough to act on and general enough to apply across domains
Seven primitives are sufficient to describe any agent step
Implicit premises:
  • The authors' experience across ~30 production systems is representative of the broader space
  • The taxonomy is exhaustive rather than just covering the most common cases
Eight patterns capture the recurring shapes of production AI workflows
Implicit premises:
  • Convergence across diverse production systems indicates genuine patterns rather than artifacts of one team's design habits

Gaps

  • incompleteThe detailed discussion of each of the eight patterns, guidance on which workflows to automate first, how patterns chain into pipelines, and implications for adoption are behind the paywall. The free portion establishes the framework but does not operationalize it.
  • uninformedThe exhaustiveness claim for seven primitives is asserted from practice but not argued from first principles. It is unclear whether the taxonomy would hold for domains the authors have not encountered (e.g., physical-world robotics workflows, scientific discovery workflows).
  • incompleteThe pattern taxonomy describes the 'shape' of human involvement (e.g., 'decides', 'gates', 'edits/approves') but does not address how to design the handoff mechanism or how to calibrate trust/autonomy levels over time.
  • incompleteThe article references the L0-to-L2 maturity model from Article #2 but does not explain how workflow discovery maps to those levels for readers who have not read the earlier articles.
