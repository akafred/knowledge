I stopped hitting Claude's usage limits - 10 things I changed

You can avoid hitting Claude's usage limits by understanding that Claude counts tokens (not messages) and adopting ten habits that minimize unnecessary token consumption: editing instead of following up, starting fresh chats, batching questions, caching files in Projects, saving preferences to Memory, disabling unused features, choosing cheaper models for simple tasks, spreading work across the day, avoiding peak hours, and enabling overage billing.

Arguments

You should edit your prompt instead of sending follow-up corrections. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Claude re-reads the entire conversation history every turn.
  • Each follow-up adds to that history, increasing cost quadratically.
  • Editing replaces the exchange rather than stacking.
Implicit premises:
  • The user's goal is to minimize token usage.
  • The edit-and-regenerate feature is available on Claude's interface.
Inferences:
  • Editing avoids the quadratic cost growth that follow-ups cause.
You should start a new chat every 15-20 messages to avoid token waste. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Token cost grows quadratically with conversation length.
  • In long chats, nearly all tokens go to re-reading history.
Implicit premises:
  • The summary-and-restart workflow preserves enough context to continue the task.
  • The cost of summarizing + starting fresh is less than the cost of continuing.
Inferences:
  • Periodically resetting the conversation keeps you in the low-cost region of the quadratic curve.
You should choose the cheapest model adequate for each task. strength: medium
Explicit premises:

  • Using Haiku for simple tasks frees 50-70% of your budget.
Implicit premises:
  • Users can reliably judge which tasks are 'simple' vs 'complex'.
  • The different models share the same rate-limit pool or budget.
Inferences:
  • Model selection is a budget allocation decision, not just a quality decision.
