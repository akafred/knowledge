TBM 420: The AI Playbook Puzzle

John Cutler from The Beautiful Mess · email · 2026-04-20 source 🖥️

AI as AmplifierEnterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessMeta-Skills & Contextual JudgmentProfessional Identity
Nobody has an AI playbook yet because the real challenge is not tactical AI adoption but the identity-threatening work of distinguishing which existing practices were always broken, which were genuinely good, which only worked due to now-vanished constraints, and developing the meta-skill of contextual judgment to navigate all three.

practical · argumentative — Argumentative essay with narrative examples. Makes claims about how organizations should and should not approach AI adoption, grounded in product management and organizational design practice.

Key terms

  • identity threat Professional Identity
    The psychological threat to professional self-concept when practices, skills, and expertise that defined your career are potentially obsoleted or transformed by AI
  • meta-skill Meta-Skills & Contextual Judgment
    The ability to read organizational context, understand why practices worked or failed in specific situations, and adapt accordingly — distinct from any individual practice
  • four buckets Meta-Skills & Contextual Judgment
    Cutler's taxonomy: (1) bad ideas AI makes worse, (2) good ideas AI supercharges, (3) genuinely new things only possible with AI, (4) the meta-skill of contextual judgment
  • COM-B
    A behavioral diagnostic framework (Capability, Opportunity, Motivation — Behaviour) used here to analyze why people fall into each trap
  • checkpoint problem / checkbox problem
    The pattern of adding AI as a checkbox to existing broken processes without questioning the underlying model

Arguments

The real AI adoption challenge is identity management, not tactical implementation. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Most tactical AI advice is derivative (reinforces broken or renames good).
  • Identity threat is the biggest threat.
  • Leaders exhibit a trio of simultaneous identity pressures (performing transformation, resisting it internally, executing with old playbooks).
Implicit premises:
  • Tactical problems are solvable with existing knowledge; identity problems require psychological work that organizations are not equipped to facilitate.
  • The supply of tactical advice exceeds demand for it; the supply of identity-transition support is negligible.
Inferences:
  • Therefore, investing in tactical AI tooling without addressing identity will fail.
You need a four-bucket taxonomy (not two) to reason about AI adoption. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Bad ideas get worse with AI (bucket 1).
  • Good ideas can be supercharged (bucket 2).
  • Some entirely new practices only make sense with AI in the loop (bucket 3).
  • Contextual judgment is the meta-skill tying them together (bucket 4).
Implicit premises:
  • Most current discourse operates with only the first two buckets, which is insufficient.
  • Bucket 3 (unimagined) and bucket 4 (meta-skill) require fundamentally different capabilities than buckets 1 and 2.
Inferences:
  • Organizations stuck in a two-bucket model will miss genuinely novel opportunities and lack the adaptive capacity to navigate ambiguity.
The more certain someone sounds about AI strategy right now, the more likely they are relying on outdated mental models. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Certainty requires pinning conclusions to existing models.
  • Many existing models are the problem, not the solution.
Implicit premises:
  • The situation is novel enough that existing models are insufficient.
  • Epistemic humility correlates with better adaptation in high-uncertainty environments.
Inferences:
  • Expressed certainty about AI strategy is a signal to investigate for hidden broken assumptions.

Narratives

  • A team's 'AI transformation plan' that automated every broken pattern: a Governance Agent to enforce gates, AI-generated PRDs, prototyping bolted into stage-gate — without questioning any underlying model.
    → Concrete illustration of bucket 1 (bad ideas made worse). Shows how AI can be applied with full organizational enthusiasm yet zero actual transformation.
  • A Dotwork team that used AI to automate friction (status updates, shared context, decision summaries) to free time for better conversations and harder creative work.
    → Concrete illustration of bucket 2 (good ideas supercharged). Contrasts with the first narrative to show the same technology producing opposite outcomes based on underlying model quality.
  • DAWs (Digital Audio Workstations) in music production: bad producers made worse music faster, good producers found new creative territory, whole new genres emerged, and some talented people had real identity crises.
    → Historical analogy validating the four-bucket framework. Makes the pattern feel familiar and survivable rather than unprecedented.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why is most AI adoption advice unhelpful?
    Because it operates in only two of four buckets and ignores the identity and meta-cognitive dimensions.
  • ✅ What is the biggest obstacle to effective AI adoption?
    Identity threat — the psychological cost of admitting that practices you built your career on may be obsolete or need fundamental rethinking.
  • ✅ How should organizations categorize their AI adoption efforts?
    Four buckets: amplifying bad, supercharging good, discovering the unimagined, and developing the meta-skill of contextual judgment.
  • ✅ What does the personal journey of AI adaptation look like?
    An eight-step progression from uncertainty through identity crisis to acceptance of ongoing adaptation.
  • ⭕ What specific organizational practices should change with AI?
    Deliberately left open — the author argues nobody has this answer yet and the work of discovering it requires being in it.

Gaps

  • incompleteBucket 3 (genuinely new practices only possible with AI) is the most interesting category but receives the least development. The author acknowledges these exist but provides zero concrete examples of what they might look like.
    Read next: Look for writing on emergent AI-native workflows: Maggie Appleton on AI interaction patterns, or Linus Lee on AI-augmented thinking tools.
  • incompleteThe COM-B analysis is applied only to individuals. The organizational and structural factors that create and reinforce the three traps (incentive structures, promotion criteria, budget cycles) are not analyzed.
    Read next: Westrum's organizational typology applied to AI adoption; Amy Edmondson on psychological safety as a precondition for the identity work Cutler describes.
  • incompleteThe argument assumes a product/tech organizational context. How the four-bucket framework applies to non-tech domains (healthcare, education, government) is unaddressed.
    Read next: Sector-specific AI adoption research; Ethan Mollick's work on AI across professions.
  • incompleteThe eight-step journey is presented as linear and individual. No treatment of how teams or organizations move through this collectively, or what happens when team members are at different stages.
    Read next: Bridges' Transition Model for organizational change; Kegan and Lahey on immunity to change.
  • illogicalThe claim that 'the more certain someone sounds, the more likely they're relying on outdated models' risks being unfalsifiable. It could also apply to Cutler's own confident claims about identity threat being the biggest obstacle.
    Read next: Philip Tetlock on superforecasting and calibrated confidence — certainty is not inherently a signal of poor reasoning.
