Why product feels so hard right now - Charity Ibhadon (WPP)

Mind the Product · video · 2026-07-08 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Burnout & RecoveryEmotional Impact of AI WorkMeta-Skills & Contextual JudgmentProfessional IdentityPsychological Safety
Product work feels harder than ever not mainly because the job changed but because a culture with no 'gold standard' fuses people's identity to the work and teaches them to mistake the physical symptoms of burnout for proof of excellence; the way through is to de-identify from work, defend recovery and boundaries, and lead through durable, unflappable 'happy high status' composure and commercial curiosity rather than chasing ever more frameworks and certificates.

practical · Interview / conversation (The Product Experience podcast): a host draws out a leader's hard-won heuristics for surviving and enjoying product work; the aim is to change how the listener works and relates to their job, not to establish a theory. — A guided dialogue, not a treatise. The thesis is built cumulatively through anecdote and restatement rather than argued deductively. Host Lily Smith steers with questions ('why care about fun?', 'how do you protect your energy?', 'what mindset for the future?'); Charity Ibhadon answers with lived examples and rules of thumb. Much of the load-bearing content is confessional (her own burnout) and hedged ('this is going to sound bad', 'these are new revelations'), so claims are held loosely and offered as personal practice, not prescription.

Key terms

  • happy high status
    A borrowed book title (Emma Ledden) she uses as a leadership stance: remaining outwardly unflappable and in visible control while the situation — or your own head — is falling apart, because a leader's projected calm sets the team's emotional weather. Explicitly a performance ('in my head it's oh my god oh my god' while the team gets 'this is a leader we trust').
  • burnout that feels like success
    The trap where the physical and behavioral symptoms of over-work (constant travel, working through a friend's wedding, stress) are misread as markers of importance and excellence — 'the more stressed I was, the more I was like I am earning that salary' — so the sufferer is motivated to intensify the very thing depleting them.
  • no gold standard
    Her account of why product is uniquely anxiety-producing: unlike medicine (diagnose, operate, patient recovers — a science), product is an art whose definition of 'good' depends entirely on context (does the company respect product, is there budget), so there is no stable target to hit and everyone just 'chases the top'.
  • commercial curiosity
    The durable, non-framework capability she predicts will matter most: caring about and understanding the company's commercials — share price, revenue, growth, competitors — and connecting your slice of the work to them, contrasted explicitly with 'your AI certificate'.
  • it's just a job
    A deliberately provocative permission ('a very wild thing to say in 2026') that work need not be one's whole identity — you will still be paid, your family will still be well, the world will be fine if it doesn't get done today — offered as the antidote to identity-fusion.

Arguments

You should de-identify from work and treat 'it's just a job' as a legitimate stance. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Work had become her whole identity and coincided with a burnout that damaged her health.
  • A three-month recovery break restored her and revealed that work cannot be her only source of fulfilment.
  • People are also mothers, fathers, spouses — the job does not have to be the whole identity.
Implicit premises:
  • Identity-fusion with work is a cause of the burnout, not merely a correlate — loosening it is therefore causally protective.
  • A person can reduce their psychological investment in work without a corresponding drop in performance or standing (she assumes you 'will still get paid if you do a good job').
Inferences:
  • If identity-fusion drives burnout, then the pressure-producing culture of product (no gold standard, perfectionism) is dangerous precisely to the most committed people — the very ones the field selects for.
  • She does not draw the structural conclusion that if the culture reliably produces this fusion, individual de-identification treats a symptom rather than the system that generates it.
Leaders should maintain 'happy high status' composure under pressure. strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • A team feeds off the leader's emotional state.
  • If the leader visibly panics, it produces a domino effect of panic.
  • Projecting calm internally makes it appear externally, giving the team a sense that things are in control.
Implicit premises:
  • Performed calm that the leader does not actually feel ('in my head it's oh my god oh my god') is sustainable and essentially cost-free to the leader.
  • The benefit to the team of a calm signal outweighs the depletion cost to the leader of suppressing their real state.
Inferences:
  • She does not draw the conclusion that emotional suppression is itself a burnout driver — so the composure remedy may quietly reproduce the very 'burnout that feels like success' she warns against, since both reward looking fine while feeling terrible.
  • If composure must be performed rather than felt, then her earlier de-identification remedy is incomplete: the leader still carries the full internal load, just invisibly.
The durable edge in the AI era is human and commercial judgment, not credentials or frameworks. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Product is an evolving art with no fixed skill list that will define it in 20 years.
  • Being nice to work with trumps qualifications because work is chosen through relationships.
  • Commercially curious PMs who connect their work to revenue, growth and competitors add durable value.
  • AI certificates and framework accumulation are not 'the thing'.
Implicit premises:
  • AI will commoditize codified, certifiable knowledge faster than it commoditizes relational and commercial judgment.
  • Individuality and 'being human' are not themselves reducible to teachable frameworks (otherwise they would fall to the same critique she levels at frameworks).
Inferences:
  • If certifiable knowledge is what AI most erodes, the anxiety-producing over-supply of product frameworks is not just unhelpful but a poor investment of scarce attention.
  • She stops short of saying which human capabilities are actually developable versus dispositional — leaving 'be more human and commercially curious' as advice without a training path.

Narratives

  • Her burnout-as-success arc: a CEO-level job with a huge team, an executive MBA done alongside, flying roughly twice a fortnight New York / LA, reading stress as proof she was earning her salary — culminating in physical symptoms (digestion problems, a sore throat, a cold that never went away), losing the job partly because she was unwell, then taking three months out to ride horses eight hours a day in Ecuador and discovering 'this is what it is to be free — my body doesn't hurt'.
    → The keystone evidence for the whole conversation: it makes concrete both 'burnout that feels like success' and the recovery-not-stress thesis, and it earns her the authority to prescribe de-identification because she is 'saying that as someone for who it was my whole identity'.
  • The henna-and-conference-call at a friend's wedding in Slovenia: having her henna done by her Indian aunties while sitting on a board meeting call, next to the mountains, thinking 'look at me getting so many things done' and only later recognising it as an unmistakable symptom of burnout.
    → A vivid single image that illustrates 'burnout that feels like success' and the retrospective-only visibility of the trap — pride in multitasking masking depletion.
  • The publisher at a high-pressure editorial site launch (announced at CES) telling the stressed host 'nobody's going to die if it's not launched on time — people will be grumpy, but they'll forget', which flipped a switch letting the host enjoy the stress rather than be overwhelmed by it.
    → The host's corroborating anecdote generalizes Charity's 'we're not building rockets' heuristic beyond product, showing the reframe is transferable and that permission often has to come from someone with authority.
  • The 'PMA' director of engineering at Justine who, amid enormous team drama, would walk around the office smiling and say 'oh, just PMA' (positive mental attitude), plus the recurring duck image — gliding serenely while its legs paddle furiously beneath the surface.
    → Models 'happy high status' by example and doubles as the tell that composure is a performance over hidden effort — the leader who looks calm is paddling hard underneath.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why does product work feel harder now than ever before?
    Because it is an art with no gold standard, over-supplied with theory and benchmarks, attracting perfectionists — so people feel perpetually inadequate — layered on an AI/layoffs backdrop of in-work and out-of-work anxiety.
  • ✅ How do you recognise, and recover from, burnout?
    Recognise it by noticing that stress has started to feel like success and by physical symptoms; recover through genuine rest and by building non-work sources of fulfilment, since burnout is driven by the absence of recovery rather than the presence of stress.
  • ✅ How do you protect your energy and stay composed under chaos day to day?
    Ruthlessly prioritize with the Eisenhower matrix, keep perspective ('we're not building rockets'), shut the laptop and get outside, and project 'happy high status' calm so the team feeds off it.
  • ✅ What mindset and capabilities will sustain a long, happy product career into an AI-shaped future?
    Individuality, resilience as ambiguity-tolerance, being genuinely nice to work with, and commercial curiosity — explicitly over frameworks and AI certificates.
  • ⭕ Is the composure she prescribes actually sustainable for the leader performing it?
    Unresolved and unacknowledged: she reveals that 'happy high status' is calm she performs without feeling ('in my head it's oh my god'), but never asks whether sustained emotional suppression is itself depleting — a tension with her own burnout thesis.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe remedy is almost entirely individual (introspect, de-identify, prioritize, perform calm) while the diagnosed causes are largely structural (a field with no gold standard, an over-productive theory/benchmark industry, AI-era layoff anxiety). She never connects the two — if the culture reliably manufactures identity-fusion and 'burnout that feels like success', individual mindset shifts treat symptoms while the generating system persists.
    Read next: Read against extract-burnout-across-org-chart (burnout as a systemic design failure requiring level-specific structural fixes) and extract-overcome-burnout-philosophy (the same structural-diagnosis-with-individual-remedy tension named explicitly via Byung-Chul Han's 'achievement society').
  • incompleteThe 'happy high status' prescription assumes performed composure the leader does not feel is sustainable and cost-free, but suppression of one's actual state is itself a recognised driver of depletion — so the composure remedy may quietly reproduce the 'looks fine, feels terrible' dynamic she identifies as burnout-that-feels-like-success. The cost side of leaderly calm is never examined.
    Read next: Emotional-labour and surface-acting research (Hochschild) on the depletion cost of performed emotion; recovery/detachment models (Sonnentag) on why suppression blocks the recovery she elsewhere prescribes.
  • incompleteRecovery is credited to a highly privileged intervention (a three-month break, horse riding in Ecuador) that she herself flags as unavailable to most; the scaled-down substitutes she offers (a quieter Friday, an introspective Saturday, a monthly gallery visit) are asserted to work but never argued to be sufficient against a structurally over-demanding job.
    Read next: Evidence on micro-recovery / daily detachment versus extended leave; whether small non-instrumental breaks meaningfully offset chronic overload or merely make it tolerable.
  • incompleteShe prescribes 'be more human, be commercially curious, be nice to work with' as the durable AI-era edge but offers no account of which of these are developable skills versus fixed dispositions, nor how someone would train them — leaving the advice motivational rather than actionable, and vulnerable to the same 'no clear starting point' complaint she levels at the framework literature.
    Read next: extract-ai-worker-identity-archetypes and the meta-skills / contextual-judgment literature on which durable capabilities are teachable; commercial-acumen development pathways for product roles.
