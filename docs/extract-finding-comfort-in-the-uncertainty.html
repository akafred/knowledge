Finding Comfort in the Uncertainty

Annie Vella · article · 2026-07-07 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

The most valuable takeaway from a gathering of leading software practitioners on AI's impact is that nobody has it figured out — and that this shared uncertainty, together with a convergent set of hard questions the field is only beginning to name, is itself the ground for progress and a source of comfort rather than alarm.

mixed · mixed — A first-person reflective synthesis of an invite-only retreat on the future of software development: narrative framing (the journey home, the room in Deer Valley) wrapped around an expository survey of eight convergent themes, closing with a normative call to action.

Key terms

  • supervisory engineering work Supervisory Programming
    A new category of engineering work — supervising, orchestrating, and evaluating AI output — emerging as implementation is delegated to agents; the author's coinage from her Masters research
  • the ledger (work ledger)
    A complete, verifiable record of everything agents do — every step and change, with the ability to roll back — variously framed as a work ledger, an audit trail, or a unified log
  • cognitive debt Cognitive Debt
    Knowledge losing its stickiness because AI unlocks so much volume that we skim everything — an extension of technical debt into the mind
  • trust debt
    The gap between what we produce and what we deeply understand about what we've produced
  • active knowledge decay
    Skills atrophying when you stop doing the work hands-on
  • Agent Experience Agent-Native Design
    Investment in the conditions that make agents effective (clear standards, good documentation, well-structured code) — the same conditions that made developers effective, hence possibly 'the new DevEx'
  • middle loop Orchestration Tax / Human Review Bottleneck
    An emerging layer of work — supervising, orchestrating, evaluating — sitting between the increasingly automated inner loop of coding and the outer loop of delivery
  • pit of success
    Platform defaults that make the safe path also the fast, easy path so that speed and safety are not in tension

Arguments

The shared uncertainty of the retreat is a source of comfort and progress rather than alarm. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Nobody — not even the sharpest minds present — has AI-in-software figured out; there is more uncertainty than certainty.
  • The group nonetheless left with a shared understanding of the sorts of questions it should be asking.
  • That shared question-set 'might be the most valuable outcome of all'.
Implicit premises:
  • Naming the right questions is a precondition to answering them.
  • Honest collective inquiry is preferable to isolated false certainty.
Inferences:
  • Therefore discovering that nobody has the answers, but everyone shares the questions, is itself the valuable and reassuring result.
Organisations should spend the first year of AI adoption preparing their foundations rather than chasing immediate productivity gains. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • AI amplifies existing conditions: organisations with strong teams, platforms and governance got faster; those without got messier.
  • Microservices taught the same lesson — without a certain level of organisational maturity the technology makes things worse, not better.
  • Several groups independently converged on prioritising platform foundations, governance, and AI-fluency uplift first.
Implicit premises:
  • Organisational readiness is a prerequisite for value, not a byproduct of adoption.
  • Expecting broad productivity gains before the foundations exist causes harm rather than benefit.
Inferences:
  • Therefore foundation-building, not productivity, is the correct first-year goal.
The profession must deliberately design the emerging human role rather than stumble into it. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Everyone agrees the software engineer's role is shifting, but nobody has a design for what it shifts to.
  • A supervisory 'middle loop' category of work is emerging, yet its skills, pathways, and dignity are undesigned.
  • Many engineers hired to write code are now being told to outsource that to AI and deserve more than platitudes.
Implicit premises:
  • Undesigned transitions leave people without pathways or dignity.
  • Those with platforms and voices bear a responsibility to shape the transition.
Inferences:
  • Therefore leaders should do the hard work of defining the new problems and pathways rather than offering reassurance.

Narratives

  • On the final flight home the author is wedged between a retired industrial chemist and a theoretical physicist reading a C++ book; the physicist thinks AI-generated code is 'pretty crap' yet has noticed engineers around him losing their jobs.
    → Opens the piece by showing the retreat's questions surfacing unprompted among strangers — the concerns are universal, not an insider preoccupation
  • About 40 practitioners, researchers and technical leaders gather in Deer Valley, Utah, in the same mountains where the Agile Manifesto was written 25 years ago, with some original signatories present — an unconference with no presentations and no handed-down agenda.
    → Establishes the stakes and legitimacy of the moment while explicitly disclaiming any attempt to reproduce the Agile Manifesto
  • Between sessions, several people going hardest on AI quietly admit that it is exhausting them, with several independently using the word 'addictive' about spinning up parallel agents.
    → Humanises the cognitive-load thesis; the private honesty is presented as the emotional core of the retreat
  • At the close, when the mic is offered for takeaways, the author challenges the group — all with platforms and voices — to help engineers by getting better at describing the new problems they get to solve, not by reassuring them that everything will be fine.
    → Delivers the normative call to action that carries the article's thesis into an obligation

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Has anyone figured out how to adopt AI in software development at scale?
    No — even the sharpest minds in the room had not; there is more uncertainty than certainty, and everyone is working it out as they go.
  • ✅ Once agents can burn through backlogs, what becomes the real bottleneck?
    Downstream human and organisational absorption — slow decisions, dependencies, market appetite — with decision fatigue replacing engineering throughput as the constraint.
  • ⭕ If code can be regenerated from a specification, what is the durable artifact?
    Left open — candidates offered include the domain model, the test suite, and the intent, but no answer is settled.
  • ⭕ How should the industry conceptualise agents — as tools, team members, or something else?
    Unresolved — no settled mental model exists (API vs. human vs. non-deterministic 'chemical vat'), and the ambiguity is slowing governance and investment.
  • ⭕ What does the software engineer's role shift to?
    Undesigned — a supervisory 'middle loop' is emerging, but its skills, pathways, and dignity have not been shaped; the field is stumbling into it.
