Travel Through the Lens of AI with Booking.com CEO Glenn Fogel

Glenn Fogel (guest); No Priors / Sarah Guo (host) · podcast · 2026-07-11 Reader 🖥️

Because no competitive advantage is a permanent moat in the AI era, an incumbent like Booking Holdings can win only by relentlessly rebuilding itself—deploying AI as a tool to serve travelers and partners better, above all through a personalized agentic travel assistant—while its real but always-assailable edge lies in the operational, supply-side and regulatory complexity that outsiders consistently underestimate.

practical · expository interview / conversational podcast dialogue — A single expert (Booking Holdings CEO Glenn Fogel) is interviewed by a VC host (No Priors, Sarah Guo). Propositions emerge through Q&A and are often restated; the thrust is prescriptive (how to run a business, deploy AI, and choose a career in the AI era) rather than a formal theoretical argument, though it carries macro observations about bubbles and labor.

Key terms

  • no moat / 'there is no such thing as a moat'
    No competitive position is permanently protected from innovation; a company may hold a real 'competitive advantage in areas' today, but it 'can go away tomorrow', so the only durable strategy is perpetual re-invention.
  • agent / personalized agentic travel assistant (Penny)
    An AI system that knows everything about a traveler, evaluates an infinite set of permutations no human could, plans and re-plans complex multi-leg trips (including miles-vs-cash trade-offs), and—crucially—serves as the single point of contact that anticipates and repairs cascading failures.
  • agency (customer agency) Agency
    The traveler's desire to make, or at least confirm, the final decision; on complex choices the agent should defer and double-check with the human, while acting more autonomously on routine ones.
  • token economics AI Unit Economics
    The live cost-management discipline of deciding which model to use, for which purpose, and when, and of tracking how many tokens a task consumes—weighed against an as-yet-unknown ROI and customer lifetime value.
  • complexity (of the travel business)
    The operational, supply-side and regulatory intricacy of actually serving travelers and partners—loading and managing inventory, helping property managers, meeting merchant-of-record rules worldwide—that outsiders underestimate and that constitutes the incumbent's real (if temporary) advantage.

Arguments

An AI-native entrant cannot simply 'come in and knock away' Booking, so would-be disruptors should understand the business deeply before committing capital. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Booking holds hard-to-replicate assets: ~8.6M alternative-accommodation listings and deep partner connectivity that an entrant must somehow obtain.
  • Serving supply is far more than loading inventory into a database—thousands of people work with hotels and property managers on demand, systems and pain points.
  • Travel is heavily and increasingly regulated worldwide, and being a merchant of record requires meeting a large body of rules that only scale players can afford.
Implicit premises:
  • Outsiders judge the business 'from afar' and systematically underestimate its operational and regulatory complexity.
  • A slick, AI-intensive front end does not by itself confer the connectivity, compliance or supply relationships that make the marketplace work.
Inferences:
  • Since the barrier is operational/regulatory depth rather than interface quality, an AI layer alone is insufficient to displace the incumbent—though, per the 'no moat' thesis, even this depth is only a temporary advantage.
AI is an incredibly beneficial tool for Booking rather than a threat that makes travel companies worthless. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • A business exists to do something of value for its customers.
  • Booking has two customers—travelers and partners—and sits in the middle as a marketplace.
  • LLMs and related AI make it a more valuable method for travelers to get the information they need and for partners to be served.
Implicit premises:
  • Whoever deploys AI best in service of those customers captures the value, and the incumbent that already owns the marketplace is well positioned to be that deployer.
  • The market's swings on the OpenAI-checkout news (Booking up ~8% when the feature was canceled) reflected outsiders' shallow read of the business, not a real change in fundamentals.
Inferences:
  • Therefore the correct posture is to build the agents oneself and use AI to serve customers better, not to fear disintermediation.
The AI jobs question is real but should be met with calibrated upskilling and honesty rather than fear or doomerism. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Technology has always displaced jobs (agrarian-to-urban, the industrial revolution, and, at Booking, machine translation erasing ~40-language human roles).
  • History also shows new jobs get created, but the rates of destruction and creation are probably not the same.
  • Some displaced people (e.g., a 50-something truck driver) cannot readily retrain, and forced dislocations have caused social harm before.
  • Government retraining programs over the last fifty years have not worked well.
Implicit premises:
  • If people come to reject technology out of fear, the society that does so will be outcompeted by societies (e.g., China) that do not.
  • Framing effects make survey-based claims of anti-AI sentiment unreliable guides to policy.
Inferences:
  • So the responsible path is continuous corporate upskilling toward AI literacy plus honest public conversation, not fear-driven rejection.

Narratives

  • Fogel joined Priceline in February 2000, taking his 1999 bonus first—then the Nasdaq peaked the week he went long Internet. Priceline's market cap collapsed from ~$30B toward a couple hundred million, the stock fell below $1, and a reverse split (to ~$6) staved off delisting. He stayed 27 years as it grew to a ~$180B peak—up roughly a thousandfold.
    → Embodies the 'fight for the customer every day / you never stop' ethos and the survival-through-reinvention thesis; also grounds the dot-com/AI parallel in lived experience.
  • Fogel recounts using Priceline's agent 'Penny' the other night to plan a complex family trip to Europe—two cabins (adults up front, young-adult kids in back), one member returning to a different city, hotel-on-arrival vs. travel-that-night decisions, restaurants—then adding frequent-flyer miles and having it work out, back and forth, that he'd pay miles for the adults and cash for the kids.
    → Concrete proof-of-value for the personalized agentic-assistant vision.
  • Around 2005 Booking.com handled hotel reservations, content, scripts and customer service in over forty languages—all translated by human beings, since there was no machine translation. Machine translation later erased all those jobs entirely.
    → Makes the abstract labor-disruption argument tangible from an operator who lived it, and sets up the speed-of-change concern.
  • Fogel fears too many of his law-school classmates chose law simply because it was a well-paid, comfortable default path, and later in life found themselves wondering 'what did I do?'.
    → Humanizes the 'choose wisely / you only get one life' values message.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How should people think about travel through the lens of AI?
    AI is a beneficial tool that lets Booking serve travelers and partners better, cheaper and more valuably—not a force that makes travel companies worthless; the market's swings on OpenAI's checkout feature overread a shallow view of the business.
  • ✅ Will agents transacting on users' behalf disintermediate Booking, and does its scale (e.g., 8.6M listings) form a durable moat?
    Booking is itself building the agents; supply connectivity, operational depth and regulatory complexity are hard for a pure-AI entrant to replicate—but Fogel insists this is a temporary advantage, not a permanent moat.
  • ✅ Should founders and CEOs keep going or sell in this environment?
    No general rule; it depends entirely on the specifics—conviction about future value, options available, and one's actual motivation (build something that matters vs. make money).
  • ✅ Will AI cause a jobs apocalypse?
    Tech-driven displacement is old news; the real risks are the speed mismatch between destruction and creation and the non-retrainable worker, met by continuous upskilling and honest debate rather than fear.
  • ⭕ What is the ROI and lifetime-value impact of the agentic tools, and are they net-accretive at scale?
    Explicitly left open—'things that we don't know yet that we're going to have to work on'; cost, ROI, LTV and repeat/loyalty effects are still being measured.
