How to write with style

Kurt Vonnegut · article · 1982 Reader 🖥️

Software Design FundamentalsTaste & JudgmentWriting as Thinking
Good writing style is not verbal ornament but the honest self-revelation of a writer who genuinely cares about a worthwhile subject and respects the reader — and it is achieved not through games with language but through caring about content, sounding like the actual person you are, keeping language simple, and cutting anything that does not illuminate the subject.

practical · Expository/argumentative essay with a first-person anecdotal frame and worked literary examples; light narrative (Vonnegut's own Indianapolis identity used as a running self-demonstration). — Originally a piece in International Paper's public-education advertising series 'The Power of the Printed Word' (© 1982 International Paper Co.), aimed at a general, non-writer audience — a democratic how-to, not a literary treatise. The byline is Kurt Vonnegut; 'harmonize.com' is only the reprint host, and the Reader 'author' field (harmonize.com) is the host, not the author.

Structure

PartRole
Framing: style as self-revelationRedefines 'style' away from ornament: everything you write reveals, accidentally and intentionally, what sort of person you are (ignorant/informed, honest/crooked, playful/humorless).
The reader-respect premiseArgues you should examine your style out of respect for readers; sloppy writing tells readers you care nothing about them and they will stop reading.
Rule 1 — Find a subject you care aboutLocates the origin of compelling style in genuine caring about content ('ideas in your head'), not in language technique; the most damning thing you can reveal is not knowing what is interesting.
Rules 2-4 — Do not ramble / Keep it simple / Have the guts to cutThe economy-and-simplicity cluster: profound things are said in near-childlike language (Shakespeare, Joyce, the Bible); eloquence must serve ideas; delete any sentence, however excellent, that does not illuminate the subject.
Rule 5 — Sound like yourselfAuthenticity of voice: write in the speech you grew up with; non-standard and first-language-colored English (Conrad, Irish, Appalachian) is beautiful; Vonnegut trusts his own writing most when it sounds like a person from Indianapolis.
Rule 6 — Say what you meanReframes his teachers' demand for 'correct' English: they did not want to make him an Englishman but to make him understood; Picasso-style / jazz-style rule-breaking sacrifices being understood.
Rule 7 — Pity the readersReading is a hard art most people never master; the writer's stylistic options are therefore neither numerous nor glamorous, because our audience are imperfect artists who need our help.
Coda — content over expression (Strunk & White / E.B. White)Commends The Elements of Style, then insists no one would care how well or badly White wrote if he did not have enchanting things to say; and that under the Constitution what you write about is unlimited.

Key terms

  • style
    The sum of revelations — accidental and intentional — that a piece of writing makes about the character and mind of its writer; who the reader is spending time with. Explicitly NOT 'literary style in the narrower sense' (which he defers to Strunk & White).
  • sound like yourself
    Write in the natural rhythms and vocabulary of the speech you heard as a child; let your first language / region show; the voice you can be trusted in is the one that is actually yours, not an imitated prestige dialect.
  • keep it simple
    Use plain, even childlike language, especially for the most profound subjects; simplicity of language is 'reputable, but perhaps even sacred'.
  • have the guts to cut
    Ruthless deletion as an act of courage: however excellent a sentence, if it does not illuminate your subject in some new and useful way, scratch it out — eloquence must be the servant of the ideas.
  • pity the readers
    A stance of reader-respect grounded in the difficulty of reading: because readers are 'imperfect artists' doing a genuinely hard job, the writer is obliged to be a sympathetic, patient teacher who simplifies and clarifies.
  • find a subject you care about
    Genuine caring about content — and believing others should care too — is 'the most compelling and seductive element in your style', more than any games with language; style begins with ideas in your head.

Note: Vonnegut only occupies the report side; he offers no rival theory of self-formation.

Arguments

You should deliberately examine and improve your writing style, whatever you are writing. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Everything you write reveals what sort of person you are.
  • Readers infer your character (and your regard for them) from how you write.
  • Careless writing signals that you care nothing about the reader, and they will stop reading you.
Implicit premises:
  • Being read — holding the reader's attention — is a good the writer should want to secure.
  • The writer owes the reader something; the writing relationship is ethical, not merely transactional.
  • The reader can accurately detect care or its absence from the prose.
Inferences:
  • If clarity is a duty owed to the reader, then deliberately difficult or experimental prose is a form of disrespect — a stance that would implicitly condemn much of the modernist canon (including the intricate Joyce sentences Vonnegut elsewhere admires), a tension he never resolves.
Sound like yourself — write in your native, childhood-formed voice rather than imitating a prestige dialect. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Your most natural style echoes the speech you heard as a child.
  • Writers whose first language or region colors their English (Conrad, the Irish, Appalachia) are distinctive and delightful.
  • You (and your readers) trust your writing most when it sounds like the actual person you are.
  • Your teachers' demand for 'correct' English really aimed at being understood, not at making you an Englishman.
Implicit premises:
  • A pre-formed authentic self exists to be 'sounded like'.
  • Authenticity of voice is legible to readers — they can tell an authentic voice from an imitated one.
  • Being trusted, not being admired for polish, is the goal.
Inferences:
  • This reframes grammatical 'correctness' as purely instrumental to clarity, which quietly undercuts prescriptivism — but Vonnegut stops short of generalizing that grammar rules are justified only when they serve understanding.
Keep language simple and cut anything that does not illuminate the subject. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Shakespeare, Joyce and the Bible render the most profound matter in near-childlike words.
  • Eloquence should be the servant of the ideas, so a sentence that does not illuminate the subject should be cut.
  • Reading is a genuinely hard art, so readers need all the help simplicity can give them.
Implicit premises:
  • The essence of any worthwhile subject can be expressed simply — verbal complexity is not the same as depth.
  • The writer's impulse to 'soar and sing like a nightingale' is a temptation to be disciplined, not indulged.
Inferences:
  • He does not consider that some subjects (technical, mathematical, densely conceptual) may resist reduction to childlike language — his exemplars are all emotional profundity, not conceptual complexity.

Narratives

  • Shakespeare's 'To be or not to be?' (whose longest word is three letters), Joyce's 'She was tired.' from 'Eveline', and the opening of Genesis — all rendering profound matter in near-childlike language.
    → Worked examples proving the simplicity rule: that the deepest things are, and should be, said in the plainest words; culminates in 'Simplicity of language is... perhaps even sacred.'
  • Vonnegut on his own Indianapolis voice — common speech that 'sounds like a band saw cutting galvanized tin' with a vocabulary 'as unornamental as a monkey wrench' — alongside Conrad's Polish-colored English, musical Irish English, and Elizabethan locutions surviving in Appalachia.
    → Self-demonstration of 'sound like yourself': the passage enacts the plain, self-deprecating regional voice it prescribes, and argues non-standard voice is beautiful ('like a very pretty girl with one eye that is green and one that is blue') and to be treasured.
  • His dream of doing with words what Picasso did with paint or jazz idols did with music — breaking all the rules of punctuation and meaning — abandoned because it would simply not be understood.
    → Cautionary example subordinating avant-garde self-expression to clarity: if you have something worth saying and wish to be understood, you must forgo Picasso-style writing.
  • The E.B. White / Strunk & White coda: he commends The Elements of Style and praises White as a great stylist, then adds that no one would care how well or badly White wrote if he did not have 'perfectly enchanting things to say.'
    → Closes the argument by returning craft to its place beneath content — technique is worthless without something worth saying — echoing Rule 1.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What is 'style', and why should an ordinary person bother to improve it?
    Style is the self-revelation your writing performs; you improve it out of respect for the reader, because careless writing loses the reader's trust and attention.
  • ✅ Where does compelling style actually come from?
    From genuinely caring about a worthwhile subject ('ideas in your head'), not from games with language; content and knowing what is interesting come first.
  • ✅ Should I write in 'correct' standard English or in my own voice?
    Sound like yourself; the aim of 'correctness' is being understood, not imitating a prestige dialect, and authentic regional voice is beautiful and more trustworthy.
  • ✅ How simple should the language be?
    As simple as possible, even childlike — profound subjects especially deserve plain language; cut whatever does not illuminate the subject.
  • ⭕ How does a novice develop a voice — or a sense of what is interesting — worth sounding like in the first place?
    Not addressed; Vonnegut treats an authentic voice and a nose for what is interesting as pre-given (by origin and character) rather than as something to be cultivated.
  • ⭕ Can every worthwhile subject be said simply, or do some resist reduction?
    Not addressed; the essay assumes profundity can always be rendered simply, drawing only on examples of emotional (not conceptual/technical) depth.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe essay is entirely of the print-essay era: it says nothing about digital, collaborative, or AI-assisted writing. Its whole model — a single author revealing a single self to a reader — does not touch what 'sounding like yourself' or 'style as self-revelation' mean when text can be machine-generated, co-written, or stylistically homogenized at scale.
    Read next: Read against Franco 'What we lose when AI writes for us' (style/voice as the Self, and its convergence under AI), Acunzo 'The best response to AI slop' (distinctive firsthand voice as the non-commoditizable signal), and the taste article (correctness vs. quality when a machine produces the prose).
  • incomplete'Sound like yourself' is under-specified to the point of circularity for a novice: it presupposes an already-articulate, trustworthy voice worth sounding like, and locates that voice in one's origin and childhood speech rather than in any developable skill. It offers no method for a beginner whose natural voice is inarticulate or undeveloped to find or grow one.
    Read next: Read a pedagogy-of-voice source (Zinsser 'On Writing Well', Peter Elbow's 'Writing With Power') and the deliberate-practice literature on how voice is cultivated rather than inherited.
  • incompleteThe simplicity argument generalizes from emotional profundity to all profundity. Every exemplar (Hamlet's soliloquy, Joyce's 'She was tired', Genesis) is emotional or narrative depth rendered plainly; none is a technically or conceptually irreducible subject. The claim that childlike simplicity fits 'the most profound subjects' may not survive contact with domains whose complexity is essential, not incidental.
    Read next: Read Ousterhout on essential vs. accidental complexity and the difference between deep-simple and simplistic; explanation theory / technical-writing craft on rendering irreducible complexity without falsifying it.
  • incompleteThe claim that non-standard voice is 'delightful' assumes a reader who is charmed by it ('like a very pretty girl with one eye green and one blue'), with no account of the many contexts where non-standard or regional voice is penalized rather than trusted. 'Sound like yourself' is treated as costless, which may reflect the privilege of a canonical author rather than the situation of a writer whose authentic voice carries real reputational cost.
    Read next: Read sociolinguistics on prestige dialects and linguistic discrimination, and the literature on code-switching, to see where authentic voice is a liability rather than a delight.
