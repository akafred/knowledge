AI isn't making developers more productive – it's making them busier

Chris Stokel-Walker · article · 2026-06-11 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

A large-scale study of 100,000+ GitHub developers reframes the AI-coding story as redistribution rather than acceleration: AI makes developers busier by ballooning code output, but net shipped value barely moves because fixed human review/integration capacity is the binding downstream constraint.

practical · expository news writeup of an empirical study — A ~768-word LeadDev news article reporting an NBER working paper (w35275) by MIT and Wharton researchers analyzing 100,000+ GitHub developers, framed for engineering managers with practitioner commentary from Alfonso Graziano (Nearform) and Josh King (&above). It reports and interprets a study rather than conducting its own analysis.

Key terms

  • busier (vs. more productive)
    A rise in task-level activity — commits, lines of code, work-in-progress — that does not convert into more delivered/shipped value; effort is redistributed, not reduced.
  • productivity
    Operationalized as a hierarchy of proxies climbing the org: coding activity (commits, LOC) at the base, pull requests in the middle, actual software releases (and ultimately end-user value) at the top; the piece treats releases/value, not commits, as the productivity that matters.
  • weak-link hypothesis
    The study authors' term for why gains attenuate: AI is effective at generating raw code, but everything after (reviewing PRs, integrating changes, managing releases) still depends on largely fixed human effort and judgment, so surplus AI code creates a downstream traffic jam.
  • the constraint / slowest stage (Theory of Constraints)
    Graziano's framing: a system's throughput is set by its slowest stage; speeding up any other stage does not raise throughput — it only grows the pile of work-in-progress in front of the real constraint, and pushing harder makes the system worse.
  • evaluator of code (vs. writer of code)
    King's reframe of the developer role: with agents generating volume, the primary human skill becomes reviewing/judging code rather than producing it; review becomes roughly half the job and the primary constraint on delivery.

Arguments

AI makes developers busier but not more productive in net terms. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • AI dramatically increases code-writing activity (up to +741% lines of code, +180% coding activity).
  • That gain attenuates as work moves up the pipeline: only +65% PRs and +20% releases.
  • Downstream stages (review, integration, release) depend on human judgment whose capacity is largely fixed (the weak-link hypothesis).
  • A system's throughput is set by its slowest stage, so speeding other stages only grows work-in-progress in front of the constraint (Graziano / Theory of Constraints).
Implicit premises:
  • Activity metrics (commits, lines of code, PR counts) are being mistaken for productivity, when the productivity that matters is delivered/shipped value.
  • The time AI saves in code generation is reabsorbed into more downstream tasks and accumulating work-in-progress rather than freed up, so faster code-writing produces more busyness, not more output.
Inferences:
  • If upstream output multiplies while the binding downstream human constraint stays fixed, the surplus necessarily piles up as WIP rather than converting to throughput.
  • Therefore observed gains at the keyboard cannot be read as net productivity gains for the organization.
Organizations must reconceive the developer as an evaluator of code and make review the primary constraint they optimize, not the final step. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • AI-coding agents clearly make individual developers faster at producing code.
  • Volume has ballooned (e.g. from 3-4 to 20+ concurrent open PRs), making review roughly half the job.
  • Many teams still treat review as the final step rather than the primary constraint on delivery.
Implicit premises:
  • Delivery throughput can only rise if the review/integration constraint is expanded or redesigned, since adding more raw code does not help.
  • Human evaluation capacity is elastic enough to be re-engineered (via changed processes) even though it is fixed under current practices.
Inferences:
  • If review is now the binding constraint, then adapting review/testing/maintenance processes — not generating more code — is where competitive advantage lies.

Narratives

  • Alfonso Graziano (AI technical lead at Nearform) illustrates the finding with Theory of Constraints: 'A system's throughput is set by its slowest stage, so if you speed up any other process the throughput does not change'; the only thing that grows is the pile of work-in-progress in front of the real constraint, and pushing harder makes the system worse.
    → Supplies the mechanistic vocabulary (bottleneck, WIP, constraint) that generalizes the study's numbers into a systems principle.
  • Josh King (CTO of &above) recounts how his firm went from three or four open PRs on a project to 20-plus, and argues developers 'still think of themselves as writers of code' when 'their actual job is now evaluators of code'; the firm overhauled how it thinks about evaluation and testing to keep up.
    → Concrete practitioner anecdote grounding the role-shift prescription and quantifying the review pile-up.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Does AI making developers write far more code actually make them (or their organizations) more productive?
    No, not in net terms: task-level output multiplies but only a fraction survives to PRs and far less to releases, because fixed human review/integration capacity is the binding constraint — so developers get busier without shipping proportionally more value.
  • ✅ Where does the AI-generated productivity get lost between writing code and delivering value?
    At the downstream human stages — PR review, integration, release management — where AI-generated volume creates a traffic jam in front of a fixed-capacity constraint (the weak-link hypothesis).
  • ✅ What should managers do about it?
    Shift the mental model from developer-as-writer to developer-as-evaluator and adapt review, testing, and maintenance processes to absorb the volume, since review is now the primary constraint on delivery.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe writeup omits the underlying study's methodology: how 'productivity' was operationalized beyond activity proxies, whether commit/PR/release counts are telemetry or self-report, how developers were assigned to (or self-selected into) the three tool generations, and what confounders (tenure, repo type, developer selection) were controlled. The headline figures cannot be assessed without the primary source.
    Read next: The primary source — NBER working paper w35275 (MIT / Wharton), 'https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w35275/w35275.pdf' — for the identification strategy and construct definitions.
  • incompleteThe article treats commits, lines of code, PRs and releases as a clean productivity ladder but never engages the quality of the extra code or whether a 20% release increase reflects real outcome value or noise. Volume up while quality/defect rate unaddressed leaves open whether the 'weak link' is human capacity, code trustworthiness, or both.
    Read next: DORA/SPACE outcome-and-stability metrics (not just throughput); studies on the maintainability and defect rate of AI-generated code to separate volume from value.
    closed byCode maintainability plummets in the AI coding era — That piece flags that its productivity ladder 'never engages the quality of the extra code' and asks for studies on the maintainability and defect rate of AI-generated code to separate volume from value. This is that study: GitClear's 623M-commit dataset measures the quality of the surplus output directly (duplication +81%, reuse -70%, legacy refactoring -74%, error-masking +47%), showing the 'weak link' is code trustworthiness/maintainability, not only fixed human review capacity.
  • incompleteThe app-store 'usage stayed flat' evidence is used to prove that AI-flooded software fails to find an audience, but the article does not isolate which released apps were AI-assisted or control for baseline market saturation, so the causal step from 'more releases + flat usage' to 'AI software attracts no audience' is asserted rather than demonstrated.
    Read next: The primary study's market-outcome methodology; app-market baseline data on release-to-usage ratios independent of AI adoption.
