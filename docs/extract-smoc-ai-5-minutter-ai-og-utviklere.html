SMOC.AI's Post — 5 Minutter: AI og utviklere

AI amplifies senior developers' effectiveness but breaks the traditional junior developer training pipeline, because the simple tasks that served as the learning on-ramp are being automated away — and juniors guiding AI-juniors compound weaknesses rather than building competence.

practical · narrative — Practical: argues for a specific AI workflow (spec-first, then code). Narrative form: first-person account of building an AI-driven company, using personal experience as evidence. Part of a serialized LinkedIn content series ('5 Minutter').

Key terms

  • AI-junior (AI as junior developer) AI Labor Disruption
    AI is extremely productive at starting and executing tasks but produces low-quality code without strong guidance. It needs architectural direction, specification, and feedback loops — exactly what a junior human developer needs, but at much higher speed and volume.
  • evig loop av iterasjoner (eternal iteration loop)
    The failure mode when AI works without clear specification: it keeps iterating toward 'ok but far from ideal' output, burning time without converging on quality. The loop is the symptom of missing architectural guidance.

Arguments

Traditional junior developer training will become unviable because AI eliminates the simple tasks that were the learning on-ramp strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • AI can now do the simple tasks that juniors used to do
  • These simple tasks were the entry gate to building experience
  • In time-critical environments, using seniors to supervise junior-AI pairs is a net slowdown
Implicit premises:
  • There is no alternative way to train juniors other than through progressively harder real tasks — classroom learning, pair programming on complex tasks, or AI-assisted learning are not considered
  • The startup context (time-critical, every decision matters) generalizes to the industry — what's true at SMOC.AI is true everywhere
  • Junior developers cannot learn by reviewing AI output or by using AI as a teaching tool — the relationship is assumed to be one-directional (junior guides AI) rather than bidirectional (AI teaches junior)
Inferences:
  • If simple tasks disappear as a training mechanism, the industry faces a pipeline crisis: where do future seniors come from?
  • The 'AI as junior dev' metaphor may be self-defeating over time — if juniors can't train, there will eventually be no seniors to guide the AI
  • Companies that figure out alternative junior training models will have a structural advantage in talent development
The optimal AI development workflow requires senior-level architectural judgment upfront strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Without specification and architecture review, AI enters an eternal iteration loop
  • With spec-first workflow, results are 'dramatically better'
  • AI code quality is often low without guidance
Implicit premises:
  • Architectural judgment is a skill that AI currently cannot provide for itself — the human must supply it
  • The cost of upfront specification is lower than the cost of iteration loops — front-loading design pays off
Inferences:
  • This workflow resembles Beck's 'Tidy First?' thesis: invest in structure before behavior. The AI context makes the case for upfront design stronger, not weaker
  • If AI needs architectural guidance, then software design skill becomes MORE valuable in the AI era, not less — contradicting the 'AI replaces developers' narrative

Gaps

  • incompleteThe post identifies the junior training crisis but offers no solution. If simple tasks are the on-ramp and they're disappearing, what replaces them? AI-assisted pair programming? Simulation environments? Apprenticeship models? The post stops at the problem statement.
    Read next: Addy Osmani on AI-assisted learning; Kent Beck on coaching with AI; literature on medical residency models (which face similar 'hours reduction' training challenges)
  • illogicalThe 'AI as junior developer' metaphor contains a self-defeating dynamic the author doesn't notice: if AI eliminates junior training, and seniors are needed to guide AI, then the pipeline that produces seniors is broken. The metaphor predicts its own obsolescence — eventually there are no seniors left to guide the AI-junior. This is the most consequential implication of the post and it goes entirely unaddressed.
    Read next: Systems thinking (Meadows) on stocks and flows — the senior developer pool is a stock that requires inflow from the junior pipeline; cutting the inflow depletes the stock over time
  • incompleteThe claim that AI code quality is 'often low' is stated but not analyzed. Low in what dimension? Maintainability? Correctness? Performance? Architecture? Different quality dimensions have different implications for when and how AI should be supervised.
    Read next: DORA 2025 findings on AI and code quality; empirical studies on AI-generated code defect rates
  • misinformedThe post assumes juniors can only learn by doing simple tasks themselves. But research on learning suggests that code review, debugging, and explaining code are equally powerful learning mechanisms — and AI makes all of these more accessible, not less. A junior reviewing AI output may learn faster than a junior writing CRUD endpoints from scratch.
    Read next: Felienne Hermans on programming education; cognitive apprenticeship literature; research on learning through code review
