Managers Are Struggling to Keep Up with the AI Productivity Boom

Mollie West Duffy (with Liz at Atlassian) · rss · 2026-05 Reader 🖥️

AI SlopHuman as Orchestrator of AgentsManaging Up & InfluenceOrchestration Tax / Human Review BottleneckOrganizational Abstraction Levels
As AI accelerates individual execution, managers become the new bottleneck; their role must shift from editor-in-chief (policing the work) to strategic guide (clearing the path) via five behavioral changes — focus on where not what, make managing up a core skill, deploy AI as an EQ coach, use AI to filter rather than flatten, and restructure check-in cadence.

practical · mixed — Practical advice piece with expository setup (the bottleneck thesis) and argumentative scaffolding for five behavioral prescriptions. House HBR genre: anecdote + research stat + named-executive quote + numbered to-dos.

Key terms

  • Editor-in-chief vs Strategic guide
    Old manager mode = quality-policing reviewer at every step; new mode = direction-setter who clears obstacles and trusts execution
  • Manager of managers Human as Orchestrator of Agents
    A human manager whose direct reports each manage AI agents — operating one abstraction level higher than 2022 management
  • AI workslop AI Slop
    Polished-looking AI-generated content that lacks real substance; cited from a prior HBR piece
  • Filter not flatten
    Use AI to surface signal (the 5 specific nuggets worth deep review) rather than to compress everything into uniform summaries
  • Altitude (of review) Organizational Abstraction Levels
    The level of detail/finality at which a manager needs to engage with a piece of work (outline vs final draft vs executive-facing)

Arguments

Managers must shift from editor-in-chief to strategic guide strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • AI accelerated execution timeline from weeks to next-day
  • Managers cannot keep up with review at the new pace
  • Managers also feel excited by team output and don't want to slow teams down
Implicit premises:
  • The manager role is preserved across the AI transition
  • Behavior change alone can bridge the throughput gap
  • Direct reports retain similar autonomy regardless of cadence shift
Inferences:
  • If the manager layer cannot scale via behavior change, the article's framing breaks — but this counterfactual is never raised
Use AI agents to police tone in your DMs strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • Time-constrained managers default to information density and skip tonal cues
  • Across hundreds of messages the misses add up
  • Garcia Valenzuela's agent surfaced consistent small failures
Implicit premises:
  • The bottleneck on warmth is attention, not capacity or willingness
  • Subordinates won't notice or mind that an AI is auditing their boss's messages on their behalf
  • AI tone-judgment is reliable across cultural contexts
Inferences:
  • If managers cannot find time for warm messages, the role spec itself is the problem; using AI to patch the gap is a symptom-management workaround
Daily syncs should replace weekly reports and biweekly product reviews strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • 'It was all too slow' (Gomes/Conveyer)
  • Junior reports can waste money/time running far in the wrong direction
Implicit premises:
  • Increased meeting frequency does not itself become a productivity tax
  • Daily syncs scale across team sizes and time zones
  • Managers have the focus surplus to attend daily syncs across all their directs
Inferences:
  • The Osmani 'Orchestration Tax is You' argument applies here too — multiplying syncs may just move the bottleneck location without raising throughput

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What should managers do when AI accelerates their team's execution past the manager's review capacity?
    Adopt five behavioral changes that shift the manager from execution-policer to direction-setter
  • ✅ Why are managers becoming bottlenecks rather than benefiting from AI like their reports?
    Their role spec assumes execution takes time; AI broke that assumption
  • ⭕ Is the manager layer still the right structural unit when AI does most of the execution?
    Not addressed
  • ⭕ What happens to manager decision-rights when AI-produced output is faster and arguably higher-quality than human review?
    Not addressed; managers' authority is treated as unchanged
  • ⭕ Who pays the cost of increased synthesis-and-coordination work (the strategic guide role) — is it just relabeled overload?
    Not addressed

Gaps

  • incompleteSame system-shift-framed-as-practice-tweak pattern recurring across the user's recent extractions (Cutler, Osmani, Reed, HBR Career-Growth-Overwhelmed). Duffy diagnoses a structural failure of the manager layer to keep pace, then prescribes individual behavior changes that leave organizational architecture, role boundaries, decision rights, and compensation untouched.
    Read next: Pair with Cutler 'AI Won't Save the Kingdoms We Built' and Osmani 'Orchestration Tax is You' for the syntopical thread. The unanswered question: when is the manager role itself the bottleneck-design, and what would an AI-era org chart actually look like?
  • incompleteTreats manager decision-rights over AI-produced output as unproblematic — but if AI generates faster, broader, and (with verification) better output than the manager's review can keep up with, the manager's authority is conventional, not epistemic. The piece never asks whose judgment is actually load-bearing.
    Read next: Cathy O'Neil on algorithmic accountability; Bainbridge 'Ironies of Automation' for what happens to operator authority when automation outpaces oversight (Paul Reed extract).
  • incompleteThe AI-as-EQ-coach example is presented as a clever augmentation, but if managers cannot find time for warm communication, that's an org-design failure being masked by a tool. The article never asks whether normalized DM-auditing by AI changes the implicit contract between manager and report.
    Read next: Surveillance/trust literature; HBR 'On Managing Yourself' for the older norm being replaced.
  • illogical'Increase frequency to course-correct faster' (daily syncs) is offered as a remedy for manager overload, but adding meetings to an already overloaded manager is the textbook overload-amplifier. Osmani's orchestration-tax argument applies to managers too.
    Read next: Cutler TBM on institutionalized overload; Osmani 'Orchestration Tax is You'.
  • incomplete'89% of leaders agree AI has accelerated work' — but the article assumes that acceleration is sustained throughput rather than a transient burst as teams adopt new tooling. No engagement with the possibility that the 'AI productivity boom' is a one-time S-curve, not the new steady state.
    Read next: DORA reports on AI's effect on engineering throughput; longitudinal AI-adoption studies.
