Getting Buy-In for Your Next Big Idea

HBR.org · rss · 2026-05-20 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Managing Up & Influence
Selling an idea upward is not a single pitch but a sustained campaign that succeeds by mobilizing allies, reducing resistance rather than overpowering it, framing the idea around mutual benefit and strategic fit, and pursuing small wins while regulating one's own emotion and ego.

practical · mixed — Conversational/argumentative with embedded narrative case studies and listener Q&A. Practical: every proposition is in service of 'here is what you should do to sell an idea upward'. The conversation form means propositions are restated informally and often co-constructed across speakers.

Key terms

  • Issue selling Managing Up & Influence
    Ashford's research construct: the sustained, multi-stage process by which a sub-executive surfaces an issue, frames it, gathers coalition, and gets organizational attention/resources. Treated as a campaign, not an event.
  • Buy-in Managing Up & Influence
    Active commitment of resources, time, and political cover, not verbal assent. Speakers explicitly distinguish nodding-along from buy-in.
  • Force-field model (Lewin)
    Organizations sit in stasis because pushing forces and restraining forces are balanced; movement comes more efficiently from reducing restraining forces than from increasing pushing forces.
  • Restraining forces Managing Up & Influence
    Specifically, the perspectives, fears, and losses that make particular stakeholders block a change. Identifying them is a precondition for reframing.
  • Scarce attention / agenda marketplace
    Organizations have a bounded executive agenda; issues compete for slots. A good idea can lose simply because attention is allocated elsewhere.
  • Small wins Managing Up & Influence
    Karl Weick's idea, used here as a tactic: shrink the ask so it is approvable, generate momentum, attract additional allies through observable outcomes.
  • Circle of trust
    The inner network (colleagues, partners, allies) used to test and refine the idea before exposing it to the wider organization.
  • Ally / blocker / fence-sitter Managing Up & Influence
    Three stakeholder categories to map before selling. Strategy: 'mobilize allies to influence fence-sitters to pressure blockers' — never confront blockers directly.
  • Issue seller
    The role of the person trying to put an issue on the organizational agenda. Note: there are often competing issue sellers in the same room.
  • Blind man and the elephant
    Metaphor for the partiality of any one person's view of the organization; used to argue that the seller should assume the executive has information the seller lacks.
  • Authenticity / executive presence
    Bailey's claim that one can speak openly about meeting dynamics ('we are now going down issue B; is this the time?') without sacrificing executive presence.

Arguments

Reduce restraining forces rather than amplify pushing forces. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Lewin's force-field model holds that organizations sit in stasis because pushing and restraining forces are balanced.
  • Increasing pushing forces provokes pushback.
  • Reducing the restraining forces (e.g., by reframing the idea so blockers lose less) yields movement without backlash.
Implicit premises:
  • The seller has enough access and time to discover what each blocker actually fears or loses.
  • Blockers' restraint is rational and addressable through reframing rather than structural (e.g., they will lose budget, status, or headcount no matter how the idea is framed).
  • The cost of reducing each restraining force is lower than the cost of increasing pushing force.
Inferences:
  • If a restraining force is structural (a zero-sum loss for the blocker), no amount of reframing will move it; the article's framework silently bottoms out here.
  • Therefore the framework works best for ideas that are positive-sum and least well for redistributive ones.
Issue selling should be treated as a campaign, not as the formal pitch meeting. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Organizations have scarce attention; issues compete for agenda slots.
  • Allies and fence-sitters can move blockers indirectly.
  • Stakeholders who have already heard and contributed to the idea will arrive at the meeting with authorship and prior buy-in.
  • Verbal assent is not buy-in; sustained organizational commitment requires sustained effort.
Implicit premises:
  • The seller has the social capital, time, and organizational permission to conduct a sustained campaign of one-on-ones.
  • The organization has a stable enough decision process that a campaign run over weeks/months retains its target.
  • The political cost of being seen as 'campaigning' is lower than the cost of being seen as transactional.
Inferences:
  • In organizations with high churn, opaque decision rights, or hostile politics, the campaign model decays into 'unrewarded politicking,' which the article does not address.
Aim for small wins rather than full approval on the first ask. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Small asks feel doable to executives; doable asks are more likely to be approved.
  • Approved small actions generate observable outcomes, which create momentum and attract allies.
Implicit premises:
  • The big idea decomposes into small wins without losing its essential leverage.
  • Small wins will accumulate rather than substitute for the big change (i.e., the organization will not declare victory after the small win).
Inferences:
  • Some big ideas are not decomposable (e.g., a structural reorganization, a platform migration); for these, the small-wins prescription either delays the change indefinitely or quietly replaces it with a watered-down version.
Mobilize allies to influence fence-sitters to pressure blockers; do not confront blockers directly. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Direct confrontation hardens blockers.
  • Fence-sitters are movable.
  • Pressure from peers is more effective than pressure from the seller.
Implicit premises:
  • Allies are willing to spend their political capital on the seller's issue.
  • The organization treats peer-coalition pressure as legitimate rather than as cabal-building.
  • Blockers do not have a hard veto (e.g., budget authority that cannot be routed around).
Inferences:
  • If the blocker holds the budget, the indirect strategy fails; the article does not say so.
Verbal assent without action is a form of resistance, not a partial success. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Allison reports leadership 'loved' her idea but did nothing.
  • People who do not act on an idea are functionally resisting it.
  • Passive resistance (delay, unresponsiveness) is observed empirically in organizations.
Implicit premises:
  • The leadership team is not simply overwhelmed by genuinely higher-priority work; the inaction is signal, not noise.
  • There exists a follow-up move (individual confirmation, finding the real blocker) that will surface the true objection.
Inferences:
  • Sometimes inaction is just overload, in which case treating it as 'real but hidden resistance' wastes the seller's time. The article does not give a test for distinguishing the two.
If the issue is important enough to the collective, the seller must accept the risk of being annoying. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Persistent advocacy is socially costly.
  • Important issues sometimes only move because someone is willing to absorb that cost.
  • If those with status do not raise the issue, no one will.
Implicit premises:
  • The seller's standing in the organization will recover from being 'annoying' — i.e., the seller is senior enough or culturally protected enough.
  • The 'collective' the seller invokes is real, not the seller's identification of self with mission.
Inferences:
  • For low-status sellers, the risk of being annoying may be career-ending; the article tacitly assumes a tenured / VP-level audience.

Narratives

  • Bailey's pursuit of a 'Black Equity at Work' certification at Harvard Business Publishing, where she anticipated that majority colleagues might worry about losing something and used data ('if it is equitable for a Black woman, it is equitable for all') to address that anticipated objection.
    → Demonstrates the proposition that anticipating 'for whom is this NOT a good idea' enables better framing; supports the force-field/restraining-forces argument concretely.
  • Bernstein's process-change story — wanting to alter a process that a specific group might experience as making their jobs harder; she addressed it head-on, listened, and adjusted.
    → Supports the prescription to talk to affected stakeholders before the wide pitch and the proposition that adjustment is part of selling.
  • Ashford's example of a woman proposing more time off / flexibility, where a client-facing colleague objected ('so we tell clients we work less for you?').
    → Concretizes the 'for whom is this not a good idea' move; shows that the seller's frame and the resister's frame can both be correct from their respective vantages.
  • Pam's listener question: a program manager at a credentialing company whose pitch about project management was derailed by a C-suite member's rabbit-hole questions.
    → Application case for the diagnostic 'is this a resistor, a competing issue seller, or just musing out loud?' Generates the prescription to investigate motive via the network rather than confront publicly.
  • Allison's listener question: a data services manager whose proposal to merge teams was praised by department leadership but never escalated.
    → Application case for the proposition that verbal assent is not buy-in and for the 'who stands to lose' question; also surfaces the partial-perspective/blind-man-and-elephant argument.
  • Bailey's allyship reverse-mentoring program: a colleague's initial proposal of year-long monthly meetings, a second-pass overcorrection to one meeting, and a final landing on three meetings over three to four months via the 'what are three other ways to solve this?' question.
    → Supports the proposition that prematurely fixing on a solution narrows the space; shows how to widen it as a receiver of pitches.
  • Ashford's irritation with tenured / chaired senior women colleagues who hesitate to raise issues about women's treatment.
    → Supports the closing proposition that those with status must accept the risk of being annoying when the cause matters.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why do executives so often pass on ideas from mid-level managers?
    They do not immediately perceive the idea's relevance to organizational performance; the burden of demonstrating relevance falls on the seller.
  • ✅ How should a seller vet their own idea before pitching it?
    Test it with a circle of trust; ask the three framing questions; weigh pros, cons, and doability.
  • ✅ How should a seller structure the campaign of selling beyond the inner circle?
    Map allies, blockers, fence-sitters, and decision-influencers; socialize one-on-one; pursue small wins; aim only for 'worth exploring' in the first formal meeting.
  • ✅ How does one tell apparent agreement (nodding) from real buy-in?
    Watch for delay, unresponsiveness, and lack of resource commitment; follow up individually to confirm and probe for the real blocker.
  • ✅ When should the seller stop and when should they persist despite feeling annoying?
    If the cause is significant for the collective, accept the social cost and continue; otherwise pick your battles by importance and timing.
  • ⭕ What should an executive who wants more upward ideas actually do differently?
    Although the conversation opens by citing Mankins and Litre that executives ought to welcome bottom-up change, the article gives no prescriptions for executives — only for sellers.
  • ⭕ How do incentive structures, decision rights, and budget authority shape which ideas survive?
    Not addressed. The framework treats organizational politics as a perceptual/framing problem rather than a resource-allocation one.
  • ⭕ How does the seller's hierarchical position, identity, or political capital change which tactics are available to them?
    The advice is presented as universal, but the cases (a VP, a tenured professor, an HBR editor) all involve senior or protected sellers; the framework's robustness for low-status or precarious sellers is not examined.
  • ⭕ Are there ideas the campaign model is structurally unable to surface (e.g., ideas about the campaign model itself, or ideas that threaten the executives whose buy-in is required)?
    Not addressed.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe article identifies executive non-perception of relevance as the root cause of failed buy-in, then assigns 100% of the remedy to the seller. The corresponding institutional remedy — executives learning to receive, sponsor allocation, structured 'bring-up' channels — is mentioned (Mankins & Litre) and immediately dropped. This is the conversation's largest hidden asymmetry: it accepts a structural diagnosis but offers only individual prescriptions.
    Read next: Cutler's work on institutional overload and operating-system design (TBM); Edmondson on psychological safety as a receiving infrastructure; sociotechnical literature on decision rights.
  • incompletePower, incentives, budget authority, and zero-sum redistribution are absent. The stakeholder map (ally / blocker / fence-sitter) is purely affective; it does not model who controls the resources, whose headcount shrinks, or whose KPIs the idea threatens.
    Read next: Pfeffer ('Power'); Bolman & Deal's political frame; March & Simon's bounded rationality and political coalitions; Christensen on incumbent self-protection.
  • incompleteThe framework conflates several distinct receiver-behaviours under 'resistance': overload-induced inaction, genuine objection, competing issue-selling, and political vetoing. They demand different responses, but the article treats them as variants of 'we need to dig deeper.' Without a diagnostic, the seller can be told indefinitely to socialize and follow up on what is in fact a hard 'no.'
    Read next: Argyris on undiscussables and skilled incompetence; Kahneman/Klein on diagnostic decision-making under ambiguity.
  • incompleteThe article assumes the seller possesses time, network, political cover, and emotional regulation as preconditions. It does not address how to do issue selling when one or more of those is unavailable — i.e., the typical condition of overloaded mid-level managers.
    Read next: Time to Think (Kline) on the conditions ideas need; Stolen Focus on attention as precondition; Fearless Change patterns for resource-poor change agents.
  • incompleteAdler's mode of incompleteness: the article reduces idea transmission to tactics and scripts ('three questions,' 'map the stakeholders,' 'aim for worth exploring'). The structural conditions under which those tactics actually work — slack in the executive agenda, trust between layers, a culture that distinguishes loyalty to leaders from loyalty to ideas — are presupposed and never named. The advice is therefore most useful exactly where it is least needed (healthy organizations) and least useful where it is most needed (organizations whose pathology is what makes upward selling necessary in the first place).
    Read next: Fearless Change (Manns & Rising) — patterns for getting ideas through resistant organizations; Time to Think (Nancy Kline); Cutler's 'institutionalized overload' essays; Donella Meadows on leverage points (rule-changes, paradigms) vs. parameter-changes.
  • illogicalLewin's force-field model is invoked to justify reducing restraining forces, but the conversation conflates two different uses: (a) reduce the *perception* of loss through framing, and (b) reduce the *actual* loss through redesign. Only (a) is illustrated in the cases. The implication that (a) generally substitutes for (b) is not earned by the cases shown.
    Read next: Original Lewin on planned change; revisionist critiques (Burnes, Cummings & Worley) on what force-field analysis can and cannot do.
  • uninformedThe article never engages with the considerable literature on why good ideas reliably fail in organizations independent of framing skill — institutional isomorphism, status quo bias of senior leaders facing reputational risk, principal-agent problems, sunk-cost commitments. The implicit theory of failure here is unidimensional (the seller framed it badly).
    Read next: DiMaggio & Powell on isomorphism; Jensen on agency costs; Hambrick on upper-echelons theory.
