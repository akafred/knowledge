A cautionary tale about AI in tech writing: Why "Trust but Verify" is the only way to scale

Betsalel (Saul) Williamson · article · 2026-07-13 Reader 🖥️

AI SlopFeedback LoopsGuardrailsHuman-in-the-LoopSpec-Driven DevelopmentVerifiability (as the Quality Bar)
The genuinely scalable use of AI in technical documentation is not to trust the LLM's raw output but to wrap it in a software-development-style pipeline -- specs as ground truth, deterministic tools (linters, formatters, style checkers) doing everything mechanical, and human 'trust but verify' review concentrated only on the residual, non-automatable judgment work (procedure validity, UX intent, omission, accountability) -- so that the 8-hour/17,500-word claim is evidence not that AI collapses the verification burden of technical writing but that a disciplined pipeline can relocate and compress it enough to make speed and rigor compatible.

practical · first-person practitioner case study wrapped around a prescriptive step-by-step framework, published as a LinkedIn article with an appended reusable prompt template — The piece is not an argument about AI-and-trust in the abstract; it is a delivery-methodology writeup (specs, tooling, review stages) offered as a transferable recipe, with the personal anecdote (17,500 words / 8 hours) functioning as the credibility hook rather than as evidence in its own right.

Key terms

  • Trust, but Verify
    specifically the 5th and final pipeline stage: after specs, shards, and deterministic tooling have handled the mechanical work, the human spends the freed-up time on reading/comprehension/procedure-testing of the residual content
  • slop AI Slop
    the predictable output of blindly trusting a probabilistic, non-deterministic process without quality controls -- bad design, code, or docs
  • shard / sharding
    splitting a large Markdown manual into small, maintainable pieces (via the BMAD method and @kayvan/markdown-tree-parser) so the LLM works in small batches and a scripted compiler reassembles them with corrected heading hierarchy
  • ground truth
    the specification documents (optionally extracted directly from the codebase) that constrain the AI so it never has to guess
  • the tools pipeline
    the named, deterministic layer (Prettier, markdownlint-cli2, custom static analysis, Vale with a custom dictionary) that catches formatting drift, broken links, and voice/terminology violations without relying on the LLM to check itself
  • Specs -> Technical Docs -> Client Docs
    a required three-stage translation cascade: rough-but-correct specs, then validated technical docs, then jargon-free client docs derived only from the validated technical docs

Arguments

Fast AI-assisted documentation production is compatible with rigor only through a disciplined, software-development-style pipeline -- not through direct trust in LLM output. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • AI cannot validate procedures/business logic, make UX/design judgment calls, know what to omit, or be accountable (p1).
  • Blind trust in LLM output yields slop because the underlying process is probabilistic and non-deterministic (p2).
  • A 5-step framework -- specs, ambiguity resolution, sharding + deterministic tooling, human proofing, and targeted verification -- was used to produce the 17,500-word manual (p3-p9).
Implicit premises:
  • The author's years of traditional technical-writing experience qualify him to judge what rigorous verification requires and to recognize slop when he sees it.
  • A framework transferable via a copy-paste prompt constitutes genuine methodological transfer, not just a description of one personal workflow.
Inferences:
  • Because AI structurally lacks validation, judgment, omission-awareness and accountability, and because deterministic tools can absorb the purely mechanical quality-control burden, a layered pipeline can convert AI's raw drafting speed into legitimate throughput without the risk that comes from trusting the model directly.
The '17,000+ words in 8 hours' framing functions rhetorically as a credibility hook establishing 'I know exactly how much rigor real technical writing requires, and I still insist on it here' -- rather than as a measured productivity benchmark. strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • The claim is stated in the opening sentence, before any methodology or definitions are given.
  • No baseline comparison to manual (non-AI) production time for a comparable manual is offered anywhere in the piece.
Implicit premises:
  • A large, round, attention-getting number (17,000+ words, 8 hours) is more persuasive as a hook than as a controlled measurement.
Inferences:
  • Because the number is unaccompanied by a methodology, a baseline, or a breakdown across the five steps, it cannot function as evidence of net productivity gain; its work in the piece is almost entirely to establish the author's stakes and expertise.

Narratives

  • The author, a veteran technical writer, recently delivered a ~17,500-word technical manual to a client in eight hours using an LLM. Because he has spent years doing the traditional, painstaking version of this work, he treats his own fast AI-assisted output with suspicion rather than pride, and narrates the specific five-step process (specs, ambiguity resolution, sharding plus automated tooling, human proofing, and final 'trust but verify' review) he used to keep the output trustworthy. He closes by offering the process as a literal, reusable prompt for other teams, then appends a post-publication correction after a colleague pointed out a naming collision with an existing framework (Specflow).
    → Serves as both proof-of-concept and structuring device for the whole piece: the personal narrative supplies the ethos (rigor-minded veteran, not an AI hype-poster), the step-by-step retelling supplies the transferable mechanism, and the closing self-correction quietly re-enacts the article's own thesis -- that even careful work benefits from an external check.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Can AI safely be used to produce large volumes of technical documentation quickly?
    Yes, but only inside a disciplined pipeline -- never by trusting the LLM's raw output directly.
  • ✅ What specifically can't AI do in a documentation workflow, that a human must still do?
    Validate procedures/business logic, make UX/design judgment calls, decide what to omit, and bear accountability for the final product.
  • ✅ How do you keep an AI-drafted corpus consistent and low-ambiguity at scale?
    Explicit specs with defined terms and edge cases, small-batch sharding, and a deterministic tools pipeline (formatting, linting, style enforcement) rather than relying on the LLM to self-correct.
  • ⭕ Is '17,500 words in 8 hours' actually a fair or meaningful productivity comparison against traditional (non-AI) technical writing?
    The piece never benchmarks against a manual-writing baseline, nor breaks down how the 8 hours were allocated across the five steps.
  • ⭕ Does the 'Trust but Verify' step itself scale, or does verification time grow with document size/complexity the same way manual writing effort would -- potentially eroding the claimed speed advantage as documents get larger?
    Not addressed; the piece asserts that verification becomes 'the bottleneck' but gives no data on how that bottleneck scales with document size.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe 'Trust but Verify' framing itself is a well-worn pattern already pervasive in AI-adoption writing (borrowed from the Reagan-era arms-control phrase and repeated near-verbatim across countless similar LinkedIn/AI-adoption pieces). What is genuinely more specific here is not the slogan but the mechanism underneath it: a concrete 5-stage pipeline with named, checkable tools (Prettier, markdownlint-cli2, custom static analysis, Vale with a custom dictionary, the BMAD method, @kayvan/markdown-tree-parser) and an explicit spec-to-client-doc translation cascade. A reader already saturated with 'trust but verify' framings should judge this piece on whether that tooling/mechanism is reusable, not on the slogan, which adds no new information by itself.
    Read next: Compare against other AI-adoption pieces the reader has already logged with the same 'trust but verify' framing to see whether any of them supply comparably concrete, named tooling rather than just the aphorism.
  • incompleteThere is no quantification of how the claimed 8 hours were actually allocated across the five steps -- in particular, no data on how long step 5 ('Trust but Verify', the step doing all the safety work) took relative to spec-writing, drafting, and tooling setup. Without that breakdown, a reader cannot tell whether verification was proportionate to the claimed output volume or was itself compressed to fit the '8 hours' headline -- exactly the kind of unfalsifiable single-anecdote claim that should trigger skepticism by the piece's own stated logic ('If you blindly trust... you are going to ship bad design, code, docs').
    Read next: Look for practitioner accounts of AI-assisted technical writing that report time-per-stage or word-count-per-hour broken out by drafting vs. verification, rather than a single aggregate figure.
  • illogicalThe piece's own thesis is 'never let AI guess' and 'never rely on an LLM to proofread its own work' -- yet the described QC layer is dominated by deterministic formatting/style tools and a single human 'proofing for flow' pass, with no description of independent fact-checking of the underlying technical claims/procedures against the actual system. The step that supposedly matters most (procedure validation, checking for 'odd assumptions in the code,' 'terms overlapping or in conflict with end-user language') is described impressionistically as a bullet list of questions, while the mechanical tooling steps are described with brand-name, checkable specificity. This asymmetry suggests the namesake verification step is the least rigorously engineered part of the pipeline it is meant to anchor.
    Read next: Look for accounts that specify concrete verification methods for procedure/business-logic correctness (e.g., running the documented steps against the live system, second-reviewer sign-off) rather than describing verification only as an unstructured reading pass.
  • uninformedThe framework assumes the specs themselves are correct ground truth ('as long as they are correct'), but does not address how errors introduced at the spec-writing stage -- itself sometimes AI-assisted ('extract this ground truth directly from the codebase') -- get caught, given that the human verification stage (step 5) happens downstream of specs, drafts, and tooling, and is described as targeting prose/flow/terminology issues rather than spec-accuracy issues.
    Read next: Look for material specifically on validating AI-extracted specifications against source systems, not just validating prose derived from specifications.
  • incompleteThe piece treats the shift of the bottleneck from generation to verification as a personal discovery specific to documentation, without situating it in the broader pattern -- already present elsewhere in this corpus -- that verification capacity, not implementation speed, is the new binding constraint across AI-assisted knowledge work generally (code included). The documentation-specific framing adds real value (named tools, a shard-based pipeline) but doesn't acknowledge it is one instance of a wider, already-observed shift.
    Read next: Cross-read against corpus material on spec-driven software development making the identical claim for code, to see whether the same verification-capacity bottleneck has a consistent shape (or different failure modes) across code vs. documentation.
