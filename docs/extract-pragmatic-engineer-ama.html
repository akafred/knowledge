Key terms

  • deterministic simulation testing (running the whole system in a hostile simulation)
    Running an entire system inside a fully deterministic, simulated environment designed to provoke and expose bugs, then using bug-probability analysis to 'rewind' to the point a bug was triggered.
  • LeetCode-style interviews
    A filtering mechanism that selects for candidates who tolerate pointless, grind-heavy corporate work — largely decoupled from the skills the job actually requires.
  • skill atrophy
    A deliberate, selectively-accepted tradeoff of letting a specific skill (e.g., hand-coding) degrade because AI now performs that task, while consciously protecting other skills (e.g., writing) from the same degradation by withholding AI from them.

Arguments

LeetCode-style (DSA) interviews will persist at large tech companies even though the tested skills are rarely used at work. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • DSA skills are rarely used at actual work.
  • A candidate willing to grind for weeks or months preparing for such interviews is likely someone who understands that pointless work is sometimes necessary.
  • Such candidates will probably fare better in Big Tech than someone who refuses to engage with meaningless tasks.
Implicit premises:
  • Interview formats function partly as an organizational-culture/compliance filter, not solely as a skill assessment.
  • Companies value and select for tolerance of bureaucratic or low-value work as an employee trait.
Inferences:
  • Because AI can now defeat DSA puzzles easily, the interview format alone no longer reliably signals grind-tolerance, which pushes companies toward in-person interviewing to preserve that filtering function while still keeping DSA questions.
Technology standards (like protocols) emerge somewhat coincidentally from a mix of technical merit and precarious external conditions — particularly whether the originating company is perceived as a non-threatening or dominant actor — rather than from predictable design. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • MCP was broadly adopted by OpenAI, Google, and Microsoft when Anthropic was not yet seen as the leading AI lab.
  • Google's Agent2Agent protocol was not adopted by major labs, attributed to Google's perceived dominant position.
  • Anthropic is now widely seen as the leading frontier lab.
Implicit premises:
  • Competing companies avoid ceding structural advantage or lock-in to a rival perceived as dominant, even when a technically comparable protocol is on offer.
  • Perceived relative competitive power, not protocol design quality alone, governs adoption decisions by rival companies.
Inferences:
  • A similarly strong protocol proposed today by Anthropic (now dominant) would likely meet the same structural adoption resistance that killed Agent2Agent.
One should use AI deliberately and selectively, based on which of one's own skills are worth preserving, rather than adopting a uniform AI-everywhere or AI-nowhere policy. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Orosz uses zero AI in his own newsletter writing (and disables Grammarly) specifically to prevent his writing skill from degrading.
  • Orosz uses AI when coding and accepts that his hand-coding ability will degrade as a result.
  • AI use does not intrinsically make work easier if the person is pushing themselves hard.
Implicit premises:
  • Skill atrophy is a near-certain consequence of habitually delegating a task to AI.
  • Not all skills are equally worth preserving for a given individual's goals — some tradeoffs are acceptable, others are not.
Inferences:
  • Readers should individually audit which of their own skills they most value keeping sharp, and consciously withhold AI assistance from exactly those tasks, rather than following a single blanket AI policy.

Narratives

  • The COVID-19 pandemic triggered layoffs at Uber that disbanded Orosz's team; he used the resulting disruption to exit and try to finish 'The Software Engineer's Guidebook,' then wrote three more books and discovered he preferred writing over pursuing a VC-funded startup idea.
    → Origin story establishing that Orosz's authority as a writer/publisher grew out of direct industry disruption and iterative discovery, not a deliberate media career plan — grounding the unity's claim that his stances are built from lived experience.
  • During Pragmatic Engineer's first year, Orosz had a near-final, unpublished exposé on poor working conditions at Dutch neobank Bunq, but withheld it after an engineer explained that Bunq's willingness to sponsor a hard-to-get visa had been a genuine, career-changing opportunity for them despite the company being a tough place to work.
    → Directly supports the proposition that his 'write about what works' editorial policy was adopted because of a single piece of ground-level counter-evidence — illustrating a willingness to revise a near-final conclusion when confronted with a perspective he hadn't considered.
  • After Pollen's CEO publicly dismissed Orosz's reporting on the company's layoffs as the work of a 'nobody' with an anti-Pollen agenda, Orosz investigated further and uncovered unpaid salaries, silently cancelled US health insurance, and a CTO-triggered $3.2M customer double-charge with no postmortem; he published the findings and contributed to a BBC documentary, and Pollen has since (per Orosz) filed what he calls bogus DMCA takedowns to try to remove the piece from search results.
    → Serves as the explicit exception to the 'write about what works' policy, showing accountability journalism still happens when reporting is dismissed rather than engaged with — and supports his stated conclusion that investigative journalism 'is just not for me.'

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why do LeetCode-style (DSA) interviews persist at Big Tech despite testing skills rarely used on the job?
    They function as a self-selection filter for tolerance of pointless corporate work, not as a genuine skill assessment.
  • ✅ Why did MCP succeed in becoming an industry-standard protocol while Google's Agent2Agent did not?
    MCP launched from a lab (Anthropic) not yet seen as dominant, so rivals could adopt it without fearing lock-in; Agent2Agent came from an already-dominant Google, which rivals avoided reinforcing.
  • ✅ Does using AI make engineering or writing work easier?
    Not for Orosz — he treats ease as a warning sign of insufficient effort, and instead manages a deliberate, per-skill tradeoff between AI use and skill preservation.
  • ⭕ When does Orosz override his default 'write about what works' editorial policy to publish critical/investigative reporting instead?
