Key terms

  • Operationalize DORA / DORA
    Not the four software-delivery metrics driving behavior. In this talk DORA functions as (a) an adapted internal survey instrument reusing DORA's publicly available questions and methodology, (b) a recognized brand/authority ('Google, doing this since 2014') used to manufacture urgency and end semantic debates, and (c) a benchmarking frame (low/medium/high/elite) against competitors.
  • Continuous improvement mindset
    The real, hard-to-change target of the whole program — a cultural disposition to 'get better at getting better', explicitly not any single number (responses, logged improvements). The metric counts are catalysts, not the goal.
  • DevOps Center of Expertise (CoE)
    A small (5-person) central change-management-and-consultancy team inside IT strategy and transformation whose job is to drive org-wide improvement — a change agent, not a platform or tooling team.
  • Kotter's 8-step change management
    The organizing scaffold for the entire rollout (urgency → coalition → vision → communicate → empower/remove barriers → quick wins → build on change → make it stick), with the last two singled out as where organizations usually fail.
  • FOMO / sense of urgency
    Deliberately manufactured urgency ('everyone else is already doing it — are we late?') built on DORA's brand and competitive benchmarking, explicitly not a real crisis.
  • Make it stick / anchoring
    Embedding the practice into the organization's formal control systems — company OKRs, CIO/CISO key priorities, topic-owners consuming department-level results, and 'the way we work' (business as usual) — so it persists; framed as the step organizations most often fail.
  • AI is an amplifier, not a savior
    A repeated one-line vision from the year's GenAI report: AI compounds an existing strong foundation and, absent one, increases chaos instead of performance.

Arguments

Operationalizing DORA at enterprise scale is a change-management problem best solved by running Kotter's 8 steps, not by the metrics themselves. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • The ultimate goal is a continuous-improvement mindset, not any number (responses or logged improvements).
  • The report/metrics serve as a catalyst and vehicle, not the mechanism of change.
  • The entire rollout is deliberately structured on Kotter's 8 steps.
  • The last two steps (build on the change / make it stick) are where programs fail and where the team invested most.
Implicit premises:
  • Measurement only changes behavior when it is embedded in a deliberate change program — a survey by itself does nothing.
  • A small central team can drive org-wide change if it builds a coalition that acts as a force multiplier.
  • The DORA/Google brand supplies borrowed authority that substitutes for a real crisis when manufacturing urgency.
Inferences:
  • Because the target is a culture (a mindset), and culture changes through people, coalitions, repetition, and anchoring rather than through numbers, the metrics are the vehicle and the change program is the lever.
Change becomes permanent only when it is structurally anchored (company OKRs, executive priorities, topic-owners, business-as-usual), not through advocacy alone.
Explicit premises:
  • Most change programs fail at the 'make it stick' step.
  • Sustaining requires the same annual cadence and kept promises.
  • At ABN AMRO the findings became CISO/CIO key priorities and metrics entered company OKRs.
Implicit premises:
  • A practice held up only by a champion team is a flow that decays once the team's effort stops.
  • Embedding the practice in the organization's formal control systems converts that flow into a durable stock.
Inferences:
  • Therefore the 'make it stick' step, though the hardest, is the decisive one for durability.
Being in a regulated industry is not a valid reason to forgo DORA-driven continuous improvement. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Regulations must be followed to keep the business a business.
  • DORA can still inform and drive continuous-improvement practices.
  • ABN AMRO, a highly regulated bank, did exactly this and made measurable progress.
Implicit premises:
  • Regulatory caution constrains how fast you deploy, not whether you continuously improve.
  • 'We're regulated' often functions as an excuse rather than a genuine constraint.
Inferences:
  • Therefore regulation and continuous improvement are compatible, as an existence proof shows.

Narratives

  • The ABN AMRO State-of-DevOps rollout: an official DevOps transformation started around 2019; two individuals built a proof-of-concept survey reusing DORA's public questions and got 960 responses with no marketing; in 2023 Julia joined the DevOps CoE and made it an official platform (with a Formula 1 theme); by 2025 it had been IT report of the year four consecutive years, ~4,000 reads, ~800 demo/workshop attendees, and ~5,000 logged improvements across 22 departments.
    → The central proof-of-concept-to-institutionalized-practice arc that grounds every prescriptive claim in the talk.
  • The cake quick-win: every year the department with the most survey responses is promised a €20 cake; it became an addictive chase, with teams theorizing about 'foul play', and 'will there be a cake?' becoming the single most-asked question.
    → Concrete illustration of a gamified quick win that sustains participation by making a boring survey fun.
  • The lucky executive sponsor: while the team was mapping stakeholders, a new CIO arrived from rival bank ING, was already a DORA fan with a developer background, and readily agreed to sponsor; his quote and photo were then reused in every talk to hit both senior-management and developer audiences at once.
    → Illustrates opportunistic coalition-building and the value of an executive sponsor who bridges organizational levels.
  • The internal podcast series: six episodes were produced to keep the change alive culturally, including a change-approval-process episode with developers who feel the pain, paired with a follow-up episode where a CIO and a senior division leader respond to that discussion.
    → Illustrates 'make it stick' through ongoing, engaging culture work rather than one-off communication.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Can a highly regulated bank use DORA to drive continuous improvement despite regulatory caution?
    Yes — regulations are followed (they remain a very regulated organization), but DORA still drives improvement; the team's answer was 'yes we can', demonstrated by ABN AMRO's rollout.
  • ✅ How do you drive org-wide change across 12,000 people with only a 5-person team?
    Build a multi-level coalition that talks for you (executive sponsor, ~40 DevOps coaches, a cross-role advocates group) planned via a power/interest matrix, and remove friction with reusable low-effort tooling (one-page info, ready-made emails, recaps, templates).
  • ✅ Why do change programs usually fail, and how do you make change stick?
    They fail at the last two Kotter steps (build on the change / make it stick); you make it stick by keeping the same cadence and structurally anchoring the practice in OKRs, executive priorities, and business-as-usual.
  • ✅ Does the change come from the metrics themselves?
    No — explicitly 'it's not about the numbers... it's about having the mindset'; the report is a catalyst, and the change comes from the surrounding change program.

Gaps

  • incompleteA single-organization, self-reported success narrated by the practitioner who leads it — survivorship/selection bias with no comparison organization and no counterfactual. 'Success' is measured largely by report readership and engagement (~4,000 reads, ~5,000 self-logged improvements) rather than by independently verified software-delivery performance or business outcomes.
    Read next: DORA/Accelerate outcome studies linking practices to delivery and organizational performance; controlled or multi-organization evidence on change-adoption results.
  • incompleteKotter's 8-step model is itself contested and weakly evidenced empirically, yet the talk adopts it as a given scaffold without engaging critiques or comparing alternatives (Lewin, ADKAR, Fearless Change patterns).
    Read next: Critical change-management literature on Kotter's model; Fearless Change (Manns & Rising) and other pattern-based or evidence-based change frameworks.
  • incompleteGeneralizability is unaddressed: the approach leans on advantages that may not transfer — a well-resourced, highly visible central CoE; a bridging executive sponsor who arrived by luck; and the borrowed authority of the DORA/Google brand. It is unclear how much of the playbook works in organizations lacking these.
    Read next: Change adoption in low-resource, low-visibility, or low-authority settings; how much of the outcome is attributable to the method versus the enabling conditions.
  • incompleteThin evidence for the durability claim itself. 'Make it stick' is asserted (OKRs, report-of-the-year four years running) but four years of sustained report engagement is not the same as a proven, self-sustaining continuous-improvement culture that would persist if the driving team were removed.
    Read next: Longitudinal studies of change persistence after a champion or program team departs; measures of institutionalization beyond engagement metrics.
