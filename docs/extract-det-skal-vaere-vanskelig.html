Det skal være vanskelig

Erik Børve Rasmussen · article · 2026-07-10 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

AI Labor DisruptionCognitive SurrenderDesirable Difficulty & Productive StruggleIronies of AutomationLearning Culture & SlackMental Models & UnderstandingTaste & JudgmentWriting as Thinking
Difficulty is the mechanism of learning, not a defect in it, so AI — a machine for removing difficulty — destroys learning wherever the process rather than the product was the whole point (school, unlike work); the remedy is therefore to redesign AI and assessment as skill-forcing 'simulators' rather than to ban them.

practical · argumentative op-ed (NRK Ytring kronikk); normative and prescriptive — A persuasive practical argument about what education should do with AI. Its engine is analogy: it imports Lisanne Bainbridge's 'Ironies of Automation' (1983) and the aviation/nuclear-control-room deskilling literature and maps it onto schooling. Bylined to Erik Børve Rasmussen, but the text self-describes as co-authored ('skriver kronikkforfatterne').

Key terms

  • ønskelige vanskeligheter (desirable difficulties) Desirable Difficulty & Productive Struggle
    Robert Bjork's principle, borrowed as the article's fulcrum: the effortful resistance in learning is not a flaw to be engineered away but the very mechanism by which learning happens.
  • autopilot vs. simulator
    The article's central design distinction (from Bainbridge): an autopilot does the job for you and deskills; a simulator forces you to do the job yourself, with feedback, and builds skill. The same AI model can be either, depending on how it is deployed.
  • stigen (the ladder)
    The sequence of deliberately-unnecessary tasks whose only value is the capability their steps build. At work AI eats the lower rungs juniors climbed; in education the whole institution is the ladder, so automating the steps leaves no ladder standing.
  • biprodukt / produkt (by-product / product)
    An inversion: the essay text is the by-product; the real product of schoolwork is the change in the writer's mind. AI lets students keep the by-product (text) and discard the product (the thinking).
  • Ironies of Automation (Bainbridge) Ironies of Automation
    The organizing frame: the paradox that the better automation gets, the more crucial and difficult the human role becomes, while the very skills the human needs atrophy from disuse.
  • systemansvar (system responsibility)
    The claim that whether a student meets the autopilot or the simulator cannot be left to a fifteen-year-old's willpower; it must be engineered at the system level through incentives, assessment forms that require visible thinking, and tools built as simulators.

  • explicitBainbridge's 'Ironies of Automation' (1983) is the governing precedent: the better the automation, the more crucial and difficult the human's role becomes, and unused skills atrophy — as Air France 447 showed 26 years after she described it.conf: high

Arguments

In education AI-as-autopilot destroys the learning it appears to assist, so the remedy is to redesign AI and assessment as skill-forcing simulators, not to ban AI. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Desirable difficulties (Bjork): the effort in learning is the mechanism, not a defect.
  • AI is almost by definition a machine for removing effort.
  • In school the text was never the point; the point was the change in the writer's mind.
  • A ban removes the autopilot but does not build the simulator.
Implicit premises:
  • The process, not the product, is the locus of learning.
  • Ease is pedagogically corrosive when the goal is building the mind.
  • The value of a school task is entirely instrumental to the cognitive change it produces, so keeping the text while losing the thinking is a total loss.
Inferences:
  • If effort is the mechanism and AI removes effort, AI removes the learning.
  • If the process is the point, a tool that preserves the output but eliminates the process is worse in school than at work.
Delegating thinking to AI is self-undermining, because the competence needed to judge AI's output erodes precisely by being unused — and in students it never forms at all. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Skills atrophy when not used (Bainbridge); to evaluate the machine you need the very competence that atrophies while you watch.
  • Those who best judged AI's answers were those who had done the tasks manually for years.
  • Greater trust in AI correlates with less critical thinking.
Implicit premises:
  • Judgment is tacit and can only be built by doing the work, not by observing outputs.
  • There is no feedback signal for eroded judgment, unlike the physical cue of a wobbling plane.
Inferences:
  • Monitoring-without-doing cannot maintain the skill required to monitor.
  • A student in whom the skill never forms is worse off than an adult in whom an existing skill decays.
Automating school tasks is categorically worse than automating work tasks, because education is the ladder that builds society's judgment. strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • At work AI eats the first drafts and routine cases that were the juniors' ladder.
  • In education every task is deliberately something the world does not need done — it consists only of steps.
  • Today's judgment was built in a school without AI.
Implicit premises:
  • The stock of societal judgment is replenished only through the training flow that education provides.
  • Deplete that flow and the next generation has nothing with which to build its judgment.
Inferences:
  • Automate the rungs and the ladder does not remain.

Narratives

  • Air France 447 crashed into the Atlantic in 2009; the pilots had thousands of flight hours, almost all on autopilot, and when the automation disengaged they lacked the hand-skill to fly the aircraft manually.
    → Concrete, high-stakes proof that skills atrophy under automation — the empirical anchor for the whole deskilling frame, made sharper by the claim that Bainbridge had described the accident 26 years before it happened.
  • The author ran a course on AI for experienced professionals in spring 2026; those who best managed to evaluate the AI's answers were the ones who had done the tasks manually for years.
    → Firsthand evidence for the claim that the capacity to judge AI is built only by having done the work — links the aviation analogy to knowledge work.
  • In 1977, engineers at British nuclear plants proposed that the most important process values be shown on directly-wired, analog instruments — a channel that cannot be falsified.
    → Analogy that defines what a school exam is: an unfalsifiable measurement channel, justifying the call for controlled, unassisted exams.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Should AI be banned from school?
    No. A ban removes the autopilot but does not build the simulator; the answer is to redesign AI tools and assessment so they force visible, unassisted thinking.
  • ✅ Why is AI in school categorically different from AI at work?
    At work the output is a real product and skill atrophy is a side effect; in school the output was never the point, so AI discards the actual product — the learning — and keeps only the by-product.
  • ✅ How can school capture AI's genuine upside (scalable one-to-one tutoring) without its downside (deskilling)?
    Build AI as a simulator rather than an autopilot and make it a system responsibility via incentives, visible-thinking assessment, and shielded unassisted work.
  • ⭕ Which difficulties are 'desirable' (they build the mind) and which are mere friction or busywork?
    Raised implicitly by leaning on Bjork's 'desirable difficulties' but never operationalized — effort is treated as monolithic.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe simulator prescription is not shown to be incentive-compatible at scale. The author concedes student incentives point to the autopilot and calls the fix a 'system responsibility,' but never specifies how a system enforces simulator-use or prevents a 'simulator' (a tool that questions your draft, adapts to your mistakes) from being gamed back into a friendlier autopilot.
    Read next: Mechanism-design and assessment-security literature; evaluations of AI tutoring systems (e.g. Khanmigo) that resist gaming; Bjork on how to structure desirable difficulties students will not circumvent.
  • incomplete'Desirable difficulty' is asserted but not operationalized. Effort is treated as monolithic, so the argument cannot distinguish difficulties that build the mind (spacing, retrieval, generation) from mere friction, toil, or busywork — not all struggle is generative, and some ease is legitimately good pedagogy.
    Read next: Bjork & Bjork on which specific difficulties are desirable; Ericsson on deliberate practice vs. rote effort; cognitive-load theory on when friction helps vs. overwhelms.
  • incompleteThe empirical base is thin for a strong, society-wide generational claim: one 319-worker Microsoft/CMU study (a correlation between AI trust and critical thinking, read as causal) plus the author's own course anecdote.
    Read next: Longitudinal studies of learning outcomes under AI-assisted vs. traditional instruction; the Dreyfus skill-acquisition model; replication of the Microsoft/CMU finding and its direction of causation.
  • incompleteThe argument is pinned to Norwegian policy specifics (the government's ban, the Malthe-Sørenssen committee) while advancing a domain-general claim about how minds learn; it does not test whether its prescriptions travel beyond the Norwegian school/university system, nor engage equity between schools that can and cannot build 'simulators.'
    Read next: Comparative ed-tech policy; UNESCO 2023 'Technology in education: A tool on whose terms?'; equity-of-access critiques of school AI infrastructure.
