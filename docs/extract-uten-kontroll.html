Uten kontroll

Johanne Refseth · article · 2026-07-04 Reader 🖥️

Attention as a Hijackable ResourceCognitive SurrenderAgency
Compulsive social-media use is a genuine, design-engineered addiction — not a failure of willpower — that can capture even a self-aware, addiction-experienced adult, invert the tool relationship until the user serves the device, and degrade attention and cognition; it is reclaimed not by resolve but by deliberately engineering friction into one's environment, which is why the right question about the young is not 'how much screen time' but 'is the use voluntary or an addiction,' and why the real stakes are keeping the next generation from starting life already hooked.

practical · first-person confessional narrative carrying an implicit polemic (NRK Ytring kronikk) — mixed: personal testimony that argues by showing — Primarily practical: it aims at action (reclaim control; refocus prevention on the next generation) and carries a policy-adjacent prescription about how research should be framed. A theoretical sub-claim rides underneath — a construct-validity argument about what screen-time studies actually measure. The argument is never stated abstractly; the descent-and-recovery story is the argument.

Key terms

  • avrusning (detox / rehab)
    The clinical language of substance detoxification, applied to social media — signalling that getting off the platform required something like withdrawal management, not mere resolve. Carried in the piece's framing that she 'måtte på avrusning'.
  • uten kontroll (out of control / without control)
    Loss of agency over one's own behaviour — the inability to govern amount, time and stopping — reframed as the defining feature of the problem (as opposed to time-on-screen)
  • hekta / avhengig (hooked / addicted)
    Genuine clinical addiction, evidenced against Modum Bad's six criteria — deliberately not a loose metaphor for 'I use it a lot'
  • redskap (tool / instrument)
    The pivot of the whole piece: 'Det var ikke et redskap for meg lenger, jeg var et redskap for den' — the technology was no longer an instrument she used but an agent that used her; agency inverted
  • de korte videoene (the short videos)
    Short-form video feeds (TikTok, Reels) singled out as the specific design form that tipped benign use into compulsion — the moment she 'felt it' and knew to stay away

Arguments

Compulsive social-media use is a genuine addiction requiring structural intervention, not a failure of willpower to be scolded away. strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • She meets 'frighteningly many' of Modum Bad's six addiction signs.
  • She had successfully quit gambling and video games by simply stopping, but that strategy failed with the smartphone.
  • Only re-engineering her environment (a separate phone, a 10-minute auto-shutoff, a 10-opens/day cap) restored function; resolve and leaving the phone at home did not.
  • The device is inescapable because it hosts indispensable infrastructure (payments, ID, transit) and her livelihood (Instagram).
Implicit premises:
  • KEY: process/behavioural addiction is equivalent in kind to substance addiction — Modum Bad's substance-addiction criteria and the cocaine analogy transfer to a phone without further argument, and 'social media addiction' is a real clinical category on par with gambling disorder.
  • Her single, self-diagnosed case generalises to 'the young' and to a whole generation.
  • A short, self-reported window of recovery is sufficient evidence that the structural intervention works.
Inferences:
  • If design engineers the addiction, the primary leverage is at the platform/regulatory level — yet she assigns the entire remedy to individuals (her own hack) and to parents, never to platform design or policy.
  • If the phone is indispensable infrastructure, then 'just carry a second phone' is a privileged workaround unavailable at population scale — undercutting the individual-remedy framing she implicitly adopts.
  • Her own success is evidence that friction beats willpower for her; she does not ask whether people without her self-awareness, income or motivation could deploy the same protocol.
Studies finding no link between youth screen time and mental health are measuring the wrong thing; the question should be voluntariness versus addiction.
Explicit premises:
  • The FHI 2026 study found no association between children's screen time and later mental-health problems.
  • You cannot assess a drug's effect by asking a currently-high addict how he feels (cocaine analogy).
  • In her own case there was no depression, yet a genuine loss of control that 'took enormous space' in her head.
Implicit premises:
  • Loss of control (addiction) is itself a harm worth measuring, independent of psychiatric symptoms.
  • The 'voluntary vs. addicted' distinction can be operationalised and measured at population scale.
Inferences:
  • She proposes a better construct but supplies no instrument for it — how one measures 'voluntary vs. addiction' at scale is left open.
  • Reframing a null result post hoc around a new, unmeasured construct risks becoming unfalsifiable, a move she does not guard against.

Narratives

  • The two-phone protocol: social media now lives on a separate device carrying an app that shuts Instagram off after ten minutes and permits only ten opens per day; the result was a 'frightening' recovery of concentration, focus and memory, and she finally finished the pile of Helge Ingstad books that had sat unread for seven years.
    → The centerpiece: lived proof that structural design, not willpower, restores agency and cognition — the empirical payload for the design-over-willpower and attention-as-resource propositions.
  • The addiction-history vignette: she quit slot machines at 13 after realising she'd lost 200 kroner ('a fortune then') and never fed one again, and abandoned video games after a Warcraft 2 binge in 1997.
    → Certifies her as strong-willed and capable of quitting, so the smartphone's defeat of her cannot be attributed to weak character — the design-over-willpower claim by elimination.
  • The reading collapse: before the smartphone she read a novel every week; when she looked at the addiction checklist she had read only a few books over several years.
    → Quantifies the opportunity cost of captured attention and makes the abstract 'attention as depletable resource' concrete and measurable.
  • The organised-activities vignette: researchers note today's children have too many scheduled activities and little free play; a reason parents give is that they must actively occupy children to keep them away from screens and the constant 'mas' to look at one.
    → Extends her n=1 into a generational pattern and reframes ordinary parenting friction as possible withdrawal management — evidence for the addiction-not-choice reading of youth screen use.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Is heavy social-media use a matter of free choice or of addiction?
    For her, largely addiction — she meets the clinical criteria and could not stop by will. She extends this to the young as a strong suspicion, not a demonstrated fact.
  • ✅ Why did the 2026 study find no link between children's screen time and later mental-health problems?
    Her answer: it measured the wrong construct (symptoms, not loss of control). This is her reframing, offered as more plausible, not as settled science.
  • ✅ How does one actually reduce a social-media addiction?
    Structurally: build friction into the environment (separate device, hard app limits) and fill the vacated time with something new — substitution over abstinence.
  • ⭕ Does the recovery last — is the person cured?
    Explicitly open: she believes the susceptibility is lifelong and relapse is close; the fix manages but does not cure.
  • ⭕ If engineered design causes the addiction, why is the remedy located entirely in individuals and parents rather than in platform design or regulation?
    Not addressed — she diagnoses a systemic cause but prescribes only personal and parental workarounds.
  • ⭕ Is 'social media addiction' a validated clinical category equivalent to substance or gambling addiction?
    Assumed throughout (via Modum Bad's substance criteria and the cocaine analogy) but never examined.

Gaps

  • uninformedThe load-bearing premise — that process/behavioural addiction is equivalent in kind to substance addiction — is asserted but never defended. She imports Modum Bad's substance-addiction six signs and a cocaine analogy onto a phone without noting that 'social media addiction' is not an established clinical diagnosis the way gambling disorder is (gambling disorder is in DSM-5/ICD-11; gaming disorder only recently and contestedly; social-media 'addiction' not at all). The category transfer that the whole argument rides on is treated as obvious.
    Read next: DSM-5 / ICD-11 status of behavioural/process addictions; the debate over pathologising heavy engagement (Billieux et al. on over-diagnosis; Andreassen's Bergen Social Media Addiction Scale as a proposed instrument and its critics); whether compulsive use is best modelled as addiction, habit, or coping.
  • incompleteThe entire argument rests on a single self-diagnosed case, yet she generalises explicitly to 'the young' and to a whole generation. She concedes she is unusually addiction-prone, which makes her a poor base-rate for the population she extrapolates to; the organised-activities anecdote is a second data point, not evidence of prevalence.
    Read next: Prevalence studies of problematic/compulsive social-media use among youth; base rates of behavioural addiction; whether her addiction-prone profile is representative or a tail case.
  • incompleteThe design-causes-the-compulsion thesis (and its corollary that structure beats willpower) is inferred from one reversal, not demonstrated. She names 'de korte videoene' and the phone's indispensability as the cause but gives no mechanism (variable-ratio reward, algorithmic ranking, autoplay, infinite scroll) and no evidence that design rather than her disposition is doing the work.
    Read next: Attention-economy and persuasive-design literature — Hari 'Stolen Focus', Chris Bailey on attention as a managed resource, Tristan Harris / Center for Humane Technology, and especially Natasha Dow Schull 'Addiction by Design' on variable-ratio machine design as the closest analogue to feed mechanics.
  • incompleteThe durability of the fix is unexamined. She concedes she is not cured and relapse is close, but the two-phone protocol is validated only by a short, self-reported window; there is no evidence it holds over time, generalises to people without her motivation or income, or survives the temptation to uninstall the limiter.
    Read next: Relapse/maintenance literature for behavioural addictions; longitudinal evaluations of digital self-control tools (do app-limiters durably change behaviour or get bypassed?); the second-phone tactic as a privileged workaround vs. a scalable one.
  • incompleteThe proposed better research question — 'voluntary or an addiction?' — comes with no operationalisation. She does not say how the voluntary/addicted distinction would be measured at population scale, and reframing a null result post hoc around a new, unmeasured construct risks unfalsifiability (any null becomes 'you measured the wrong thing').
    Read next: Construct-validity methodology; validated behavioural-addiction screening instruments and their psychometrics; whether 'loss of control' can be measured without circularity at scale.
  • incompleteLocus-of-remedy mismatch: she diagnoses engineered design as the cause but places the entire remedy on individuals (her own hack) and on parents, never on platform design, defaults, or regulation. If capture is engineered, individual friction is treating a systemic cause with a personal workaround — and one that presumes you can afford and coordinate a second device.
    Read next: Regulatory and design-level approaches: EU Digital Services Act provisions on addictive design, age-verification and 'addictive feed' bans (New York, California), and the live Norwegian debate over an aldersgrense for social media; systems-thinking framing of where leverage actually sits when the cause is structural.
