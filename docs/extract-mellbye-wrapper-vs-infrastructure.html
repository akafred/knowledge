Moats & DefensibilityRiding Model Improvements
AI companies must determine early whether they are building durable infrastructure — characterized by switching costs, customer unlearning, and accumulated institutional knowledge — or mere wrappers that platform providers will absorb in their next release.

practical · argumentative — A practical argument aimed at AI company builders, providing a diagnostic framework (the 'infrastructure test') to evaluate whether their product is durable or a vulnerable wrapper. Uses concrete examples (Chegg, Consigli, Strise, Figma) to support each point.

Key terms

  • Wrapper (wrapper) Moats & Defensibility
    A company that has not built anything proprietary, merely layering on top of a platform it does not own — vulnerable to absorption by the platform provider.
  • Infrastructure test (infrastrukturtest) Moats & Defensibility
    A four-question diagnostic to determine whether a product is durable infrastructure or a vulnerable wrapper.
  • Unlearning (avlæring) Moats & Defensibility
    The process by which a customer loses the internal capability to perform a function themselves, making them dependent on the provider regardless of switching costs.
  • Switching costs (byttekostnader, implied) Moats & Defensibility
    The practical effort required for a customer to replace one solution with another — necessary but not sufficient for durability.
  • Institutional knowledge (institusjonell kunnskap) Moats & Defensibility
    Organization-specific accumulated decisions, risk assessments, and compliance history that belong to the customer and cannot be transferred to a shared model.

Arguments

AI companies must determine early whether they are infrastructure or wrappers, because platform absorption is inevitable and fast. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Most AI companies start as wrappers on platforms they don't own.
  • Platform providers will absorb wrapper functionality in future releases.
  • The speed of displacement has accelerated from quarters to releases with LLMs.
Implicit premises:
  • Being absorbed by a platform means the wrapper company loses its reason to exist.
  • Early self-awareness about wrapper status allows a company to pivot toward infrastructure.
Inferences:
  • The four-question infrastructure test should be applied as early as possible in a company's lifecycle.
Customer unlearning is a more durable moat than switching costs. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • A customer with high switching costs can still switch.
  • A customer who has unlearned has lost the ability to choose — independent of whether they know it.
Implicit premises:
  • Switching costs are quantifiable and can be overcome with sufficient motivation or a sufficiently better alternative.
  • Unlearning is a cognitive/organizational change that is qualitatively different from a cost barrier.
Inferences:
  • Question 2 ('Has the customer stopped doing this job themselves?') is the most important of the four infrastructure questions.
Chegg was destroyed because it was a wrapper, not infrastructure. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Chegg had 19 million customers.
  • When ChatGPT launched, Chegg's stock fell ~48% in one day.
  • Chegg's core offering suddenly existed elsewhere — cheaper, faster, simpler.
  • Chegg itself warned investors about AI pressure.
Implicit premises:
  • Chegg's homework help did not create customer unlearning — students could easily switch to ChatGPT.
  • Chegg had not accumulated irreplaceable institutional knowledge.
Inferences:
  • Chegg would have failed all four infrastructure-test questions.

Narratives

  • Chegg had 19 million customers but lost 48% of its stock value in a single day when ChatGPT offered equivalent homework help cheaper and faster.
    → Cautionary tale illustrating the existential risk of being a wrapper.
  • Strise accumulates bank-specific compliance decisions, risk assessments, and resolved cases over time, creating institutional knowledge that belongs to the bank alone. Switching would mean starting over while the regulator watches.
    → Positive example of infrastructure: demonstrates unlearning and accumulated institutional knowledge as moats.
  • Figma's stock fell 7% when Claude Design launched, revealing market uncertainty about whether Figma is infrastructure or wrapper.
    → Illustrates the ambiguity of the wrapper/infrastructure distinction even for mature companies.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How can an AI company determine whether it is building something durable or merely a wrapper?
    Apply the four-question infrastructure test: switching costs, customer unlearning, usage-driven improvement, and free-feature risk.
  • ✅ Why are AI wrappers more vulnerable now than analogous companies were in previous technology waves?
    LLMs compress the displacement timeline from quarterly earnings cycles to software release cycles.
  • ✅ Which of the four infrastructure questions matters most?
    Question 2 (customer unlearning) — it describes an irreversible change in the customer, not just a cost barrier.
  • ✅ What does it look like when an AI company is genuinely infrastructure?
    Examples: Consigli (passes all four) and Strise (accumulates bank-specific institutional knowledge).

Gaps

  • incompleteThe four-question framework is presented without evidence of its predictive validity. It is asserted as useful but not tested against historical cases beyond the selected examples.
    Read next: Compare with established frameworks for competitive moats (e.g., Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers, or Christensen's disruption theory) to evaluate whether the four questions add genuine diagnostic power.
  • incompleteThe post does not address the possibility that wrappers can deliberately evolve into infrastructure — the transition path is left unexplored. The binary framing (wrapper vs infrastructure) may be too rigid.
    Read next: Look for case studies of companies that started as wrappers and successfully built infrastructure moats over time.
  • uninformedThe Figma example conflates stock-price reaction with actual strategic vulnerability. A 7% drop on a competitor announcement is not evidence that Figma is a wrapper; it may simply reflect short-term market uncertainty.
    Read next: Examine Figma's actual switching costs, network effects, and design-system integrations to make an independent assessment.
  • incompleteThe concept of 'unlearning' is introduced as the strongest moat but is not analyzed for its ethical implications — is it desirable for vendors to engineer customer dependency through capability atrophy?
    Read next: Consider the ethics of vendor lock-in through deliberate or incidental deskilling of customer organizations.
