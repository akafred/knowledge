The mechanical, judgment-free first phase of incident response — SSHing in, pulling logs from multiple sources, scanning for known failure patterns, and mapping them to the Four Golden Signals — is repetitive search rather than debugging, so it can be automated into a deliberately read-only, locally-run CLI that hands the responder a structured incident brief in under a minute, reserving the actual diagnosis and any state-changing action for the human.

practical · build log / how-to — a first-person 'how I built it' walkthrough of an open-source CLI (incopilot): architecture, module pipeline, setup commands, and design-decision rationale, ending with a roadmap — A ~820-word single-practitioner build log for an open-source Python tool that automates first-pass incident triage. It is prescriptive and rationale-driven ('why Python', 'why read-only', 'why local'), grounded in the author's own on-call experience rather than in measured outcomes — the test of every claim is 'does this get you to diagnosis faster', asserted, not demonstrated. The 'AI' framing is largely aspirational: the shipped version is deterministic pattern-matching, with LLM summarization named only as a future roadmap item.

Key terms

  • incident copilot (incopilot)
    An open-source Python CLI that automates the entire first-pass triage of a production incident — collecting logs, detecting failure patterns, and generating an incident brief — so the engineer skips the manual search and goes straight to problem-solving. 'Copilot' here means an assistant that prepares context, not one that flies the plane: it never diagnoses or acts.
  • the first 15 minutes
    The opening phase of every incident, before real diagnosis begins — a 15-to-45-minute manual loop of pulling logs and guessing what to check. The author's key move is to classify this phase as searching, not debugging: repetitive, low-judgment work that is 'largely automatable'.
  • read-only (design constraint)
    The deliberate rule that the tool never writes to the system, restarts services, or executes state-changing commands — enforced via a config-level safe-command list. Framed as a feature, not a limitation: it makes the tool safe to run during an active incident and usable by any on-call engineer regardless of system familiarity, because it 'cannot make a bad incident worse'.
  • collectors / analyzer / reporter (the module pipeline)
    The three-stage coined pipeline: collectors pull logs from systemd journal, Docker containers, or raw files; the analyzer detects high-signal events (OOM kills, 5xx spikes, disk exhaustion, network timeouts, service restarts) and normalizes lines; the reporter maps findings to the Four Golden Signals and emits a structured brief in markdown and JSON.
  • Four Golden Signals (latency, traffic, errors, saturation)
    The Google-SRE monitoring taxonomy the reporter maps every detected failure pattern onto, giving the brief a familiar, structured shape rather than a raw log dump.

Propositions

  • explicitThe first phase of any incident is not debugging but searching — you don't yet know what broke, which service is responsible, or where to look — and this search work is repetitive, stressful, low-judgment, and 'largely automatable', costing 15-45 minutes per incident and thousands of engineering hours per year across teams.conf: high
  • explicitdevelopedThe tool is designed to sit strictly on the data-gathering side of the human/automation boundary: it collects logs, detects high-signal patterns, maps them to the Four Golden Signals, lists the specific triggering log lines, and assembles a structured brief — and is deliberately read-only, never writing, restarting, or executing state-changing commands — so the responder's diagnosis and every corrective action remain entirely human.conf: high
    supportsPresentation: The Ironies of A^2 I^2 (Ironies of Automation and Artificial Intelligence in Incidents) · p16 — Reed's human-factors finding is that AI used as explanation-only / data-gathering / attention-redirection outperforms AI used to make recommendations, especially as task complexity rises. incopilot's design is a concrete instance of that prescribed pole: it is read-only, it assembles evidence and redirects attention (which log lines fired, which Golden Signal), and it never prescribes an action or takes one — diagnosis and remediation stay human. The tool's boundary placement is exactly what Reed argues is the safe way to put automation into incidents.
    refines11 Tips For AI Coding With Ralph Wiggum · p14 — Pocock's Ralph technique presupposes that a feedback loop plus explicit scope can SUBSTITUTE for human judgment on a bounded task, relocating the human to up-front scoping and after-the-fact verification. incopilot draws the human/automation boundary more conservatively: rather than substituting for judgment, it places the boundary BELOW judgment entirely — automating only the mechanical, pre-diagnostic search, structurally forbidding the tool from acting (read-only), and leaving both the diagnosis and every action with the human. Same underlying design question (where does automation stop and human judgment begin), a stricter and safer answer for a high-consequence, non-deterministic context.

Arguments

The mechanical first-pass of production-incident triage should be automated into a read-only, locally-run CLI so responders start from a structured brief and reserve their judgment for diagnosis and action.
Explicit premises:
  • The first phase of an incident is searching, not debugging, and this search is repetitive, low-judgment, and largely automatable.
  • Logs contain the answer almost every time; the only problem is manually finding where it is buried, which an automated tool can do in under 60 seconds.
  • Pattern recognition across large volumes of text, rapid signal extraction, and structured summarization are exactly what automation/AI handles well.
  • A read-only tool cannot make a bad incident worse, so it is safe to run during an active incident and by any on-call engineer regardless of system familiarity.
  • Local execution with no external API calls keeps the tool usable in data-residency-constrained and security-sensitive environments.
Implicit premises:
  • The early-triage 'search' work is genuinely low-judgment and cleanly separable from diagnosis, so automating it is safe and does not degrade the human's later reasoning.
  • Faster context assembly reliably improves incident outcomes — a brief beats a blank page even though nothing measures resolution time.
  • The tool's pattern-detection is accurate enough that surfacing 'likely causes' helps rather than misleads, i.e. its false-positive cost is low enough to ignore.
Inferences:
  • If the costly early minutes are mechanical search and automation does search well, then automating that phase recovers the time and cognitive load without touching the judgment-heavy part.
  • If the tool is read-only and local, then the two obvious objections to automating incident work — making things worse, and leaking data — are structurally defused, so the automation is safe to adopt.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why are the first 15 minutes of an incident the hardest, and why are they automatable?
    Because that phase is searching, not debugging — you don't yet know what broke or where to look — and search across large volumes of logs (pattern recognition, signal extraction, structured summarization) is exactly the kind of work automation handles well.
  • ✅ How do you make an automated tool safe to run during an active incident and usable by any on-call engineer?
    Make it deliberately read-only (never write, restart, or execute state-changing commands, enforced by a safe-command list) and run it locally with no external API calls — so it cannot make a bad incident worse and cannot leak data.
  • ⭕ Does automating the first-pass triage actually reduce mean-time-to-resolution or improve incident outcomes?
    Unaddressed. The piece quantifies only the toil removed (15-45 minutes of search, thousands of engineering hours) and asserts responders 'skip straight to actual problem-solving', but offers no measurement that a faster brief shortens resolution or improves outcomes.
  • ⭕ How often does the pattern detection surface the wrong likely cause, and what does anchoring on a misleading brief cost?
    Unaddressed. There is no accuracy, precision, or false-positive analysis, and no discussion of the risk that a confident-looking but wrong brief steers a responder in the wrong direction.

Gaps

  • incompleteNo production evidence that the tool reduces MTTR or improves incident outcomes. The entire benefit case rests on toil removed (15-45 minutes of manual search, 'thousands of engineering hours lost per year') plus the assertion that responders can then 'skip straight to actual problem-solving' — but time-to-brief is not time-to-resolution, and nothing here is a controlled before/after or a field measurement. For a rigorous adopter this is the first missing thing: a build log's plausible mechanism, not demonstrated impact.

  • incompleteNo evaluation or false-positive analysis of the pattern detection. The tool surfaces 'likely causes' and 'what the evidence suggests', but there is no accuracy/precision/recall figure, no false-positive-rate discussion, and no account of the downside case — a fast, confident, but wrong brief. This matters acutely for incident automation specifically: the human-factors literature finds the risk is asymmetric — automation that surfaces misleading patterns can make outcomes far worse than no automation, and responders cannot detect in real time which regime they are in — precisely the analysis a single-practitioner build log omits.
    Read next: Read J. Paul Reed on the ironies of automation and AI in incidents (the asymmetric-risk / nurses-study finding), plus eval methodology for automated triage/alerting: precision/recall, false-positive cost, and joint human-AI testing rather than testing the detector in isolation.
  • incompleteThe deskilling and automation-complacency risk is unexamined. Automating the first-pass search is exactly the routine practice that builds the diagnostic intuition responders need when the tool is wrong, absent, or facing a novel failure; a read-only brief that confidently names likely causes can also anchor a responder on the tool's hypothesis. The piece treats the search/diagnosis boundary as clean and cost-free, never asking what erodes when the mechanical part is permanently delegated.
