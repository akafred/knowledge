Assumptions as code: SiriusXM's approach to platform prioritization

DX · video · 2026-04-10 source 🖥️

Decision-Making PracticesNon-Engineers BuildingOrganizational LegibilityPlatform Engineering
SiriusXM's platform engineering team built a weighted, multi-factor prioritization framework (evolved from RICE) that makes trade-offs explicit through numeric weights on impact factors, and then extended it with an 'assumptions as code' system that stores organizational assumptions in a Git repository so AI can surface conflicts, staleness, and invisibility at the moment of decision-making.

practical · panel discussion / podcast episode — A DX Engineering Enablement podcast featuring Eleanor Milman (Senior Staff PM, Platform Engineering) and Mina Towadras (Associate Director, Platform Engineering) at SiriusXM, hosted by Justin Rio. The discussion covers a concrete prioritization framework evolved from RICE, an 'assumptions as code' system, and the expanding builder persona.

Key terms

  • assumptions as code Organizational Legibility
    Organizational assumptions (about users, usage patterns, incident frequency, etc.) stored in a Git repository as structured data, version-controlled and queryable by AI, so they can be validated, updated, and surfaced during planning.
  • faulty states (of assumptions)
    Three failure modes: conflicting (two teams hold contradictory assumptions), invisible (a team does not know an assumption exists elsewhere), and stale (an assumption was once valid but is no longer).
  • impact factors
    The decomposed dimensions of value used in the prioritization formula: developer speed, runtime reliability, cost reduction, security/compliance, data-drivenness, platform efficiency, user trust. Each is independently weighted.
  • weights
    Numeric percentages assigned to each impact factor by leadership, reflecting quarterly strategic priorities. Changing a weight immediately re-ranks the project backlog.
  • builder persona Non-Engineers Building
    Non-developer individuals (marketing strategists, product managers, etc.) who build software artifacts using AI tools and low-code approaches, expanding the user base of platform engineering beyond traditional software developers.
  • twice removed from revenue
    Platform engineering builds tools for developers who build products that generate revenue -- two degrees of separation from the bottom line, making direct revenue-based prioritization impossible.

Arguments

Platform engineering needs a custom prioritization framework, not off-the-shelf RICE. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • RICE lacks urgency, which platform teams need for hard deadlines (vendor contracts, company launches).
  • RICE's single 'impact' dimension is too coarse for platform engineering's multi-stakeholder value.
  • Platform engineering is twice removed from revenue, so standard product metrics fail.
Implicit premises:
  • A prioritization framework must reflect the actual dimensions along which a team creates value.
  • Internal platform teams face fundamentally different constraints than customer-facing product teams.
Inferences:
  • Therefore, impact must be decomposed into multiple independently weighted factors.
  • Therefore, urgency must be an explicit input, not smuggled in through override mechanisms.
Codifying assumptions in a repository and surfacing them via AI improves decision quality at scale. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Assumptions can be conflicting, invisible, or stale -- all leading to poor decisions.
  • Manual research of assumptions is too expensive for smaller initiatives.
  • AI can cheaply surface relevant prior assumptions at the moment of planning.
Implicit premises:
  • Most organizations have no systematic way to track the assumptions behind past decisions.
  • The cost of a wrong assumption increases with organizational scale.
  • Assumptions degrade over time without active maintenance.
Inferences:
  • Therefore, storing assumptions as structured, version-controlled data enables AI-assisted recall.
  • Therefore, the human-AI split should be: AI handles recall, humans handle judgment and validation.
Platform engineering must prepare for the 'builder' persona as a first-class user. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • GitHub saw 25% YoY user growth, which is unlikely to be entirely new professional developers.
  • Non-developers are already building software using AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT-assisted Python apps).
  • These builders will proceed regardless of whether platform engineering supports them.
Implicit premises:
  • Unsupported builders create security, maintainability, and supportability risks.
  • It is better to provide guardrails than to try to prevent non-developers from building.
Inferences:
  • Therefore, platform teams need to understand builder needs (scale, data access, security) and provide appropriate golden paths.
  • Therefore, documentation and AI-readable skills become critical infrastructure.

Narratives

  • Eleanor was the first product manager hired into platform engineering by Jared Winsky. Engineering counterparts were doing great prioritization at the team level, but it did not scale across five teams and 700+ developers. They tried RICE, it felt wrong immediately, and they iterated over three years to the current framework.
    → Origin story establishing why a novel framework was necessary and how it evolved organically rather than being designed top-down.
  • Eleanor and another PM attempted to prioritize a delivery team's full backlog of smaller tasks but abandoned the effort because it was too laborious to research assumptions for each item. This failure motivated the AI-assisted assumptions-as-code approach.
    → Demonstrates the scaling problem that assumptions-as-code solves -- manual assumption research does not scale to smaller work items.
  • Mina and Eleanor met at Pivotal Labs, whose values were 'do the right thing, do what works, and be kind.' This informs the three-mode design: deep-dive (do the right thing), standard (balanced), and rapid (do what works).
    → Cultural anchor connecting the pragmatic design philosophy to shared professional history.
  • A non-developer who was one of the highest users of their chatbot was discovered to be building a Python app by asking ChatGPT questions, with no coding background.
    → Concrete example illustrating the emergence of the builder persona and why platform engineering must adapt.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How should platform engineering prioritize when it cannot use revenue as a metric?
    Decompose impact into multiple weighted factors (developer speed, runtime reliability, cost reduction, security/compliance, data-drivenness, efficiency, user trust) and let leadership tune weights quarterly based on strategic signals.
  • ✅ How do you maintain alignment with leadership while preserving team autonomy?
    The framework serves as a shared contract: leadership sets weights (strategic direction), teams rate projects within those weights (tactical autonomy). Data (e.g., high quality scores) provides empirical backing for decisions.
  • ✅ How can AI improve platform engineering prioritization?
    AI handles recall (surfacing existing assumptions from a Git-backed repository) while humans retain judgment (validating assumptions and making final decisions). Three modes (rapid, standard, deep-dive) serve different time/depth trade-offs.
  • ⭕ How should platform engineering adapt to non-developer builders?
    Partially addressed: the team is doing user research to understand builder needs (scale, data access, security requirements) and plans to provide golden paths and AI-readable documentation. Specific solutions are still being developed.
  • ⭕ What is the actual formula or algorithm used in the prioritization framework?
    The formula is referenced but never fully specified. We know it includes weighted impact factors, urgency, confidence, and effort, but the exact mathematical relationship is not disclosed.
