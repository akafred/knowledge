TBM 430: Incubate, Compound, Refinance, Liquidate

John Cutler · email · 2026-07-12 Reader 🖥️

AI as AmplifierDecision-Making PracticesOrganizational DebtPlatform EngineeringTechnical DebtVelocity Debt / Debt Accelerator
Software assets, teams, and initiatives can be read as a portfolio positioned by the value they create against what they cost to carry -- moving between Incubate, Compound, Refinance, and Liquidate over time -- and AI does not change this underlying economic physics of complexity, coupling, and decay, only the cost, speed, and feasibility of accelerating whichever direction, healthy or pathological, the portfolio is already moving.

practical · expository — Builds a diagrammatic 2x2 conceptual framework (value created x carrying cost) and a financial-portfolio metaphor (incubate/compound/refinance/liquidate), then applies it to AI. Explicitly self-described by the author as informal 'Sunday-morning diagramming and thought-provocation,' not a worked-out theory -- the diagrams carry as much argument as the text, in classic Cutler style.

Key terms

  • Incubate / Compound / Refinance / Liquidate
    The four quadrants of the value-created x carrying-cost 2x2: Incubate = low value, low carrying cost (cheap unproven bet); Compound = proven value, still cheap to carry, returns expanding; Refinance = rising carrying costs as the asset scales, interest starting to consume returns, needing restructuring; Liquidate = remaining value no longer justifies the cost of carrying the asset or its debt.
  • carrying cost
    What it costs to change, operate, and support a software asset over time -- the second axis of the 2x2, alongside value created. Used qualitatively throughout; never assigned a metric or unit.
  • Build Trap
    The pattern where Incubate fails to become Compound (value never proven) but the team keeps building anyway, so carrying cost keeps rising -- 'turning a cheap experiment into an expensive obligation.'
  • Zombie Portfolio
    Old, low-value assets kept alive past their economic justification -- used only in the AI section as one of four situations AI interacts with.
  • Mandate Trap
    The platform-strategy failure of mistaking compulsory usage for evidence that the platform improves anyone's economics.

Propositions

  • explicitA software asset's position on a 2x2 of value-created versus carrying-cost implies one of four basic economic postures: incubate, compound, refinance, or liquidate.conf: high

  • explicitTechnical debt is a financing choice -- accepting higher future carrying costs in exchange for reaching value sooner -- and whether it is rational leverage, a dangerous obligation, or a liability depends entirely on which portfolio quadrant the asset carrying it occupies, not on any intrinsic property of the debt itself.conf: high
  • explicitLiquidation is not inherently a failure; refusing to liquidate -- continuing to build an asset that has never proven its value (the Build Trap) -- is the more common and more damaging failure.conf: high
  • explicitA platform's worth is determined by whether it measurably improves the value/carrying-cost economics of the assets that depend on it -- not by how much it ships or how many teams are made to use it.conf: high

  • explicitPortfolio health is not just a snapshot of the current mix of assets -- it is also a function of what is flowing between categories, how fast, and whether anyone is intentionally managing that movement.conf: high
  • explicitAI does not eliminate complexity, coupling, coordination, uncertainty, or decay in a software portfolio; it changes the cost, speed, and feasibility of responding to them -- including the feasibility of responding badly.conf: high

  • explicitBecause AI enters an existing, non-neutral portfolio (a specific mix of bets, winners, drag, and aging assets), the operative question is not whether AI makes work faster but which movement -- toward healthier or more pathological economics -- it is being used to accelerate.conf: high

Arguments

The critical question when deploying AI into any part of a software organization is 'which movement are we asking AI to accelerate,' not 'will this make us faster.' strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • AI enters an existing portfolio with a particular mix of bets, winners, drag, and aging assets -- it does not enter a neutral system.
  • The same AI capability can improve the economics of the portfolio or deepen the pattern that created the problem, depending on where it's applied.
Implicit premises:
  • Speed itself is not intrinsically valuable -- it is valuable only when applied to a movement that improves portfolio economics.
  • Organizations cannot reliably predict in advance which direction a given AI application will accelerate without first locating the asset on the portfolio diagram.
Inferences:
  • If AI's effect depends on the direction of the movement it accelerates, then speed alone is not a sufficient decision criterion for adopting AI in a given system.
  • Because organizations don't reliably know their portfolio position in advance, asking 'faster in which direction' functions as a diagnostic prerequisite to using AI safely, not an afterthought.
Technical debt should be evaluated relative to an asset's portfolio position rather than treated as a uniformly good or bad practice. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Debt is defined as accepting higher future carrying costs in exchange for reaching value sooner.
  • In Incubate, debt is dangerous because the asset's value is unproven.
  • In Compound, debt can be rational leverage if it helps expand returns.
  • In Refinance, the debt's interest is consuming too much of the returns, so restructuring is the needed move.
  • In Liquidate, the asset no longer creates enough value to justify either the debt or the cost of repaying it.
Implicit premises:
  • The moral/practical valence of a debt-taking decision is not a property of the code itself but of the economic context (quadrant position) the code sits in.
Inferences:
  • Since the same technical choice (taking on debt) yields opposite outcomes depending on quadrant, judging debt in isolation from portfolio position is a category error.
  • The question 'is this debt good or bad' must be replaced with 'which quadrant is this asset in.'

Narratives

  • The generic arc of a startup: begins as a cheap, low-value Incubate bet; some prove their value and move to Compound; as they scale, carrying costs rise, pushing them to Refinance; a healthy company then either restores the economics or eventually Liquidates. Other bets never prove value, and if the team keeps building anyway, the product moves right without moving up -- the Build Trap.
    → Illustrates the motion/arc idea and introduces the Build Trap as a named failure pattern; serves as the archetypal case the rest of the piece (the AI section especially) keeps returning to.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What value does this asset create, what does it cost us to carry, and what should we do next?
    Answered structurally by the 2x2 diagram: the answer is read off the asset's quadrant position (Incubate/Compound/Refinance/Liquidate).
  • ✅ Which direction is an asset moving, how quickly, and is that movement intentional?
    Answered by the 'Motion and Arc' and 'An Intentional Portfolio' sections: movement can be intentional, an unavoidable byproduct of growth/aging, or accidental (oversight, avoidance, negligence).
  • ✅ Which movement are we asking AI to accelerate?
    Answered qualitatively via four worked situations (Build Trap, Limits to Growth, Success Trap, Zombie Portfolio), each showing AI can accelerate a healthy or pathological movement depending on how it's applied -- though no decision procedure is given for telling which in advance.
  • ⭕ How do you actually measure 'value created' and 'carrying cost' for a real software asset, in practice?
  • ⭕ Who has the organizational authority to decide an asset is in the Build Trap or should be liquidated, and what happens when that decision threatens careers, teams, or headcount?

Gaps

  • incompleteThe entire framework depends on placing an asset on the value-created x carrying-cost 2x2, but the piece never operationalizes either axis -- no metrics, proxies, or even a single worked numeric example are given for how a team would actually locate a real asset on the diagram. Every judgment in the piece (Incubate vs. Compound, rational leverage vs. dangerous debt) presupposes an assessment the piece itself never shows how to make.

  • incompleteCutler opens by conceding this is 'absolutely not a new idea' (software as a portfolio of assets/investments/bets/liabilities) but never names or differentiates from the obvious antecedents his own framing invites comparison to -- most directly the BCG growth-share matrix (Question Marks/Stars/Cash Cows/Dogs maps closely onto Incubate/Compound/.../Liquidate) and Kent Beck's Explore/Expand/Extract company-lifecycle model already in this corpus. The self-effacing 'none of the underlying ideas are especially new' reads as honest but is never cashed out into an actual comparison of what, if anything, the carrying-cost axis adds.
    Read next: The explore-expand-extract concept (Beck's 3X) already in this corpus for a direct lifecycle-model comparison; BCG growth-share matrix literature for the portfolio-management antecedent.
  • incompleteThe piece analyzes portfolio movement from a God's-eye systems view and never addresses the political economy of admitting an asset belongs in Liquidate or a Build Trap -- whose careers, teams, and budgets depend on the asset continuing, who has the authority to call the move, and what happens when the people who benefit from an asset's continuation are also the people who would have to authorize its liquidation.   • incompleteEach of the four AI walkthroughs (Build Trap, Limits to Growth, Success Trap, Zombie Portfolio) is structured as a symmetric 'AI can help X, or AI can make X worse' pair, with no criterion offered for telling which effect will occur in a given organization before the fact.     Read next: extract-tbm-415-demand-mix-discovery-ai-dysfunction-multiplier (the sibling gap about AI's heterogeneous effects); decision-making concept for what a genuine ex-ante diagnostic would require.
  • uninformedThe 'Zombie Portfolio' term is used in the AI section as if it were already established, but no such trap was defined in the earlier 'The Traps' section (which named only Build Trap, Limits to Growth, and Success Trap). The piece also asserts all its central claims (the four traps, the six platform traps, the debt-by-quadrant claims) with no citation, case study, or empirical data -- every claim rests entirely on the stipulated diagram, not on evidence external to it.
