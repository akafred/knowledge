3 Ways AI Can Free Organizations from Legacy Workflows

Ganna Pogrebna · article · 2026-05-07 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Enterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessLegacy & Organizational Forgetting
Organizations' biggest competitive constraint is not missing capability but accumulated legacy — obsolete metrics, outdated brand identity, and entrenched customer myths — and AI's distinctive value lies not in automation per se but in providing objective, depersonalized evidence that makes inherited assumptions indefensible and thereby enables 'organizational forgetting.'

practical · prescriptive case-study — A practitioner-oriented HBR article that presents three case studies of organizations using AI to overcome legacy constraints. The argument is structured as problem-solution pairs, each illustrating a different facet of 'organizational forgetting.'

Key terms

  • organizational forgetting Legacy & Organizational Forgetting
    The deliberate requirement that an organization moves on from its past — retiring obsolete practices, metrics, assumptions, and identities that no longer serve its competitive position
  • corporate memory Legacy & Organizational Forgetting
    The accumulated legacy of workflows, assumptions, metrics, and identities that organizations carry forward, often unconsciously, and that constrain future competitiveness
  • contradiction matrix
    A visual evidence display generated by AI showing how fragmented and incoherent an organization's messaging has become across documents
  • multi-channel path completion rate
    The proportion of customers who complete a purchase journey across digital and physical touchpoints combined — a replacement KPI for the legacy in-store-only conversion metric

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What are the main forms of legacy constraint that hold organizations back?
    Three forms: obsolete performance metrics, outdated brand identity, and entrenched customer myths.
  • ✅ Why is organizational forgetting so difficult in practice?
    Emotional attachment, political friction, and insider blindness prevent people from retiring what they built.
  • ✅ How can AI help organizations let go of outdated practices?
    By providing objective, depersonalized, scale evidence that makes legacy assumptions indefensible.
  • ⭕ What makes AI-generated evidence more effective than human-generated evidence for driving organizational change?
    Asserted but not rigorously argued — the article claims AI removes emotional friction but does not compare with human-led analysis.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe article treats AI-generated evidence as inherently 'objective' and 'depersonalized' without acknowledging that AI systems embed their own biases (training data, configuration choices, metric selection). The consultancy configuring the GPT-4 model for FengSys made subjective choices about what counts as 'contradictory' or 'obsolete.'
    closed byTBM 428: Yes, Robot. Yes, Boss? — Supplies the missing critique: AI-generated evidence is not inherently objective. Cutler attacks the objectivity myth from the intent/weaponization angle (AI 'willingly complies' to build a defensible case and makes agendas easier to operationalize), the non-neutrality the 3-ways piece failed to acknowledge.
  • incompleteNo discussion of what to do when AI-surfaced evidence is ambiguous or contested. All three cases present clean, unambiguous results — the AI finds the truth and resistance dissolves. Real organizational data is messier.
    closed byTBM 426: The Trouble With Mirrors — That extraction flagged that all three of its cases show AI surfacing clean, unambiguous truth after which 'resistance dissolves', with no account of ambiguous or contested evidence and 'messier' real data. TBM 426 supplies exactly that account: organizational truth is multi-party, positional, framed, and continuously negotiated, so surfaced 'truth' does not dissolve resistance — seeing clearly does not make people ready, safe, aligned, or empowered to act, and un-stewarded exposure invites self-protection over change.
  • uninformedThe concept of 'organizational forgetting' is attributed to an MIT Sloan Review article but the broader academic literature on organizational unlearning (Hedberg 1981, Tsang & Zahra 2008) is not engaged. This literature distinguishes intentional from accidental forgetting and identifies structural conditions for each.
  • illogicalThere is a tension between P5 (insiders can't see their own incoherence) and the article's reliance on insider testimonials (Vesna, Leonor, Kevin) as evidence that the AI solution worked. If insiders are too close to judge, their post-hoc endorsements are also potentially unreliable as evidence of success.
