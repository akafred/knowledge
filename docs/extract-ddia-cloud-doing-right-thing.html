Designing Data-Intensive Applications: The Cloud & Doing the Right Thing

Mental Models & Understanding
Cloud computing changes the technical and economic architecture of data systems in ways that demand new operational thinking, while the growing power of data-intensive applications creates ethical obligations for engineers to consider the societal consequences of what they build.

practical · mixed — Combines expository technical analysis (cloud vs self-hosting tradeoffs) with argumentative ethical reasoning (engineers' responsibility). The excerpts are from a technical book but framed by a newsletter author who contextualizes and recommends action.

Key terms

  • cloud native
    Architecture designed from the ground up to build upon lower-level cloud services (object storage, etc.) rather than merely running traditional software on cloud VMs; characterized by separation of storage and compute, multitenancy, and layered service composition
  • separation of storage and compute
    Cloud-native design pattern where data storage (e.g., S3) and computation (CPU/RAM) are disaggregated onto separate infrastructure, connected by network, rather than co-located on the same machine
  • algorithmic prison
    The condition of being systematically excluded from jobs, travel, insurance, financial services, and other societal participation by automated risk-scoring systems, without proof of guilt or meaningful appeal
  • vendor lock-in
    The situation where lack of standard APIs and migration paths between cloud services makes switching prohibitively expensive, giving the provider disproportionate power over the customer
  • capacity planning becomes financial planning
    In cloud environments, the operational challenge shifts from provisioning physical resources to managing metered billing — understanding spend, avoiding waste, and planning for resource quotas
  • systems thinking Mental Models & Understanding
    Analyzing an entire system including human interactions and feedback loops, not just the computerized parts, to predict unintended consequences of data-driven decisions

Arguments

Cloud vs self-hosting is a nuanced tradeoff, not a one-way migration strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Cloud is cheaper for variable/spiky workloads
  • Self-hosting is often cheaper for predictable workloads with existing expertise
  • Cloud eliminates machine-level operations but introduces new operational challenges (integration, cost management, quotas)
  • Cloud creates vendor lock-in risk, especially without standard APIs
Implicit premises:
  • Organizations have stable enough requirements to make meaningful long-term infrastructure commitments
  • The cost comparison is primarily economic — political, sovereignty, and security dimensions are secondary
Inferences:
  • The optimal strategy for most organizations is a hybrid approach, not all-cloud or all-self-hosted
  • As cloud APIs remain non-standard, the switching cost problem will worsen over time as more services are adopted
Engineers have a personal ethical responsibility for the data systems they build strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Software systems have far-reaching societal consequences, both intended and unintended
  • Data is often about people — their behavior, identities, interests
  • Professional ethics codes exist (ACM) but are rarely enforced or discussed
  • Technology is not inherently good or bad; it depends on how it's used
Implicit premises:
  • Engineers have meaningful agency in what gets built — they are not merely executing orders
  • The market and employers will not spontaneously ensure ethical outcomes without individual engineer resistance
  • Ethics is not reducible to legal compliance
Inferences:
  • Engineers who focus only on technical excellence while ignoring consequences are being professionally negligent
  • The industry needs institutional mechanisms (not just individual conscience) to enforce ethical standards
Algorithmic decision-making can systematically harm individuals through bias, lack of accountability, and feedback loops strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • ML systems amplify bias present in training data
  • Protected traits correlate with non-protected features (e.g., postal code correlates with race)
  • Algorithmic decisions lack accountability — no clear party is responsible for errors
  • Feedback loops can trap individuals in downward spirals
Implicit premises:
  • Statistical accuracy at the population level does not justify harm to wrongly-classified individuals
  • The burden of proof should rest on the system deployer, not the individual harmed
  • Market incentives favor false negatives (rejecting good applicants) over false positives (accepting bad ones), systematically biasing toward exclusion
Inferences:
  • The combination of bias amplification and feedback loops means algorithmic systems can create new forms of structural inequality that are harder to detect and challenge than explicit discrimination
  • Individual recourse mechanisms are insufficient — systemic regulation is needed
We have voluntarily built a mass surveillance infrastructure that rivals or exceeds totalitarian systems strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • Internet-connected microphones exist in nearly every inhabited space
  • Location, social, financial, and health data collection is nearly unavoidable
  • People accept corporate surveillance because the benefits of digital technology are great
  • Sensor accuracy and analysis algorithms will only improve
Implicit premises:
  • Corporate data collection, once established, can be co-opted by state actors through legal or extralegal means
  • The 'voluntary' nature of consent is illusory when opting out means foregoing essential services
  • The current benign use of surveillance data does not constrain future use
Inferences:
  • The line between 'data-driven services' and 'surveillance' is one of intent, not of capability — and intent can change

Narratives

  • The thought experiment of replacing 'data' with 'surveillance' in common business phrases ('data-driven organization' becomes 'surveillance-driven organization')
    → Supports the proposition that corporate data collection is functionally equivalent to surveillance; forces the reader to see familiar practices as sinister
  • The credit score feedback loop: a good worker hit by misfortune gets a bad credit score, which prevents employment, which worsens the credit score further
    → Demonstrates that algorithmic feedback loops can turn temporary misfortune into permanent exclusion — supporting the argument that predictive systems create new forms of structural inequality
  • German gas stations introducing algorithmic pricing, which led to tacit collusion and higher consumer prices
    → Shows that algorithmic feedback loops can harm at a market level, not just individual level — algorithms can learn anti-competitive behavior without explicit programming
  • Martin Kleppmann's journey from DDIA 1st edition (2016) to 2nd edition (2026) — startup founder, LinkedIn, years of research, now with co-author Chris Riccomini
    → Establishes the authority and rigor of the source; implies that the ethical chapter is not an afterthought but the culmination of a decade of thinking about data systems
  • Totalitarian regimes dreaming of putting a microphone in every room — contrasted with our voluntary adoption of smartphones, smart TVs, and voice assistants
    → Supports the surveillance argument by making the historical comparison visceral — what was once the aspiration of dictators is now our daily reality, accepted willingly

Questions addressed

  • ✅ When should an organization use cloud services versus self-hosting?
    It depends on existing expertise, workload predictability, and control requirements. Cloud suits variable workloads and teams without deep ops experience; self-hosting suits predictable workloads with existing expertise. Hybrid approaches are common.
  • ✅ How does cloud computing change system architecture at a technical level?
    Cloud-native architecture separates storage from compute, builds higher-level services on lower-level ones (layering), and uses multitenancy. This differs fundamentally from merely running traditional software on cloud VMs.
  • ✅ How does the cloud change the role of operations?
    Operations shifts from machine-level administration to service integration, financial planning (metered billing replaces capacity planning), security management, and monitoring cross-service interactions.
  • ⭕ What ethical responsibilities do software engineers have regarding data systems?
    Engineers bear personal responsibility for consequences and must consider bias, accountability, feedback loops, and surveillance. But the text acknowledges ethics is 'not a checklist' — it's an ongoing process of reflection and dialogue.
  • ⭕ How can we prevent algorithmic bias and feedback loops from causing harm?
    The text identifies the problems clearly but offers only general directions: systems thinking, moral imagination, accountability mechanisms. Specific institutional or technical solutions are not provided.
  • ⭕ How should engineers concretely exercise ethical judgment when employed by organizations with misaligned incentives?
