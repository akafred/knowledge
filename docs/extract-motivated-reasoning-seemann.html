theoretical · reflective essay — A metacognitive essay that applies the concept of motivated reasoning to the author's own recent series of AI-critical articles. More confessional than argumentative — the primary move is epistemic self-audit rather than persuasion.

Key terms

  • motivated reasoning
    The tendency to arrive at desired conclusions through reasoning processes heavily influenced by personal motivations, often subconsciously; smarter people are more susceptible because they can construct more convincing rationalizations
  • love of the craft Intrinsic Motivation & Making
    Intrinsic attraction to programming as puzzle-solving that made decades of otherwise uninteresting business domains engaging
  • counterpoints to techno-optimism AI Backlash
    Dissenting perspectives that question AI's net benefits, serving an epistemic balancing function even if most specific concerns prove wrong

Propositions

  • definitionalMotivated reasoning is a cognitive bias in which personal motivations unconsciously steer reasoning toward desired conclusions.
  • empiricalMore intelligent people are more susceptible to motivated reasoning because their superior mental resources allow them to construct more convincing rationalizations.
  • self-assessmentThe author's recent AI-critical articles are likely influenced by motivated reasoning.
  • self-assessmentThe primary non-economic motivation biasing the author is love of programming as craft — solving puzzles, not managing entities that solve puzzles.
  • evaluativeBeing told to become a 'manager of agentic LLMs' is functionally equivalent to becoming a manager, which the author explicitly rejected decades ago.
  • probabilisticEven if ninety percent of the author's AI concerns are irrelevant, the remaining ten percent could contain real problems.
  • normativePosting dissenting opinions has epistemic value as counterpoints to techno-optimism, even when some concerns are wrong.
  • evaluativeThe last few decades of the software development job market — where enjoyable work was also well-paid — are most likely abnormal.

Gaps

  • analyticalThe essay never identifies which specific AI concerns the author believes are most likely to survive the motivated-reasoning filter. The '10% that might be real' is left entirely unspecified.
  • evidentialThe claim that smarter people are more susceptible to motivated reasoning is stated without citation or nuance. The underlying research (Kahan et al.) shows this primarily for identity-protective cognition on politically charged topics, not necessarily for professional self-interest.
  • omissionThe essay treats 'love of craft' and 'economic incentive' as the only two motivational roots, but does not consider identity/status — being a recognized expert in a field that AI might devalue — as a distinct and potentially stronger bias source.
