T*D

Andrea Laforgia · article · 2026-04-10 Reader 🖥️

Pair & Mob ProgrammingTest-Driven DevelopmentTrunk-Based & Continuous Delivery
When software teams genuinely pursue speed, quality, and shared ownership simultaneously, they naturally converge on three mutually reinforcing practices — test-driven development, trunk-based development, and team-focused development (pairing/mobbing) — which the author brands as T*D.

practical · essay / opinion piece — A practitioner's argument that three specific engineering practices — TDD, trunk-based development, and collaborative programming — are the natural convergence point for teams pursuing high performance. Blends historical evidence with advocacy.

Key terms

  • T*D Test-Driven Development
    The union of test-driven development, trunk-based development, and team-focused development — treated as a single convergent pattern rather than three independent practices.
  • Team-Focused Development Pair & Mob Programming
    Collaborative programming practices (pair programming, mob/ensemble programming) where multiple developers work on the same code simultaneously.
  • Natural landing point / natural outcome
    The claim that these practices emerge inevitably when teams optimise for the right goals, rather than being imposed externally.
  • Trunk-based development Trunk-Based & Continuous Delivery
    Integrating code into a single mainline branch at least daily, avoiding long-lived feature branches.
  • Illusory safety Trunk-Based & Continuous Delivery
    The false sense of security provided by long-lived feature branches, which defer rather than reduce risk.

Arguments

TDD, TBD, and team-focused development are the natural convergence point for high-performing teams. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • These practices were independently discovered by multiple teams across decades before being formally named.
  • When teams remove friction and pursue quality and speed, these patterns tend to emerge.
  • Each practice has deep historical roots and strong empirical support.
Implicit premises:
  • Practices that are independently re-discovered across contexts are more likely to be genuinely effective than invented methodologies.
  • The fact that something emerges 'naturally' makes it more legitimate than something imposed.
  • High performance is a single coherent goal that all teams should share.
Inferences:
  • From independent re-discovery to naturalness (inductive generalisation)
  • From naturalness to prescriptive recommendation (is-to-ought)
Long-lived feature branches are riskier than trunk-based development. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Isolated code leads to painful merges and delayed feedback.
  • Continuous Integration makes bugs dramatically easier to find and remove (Fowler).
  • DORA research identifies TBD as a key differentiator for elite performers.
Implicit premises:
  • The risk of small frequent integrations is always lower than the risk of large infrequent integrations.
  • The team has sufficient automated test coverage and deployment automation to make frequent integration safe.
Inferences:
  • Empirical correlation (DORA data) treated as evidence of causation.
Collaborative programming (pairing/mobbing) is worth the apparent cost. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Paired programmers produce 15–50% fewer defects (Williams & Kessler).
  • Microsoft research shows paired developers write cleaner, more maintainable code.
  • These practices accelerate onboarding and eliminate knowledge silos.
  • Developers report higher job satisfaction.
Implicit premises:
  • Defect reduction and knowledge sharing outweigh the cost of having multiple people on one task.
  • Typing speed / lines-of-code-per-person is the wrong efficiency metric.
Inferences:
  • Research findings from specific contexts generalised to all teams.

Narratives

  • The author's personal journey of independently discovering agile, Scrum, pairing, and TDD through practice before learning they had formal names.
    → Establishes ethos (practitioner credibility) and supports the 'natural emergence' thesis.
  • Kent Beck's story of rediscovering TDD from an 'ancient book about programming' that described matching input and output tapes.
    → Reinforces the theme that TDD is a perennial rediscovery, not a fad.
  • The cautionary tale of younger developers raised on GitFlow who see TBD as heresy.
    → Positions resistance as generational unfamiliarity rather than reasoned disagreement.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What practices distinguish high-performing software teams?
    TDD, trunk-based development, and team-focused development (pairing/mobbing), unified as T*D.
  • ✅ Are these practices modern inventions or do they have deep historical roots?
    All three have roots going back decades (1950s–1970s), predating their modern names.
  • ✅ Why do teams resist these practices if they are so effective?
    Resistance stems from fear, habit, narrow efficiency metrics, and lack of experience with the practices.
  • ⭕ How do these three practices relate to each other?
    The article implies mutual reinforcement but never explicitly maps the causal dependencies between the three practices.
  • ⭕ Under what conditions might these practices NOT be the right choice?

Gaps

  • scope limitationThe article never addresses contexts where T*D may not apply or may need significant adaptation — solo developers, open-source projects with asynchronous contributors, research/exploratory coding, or very early-stage prototyping.
    Read next: Investigate when trunk-based development or pairing breaks down (e.g., distributed open-source, 1-person teams, spike work).
  • missing causal mechanismThe three practices are presented as a unified triad (T*D) but the article never explains why these three specifically, or how they causally reinforce each other. The mutual reinforcement is implied but not argued.
    Read next: Look for literature on the interaction effects between TDD and CI/CD, and between pairing and TDD adoption success.
  • survivorship bias riskThe 'natural emergence' argument draws on teams that succeeded. Teams that tried these practices and failed, or high-performing teams that use different approaches, are not discussed.
    Read next: Examine DORA data more carefully for whether TBD is necessary or merely correlated; look at high-performing teams using different workflows (e.g., Linux kernel's patch-based model).
  • evidence selectivityThe pairing research cited (Williams & Kessler 2000) is over 25 years old. More recent and nuanced studies on pair programming effectiveness are not referenced.
    Read next: Review recent meta-analyses on pair programming effectiveness, particularly in remote/distributed settings.
  • is-ought gapThe article moves from 'these practices emerge naturally' (descriptive) to 'these are the only sensible way to work' (prescriptive) without bridging the logical gap. Natural emergence does not entail optimality.
    Read next: Consider evolutionary arguments: not all convergent outcomes are optimal; they may reflect local optima or shared biases in the practitioner community.
