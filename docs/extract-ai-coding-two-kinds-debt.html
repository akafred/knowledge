AI coding creates two kinds of debt. You're only measuring one

Antonija Bilić Arar · article · 2026-06-18 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Cognitive DebtCognitive SurrenderDesirable Difficulty & Productive StruggleDORA & Engineering MetricsLearning Culture & SlackMeasuring AI ImpactMental Models & UnderstandingMeta-Skills & Contextual JudgmentPair & Mob ProgrammingPull Requests & Async Code ReviewTechnical Debt
AI-generated code creates a second, largely invisible kind of debt — cognitive debt, the erosion of shared team understanding of what a system does and why, distinct from ordinary technical debt in the code itself — and it is a genuine measurement blind spot: satisfaction scores, NPS and mentor ratings can hold steady or rise while it accumulates, so organizations must replace satisfaction-based signals with trajectory-based skill measurement and deliberate team-level questions to catch it before it becomes irreversible.

practical · mixed (expository argument built on interview testimony and two case studies) — Imports one theoretical construct (Storey's 'cognitive debt', itself grounded in Naur's programming-as-theory-building) but the piece's payload is operational: a controlled study, a longitudinal internship dataset, and a set of concrete organizational practices. Structured as journalism (direct quotes from Storey and Jurić) rather than first-person argument.

Key terms

  • Cognitive debt Cognitive Debt
    The accumulated loss of understanding of what a system does, why, and who on the team knows what — a property of the system/project, not of an individual ('there is no my cognitive debt'). You can have clean, well-tested code and still carry it if the people who built it can no longer account for it, or if understanding is concentrated in one person.
  • Technical debt Technical Debt
    Used strictly as the contrast term: a known, well-understood engineering tradeoff that leaders already know how to find and repay (refactor). Its familiarity is what makes cognitive debt's unfamiliarity and irreversibility legible.
  • Productive struggle Desirable Difficulty & Productive Struggle
    The 'wrestling with a problem' that Jurić identifies as the actual mechanism of learning, and which AI removes when used for generation-and-acceptance rather than generation-and-inquiry. Never defined precisely — used descriptively, not operationally.
  • Dunning-Kruger gap
    Operationalized narrowly as the numeric difference between an intern's self-rating at internship start and their retrospective self-rating of that same starting point, measured today. Used as a proxy for miscalibrated self-awareness of one's own skill trajectory.

Arguments

Cognitive debt is real, distinct from technical debt, and current engineering metrics fail to detect it. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Cognitive debt is the accumulated loss of who-understands-what, not a property of the code
  • Clean, well-tested code can still carry cognitive debt
  • Standard metrics don't surface it because it's an unknown blind spot
Implicit premises:
  • Existing engineering metrics were built to track velocity, quality and delivery stability (i.e., technical-debt-adjacent properties), not distributed tacit understanding
  • A construct that doesn't move deployment/velocity/satisfaction metrics stays invisible to leaders who manage by those metrics
Inferences:
  • Generalizes from one expert's classroom observation to a systemic organizational property
  • Infers metric-blindness from the definitional contrast with technical debt, which existing metrics were designed around
Satisfaction-based metrics actively mask the decline that trajectory-based skill measurement reveals. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Infobip cohort data: skill growth fell across all four dimensions between pre-AI and AI-era cohorts
  • Satisfaction, NPS and mentor scores stayed flat or rose over the same period
  • R10 and R8 used the same tool at the same frequency but had opposite outcomes depending on cognitive posture
Implicit premises:
  • Self-assessed skill trajectory is itself a sufficiently valid proxy for real skill development, without independent (e.g., objective test-based) validation
  • The 2022–2026 cohort comparison isn't confounded by other era-level changes (program content, hiring bar, mentor turnover) unrelated to AI usage
Inferences:
  • Moves from a correlational before/after cohort comparison to a causal attribution to AI usage specifically
  • Generalizes from a 96-respondent, self-selected alumni survey to an organization-wide measurement prescription

Narratives

  • Storey's entrepreneurship classroom: students moved fast with AI, shipped to real users, but lost track of what they were building and who understood which parts; one person who understood the AI-generated code couldn't safely change it either.
    → Origin story that defines the term through a lived example rather than an abstract definition — establishes that cognitive debt is about who-understands-what, not code quality
  • Anthropic's January 2026 randomized trial: 52 junior developers, AI-assisted group 17% lower on comprehension tests.
    → Supplies external, quantitative, non-anecdotal validation of the mechanism before the piece turns to Infobip's own (less rigorous) data
  • Infobip's 8-cohort, 225-intern internship program, 2022–2026, with the Winter 2023 vs. Summer 2025 comparison and the R10 vs. R8 vignette in the Winter 2026 cohort.
    → The article's centerpiece evidence — real career-span data plus a concrete individual-level illustration of the posture mechanism
  • A quoted, unnamed engineer: 'sometimes it scares him that he created this system and it works and it's running, and he's forgotten what it was he was even trying to create in the first place.'
    → Vivid, affective illustration of individual-level cognitive debt as lived experience — foreshadows the closing claim that this loss cannot be scripted back

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What is cognitive debt and how does it differ from technical debt?
    Cognitive debt is loss of shared understanding of what a system does and why and who knows what; technical debt is about the code. They are independent — you can have one without the other.
  • ✅ Does using AI to write code necessarily harm learning?
    No — outcome depends on cognitive posture (engaged inquiry vs. passive acceptance), not on frequency or amount of AI use, per the R10/R8 comparison and the Anthropic RCT's generation-vs-inquiry distinction.
  • ✅ Can standard engineering metrics (satisfaction, NPS, mentor ratings) detect cognitive debt?
    No — Infobip's data shows these metrics can rise while skill growth (the article's proposed better proxy) falls.
  • ✅ Can cognitive debt, once formed, be repaid the way technical debt is repaid (e.g., via refactoring)?
    No, per Storey — there is no equivalent repayment mechanism; the only lever is prevention.
  • ⭕ Do the prescriptions (mentor-as-coach, AI-free checkpoints, try-first-check-second) generalize beyond a formally resourced internship program to ordinary engineering teams without a dedicated mentor role?
  • ⭕ What is the actual causal mechanism connecting 'productive struggle' removal to durable skill formation — is it about attention, retrieval practice, error-correction, or something else?

Gaps

  • incompleteThe 'measure trajectory, not satisfaction' prescription rests entirely on Infobip's own internal, non-independently-audited alumni survey (N=96, self-selected respondents, retrospective self-assessed skill ratings) — the very instrument vulnerable to the Dunning-Kruger miscalibration the study itself measures. The article pairs this with a rigorous RCT (Anthropic, objective comprehension testing) but never flags that its own headline metric is far weaker methodologically than the study it borrows credibility from.
    Read next: Cross-reference the Anthropic RCT's comprehension-test methodology (extract-cognitive-surrender#p4) against Infobip's self-report design; look for Storey's own published paper on triple-debt measurement (referenced elsewhere in this corpus as arXiv 2603.22106, per extract-osmani-intent-debt) to see whether it offers a non-self-report instrument.
  • incompleteThe Infobip pre-AI vs. AI-era cohort comparison is a non-randomized, before/after natural comparison spanning 2022–2026 — a period during which the internship's content, mentor selection, hiring bar or applicant pool could plausibly have shifted for reasons unrelated to AI. The piece treats the causal attribution to AI as settled ('This confirmed that the problem wasn't the quality of the program') without directly ruling out these confounds.
    Read next: DORA/Accelerate-style guidance on longitudinal measurement validity and confound control; look for a matched or randomized version of this comparison (e.g., interns with AI access experimentally withheld) that would isolate the AI effect from era effects.
  • incompleteThe Anthropic RCT is cited only in paraphrase (52 junior developers, 17% lower comprehension, 'nearly two letter grades') without reporting the comparison arms, confidence intervals, or how comprehension was tested. This corpus already extracted a related but not identically-framed version of what appears to be the same underlying finding (generation-only vs. conceptual-inquiry posture, rather than 'used AI assistants' vs. a control) — the two paraphrases have not been reconciled against Anthropic's original publication.
    Read next: extract-cognitive-surrender.json#p4 for the alternate framing of the same study; anthropic.com/research/AI-assistance-coding-skills for the primary source to resolve which comparison arms each paraphrase actually describes.
  • incompleteStorey's claim that cognitive debt 'has no equivalent' repayment mechanism is stated as an absolute, but the article never distinguishes understanding that was never built (plausibly unrecoverable) from understanding that was once held and has since decayed through disuse (plausibly recoverable via deliberate re-engagement, e.g. re-reading code or asking an agent to explain it back). This corpus's own Osmani extractions raise exactly that distinction and land on the opposite answer — that cognitive debt is partially recoverable this way — without either piece acknowledging the other's position.
    Read next: extract-osmani-orchestration-tax-is-you#g5 (stock-and-flow model of cognitive debt, replenishment via re-reading) and extract-osmani-intent-debt#p5 (agents can 'partially pay down cognitive debt... re-explain the code') — see relations below for the direct tension.
  • incompleteThe concrete prescriptions (mentor-as-coach, AI-free checkpoints, try-first-check-second, visibility of professional standards) are demonstrated only inside a structured, resourced internship program with a dedicated mentor per intern. The piece never asks whether or how they generalize to an ordinary team of mid-level or senior engineers with no formal mentor role, or to distributed/remote teams that lack the ambient 'who's the go-to' awareness the piece assumes exists in a colocated cohort.
    Read next: Golden-path / platform-engineering literature on embedding good practice without a bespoke mentor relationship; pair/mob-programming literature on how tacit-knowledge transfer happens outside formal mentorship for non-junior engineers.
