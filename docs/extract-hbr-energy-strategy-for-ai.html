Your Company Needs an Energy Strategy for AI's Next Phase

Eric Yanfei Zhao · rss · 2026-06 Reader 🖥️

AI Unit EconomicsChangeability & OptionalityCompute & Energy InfrastructureJevons Paradox
Because the AI value stack's scarce control point is migrating from models and GPUs down to electricity itself, every enterprise — not just hyperscalers — must now treat energy access as a board-level strategic variable and build distinctive capability to measure, reduce, contract for, and geographically place its AI compute before scarcity becomes an operating emergency.

practical · argumentative — HBR-style strategy piece. Frames a diagnostic (the Great Value Loop) and then prescribes a five-action playbook for executives. Practical: the propositions are in service of 'you should do X about your AI energy posture.'

Key terms

  • Great Value Loop Compute & Energy Infrastructure
    A recurring pattern in technology transitions where value pools at whichever layer is currently scarce, then migrates downward in the stack as that layer commoditizes and a new underlying constraint emerges.
  • Intelligence per watt Compute & Energy Infrastructure
    A management metric — output (tokens, inferences, useful AI work) per unit of electricity consumed — that the authors want elevated to a tracked KPI alongside cost and accuracy.
  • Optionality (vs. ownership) Changeability & Optionality
    Contractual rights to power (PPAs, VPPAs, green tariffs, colocation reservations) that secure access without the firm having to own generation assets.
  • Jevons paradox Jevons Paradox
    Borrowed from economics: efficiency gains in AI inference lower per-unit cost, which expands viable use cases and therefore increases total energy demand rather than decreasing it.
  • Energy & physics era
    The fourth era of the Great Value Loop, in which the bottleneck has moved out of the digital domain entirely and back into physical infrastructure: electricity, cooling, land, grid connections, permitting.
  • Compute and Energy Council
    A standing cross-functional governance body (CIO + CFO + procurement + sustainability/ops) with formal veto power over major AI deployments.

Arguments

Enterprises must build distinctive energy-access capability now. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Electricity demand from data centers will more than double by 2030 (IEA).
  • Power is local, permitted, slow to build, and politically contested.
  • Hyperscalers are already moving upstream into generation, signaling the bottleneck.
  • Efficiency gains will not reduce total demand (Jevons).
Implicit premises:
  • Firms that wait until scarcity is acute will pay a structural penalty.
  • Distinctive energy access is a durable source of competitive advantage, not a commodity to be procured at market.
  • The enterprise has time-horizon and balance-sheet capacity to enter long-duration contracts now.
Inferences:
  • Firms with weaker procurement capabilities or shorter planning horizons will be disproportionately penalized.
  • Geographies with constrained grids will see capital flight in AI workload placement.
Non-hyperscalers should pursue optionality (long-term contracts, multi-region placement) rather than ownership. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Owning generation is outside most enterprises' competitive zone.
  • PPAs, VPPAs, green tariffs, and colocation reservations are available instruments.
  • Hyperscalers' own moves prove the value of upstream contractual positioning.
Implicit premises:
  • Capital markets and regulators will continue to support these instruments at scale.
  • Cloud providers will continue to offer transparency into regional power sourcing — or buyers can demand it.
Inferences:
  • Procurement organizations will become a strategically more important function in AI-heavy firms.
  • Sustainability and finance functions will need to merge analytically.
AI workload design and routing should be optimized for energy intensity. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Many firms send simple tasks to frontier models.
  • Smaller models, caching, quantization, batching, and region-shifting reduce energy draw.
  • These levers are available to firms with no control over generation infrastructure.
Implicit premises:
  • Engineering teams have the visibility and incentive to do this optimization once measurement exists.
  • The energy savings are large enough to matter at the level of strategic decisions, not just operating expense.
Inferences:
  • 'Intelligence per watt' could become a benchmark category that vendors compete on publicly.
  • Cloud-provider routing services may emerge as energy-aware orchestrators.
Energy decisions require new cross-functional governance (the Compute and Energy Council). strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Energy spans technology, finance, procurement, and sustainability — no single function owns it.
  • Existing approval processes do not require model-efficiency or grid-risk review.
Implicit premises:
  • A committee with veto power will actually be empowered by the CEO/board.
  • Adding governance overhead will not meaningfully slow AI deployment velocity (or, if it does, that's an acceptable trade).
Inferences:
  • Firms without a CSO or strong sustainability function will struggle to staff the council credibly.
  • Veto-bearing councils may collide with product organizations that view AI as a speed game.

Narratives

  • Microsoft signs a 20-year PPA with Constellation to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1 (Crane Clean Energy Center), ~835 MW carbon-free.
    → Concretizes the claim that hyperscalers are moving upstream into generation and that long-duration contracts are the instrument of choice.
  • Meta launches RFP targeting 1-4 GW of new U.S. nuclear generation.
    → Evidence that AI demand has reached scales that pull firms into civil-nuclear procurement — frames the urgency.
  • AWS acquires Talen's data-center campus adjacent to Susquehanna nuclear station; Google contracts with Kairos Power for advanced nuclear.
    → Supports the proposition that compute placement is now an energy decision.
  • DeepSeek's 2025 release dramatically lowered reported training costs.
    → Sets up the Jevons-paradox counter — efficiency doesn't save us.
  • Pinterest optimizes recommendation systems with infrastructure-budget-aware design and Pixie for real-time recs.
    → Demonstrates that demand-side optimization is achievable for non-hyperscaler firms.
  • Salesforce's Sustainable AI framework reports energy at workload level to inform model selection.
    → Shows that 'intelligence per watt' as a management metric is operationally feasible today.
  • European firms (Spotify, H&M) shifting analytics workloads to Nordic data-center regions.
    → Concretizes the 'redesign where compute runs' recommendation.
  • GM, H&M, AB InBev, Target signing large corporate VPPAs — originally framed as sustainability, now extended for AI demand.
    → Shows that the instruments for optionality already exist and are being repurposed.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What is the next strategic bottleneck for AI?
    Electricity / physical energy infrastructure.
  • ✅ Why doesn't model efficiency (e.g., DeepSeek) solve the energy problem?
    Jevons paradox — efficiency expands demand more than it reduces unit cost.
  • ✅ Should non-hyperscalers vertically integrate into power generation?
    No — pursue contractual optionality instead.
  • ✅ How should enterprises operationalize energy as a strategic input?
    Five-action playbook plus a Compute and Energy Council.
  • ⭕ Who, organizationally, owns the AI-energy strategy decision today?
    The article proposes a council but does not engage with the reality that in most firms no one owns this and the article assumes the CEO will appoint and empower the council.
  • ⭕ Should energy constraints shape which AI products and features get built in the first place?
    The article treats energy as a deployment and procurement question, not as a constraint on product strategy or AI architecture choices upstream of build decisions.
  • ⭕ What if power simply isn't available at scale where firms need it, even with optionality contracts?
    The article assumes optionality contracts translate to actual delivered power; grid-build timelines and political contestation are mentioned but not engaged with as binding constraints.

Gaps

  • incompleteTreats energy as a procurement-and-deployment problem (route smaller models, sign PPAs, pick Nordic regions) rather than as a strategic constraint that should reshape AI architecture decisions — e.g., whether to deploy agentic loops at all, whether to build retrieval-heavy systems vs. cached responses, whether to build AI features whose value-per-watt is negative.
    Read next: Sasha Luccioni and Hugging Face on AI energy accounting; Andy Masley and effective-altruist energy debates; Kate Crawford 'Atlas of AI' on AI's material substrate.
  • incompleteThe proposed governance body (CIO + CFO + procurement + sustainability) reproduces the assumption that AI is something the IT/procurement axis manages. It does not engage with the possibility that energy-bounded AI is a board-level capital-allocation question on par with major capex, nor that engineering leadership (CTO, head of AI) should own intelligence-per-watt as a product KPI.
    Read next: Recent CTO-track writing on AI infra strategy; capital-allocation literature on industrial transitions.
  • incompleteJevons is invoked rhetorically but the article doesn't engage with the implication: if demand will always grow to consume available efficiency gains, then efficiency-focused recommendations (Action 1, Action 2) only delay the bottleneck for the individual firm without changing the aggregate trajectory. The piece offers no view on whether firms should care about aggregate trajectory.
    Read next: Climate-economics literature on rebound effects; Vaclav Smil on energy transitions.
  • incompleteNo discussion of grid externalities — what happens to non-AI users and communities when hyperscalers and incumbents lock up 20-year nuclear PPAs and Nordic colocation. The framing is purely firm-strategic; the political economy is mentioned ('politically contested') but not analyzed.
    Read next: Reporting on data-center power disputes in Virginia, Ireland, and the U.S. Midwest; energy-justice literature.
  • incompleteThe Great Value Loop framework is presented as a recurring pattern but the article does not interrogate whether the energy era differs structurally — physical infrastructure has 20+ year build timelines, hard geographic constraints, and political sovereignty implications that the previous three layers (connectivity, attention, compute) did not.
    Read next: History of electrification (Hughes 'Networks of Power'); literature on infrastructure lock-in and path dependence.
  • uninformedDoes not engage with on-device / edge inference as an alternative architectural lever for reducing data-center electricity demand. The five actions all assume cloud-resident inference.
    Read next: Apple Intelligence and on-device LLM literature; small-language-model research from Mistral, Microsoft Phi, etc.
