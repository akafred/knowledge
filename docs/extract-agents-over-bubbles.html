Agents Over Bubbles

Ben Thompson (Stratechery) · article · 2026-03 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Agent HarnessAgentic AIAI Bubble DebateCompute & Energy InfrastructureAgencyMoats & Defensibility
The emergence of AI agents — which integrate models with software harnesses and multiply the output of a small number of agentic humans — means the massive AI infrastructure investment is justified demand rather than speculative bubble, and positions integrated model-plus-harness companies (Anthropic, OpenAI) as the durable winners in the value chain.

practical · argumentative — The article argues a specific thesis — that AI is not in a bubble — by marshaling evidence from industry trends, economic logic, and the evolution of LLM paradigms. It is practical in that it draws investment and business-strategy conclusions.

Key terms

  • agent / agentic workload Agentic AI
    A software system where a harness directs a model, uses deterministic tools to verify results, and operates without continuous human involvement — distinct from chatbots or reasoning models
  • harness Agent Harness
    The software layer that controls the model, manages tool use, and orchestrates agentic workflows (e.g., Claude Code). The harness is what differentiates agents from raw models.
  • agency (human) Agency
    The initiative and willingness of a human to direct AI toward productive ends; Thompson argues agents reduce the number of humans who need this quality
  • integration vs. modular Moats & Defensibility
    Borrowed from Clayton Christensen / Thompson's prior work: profits flow to integrated (differentiated) parts of a value chain, not modular (commoditized) parts
  • paradigm (LLM paradigm)
    A distinct mode of LLM usage: (1) chatbot, (2) reasoning model, (3) agent — each with qualitatively different compute profiles and user relationships

Arguments

We are not in an AI bubble — the massive capex investment is warranted. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Agents dramatically increase compute demand per task (multiple model calls, CPU for harness, tool use).
  • A small number of agentic humans can generate enormous compute demand by controlling multiple agents.
  • Enterprise economic incentives (cost savings + top-line growth) create irresistible pull toward agent adoption.
Implicit premises:
  • Demand that is driven by measurable enterprise ROI is fundamentally different from speculative demand.
  • The three paradigms represent a monotonically improving trajectory that will continue.
Inferences:
  • The combination of fewer humans needed, higher compute per task, and strong economic incentives means demand will exceed supply for the foreseeable future.
Anthropic and OpenAI are not overvalued because they own the integration point in the AI value chain. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Agent quality depends on model-harness integration, not model alone.
  • Profits flow to integrated (differentiated) parts of value chains, not modular (commoditized) parts.
  • Microsoft's Copilot Cowork abandoned model-agnosticism in favor of Anthropic's integrated stack.
Implicit premises:
  • The integration advantage is durable — competitors cannot replicate it easily.
  • The value-chain structure from hardware/software applies analogously to model/harness.
Inferences:
  • If models require integrated harnesses to deliver agent-quality experiences, then the model layer is not commoditized, and integrated providers capture surplus value.
Enterprises will replace human coordination layers with agents, leading to significant workforce reductions. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • A small number of people drive most value in organizations; the rest form a coordination apparatus.
  • That apparatus carries massive coordination costs with diminishing returns.
  • Agents tilt toward pure acceleration without the drag of human coordination.
Implicit premises:
  • Human coordination costs are the primary drag on organizational productivity.
  • Agents can adequately perform the roles currently filled by 'human cogs' in the organizational machine.
Inferences:
  • Companies will cut more rather than less, rebuilding scale with agents, or face competitive disadvantage from AI-native entrants.

Narratives

  • The three LLM paradigms: ChatGPT (2022), o1 (2024), Opus 4.5 + Claude Code (late 2025)
    → Historical scaffold that makes the agent paradigm feel like inevitable progression rather than hype
  • Opus 4.5 released quietly in November 2025, but became transformative only when Claude Code harness was updated in December
    → Key evidence that the harness — not the model — is the differentiator, supporting the integration argument
  • Microsoft's pivot from model-agnostic Core AI strategy to Copilot Cowork built on Anthropic's integrated stack
    → Serves as empirical falsification of the 'models are commodities' thesis
  • Asus CFO Nick Wu downplaying the MacBook Neo as 'just content consumption'
    → Illustrates that consumers primarily consume rather than produce, setting up why enterprise (not consumer) is the real AI market
  • Horace Dediu's argument that Apple made 'The Most Brilliant Move in Corporate History' by not building its own model
    → Serves as the strongest counter-argument to Thompson's thesis, which he then systematically dismantles via the agent integration argument

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Is the current AI investment a speculative bubble?
    No — agents create justified demand through higher compute per task, fewer humans needed, and irresistible enterprise economics.
  • ✅ Are AI models being commoditized, and if so, where will profits flow?
    Models alone may commoditize, but agents require model-harness integration, which is differentiated. Profits flow to Anthropic and OpenAI as integration points.
  • ✅ Why is AI compute demand exceeding supply despite limited consumer adoption?
    Agents multiply compute per user, and a small number of agentic humans can drive enormous demand without requiring mass adoption.
  • ⭕ Will AI primarily eliminate jobs or create new productivity?
    Thompson argues both — enterprises will cut coordination layers and use agents to make remaining high-value employees more productive — but acknowledges 'this is going to get ugly.'
  • ⭕ Is Apple's strategy of licensing models and building its own harness viable?
    Thompson is skeptical but leaves open the possibility that consumers don't care about agent quality, in which case Apple's approach suffices.

  • incompleteThe durability of model-harness integration as a moat is asserted but not deeply examined. Could open-source harnesses (e.g., LangChain, custom orchestrators) eventually commoditize the harness layer too?
    Read next: Explore whether open-source agent frameworks are closing the gap with proprietary harnesses like Claude Code.
    closed byHugging Face's Clem Delangue: Stop Comparing Engines to Cars — Directly addresses whether the harness moat can be commoditized: open models only look weaker because harnesses were optimized for proprietary APIs, implying open harness+model stacks can close the gap (p1, p3).
  • incompleteThompson's claim that agents can replace 'human cogs' in organizational coordination assumes agents can handle ambiguous, political, and relational tasks — not just execution tasks like coding. No evidence is offered for this.
    Read next: Research on AI in organizational coordination, decision-making, and management tasks.
  • incompleteThe article focuses almost entirely on coding agents as the exemplar of the agent paradigm. Whether agents generalize to non-coding domains with equal effectiveness is unexamined.
    Read next: Look for agent applications outside software engineering — legal, financial, scientific, operational.
    closed byBuilding AI Agents For The Enterprise — Documents agent effectiveness outside coding — L'Oreal conversational analytics and Lyft customer support with quantified results — addressing whether agents generalize beyond software engineering.
  • incompleteThe social and political consequences of the 'impending wave of layoffs' are acknowledged but not analyzed. Thompson says 'I'm not advocating for this outcome, rather analyzing why it is probably going to happen' — but the systemic effects (inequality, political backlash, regulatory response) are left entirely unexplored.
