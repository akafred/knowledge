As agentic engineering matures into human-built-and-operated 'software factories,' the decisive questions of software engineering shift from raw automation to governance — where human judgment and sign-off sit in the automated loop, how platforms (rather than universal upskilling) safely absorb citizen developers, what would keep developer tooling relevant, and how organizations manage the widening gap between teams that codify their processes early and those that don't.

practical · expository — A practitioner's reflection organizing four themes from an unconference into a forward-looking view of agentic software engineering; largely expository and prescriptive, with predictive threads about where the discipline is heading.

Key terms

  • software factory Software Factory
    The human-built-and-operated backbone that builds the software system — the automated delivery machine that codifies development steps into reusable agentic loops — as distinct from the product it produces; developers build, operate, and set the constraints under which it is allowed to evolve
  • hand-off points
    Explicit points in an otherwise automated pipeline where human interaction and sign-off is required — idea→plan, plan→execute, change-request→deploy, incident→remediation — chosen according to business needs and appetite for risk
  • citizen developers Citizen Developer
    Non-engineers who build applications with AI assistance, without traditional software-engineering training
  • background agents / guardrail agents
    Highly specialized, increasingly autonomous agents that compensate for lower-quality code produced by coding agents and protect system invariants — e.g. refactoring code, applying reliability patterns, writing missing tests, reorganizing test suites to cut execution time
  • growing gaps
    The widening divergence between teams/companies that codified their processes early and reap agentic acceleration and peers who are slow to adapt

Arguments

Platforms that separate concerns, not universal software-engineering upskilling, are the right way to safely absorb citizen developers. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • With AI, citizen developers can build applications but tempt organizations back into 'they build it, you run it'.
  • Training citizen developers to have risk intuition pays off, but requiring everyone to acquire baseline engineering skills is not the only path.
  • Platforms can offer user-level abstractions on a self-healing foundation and ship access/data guardrails that make failure modes disappear.
Implicit premises:
  • Baseline engineering competence cannot be scaled to every citizen developer quickly enough.
  • Encoding safety into a shared platform is cheaper and more reliable than distributing that responsibility to each non-engineer.
Inferences:
  • Therefore the burden of safe operation should be absorbed by a constrained platform rather than by upskilling every builder or by an engineering team operating others' apps.
Human judgment remains essential but should be concentrated at a few explicit hand-off points rather than spread across the whole pipeline. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • The rest of the loop (implementation, verification, remediation collection) can be automated with the right tools and guardrails.
  • Each hand-off (idea→plan, plan→execute, change-request→deploy, incident→remediation) is a natural anchor where business knowledge and trade-offs matter.
  • Each hand-off can itself be augmented — risk measures, spec/regulatory checks, auto-collected incident summaries — to support the decision.
Implicit premises:
  • Concentrating judgment at defined checkpoints keeps delivery legible and in check without blocking automation everywhere.
  • The chosen checkpoints correspond to where irreversible or high-stakes decisions actually occur.
Inferences:
  • Therefore explicit, augmentable hand-off points are the mechanism for keeping human oversight meaningful in an automated factory.
Agentic engineering will widen the gap between organizations, favoring early process-codifiers and small teams. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Agentic engineering amplifies both good and bad practices.
  • It is easier for one member of a small team to build shared guardrails and skills in code.
  • Existing companies risk fragmentation on process and tech stack, and shared-tooling efforts depend on org structure and reuse culture.
Implicit premises:
  • Amplification compounds over time rather than being a one-off boost.
  • Organizational structure and culture change more slowly than the tooling that rewards codification.
Inferences:
  • Therefore divergence between fast and slow adapters is the expected trajectory absent deliberate investment in shared platforms and reuse.

Narratives

  • The 'holy grail' of a self-evolving system where a feature request is 'magically built' into the existing system, from which the author derives the name 'software factory' — the backbone that humans build and operate.
    → Frames the aspirational endpoint of the whole piece and fixes the enduring human role (building/operating/constraining the factory) against it
  • The self-healing database example: a foundation that automatically increases volume before running out of space or adds an index to speed up expensive queries, making those failure modes 'disappear' for the user within set constraints.
    → Concretizes how a platform can absorb operational concerns so citizen developers need not know about them from the outset

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Where do developers sit in the automated software-factory process?
    They build and operate the backbone that builds the system and set the constraints under which it evolves, exercising judgment at explicit hand-off/sign-off points chosen by business need and risk appetite.
  • ✅ How do we avoid a culture of throwing AI-generated apps over the fence for others to operate?
    Partly by training citizen developers' risk intuition, but chiefly by building platforms that separate concerns, offer safe abstractions on a self-healing foundation, and ship access/data guardrails.
  • ⭕ Will we still need IDEs?
    IDEs are increasingly bypassed for coding CLIs, but could remain relevant by exposing deterministic tools, compilation/test access, agent-output annotation, agent-skill support, multi-session orchestration, and telemetry insights — likely as a new bundled product.
  • ⭕ What organizational structures and team shapes will succeed with agentic engineering?
    Left explicitly open: it is clearer where human judgement is needed than what org shapes will prevail or correlate with successful outcomes.
