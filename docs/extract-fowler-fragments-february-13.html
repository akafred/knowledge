As LLMs become established coding collaborators, software development is reorganizing around 'supervisory programming' in which developers herd fleets of agents — a shift that recasts the profession's central challenges as preserving a team's shared understanding of the system (cognitive debt), sustaining a developer experience that is effectively identical to agent experience, and containing the work-intensification and context-switching that driving many agents creates.

mixed · mixed — A set of loosely connected fragments — field notes from the Thoughtworks Future of Software Development Retreat plus reactions to others' posts. Mostly expository (reporting attendees' observations) with argumentative interludes (Fowler's cognitive-debt reframing, his inclination on team size) and forward-looking conjecture.

Key terms

  • cognitive debt Cognitive Debt
    The accumulating cost of a team losing its shared 'theory of the system' — the understanding of why design decisions were made and how parts fit together; Fowler maps it onto his technical-debt metaphor, where the cognitive equivalent of cruft is ignorance of the code and its domain
  • cruft
    The bad stuff itself that accumulates in a codebase (poor module boundaries, bad naming); Fowler distinguishes it from the debt metaphor, which is his tool for reasoning about the costs cruft imposes — cognitively, the equivalent of cruft is ignorance
  • The Genie The Genie (Beck)
    Fowler's (Beck-derived) name for the LLM as a code-generating collaborator; invoked as a party to whom the same costs and legibility benefits apply as to humans ('The Genie's Galaxy Brain', 'The Genies')
  • Developer Experience = Agent Experience
    The thesis, credited to Laura Tacho, that the practices that improve developer experience also improve LLM effectiveness — 'The Venn Diagram of Developer Experience and Agent Experience is a circle'
  • supervisory programming / supervisory programmer
    The anticipated shift from programmers engaged directly with the code to programmers herding a bunch of agents while remaining accountable for the code generated under their watch
  • two-pizza team
    The team-size heuristic Fowler invokes to argue teams will not shrink to 'one-pizza' size just because 'LLMs don't eat pizza'; the size balances collaboration benefits against coordination costs
  • so January
    A quip that outdated dismissals of LLM capability are already stale — 'some negative opinions of LLM capabilities are so January' — given how fast models have improved

Arguments

Loss of shared system understanding should be managed deliberately as a form of debt — cognitive debt — analogous to but distinct from technical debt. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Storey's student teams could no longer make simple changes by weeks 7-8, and the real problem was not messy code but that no one could explain why design decisions had been made or how parts fit together — their shared theory of the system had fragmented.
  • In Fowler's technical-debt framing, 'cruft' is the bad stuff itself, while the debt metaphor is a way to reason about the costs cruft imposes: pay interest or pay down principal.
  • The cognitive equivalent of cruft is ignorance of the code and the domain it supports.
Implicit premises:
  • A team's ability to change software depends on shared understanding, not only on code quality.
  • The interest-versus-principal cost logic that works for technical debt transfers cleanly to knowledge.
Inferences:
  • Therefore one either accepts higher ongoing costs (harder future changes) or makes an explicit investment to gain knowledge, choosing by relative cost — and these costs now fall on both humans and the Genie.
Investment in developer experience is simultaneously investment in agent effectiveness, so the two are worth funding together. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Tacho's claim that the Venn diagram of Developer Experience and Agent Experience is a circle.
  • Smooth tooling and clear information about the development environment help LLMs figure out how to create code quickly and correctly.
  • There is growing evidence that good modularity and descriptive naming are as good for the transformer as for human ('squishy') neural networks.
Implicit premises:
  • LLMs and humans face structurally similar comprehension bottlenecks when navigating a codebase.
  • Even a high-capability model (the Genie's 'Galaxy Brain') still benefits from a legible codebase rather than merely tolerating a confusing one.
Inferences:
  • Therefore the same practices serve both audiences and DevEx work is dual-purpose — though it is telling that execs will make this effort for the robots but not for the humans.
LLMs will not shrink development teams; teams of roughly the same size will do much more. strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • The two-pizza team size effectively balances the benefits of human collaboration against the costs of coordination.
  • LLMs 'don't eat pizza' — agents add capacity without adding to the human coordination unit.
Implicit premises:
  • The binding constraint on human team size is coordination cost, not the volume of code that can be produced.
  • Adding agent capacity raises throughput without changing the human coordination calculus.
Inferences:
  • Therefore the equilibrium human team size is stable and the added agent capacity increases output rather than reducing headcount.

Narratives

  • In an entrepreneurship course, student teams shipped features fast but hit a wall by weeks 7-8, unable to make even simple changes without breaking things; they blamed technical debt, but the real problem was that no one could explain why decisions had been made or how the parts fit — their shared theory of the system had fragmented. They had accumulated cognitive debt faster than technical debt, and it paralyzed them.
    → Concrete illustration that grounds the cognitive-debt concept and motivates Fowler's debt-metaphor reframing
  • Senior developers who were very resistant to using LLMs were put through an exercise that forced hands-on work with them; a third were instantly converted to being very pro-LLM. As was quipped, some negative opinions of LLM capabilities 'are so January'.
    → Evidence that experience, not argument, shifts skeptics — and that model capabilities move fast enough to date opinions within weeks
  • A domain rename of 'person' to 'contact' spans function names, field names, documentation, and test cases, so a simple search-replace is not enough; rather than run an LLM over the whole codebase, the LLM chooses to orchestrate the IDE's refactoring capabilities on each site it finds — and an attendee noted that renames analyzed in an IDE occur in clusters like this.
    → Concretizes the thesis that LLMs should choreograph deterministic IDE features rather than replace them
  • An eight-month study of a ~200-employee U.S. technology company found that generative AI led employees to work at a faster pace, take on a broader scope of tasks, and extend work into more hours of the day, often unbidden; once the novelty faded, workload creep drove cognitive fatigue, burnout, and lower-quality work.
    → Evidence for the work-intensification cost of AI adoption and a counterweight to the productivity-surge optimism

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What role do senior developers play as LLMs become established?
    They retain a bright future by focusing more on architectural issues and by managing LLM agents much as they would manage junior developers.
  • ✅ Will junior developers be replaced by untiring agents?
    No — juniors will still be needed (partly for their open-minded LLM fluency) and can use LLMs as a mentor; it is mid-level developers who face the greatest challenge.
  • ✅ How should we think about the loss of a team's shared understanding of its system?
    As cognitive debt, reasoned about with the technical-debt metaphor: cruft is ignorance of code and domain, and one pays interest or pays down principal by investing in knowledge.
  • ✅ Should organizations still invest in Developer Experience given LLMs?
    Yes — because Developer Experience and Agent Experience coincide, the same investments benefit both humans and LLMs.
  • ⭕ What will programming work look like as agents proliferate?
    A shift toward supervisory programming — herding many agents while remaining accountable for their code — with context-switching as the key emerging problem and effective workflows still to be discovered.
