Hands-On Leadership & the PendulumTeam AutonomyTechnology Leadership (Heis)
Heis is a podcast about technology leadership across the different organizational floors of a company — riding the elevator up and down to talk with leaders at every level — built on the conviction that autonomous teams still need leadership, that traditional management does not fit the team model, and that the leaders modern technology companies need are people with hands-on experience from working in teams and with technology.

practical · expository — a mission statement / show introduction in conversational interview form — Two-host introduction. Roughly 2.5 minutes. Truls opens with the metaphor; Audun delivers the show's substantive thesis about leadership and the team model. Speakers were inferred from self-introductions in the transcript — first speaker introduces 'Heis' as a metaphor and then asks Audun to explain why people should listen, so the opener is Truls (he later identifies himself by name); Audun then speaks at length about leadership.

Arguments

Organizations using autonomous-team models need a new kind of leadership — one that plays on the team rather than directs it — and that leadership must come from people with hands-on team and technology experience.
Implicit premises:
  • Leadership style and operating model must be coherent with each other — a team-based operating model cannot be paired with hierarchical leadership without dysfunction
  • Outsider leaders without team/technology experience cannot intuit what 'playing on the team' actually requires
  • NAV's experience generalizes: what the hosts saw there reflects a broader pattern in technology organizations
The Heis podcast is broadly relevant — its audience is not just engineers at tech firms but anyone navigating modern organizational leadership.
Implicit premises:
  • If technology reshapes leadership requirements universally, then discussions of technology leadership are universally relevant
  • Listeners outside obvious tech roles will recognize their own situation in the show

Narratives

  • Truls and Audun spent eight years together as technology principals at NAV, where they observed firsthand both the value of team autonomy and the inadequacy of old-style leadership for that operating model.
    → Establishes shared experiential authority and grounds the abstract thesis (autonomy + new leadership) in a concrete organizational context — NAV, Norway's largest public-sector technology organization, widely studied as a team-model example.
  • Truls has recently moved from NAV to the private sector (now at what is almost certainly Entur, mistranscribed as 'EU' by Whisper).
    → Signals that the hosts' perspective spans public and private sector technology leadership, broadening the implied scope of the show beyond NAV-style public-sector organizations.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe thesis that traditional leadership doesn't work with autonomous teams is stated as observed fact ('vi så i NAV') but never specified. Which behaviors of old-style leadership failed? What were the visible symptoms? Without examples, the claim functions as a slogan rather than an argument — appropriate for a welcome episode, but the show will need to deliver the substance.
  • incomplete'Leadership that plays on the team' (spiller på lag med teammodellen) is the show's positive claim but is left almost entirely uncashed in this episode. The metaphor is suggestive (cooperative, aligned) but does not yet distinguish this style from related ideas — servant leadership, Team Topologies-style enabling teams, or Cutler-style 'conditions over directives' leadership.
    closed by10) Una Aamodt Mathisen om å lede når du ikke har fasiten — Cashes out what 'leadership that plays on the team' means in practice: publicly acknowledging not knowing, a three-phase role (listening, hypothesis-shaped direction, closing the learning loop), direction and autonomy as mutually constitutive, and restraint toward distrust signals.
  • incompleteThe argument that this kind of leadership requires experience 'from working in teams and with technology' is asserted but not defended. It excludes by implication leaders from non-engineering backgrounds; whether and why that exclusion is justified is not addressed.
  • incompleteThe 'every company is a technology company' claim is delivered as a closer without supporting argument. It is a common premise in contemporary tech-leadership discourse but the episode does not engage with the well-known counter-readings (that 'tech company' loses meaning when applied universally; that technology intensity is a spectrum, not a binary).
  • incompleteThe elevator metaphor implies vertical movement between hierarchical levels, but the show's stated commitment is to a team-model world where hierarchical levels are deliberately flattened. There is a latent tension between 'visit leaders on every floor' (which implies floors exist as distinct strata) and 'leadership that plays on the team' (which implies the strata are less salient). The episode does not address this tension.
