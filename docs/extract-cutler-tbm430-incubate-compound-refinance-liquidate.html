TBM 430: Incubate, Compound, Refinance, Liquidate

John Cutler · email · 2026-07-12 Reader 🖥️

AI as AmplifierCoordination CostLegacy & Organizational ForgettingPlatform EngineeringTechnical Debt
Software assets have a position and a trajectory in a value-vs-carrying-cost economy (incubate, compound, refinance, or liquidate), and because AI only changes the cost/speed/feasibility of moving through that space rather than eliminating the underlying complexity and coordination it's built from, the right question about any AI adoption is not whether it makes work faster but which portfolio movement it is accelerating.

practical · mixed — Primarily expository: presents and defines a 2x2 portfolio framework (value vs. carrying cost), its quadrants (Incubate/Compound/Refinance/Liquidate), and named failure patterns (Build Trap, Limits to Growth, Success Trap, platform traps). But it closes with an explicit argumentative claim about how AI adoption should be evaluated, and drops normative assertions along the way ('liquidation is not necessarily failure'), so it argues for a way of deciding, not just for understanding.

Key terms

  • Carrying cost
    The ongoing cost of operating, maintaining, supporting, and coordinating around a software asset over time — distinct from and often larger than the cost of building it.
  • Debt (technical debt)
    A financing choice: accepting higher future carrying costs in exchange for reaching value sooner. Its moral status (productive leverage, expensive constraint, or liability) is not intrinsic to the debt but depends on where the carrying asset sits in the value/cost space.
  • Movement / portfolio movement
    The direction, speed, and intentionality with which an asset's position on the 2x2 changes over time — treated as more diagnostically important than the static position itself.
  • Build Trap
    The specific movement pattern where an asset in Incubate fails to prove value and become Compound, but the team keeps building anyway — moving right (rising cost) without moving up (rising value).
  • Platform (good vs. bad) Platform Engineering
    A platform is 'good' or 'bad' purely by whether it improves the value/cost economics of the assets depending on it — not by its own usage numbers or shipping velocity.

Arguments

The right question for evaluating AI adoption is not 'will this make us faster' but 'faster in which direction' — i.e., which portfolio movement is being accelerated. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • AI does not enter a neutral system; it enters a portfolio with a particular mix of bets, winners, drag, and aging assets.
  • The same AI capability can improve portfolio economics or deepen the exact pattern that created the problem — e.g., helping a Build Trap ship faster without more evidence of value, or helping a Success Trap over-optimize its current winner instead of exploring what's next.
Implicit premises:
  • Speed itself is not inherently valuable; speed in the wrong direction accelerates dysfunction rather than resolving it.
  • The value of any engineering intervention, including AI, can only be judged relative to an asset's or team's current position and trajectory in the portfolio, not in the abstract.
Inferences:
  • An AI rollout should be preceded by a portfolio-position diagnosis (which quadrant/trap is this team or asset in) rather than a blanket capability rollout applied uniformly across an org.
  • Two teams using identical AI tooling could see opposite outcomes — one escaping a trap, one entrenching it — purely because of differing starting portfolio positions, which would make aggregate org-wide 'AI productivity' metrics close to meaningless unless segmented by portfolio position.
Liquidating a software asset is often the economically correct move, not a failure, and the more dangerous failure mode is refusing to liquidate. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Some bets never prove much value; when the team keeps building anyway, the product moves right (cost rises) without moving up (value rises), turning a cheap experiment into an expensive obligation.
  • An asset's remaining value can fall below what it costs to keep carrying it.
Implicit premises:
  • Organizations have an asymmetric bias toward continuing sunk investments rather than toward over-liquidating (a version of sunk-cost/loss-aversion behavior), which is why the corrective message needs to be 'liquidate more readily,' not 'liquidate carefully.'
  • Liquidation decisions are organizationally hard to make for reasons independent of their economic correctness (politics, identity, sunk effort) — otherwise the reminder that 'liquidation is not failure' would be unnecessary.
Inferences:
  • Organizations likely need an explicit, low-stigma process or ritual for retiring software assets, analogous to how finance has normalized processes for writing off bad investments, rather than leaving liquidation to informal, reputationally-costly individual calls.

Narratives

  • The arc of a startup: begins with low value and low carrying cost (a cheap bet); some bets prove value and move toward Compound; as they scale, carrying costs rise, pushing them toward Refinance; a healthy company then either restores the economics or eventually Liquidates when remaining value no longer justifies the carrying cost.
    → Establishes the expected, healthy lifecycle trajectory through all four quadrants, which then serves as the baseline against which the Build Trap and other traps are defined as deviations.
  • The named traps — Build Trap (Incubate fails to become Compound but keeps costing), Limits to Growth (Compound succeeds, then steadily gives way to Refinance), Success Trap (current winners dominate so completely the next generation of Incubate assets disappears) — each described as a specific, recognizable failure of the expected movement.
    → Converts an abstract 2x2 diagram into a diagnostic vocabulary — memorable labels a reader can use to pattern-match their own organization's dysfunction, which is what makes the framework 'actionable' rather than purely descriptive.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Is technical debt inherently good or bad?
    Neither — debt is a financing choice whose value depends entirely on the asset's quadrant: dangerous in Incubate (obligations before value is proven), rational leverage in Compound, a signal to restructure in Refinance, and a reason to Liquidate once it no longer creates enough value to justify itself.
  • ✅ Does AI make software organizations uniformly faster or better?
    No — AI's effect depends entirely on which existing portfolio movement it accelerates; the same capability can resolve or deepen the exact dysfunction present in a given quadrant or trap.
  • ⭕ How would 'value' and 'carrying cost' actually be measured or agreed upon in a real organization, so that an asset's quadrant placement is more than an eyeballed guess?
    Not addressed. The post treats placement on the 2x2 as self-evident ('place each software asset according to the value it creates and what it costs to carry') without discussing how contested, political, or time-horizon-dependent that placement typically is in practice.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe framework never explains how 'value' and 'carrying cost' would actually be measured or estimated in a real organization — placement on the 2x2 is presented as if self-evident, but in practice these are contested, political judgments (whose carrying cost? measured how? over what time horizon?) that determine which quadrant an asset lands in, and therefore which portfolio move looks correct.
    Read next: Look at how quantified engineering-management approaches operationalize similar axes — e.g. tech-debt quantification tooling, DORA metrics as a proxy for carrying cost/friction, or finance's own NPV/IRR discipline for comparing bets — to see whether a rigorous measurement layer could be bolted onto Cutler's diagram, or whether the ambiguity is actually load-bearing (forcing an explicit conversation being more valuable than a false-precision score).
  • incompleteThe analogy to financial debt and refinancing is pushed hard (incubate = seed bet, compound = growth, refinance = restructure debt, liquidate = write off) but the post never examines where the software case genuinely diverges from finance — software 'debt' compounds through coupling and lost institutional knowledge in ways that aren't just an interest rate, and 'refinancing' a codebase (a rewrite or major refactor) carries execution risk that refinancing a loan does not.
    Read next: Cross-read against critiques of the technical-debt metaphor itself (Ward Cunningham's original, narrower framing vs. its looser popular use) and against systems-thinking treatments of software complexity (Meadows on stocks/flows, or DeMarco/Lister on the limits of financial metaphors for engineering work) to pressure-test where 'software as a portfolio of financial assets' breaks down — particularly around irreversibility and knowledge loss, which have no clean financial equivalent.
  • uninformedThe term 'Zombie Portfolio' is used once, in the AI section, without ever being defined or placed on the diagram — the reader has to infer it means a portfolio dominated by aging, over-carried assets nobody liquidates, but this is guesswork since the post's own explicit vocabulary (Incubate/Compound/Refinance/Liquidate) doesn't include it.
    Read next: Check other issues of The Beautiful Mess newsletter for a prior or later post that defines 'Zombie Portfolio' explicitly — this reads like a term from elsewhere in Cutler's series that the post assumes the reader already knows.
  • incompleteThe six platform traps (False Economies of Scale, Platform Too Early, Platform Too Late, Adoption Tax, Mandate Trap, Dependency Bottleneck) are listed but never explicitly re-mapped onto the core 2x2 mechanics — e.g. which trap corresponds to a platform misdiagnosed as 'Compound' when it's actually 'Refinance,' or how 'Adoption Tax' amounts to carrying cost being moved off the platform's own ledger and onto its consumers' ledgers, undercounting the platform's true cost.
    Read next: Worth doing directly: re-map each of the six platform traps onto the quadrants/movements framework to see whether they're genuinely new failure modes or just special cases of Build Trap / Success Trap applied to shared infrastructure — useful raw material for a follow-up note connecting this concept to platform-strategy and internal-developer-platform literature.
