2) Hans Christian Holte dusjer i informasjon

Truls Jørgensen og Audun Fauchald Strand — Heis Podcast · podcast · 2025-10-20 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Go to the Floor (Gemba)Public-Sector DigitalizationTeam AutonomyTechnology Leadership (Heis)
What senior public-sector leadership at NAV's scale actually consists of is the disciplined acceptance that you cannot retain most of the information that washes over you — that you must filter to a few central themes, lean heavily on competent people around you, deliberately ride the elevator between strategic altitude and the work itself ('T-ledelse'), and treat technology development not as a domain you keep up with personally but as the most potent strategic lever you must integrate with legal, professional, and user-experience perspectives to bring the whole organization, not just the directorate, forward.

practical · expository — long-form interview with a senior Norwegian public-sector leader; conversational but substantive, with the host pair pressing on craft-of-leadership questions (information processing, delegation, tech-company identity, how leadership rides between organizational levels) — Hans Christian Holte is at the time of recording director of NAV (Norway's labor and welfare directorate, 22,000 employees, ~635 billion NOK in benefits annually). Previously director of Skatteetaten (tax authority) and Difi (Directorate for Public Administration and ICT). The hosts (Truls and Audun) both worked under him at NAV. The episode's title quotes Holte's own past phrase 'dusjer i informasjon' ('I shower in information') — Truls cites it back to him and asks him to unpack it.

Propositions

  • explicitWhat is most worth thinking about at NAV's scale is not the money flowing out but the human follow-up — helping people into working life — which sits alongside the payments and must be reconciled with them.
  • explicitA senior public-sector leader cannot retain most of the information directed at them and must accept this as a working condition.
  • explicitThe capacity to operate as NAV director is acquired through mengdetrening — a developmental path of progressively wider responsibility, more delegation, and less detail-orientation — not given by background.
  • explicitWithout placing enormous responsibility on the senior leadership team around him, Holte could not function as NAV director.
  • explicitEvery modern top leader fails — 'nearly as a top leader' — if they do not internalise what technology can mean for their organisation.
  • explicitNAV is a technology company — but an 'unredeemed' one, because technology has not yet meaningfully reshaped the NAV-office work where counsellors meet citizens.
  • explicitdevelopedLeadership of autonomous teams has two failure modes — too much traditional control and too much berøringsangst (fear of touching) — and the task is the balance: tight enough to point direction, not intrusive in execution.
    refines ←How Fast-Growing Companies Can Make Better Decisions · p4 — Supplies a concrete mechanism — curated decision options plus outcome-based accountability — for the balance Holte names between over-control and berøringsangst.
  • explicitdevelopedTeams themselves want strategic direction — Holte's experience is that 'this is what the teams actually want' — so withholding direction in the name of autonomy is a leadership failure.
    extends ←10) Una Aamodt Mathisen om å lede når du ikke har fasiten · p4 — Takes Holte's observation that teams want strategic direction to a stronger conceptual claim: direction and autonomy are mutually constitutive — drift without direction, literal compliance without autonomy.
  • explicitT-ledelse — broad strategic across the top, episodic deep dives down into specific areas — is the right shape for senior leadership at NAV's scale.
  • explicitA permanent translation function between organisational levels risks outliving its necessity — like real elevator operators who kept their jobs after elevators were automated because passengers were afraid to ride without them.
  • explicitA NAV-directorate reorganisation that the directorate finds 'super cool' but which has no effect outside the directorate has no value.
  • explicitHolte's deliberate posture on hot new technologies is to lag a bit behind — the bigger his responsibility, the less appetite and capacity for being on the front foot of any specific technology; he leans on others and adds value at the level of recognising barriers and strategic significance.
  • explicitTechnologists often answer from too narrow a base — strong on users, but weak on legal frame and on the citizen's context before and after a touchpoint — and need to be integrated with a wider professional view.
  • explicitThe best leadership meetings leave everyone slightly dissatisfied.
  • explicitThe NAV office work — supporting the human counsellor-citizen encounter with technology — has more unknown variables and is harder than benefits modernisation, which is why people stay long at NAV and keep getting new jobs.
  • implicitEngaging top leaders directly with practitioners (e.g., the hosts' 'technology coffees') reshapes the top leader, not only the practitioners — it is bidirectional.

Arguments

The senior public-sector leader's information discipline consists not in trying to retain more but in accepting structural forgetting, holding only a few central themes, and trusting a strong team to hold the rest.
Implicit premises:
  • Senior-leader information capacity does not scale with organisational scope — but distributed retention across a competent senior team does
  • Being dissatisfied with one's own retention is the correct affect; trying to fix it would mean either narrowing scope or pretending to retain what one cannot
  • The acceptance of loss is itself a learned discipline (mengdetrening), not a personal trait
A top leader's most potent value-add is integrating technology development with legal, professional and user-experience perspectives in a helicopter view — not being technologically current.
Implicit premises:
  • The helicopter view is not a substitute for technical depth — it is a different function that depends on technical depth existing below it
  • Integration is itself a skilled act that nobody else in the organisation is positioned to perform
The autonomous-team model in a regulated public-sector organisation requires leadership that gives strategic direction without becoming intrusive — and most failure modes are leadership pathologies, not team pathologies.
Implicit premises:
  • The cost of intrusive leadership is observable; the cost of berøringsangst is invisible (teams quietly drift)
  • In a regulated context, autonomy without legal integration becomes a liability, which is why integration is the leadership task
A heis-fører model of leadership — a permanent translator between organisational floors — risks ossifying; T-ledelse (episodic deep dives from a strategic top) is the better shape.
Implicit premises:
  • Roles created to compensate for missing shared understanding can become barriers to building that shared understanding
  • A leader who has been ON the team floor recently can translate without needing a dedicated translator

Narratives

  • Holte's first leadership job, around year 2000, was running Norway's Y2K response across state administration — a temporary project-leader role with a 'very clear endpoint' that gave him a broad view of statsforvaltningen (state administration) and of how vulnerable society is to technology.
    → Origin moment — frames the whole subsequent career as a person who chose the public sector over dot-com offers (which would have crashed within months) and who has had technology and broad public-sector view intertwined since day one.
  • Holte spent the year 2000 turnover in a ballroom in R5, Akersgata, in the government quarter, tracking the rollover from east to west across the world. Nothing dramatic happened. The most memorable moment was scoring a hole-in-one in PC Golf against Lars van Marion. He went to a party. A host responds: 'it could have gone really badly if we hadn't done this job in advance — Rikstrygdeverket [the predecessor agency to NAV], for example. Åge Engebretsen, who still works here, can tell you.' Holte: 'We will never know. That's the thing. It's nice to have Australia as a test environment.'
    → Closing illustration of the implicit thesis: senior public-sector leadership succeeds when nothing visible happens, because invisible preparation has neutralised the crisis. The Australia line is the ultimate dusjer-i-informasjon move — let other time zones absorb the first wave so you only have to react to what survives.
  • When Audun joined NAV in 2016, NAV did not have a shared identity as a technology company; IT was a support function. The identity shift to 'NAV is a technology company' is something Holte has both pushed and lived through.
    → Concrete grounding for the abstract claim about technology and leadership — places the identity shift in a specific window and ties it to Holte's tenure.
  • At Skatteetaten under Holte's directorship, technology turned the agency's relationship with taxpayers around through new technological solutions. Earlier still, at Difi (Direktoratet for forvaltning og IKT) — his job before Skatt — technology was the working field itself, cross-cutting across government.
    → Establishes that the 'technology as strategic lever' frame is not new for Holte at NAV — he has applied it at each successive public-sector posting.
  • NAV has carried out an organisational change in the directorate that tries to fuse product-development teams with the rest of the organisation — jurists, domain experts — into cross-functional teams in different departments. NAV is among the organisations that have tried hardest at this in Norway and possibly come the furthest, but it is hard: different professional principals have struggled to work together, and 'some people need to run a kind of elevator' between the different floors of the organisation.
    → The concrete operational story behind the heis metaphor — NAV is actively trying to make the team-model + cross-functional integration work, and the heis between floors is the proposed mechanism.
  • On the arbeidsavklaringspenger area, NAV had 'tough rounds' — they didn't think broadly enough; they didn't take in the practice of the large operating organisation well enough when working on solutions. Both leadership (who were oriented and made decisions) and technologists are implicated.
    → Concrete failure case for the 'technologists answer from too narrow a base' argument — Holte does not present himself as a critic outside the failure but as a participant in it.
  • Holte had applied for a job as a programmer at Skattedirektoratet right after upper secondary school. He did not get the job. Years later, returning as Skattedirektør (director of the tax administration), he 'mentioned' the rejected application.
    → Light biographical detail that places Holte on the technologist-becoming-leader path that the hosts (in episode 0) framed as the show's aspiration — and quietly underlines the developmental thesis (mengdetrening: trajectories accumulate over decades).
  • The hosts' 'teknologikaffe' (technology coffee) sessions with Holte. Audun: 'we thought we technologists would come and influence you. But we ended up being influenced too. And got new perspectives. And understood more of the big intractable problems. That's part of the background for this podcast.' Holte: 'I noticed you had something on your hearts. I think it is good, and I got a lot out of it too.'
    → Operationalises the T-ledelse / heis claim with the hosts' own lived case — and quietly explains the genesis of Heis itself. The translation between floors changed the top leader as much as it changed the practitioners.

Questions addressed

  • ⭕ What does Holte mean by 'dusjer i informasjon' in operational terms?
    answered — not just volume but velocity-plus-asymmetric-preparation; the discipline is to accept structural forgetting, hold a few central themes, and rely on a strong team to hold the rest
  • ⭕ How does a senior public-sector leader actually process and filter information at scale?
    answered — through mengdetrening over 25 years, progressive delegation, episodic deep dives into crises (allmenndannelse-style), and acceptance of dissatisfaction with one's own retention
  • ⭕ Does Holte think of NAV as a technology company, and what does that change about how he leads?
    answered — yes, and it changes the leader's value-add to integrating technology with legal/professional/user perspectives in a helicopter view, not being technologically current personally
  • ⭕ How does Holte navigate the tension between autonomous teams and traditional governance?
    answered — by naming the two ditches (over-control vs. berøringsangst), giving strategic direction without intrusive execution-time governance, and integrating teams with juridical and domain expertise
  • ⭕ What is the right shape for a top leader's relation to organisational levels?
    answered — T-ledelse (broad top, episodic deep dives) rather than a permanent heis-fører translation role that risks ossifying
  • ⭕ What about the people on the other side of the information shower — the counterparties who have prepared their best case for the slot?
    left_open — Holte describes their preparation as a given but does not address what it costs them, or what the system-level effect is when many people compete for scarce senior-leader attention
  • ⭕ How does Holte decide which technology trend to lean on others for vs. which to engage directly?
    left_open — he says he lags behind on hot new tech and leans on others; he does not specify the selection mechanism — when does a technology cross the threshold from 'others handle' to 'I need to engage'?
  • ⭕ How does Holte know when his attempted strategic direction has actually landed in the team-level execution?
    left_open — he asserts that teams want direction and that NAV is trying to integrate cross-functionally, but the feedback loop from execution back to him is not described
  • ⭕ What about the political dimension — Holte mentions politicians 'squinting at' the user-experience unit; what is the political cost of his current bets?
    left_open — alluded to but not elaborated

Gaps

  • incompleteThe cost side of the information shower is not addressed. Every person who 'prepares and wants to land maximum points in their slot' is paying a cost: compressing their best case, choosing what to leave out, presumably also accepting that most of their points will be among those that 'rinse down the drain.' A senior-leader-centric view of information overload treats it as a load problem on the receiver; a system view would ask what happens to the people producing the information for the shower and what the aggregate organizational effect is when many serious efforts produce one-slot compressions that the receiver does not retain. Holte's metaphor invites this question and the conversation does not pick it up.
  • incompleteThe criteria for which 'few central themes' Holte holds are not made explicit. He mentions specific filters: digital representation as a near-term must-solve at NAV offices; the NAV-office counsellor as a long-term focus; user experience as his current energise-zone. But how those particular bets emerged from the shower — what got filtered out, by what test — is not described. This is the operationally most interesting question and the most strategically sensitive; an answer might also be politically costly to give.
  • unaddressedThe piece does not address what happens when Holte's filtering is wrong — when a theme that should have made it through the filter did not. The arbeidsavklaringspenger case is named as a failure of broad-enough thinking, but the role of his own information processing in that failure is not interrogated. A self-aware leader could productively unpack the failure mode of accepted forgetting: the information you accept losing may include exactly the information you should have kept.
  • incompletePublic-sector context shapes what Holte can say. As sitting NAV director he must be careful about politically loaded territory (the stat/kommune split at NAV offices, decisions about digital identity, the politics around the user-experience unit). The conversation rides these edges and signals them ('certain politicians are squinting at us') but cannot fully unpack them. This is a structural feature of the format, not a flaw — but it limits how much of Holte's actual filtering criteria can surface.
  • incompleteThe 'T-ledelse' reframe of the hosts' heis-fører concept is elegant, but the question of where T-ledelse fits in a 22,000-person organisation with multiple levels — and whether T-ledelse from the very top can substitute for translation roles at the middle — is not resolved. The hosts' own concept (heis-fører at middle levels) and Holte's reframe (T-ledelse from the top) may not be alternatives at all; the conversation does not address whether both are needed.
  • irrelevantThe episode does not address the dusjer-i-informasjon dynamic outside the senior-leader context — at lower organisational levels (where 'preparation by counterparties' looks very different), or in private sector (where Holte has no recent experience). The framing is specific to Holte's altitude in Norwegian public-sector leadership, and the conversation honours that specificity rather than over-generalising — which is appropriate for the form, though a syntopical reading across episodes might generalise it.
