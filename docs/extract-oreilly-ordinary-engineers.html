Ordinary Engineers, Not Heroic Inventors

Tim O'Reilly · rss · 2026-07-07 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

AI-Native CompanyCode Was Never the BottleneckEnterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessForward-Deployed EngineerLearning Culture & SlackSkill Infrastructure (Ding)Technology Diffusion & Transformation Curves
Technological and economic leadership — for nations and companies alike — is won not by inventing or dominating the 'leading sector' of a new technology but by diffusing a general-purpose technology like AI more broadly and patiently than rivals across ordinary, unglamorous work, a slow organizational (not technical) process that depends on skill infrastructure and on incentives that reward the 'ordinary engineers' who actually do the diffusing.

practical · mixed — Builds a theoretical historical explanation (Ding's diffusion theory vs. leading-sector theory, evidenced by the Japan/US case and the electrification precedent) in order to argue practically: companies and countries should stop chasing frontier-model capability and instead invest in organizational skill infrastructure and incentive design. The historical/theoretical apparatus is entirely in service of a prescriptive conclusion about corporate and national AI strategy.

Key terms

  • Leading Sector (LS) theory Skill Infrastructure (Ding)
    Ding's name for the conventional account of technological revolutions: new technologies create fast-growing industries (steel, railroads, automobiles, semiconductors), and whichever country/company dominates invention in that industry captures the monopoly profits and economic linkages — 'if you win the leading sector, you win the era.'
  • Diffusion theory
    Ding's alternative account: national/corporate economic leadership comes from diffusing a general-purpose technology more quickly and broadly across the whole economy than rivals, not from inventing it. The win goes to whoever embeds the technology into the widest range of ordinary productive work.
  • General-purpose technology (GPT)
    A foundational technology (steam engine, electricity, the computer, and by extension AI) that doesn't just create profit in one industry but spreads across the whole economy over decades.
  • Skill infrastructure Skill Infrastructure (Ding)
    Ding's term for the education and training systems that widen the pool of people who can actually work with a GPT — the institutions that build engineering skill at scale, standardize good practice, and tie research to industry. O'Reilly extends it to a firm-level analogue: the internal apparatus for spreading skill and compounding what employees learn.
  • Ordinary engineers, not heroic inventors
    Ding's (quoted) formulation: when widespread GPT adoption is the priority, the people and institutions that matter are the broad base of practitioners who diffuse the technology into everyday work, not the famous inventors/researchers celebrated by the leading-sector narrative.
  • AI-native (vs. AI-assisted) AI-Native Company
    Guido's distinction (quoted): AI-assisted means giving people tools without changing the system around them; AI-native means rebuilding the operating system of the organization — standardized toolchain, explicit rules, a capability ladder, adoption sprints, reusable artifacts, and safe autonomy — so diffusion compounds.
  • Sovereign AI
    Redefined here as 'AI designed for diffusion, not just raw increases in capability' — a predictable consequence of a GPT diffusing into different societies, firms and institutions that adapt it to their own needs, rather than a matter of national competition for frontier capability.
  • Mechanism design
    Described as 'reverse game theory': starting from the outcome you want and designing the rules of the game (incentives) to produce it — applied here to the problem of getting employees to share AI-learned workflows.

Arguments

National and corporate technological leadership is won by successfully diffusing a general-purpose technology across ordinary work, not by inventing or dominating its 'leading sector.' strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Japan dominated the 1980s leading-sector hardware industries but the US retained information-revolution leadership.
  • The US did so by diffusing computing broadly via software rather than by out-competing in hardware.
  • General-purpose technologies (steam, electricity, computers) create economy-wide gains only through decades-long diffusion, driven by skill infrastructure.
Implicit premises:
  • Historical technology transitions (steam, electricity, computers) are a valid analogy for the current AI transition.
  • 'Diffusion' and 'skill infrastructure,' developed as nation-level concepts, transfer cleanly to firm-level training and tooling programs.
Inferences:
  • If the analogy holds, competitive advantage among AI companies and AI-adopting countries will ultimately go not to whoever has the frontier model but to whoever most successfully embeds AI into ordinary work across the whole organization or economy.
Diffusion takes decades because it is fundamentally an organizational problem, not a technical one. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Early electrified factories saw no productivity gain because they preserved old shaft-and-belt plant layouts.
  • Gains appeared only once a new generation of engineers redesigned plants around what electricity made possible.
  • This reorganization know-how had to be built person-by-person, plant-by-plant (Bessen's 'learning by doing').
Implicit premises:
  • The mechanism that delayed electrification's productivity payoff (habitual reuse of old structures) is the same mechanism delaying AI's payoff today.
Inferences:
  • Frontier-model availability will diffuse 'naturally' and fast (as PC speed once did), so the real competitive variable in enterprise AI is organizational-redesign speed, not model access.
Firm-level AI diffusion additionally requires solving an incentive problem that national-level diffusion does not face in the same way.
Explicit premises:
  • National skill infrastructure is close to a public good — educating more engineers benefits the whole economy regardless of who captures the immediate return.
  • Inside a firm, an employee who automates part of her own job under an indispensability-rewarding incentive system harms her own position by sharing that knowledge.
Implicit premises:
  • Firms will not spontaneously reward information-sharing without deliberate incentive redesign.
  • Workers behave, at least partly, according to rational self-interest regarding job security.
Inferences:
  • Firms that fail to redesign incentives to reward sharing will see adoption stall among a small 'kernel of great people' while broad diffusion never happens, even with abundant model access.

Narratives

  • Japan's 1980s dominance in semiconductors, consumer electronics, and hardware, followed by its failure to overtake the US in the information revolution.
    → The essay's evidentiary backbone: supports diffusion theory over Leading Sector theory as the explanation for how national economic power actually shifts.
  • Paul David's 'Dynamo and the Computer' account of early factory electrification — motors bolted onto old shaft-and-belt layouts producing no productivity gain, until a new generation of engineers redesigned plants around what electricity made possible.
    → Supports the claim that diffusion is organizational rather than technical, and grounds the essay's later speculation about centralized vs. decentralized AI compute (grid vs. unit-drive).
  • James Bessen's 'Learning by Doing' and Arthur Herman's 'Freedom's Forge' (Bill Knudsen's shop-floor-to-CEO rise and the WWII industrialization of US auto manufacturing into defense production).
    → Supports the claim that organizational know-how accumulates bottom-up, person by person, team by team — not from a central plan or a single heroic figure.
  • Dan Guido's account of making Trail of Bits 'AI native,' including its six-step playbook (standardize toolchain, write down rules, build a capability ladder, run adoption sprints, package learning, make autonomy safe) and its specific tool-use rules (e.g., Cursor barred from client code).
    → Illustrates concretely what firm-level 'skill infrastructure' looks like in practice, and supports the claim that AI-native requires system redesign, not just tool provision.
  • DJ Patil's account of the unified, clean data infrastructure ('the tidy house') that let Devoted Health move quickly once AI arrived.
    → Supports the claim that boring infrastructure work (data organization) is itself part of skill infrastructure and a precondition for fast AI diffusion.
  • Historical railroad-gauge incompatibility as a cautionary analogy for AI standards.
    → Supports the argument for interoperable protocols as a middle layer between one universal AI system and fragmented incompatible ones.
  • The Unix/Bell Labs research-community history of shared software development despite AT&T's proprietary licensing.
    → Supports the claim that open source's real driver of collaborative diffusion is modular, protocol-centric architecture, not licensing per se.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why did Japan, despite leading semiconductors, consumer electronics, and hardware in the 1980s, not overtake the US in the information revolution that followed?
    Diffusion theory: the US won not by competing in the leading sector (hardware) but by diffusing computing more broadly and quickly via software across the whole economy.
  • ✅ What actually drives national (and corporate) economic leadership through a technological revolution — invention or diffusion?
    Diffusion, mediated by skill infrastructure, not invention or capability leadership in a leading sector.
  • ✅ Why does technology diffusion take decades rather than happening as soon as the technology becomes available?
    Because it is an organizational reorganization problem (redesigning work around what the technology makes possible), not a technical availability problem — illustrated via electrification.
  • ✅ What should companies do differently in their AI strategy given diffusion theory?
    Build firm-level skill infrastructure / an 'AI-native operating system' (Guido's six steps, Mollick's leadership/lab/crowd model) instead of treating AI strategy as procurement or a moonshot lab.
  • ⭕ How does a firm solve the incentive problem where sharing an AI-learned workflow can threaten the sharer's own job security?
    Named as a mechanism-design problem and gestured at ('reward sharing so it raises status rather than lowers it'), but no concrete compensation, promotion, or governance mechanism is specified.
  • ⭕ Should AI compute and models be centralized (frontier labs, big models via API) or decentralized (many small, rightsized, possibly open-source models)?
    Raised via the electrification grid/unit-drive analogy and left as an open design choice ('we should design for a future of multiple AIs'), not resolved.
  • ⭕ What does 'sovereign AI' actually mean once diffusion, not frontier capability, is understood as the real game — and does this reframing change how AI policy debates are currently conducted?
    Redefined conceptually (AI designed for diffusion, adapted to different societies) but the essay does not address whether or how this reframing would change the actual national AI policy and arms-race discourse it criticizes.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe essay names the firm-level incentive problem (sharing a workflow that automates your own job can be self-defeating) and invokes 'mechanism design' as the fix, but never specifies a concrete mechanism — no compensation model, promotion criterion, or governance structure is proposed beyond 'reward sharing so it raises status.'
    Read next: Mechanism-design literature on incentive design inside firms (e.g., Milgrom & Roberts); the corpus's own psychological-costs-of-AI-adoption and status-theater material for how real organizations have (or haven't) solved this in practice.
  • uninformedDing's Leading-Sector-theory-vs-diffusion-theory dichotomy is presented as though it were the field's main framework, without engaging the substantial pre-existing literature on general-purpose technology diffusion (Bresnahan & Trajtenberg's original GPT economics) or classical diffusion-of-innovation theory (Rogers), leaving unclear how novel Ding's contribution actually is relative to this scholarship.
    Read next: Bresnahan & Trajtenberg, 'General Purpose Technologies: 'Engines of Growth'?'; Everett Rogers, Diffusion of Innovations.
  • illogicalA single historical case comparison (Japan vs. the US, 1980s-1990s) is used to license a general theory that is then applied to firms and to the current AI moment with little qualification — but this one comparison cannot by itself rule out Japan's well-documented 1990s asset-price collapse, keiretsu financing structure, and demographic decline as competing explanations for its stagnation relative to diffusion theory.
    Read next: Literature on Japan's 'lost decades' (asset bubble collapse, structural/demographic explanations) to test whether diffusion theory survives as an explanation once the standard macroeconomic account is on the table.
  • incompleteThe argument for decentralized, rightsized AI models over centralized frontier models is made entirely by historical analogy (electrification's unit-drive vs. grid) but never engages current model-training economics — the capital intensity and returns-to-scale that presently favor centralization — which could make the electrification precedent misleading for AI specifically.
    Read next: The corpus's compute-infrastructure and ai-unit-economics material on why frontier-model economics currently favor centralization, to test whether the electrification analogy actually holds for AI.
  • incompleteThe 'ordinary engineers, not heroic inventors' thesis assumes a steady supply of trainable ordinary engineers, but doesn't address the corpus's recurring concern that the traditional on-ramp for junior engineers (writing code) is itself being displaced by agents — if that on-ramp collapses, it's unclear where the next generation of 'ordinary engineers' who do the diffusing work comes from.
    Read next: The corpus's ai-labor-disruption and systemic-apprenticeship material (e.g., extract-stopped-arguing-whether-ai-changes-software#p8 on the disappearing entry-level rung) for the supply-side half of this diffusion story.

Closes gaps elsewhere

  • Why Enterprise AI Has a Leadership Problem · g2 — Ding's 'skill infrastructure' argument — that GPT diffusion depends on education/training systems widening the pool of capable practitioners, not on capital deployed to the technology itself — makes the case for why an infra-heavy spending split is backwards, directly answering the gap's call for infrastructure-first vs. people-first sequencing literature.
  • Kunstig intelligens gir neppe en ny, industriell revolusjon · g3 — Paul David's 'Dynamo and the Computer' account (cited here in full) supplies one of the concrete diffusion-lag mechanisms the gap says is missing from the productivity-paradox debate: electrification's payoff was delayed because factories kept old plant layouts, not because the technology itself was weak.
  • Please stop the AI Confidence Theater · g4 — The LS-theory/diffusion-theory distinction directly names the tension the gap flags: demonstrated capability ('AI really can do crazy stuff') and diffused, reliable value are different axes, and Japan's hardware dominance without economic overtake is a historical case where capability leadership failed to translate into economic leadership.
  • We've Stopped Arguing About Whether AI Changes Software · g5 — Diffusion theory (vs. leading-sector theory), evidenced by the Japan/US case, gives the mechanism for why a compounding adoption gap becomes a structural moat rather than a closable lag — it's decades of skill-infrastructure accumulation that laggards cannot fast-forward, not flywheel logic alone.
