Key terms

  • addiction (clinical sense)
    A behavioral pattern — persistent use, craving, difficulty stopping, adverse consequences — with a biological basis, useful for treatment regardless of whether it is an APA-recognized disorder.
  • addiction (legal/adjudicative sense)
    A fact that must be established with enough rigor to assign liability and damages — requiring proof of causation and of a loss of control that could not have been resisted, not merely a diagnostic label.
  • dopamine theory of addiction
    The claim, invoked by a Stanford psychiatrist expert witness who called smartphones “the modern-day hypodermic needle,” that dopamine release from platform design is the operative causal mechanism of addiction — which the authors say has been harshly criticized as too simplistic and whose importance has been “wildly exaggerated.”

Arguments

Courts should not recognize 'social media addiction' claims under an evidentiary standard as permissive as the one clinicians use — the legal standard must be stricter. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Addiction's criteria are behavioral, not physical-disease criteria, and can be altered by changing habits.
  • The dopamine mechanism commonly cited as the causal pathway has been overstated and criticized as too simplistic.
  • No available test can distinguish an urge that wasn't resisted from one that couldn't be resisted, or isolate the specific cause of psychological harm.
  • Unwarranted expansion of liability risks chilling protected speech and generalizing to other 'loose-criteria' industries.
Implicit premises:
  • Civil liability requires demonstrated, not merely clinically-plausible, causation.
  • A diagnostic category useful for treatment is not automatically a legally valid fact for assigning fault.
  • The consequences of getting the legal standard wrong (industry-wide liability exposure, free-speech chilling) are severe enough to justify caution even if it means some genuinely harmed plaintiffs go uncompensated.
Inferences:
  • If no current test can establish loss of control at the individual level, then any court finding of liability under today's science necessarily rests on inference and persuasion rather than demonstrated fact — a conclusion the authors imply but do not state this starkly.
  • The same evidentiary gap would apply to any future litigation over engineered-engagement harms from other technologies (e.g., AI companion apps), not just social media — the argument generalizes beyond its stated scope.
'Social media addiction' is a legitimate and useful clinical diagnosis, independent of its legal status.
Explicit premises:
  • The behavioral pattern resembles standard addiction criteria (persistent use, craving, difficulty stopping, adverse consequences).
  • Clinicians already treat it with cognitive behavioral therapy and other tools used for substance addiction.
Implicit premises:
  • Diagnostic utility does not require formal APA/DSM recognition.
  • Treatment goals (helping regardless of cause) justify a looser diagnostic standard than adjudication requires.

Narratives

  • Kaley G.M.'s case: a 20-year-old who alleged a decade-long social media addiction causing anxiety, depression, suicidal thoughts, and body dysmorphia; her lawyer called it 'easy as ABC' and accused the platforms of building 'addiction machines'; the defense argued her distress predated her platform use; one of her two therapists testified social media rarely came up in their sessions.
    → Grounds and personalizes the abstract cause-vs-consequence problem (p3, p10) and sets up the stakes discussed throughout — a concrete instance where 'addiction' is asserted as legal fact but the evidence for causation is genuinely contested.
  • A Stanford psychiatrist, serving as an expert witness in Kaley's case, called smartphones 'the modern-day hypodermic needle, delivering digital dopamine 24-7 for a wired generation.'
    → Exemplifies the vivid, courtroom-persuasive but (per the authors) scientifically overstated dopamine framing they go on to critique (p5).

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Does 'social media addiction' exist?
    Yes in the clinical sense (a reasonable diagnosis by resemblance to standard addiction criteria); not established as a legal/causal fact sufficient for liability.
  • ✅ What evidentiary standard should courts apply to addictive-design liability claims?
    A stricter standard than the one that satisfies clinicians, given the stakes (uncapped liability, free-speech chilling, spillover to other industries).
  • ⭕ What specifically causes the alleged psychological harms — social media use itself, or preexisting conditions that also drive heavy use?
    The authors explicitly state this cannot currently be known with available tools.
  • ⭕ What evidence, if any, would satisfy the stricter legal standard the authors call for — is it a temporarily unmet scientific bar, or a category error to demand proof of this kind at all in an adjudicative setting?

Gaps

  • incompleteThe authors argue no current test can establish loss-of-control or causation, but never specify what evidence (if any) would satisfy them, nor whether they view a rigorous test as a future scientific possibility or view the demand itself as a category error for this kind of harm.
    Read next: Toxic-tort and product-liability doctrine on how courts have historically handled irreducible causal uncertainty in other contested-etiology harms (asbestos-linked disease, PTSD claims, Daubert standard for expert scientific testimony) rather than requiring a currently-impossible certainty.
  • incompleteDismissing the 'dopamine theory' as overstated is not the same as showing platform design isn't causally implicated by some other mechanism (variable-ratio reward schedules, infinite scroll, algorithmic ranking) — the piece doesn't engage with the design-mechanism literature at all, leaving exactly the gap this corpus's own 'Uten kontroll' extraction already flagged (its g3) unaddressed from the other direction.
    Read next: Natasha Dow Schull's 'Addiction by Design' on variable-ratio machine design as the closest analogue to feed mechanics; behavioral-design literature on engagement-optimized feeds, independent of the dopamine-specific framing this piece rejects.
  • uninformedThe piece cites 'one study' suggesting ~$500B in sought compensation without naming or vetting it, and doesn't engage the broader empirical debate on social media and adolescent mental health (e.g., whether population-level correlational evidence supports or undercuts an 'addiction' framing at all).
    Read next: Jonathan Haidt's 'The Anxious Generation' and its empirical critics (Candice Odgers, Andrew Przybylski) for the state of that debate.
  • illogicalThe authors' own slippery-slope argument — that recognizing social-media-addiction liability would necessarily extend to gambling and fast food — is asserted rhetorically, not demonstrated; it mirrors the same kind of unproven causal leap the piece criticizes plaintiffs' lawyers for making about dopamine and addiction.
    Read next: Legal literature on whether liability doctrines actually generalize across analogous industries in practice (e.g., the doctrinal legacy of tobacco litigation vs. the slippery-slope fears voiced at the time).
