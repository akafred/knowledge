Why OpenAI and Anthropic Are Becoming Consultants

AI Daily Brief · email · 2026-05-05 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Selling AI models is not enough — the real bottleneck to enterprise AI transformation is organizational readiness, not model capability — which is why OpenAI and Anthropic are both pivoting to embedded consulting ventures backed by billions in PE capital, and why model-capability innovation will structurally outpace organizational absorption for the foreseeable future.

Key terms

  • forward-deployed engineer (FDE) Forward-Deployed Engineer
    An applied AI engineer who works alongside a customer's own team to identify high-impact AI use cases and build custom solutions — the core delivery model for Anthropic's and OpenAI's new consulting ventures.
  • Transformation Paradox Enterprise AI Adoption & Readiness
    Microsoft's framing: only 4% of organizations have both high individual AI capability and high organizational readiness ('frontier'); most are either stalled or suffer 'blocked agency.'
  • blocked agency Enterprise AI Adoption & Readiness
    The condition where individual employees have high AI capability but are constrained by low organizational readiness — the biggest and most underreported category in Microsoft's findings.
  • last mile (of AI deployment) Enterprise AI Adoption & Readiness
    The gap between having capable AI models and actually integrating them into organizational workflows to produce value — proving far longer and harder than anticipated.
  • Buy and Hope Enterprise AI Adoption & Readiness
    The anti-pattern of purchasing AI licenses, sending an excited email, and hoping transformation follows without structural organizational change.

Arguments

AI companies must become consultants to capture the value their models create. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The last mile of AI deployment is proving far longer than expected.
  • Organizational readiness, not model capability, is the bottleneck.
  • Organizational factors have 2x+ the impact on AI outcomes vs individual factors.
Implicit premises:
  • Model-as-product alone cannot capture the value — vertical integration into services is necessary.
  • Traditional consulting firms lack the technical depth to deliver embedded AI engineering.
Inferences:
  • If the bottleneck is organizational, and selling models alone doesn't fix organizations, then model providers must provide organizational transformation services.
The capability-absorption gap is structural and long-lasting, not a temporary growing pain. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Model innovation outpaces organizational absorption.
  • Only 4% of organizations are at the frontier of both individual capability and org readiness.
  • Agent adoption is growing 15x YoY while organizational readiness lags.
Implicit premises:
  • The timeline for full enterprise AI transformation is 10-20 years.
  • Organizational change is inherently slower than technological change.
Inferences:
  • Multiple data points converge on the conclusion that the gap is widening, not closing.
Model capability is the wrong regulatory target for AI oversight. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • How models are used is the primary issue, not what they can do (Ball & Buchanan).
  • GPT-5.5 and Mythos have roughly equivalent automated hacking capabilities, making capability thresholds arbitrary.
  • Our benchmarks for AI risk are dangerously bad (Mollick).
Implicit premises:
  • Meaningful vetting criteria may be impossible to write given current measurement tools.
  • Pre-deployment gates create false confidence without addressing post-deployment risk.
Inferences:
  • If you can't measure the thing you want to regulate, the regulation will be either toothless or arbitrary.

Narratives

  • The Great AI Deployment Reckoning: after years of 'AI will transform everything' hype, both OpenAI ($10B) and Anthropic ($1.5B) simultaneously launch PE-backed consulting ventures — an admission that the model-selling era alone was insufficient and that the real challenge is organizational transformation.
    → Establishes the central thesis: the companies that built the most capable AI models in history cannot capture value without also solving the messy, slow, human problem of organizational change.
  • The Mythos Regulatory Whiplash: the Trump administration revoked Biden-era AI safety mandates on day one, only to reverse course after the Mythos incident exposed the political risk of a laissez-faire approach. Experts across the spectrum argue the proposed solution (pre-release model vetting) targets the wrong thing.
    → Illustrates that AI governance faces the same measurement and organizational readiness problems as AI deployment — political pressure demands action but the technical community says the proposed action is neither feasible nor effective.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why are both OpenAI and Anthropic launching enterprise consulting ventures simultaneously?
    The last mile of AI deployment is far longer than expected; organizational readiness is the bottleneck, and selling models alone doesn't fix organizations.
  • ✅ What is the actual bottleneck to enterprise AI adoption?
    Organizational factors (culture, manager support, talent practices) — not model capability or individual skills — with 2x+ the impact on outcomes.
  • ✅ Why is selling AI models alone insufficient as a business model?
    Most organizations aren't structured to absorb AI; the value requires embedded transformation work that model sales don't provide.
  • ✅ What does the Microsoft Work Trend Index reveal about organizational AI readiness?
    Only 4% of orgs are at the frontier; the biggest category is 'blocked agency' — high individual capability constrained by low org readiness.
  • ✅ What common organizational approaches to AI adoption reliably fail?
    Buy and Hope, Contain and Delegate, and Outsource the Knowledge — all three avoid the hard work of embedded organizational change.
  • ✅ Why is the Trump administration reversing its deregulatory stance on AI?
    The Mythos incident created political risk of blowback from AI-enabled cyberattacks.
  • ✅ Is model capability the right target for AI regulation?
    No — experts argue usage/deployment is the right target, and current benchmarks are too poor to support meaningful capability-based vetting.
