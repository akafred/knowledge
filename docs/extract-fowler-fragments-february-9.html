As LLMs absorb more of the work of building software, the pressing questions are less about model capability than about what humans stand to lose — the team's understanding of the system (cognitive debt), the intellectual craft-joy of model-building, and even the meaning of 'source code' as behavior shifts from deterministic text toward non-deterministic natural-language prompting.

mixed · mixed — A fragmentary, reflective collection of thoughts from an open-space gathering (under Chatham House Rule) plus curated links. It poses open questions and floats speculations about practice rather than arguing a single sustained thesis.

Key terms

  • cognitive debt Cognitive Debt
    The loss of the development team's understanding of both the domain and the software when much of the building work is sent off to LLMs, leaving the team knowing less about what was built and why
  • The Genie The Genie (Beck)
    Fowler's metaphor for the LLM (from his 'who-is-llm' article) — a powerful entity you delegate to but cannot fully rely on to keep track of everything
  • model building
    The process of coming up with abstractions that help one reason about the domain the code supports; a distinct intellectual pleasure of programming, separate from feature delivery
  • sideways move into non-determinism Non-Deterministic Software
    The trade-off whereby eliciting behavior through prompts and natural-language context raises the level of abstraction but yields non-deterministic behavior rather than deterministic execution
  • future source code Non-Deterministic Software
    A speculated durable, non-human, possibly non-textual semantic representation of a system (in the spirit of Language Workbenches' persisted models) designed to maximize expression with minimal tokens, rendered to humans via projectional editors
  • LLMs as drug dealers
    An overheard framing that LLMs supply output ('give us stuff') but care neither about the resulting system nor about the humans who develop and use it, because each cycle renews their context

Arguments

Delegating software work to LLMs threatens to erode the team's understanding of what is built and why, so teams should engineer a deliberate step to consolidate that understanding. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Normally, building a system is how developers gain understanding of the domain and the software.
  • When much of that work is sent to LLMs, the team may learn less.
  • In TDD, the refactor step is where developers consolidate understanding and embed it in the codebase.
Implicit premises:
  • Lost team understanding has real downstream consequences for maintaining and evolving the system.
  • Understanding does not accrue automatically from LLM-produced code; it must be deliberately recovered.
  • A practice analogous to refactoring could serve as the missing consolidation step for LLM-driven work.
Inferences:
  • Therefore a deliberate understanding-consolidation practice (e.g., asking the LLM to explain its code) is needed to offset cognitive debt.
LLMs may diminish the joy of programming, but that joy — especially model-building — is worth protecting and may itself be the key to working well with LLMs. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Programmers fear LLMs will remove much of the fun from programming, not just their jobs.
  • Feature-delivery joy improves as LLMs get more capable.
  • Model-building is a separate joy that LLMs may cause developers to neglect.
Implicit premises:
  • The abstractions produced by model-building are what let humans reason about, and steer, LLM-driven systems.
  • Neglecting model-building would weaken both the craft and the human's ability to direct the LLM.
Inferences:
  • Therefore model-building should be preserved and may be reframed as an essential LLM-collaboration skill rather than a casualty of it.

Narratives

  • In TDD, refactoring is the step where developers consolidate their understanding and embed it into the codebase — offered as a template for a still-missing step that would let teams understand what LLMs are up to.
    → Grounds the proposed remedy for cognitive debt in an established, familiar practice
  • Almost two decades ago Fowler became interested in Language Workbenches — tools built on a persisted semantic model with projectional editors — which never had significant impact but whose ideas the rise of LLMs might now revive.
    → Uses prior art that failed to take hold to lend plausibility to a speculation about future source code
  • An overheard line — 'LLMs are drug dealers, they give us stuff, but don’t care about the resulting system or the humans that develop and use it' — paired with the observation that the LLM renews its context every cycle.
    → Distills the misaligned-incentive critique into a memorable image

Questions addressed

  • ⭕ Does sending much of the building work to LLMs mean the team learns less, and what are the consequences?
    Raised as the problem of 'cognitive debt'; a deliberate consolidation step and asking the LLM to explain its code are floated, but the consequences and remedies are left open.
  • ⭕ Do we need a step, analogous to TDD's refactor, to ensure the team understands what the LLMs build?
    Posed as an open need; the fairy-tale explanation tactic is offered as one instance, but no defined practice, cadence, or acceptance criteria is given.
  • ⭕ Will LLMs remove the fun from programming?
    Partly: feature-delivery joy is expected to improve, while model-building joy is threatened — though model-building may become a key skill for collaborating with LLMs.
  • ⭕ In the age of LLMs, will there still be such a thing as 'source code', and what will it look like?
    Left open; speculates it may become a non-human, possibly non-textual, token-minimal semantic model in the spirit of Language Workbenches, with projectional editors for humans.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe post names 'cognitive debt' as a risk but offers no way to detect or measure it, and no evidence about its actual consequences — leaving the central worry asserted rather than examined.
    Read next: Look for empirical work on skill and comprehension erosion under AI-assisted development (e.g., the MIT 'cognitive debt' study) and metrics for team understanding.
  • incompleteThe proposed understanding-consolidation step 'analogous to TDD's refactor' is gestured at but never specified — no defined practice, cadence, artifact, or acceptance criteria beyond the fairy-tale-explanation tactic.
    Read next: Read on comprehension practices for AI-written code: decision records/ADRs, code walkthroughs, and structured explanation or knowledge-consolidation rituals.
  • illogicalThe 'drug dealer' critique infers indifference to long-term system health from the technical fact that an LLM renews its context each cycle, conflating statelessness with a normative lack of care; it does not consider that persistent memory or explicit long-term-context mechanisms could change the incentive.
    Read next: Read on agent memory and long-running-context approaches that could give an LLM stake in a system's ongoing health.
  • incompleteThe speculation that a non-human, non-textual semantic model becomes 'future source code' does not address how humans would review, debug, version, or trust a representation they cannot read directly — the very problems that helped keep Language Workbenches and intentional programming marginal.
    Read next: Read critiques of projectional editing and intentional programming, and work on reviewability/verification of AI-generated artifacts.
