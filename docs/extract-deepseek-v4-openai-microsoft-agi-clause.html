DeepSeek v4, and the end of the OpenAI/Microsoft AGI clause

A snapshot of late-April 2026 in AI: a Chinese open-weights lab is now pricing the frontier into the floor, the AGI clause that supposedly governed the OpenAI/Microsoft relationship has been quietly written out of existence, and Willison's own LLM tooling has to be rebuilt around the assumption that model output is no longer just text — together describing an industry where the economics, the legal scaffolding, and the developer abstractions are all reorganizing around the same new reality of multi-modal, agentic, commoditized models.

practical · mixed — A weekly newsletter compilation by Simon Willison. Three main expository pieces (DeepSeek V4 release notes, a historical/legal investigation into the OpenAI-Microsoft AGI clause, and a developer log entry about LLM 0.32a0) plus a long tail of curated links, quotes, and notes. The newsletter is primarily practical/observational — reporting what just happened in AI in a single week — but the AGI-clause piece is implicitly argumentative (the clause is dead) and the curated tail surfaces several normative debates (vibe-coding, LLM bans in open source, vegan models).

Key terms

  • AGI clause
    The provision in the Microsoft-OpenAI agreement that Microsoft's commercial IP rights to OpenAI's technology would terminate once AGI was achieved — with the definition of AGI evolving from the 2018 Charter language ('highly autonomous systems that outperform humans at most economically valuable work') to a $100B-profit-threshold definition (2023 internal docs) to an 'independent expert panel' verification process (October 2025) to functional irrelevance as of April 27, 2026
  • Mixture of Experts (MoE) Inference Optimization
    Architecture where only a subset of model parameters (the 'active' experts) are used per token — DeepSeek-V4-Pro has 1.6T total parameters but only 49B active; Flash has 284B total / 13B active. This is what enables their aggressive pricing.
  • active parameters AI Unit Economics
    The fraction of an MoE model's weights that are actually used to compute each token — directly governs inference cost. Willison emphasizes the active vs total split as the key economic lever.
  • single-token FLOPs AI Unit Economics
    DeepSeek's stated efficiency metric: V4-Pro uses 27% of the FLOPs and 10% of the KV cache of V3.2 at 1M context; V4-Flash uses 10% of FLOPs and 7% of KV cache
  • streaming parts
    Willison's new LLM abstraction: a model response is a stream of typed events (text, reasoning, tool_call_name, tool_call_args, tool_output, image, audio) rather than a stream of text chunks
  • messages array
    The prompt input format adopted by LLM 0.32a0 — a sequence of {role: user|assistant, content} objects, mirroring the OpenAI chat completions API rather than LLM's older 'conversation' object model
  • pelican riding a bicycle Evals
    Willison's signature personal benchmark — asking each new model to produce an SVG of a pelican riding a bicycle. Used as a vibe-check across model generations.
  • software brain
    Borrowed from Nilay Patel: the disposition to model the world as information flows and to see everything as something to be automated; Patel's diagnosis for why AI is unpopular even as ChatGPT usage rises
  • vegan models Open-Weight Models
    Willison's term for LLMs trained entirely on licensed or out-of-copyright data — the 'talkie' 1930 base model qualifies; its chat variant doesn't, because Claude Sonnet/Opus 4.6 were used as judges and synthetic conversation partners during fine-tuning
  • contributor poker Trust
    Loris Cro's framing for Zig's blanket LLM ban: in open source you bet on the contributor, not on the contents of their first PR — reviewing an LLM-written PR yields no future trustworthy contributor, so the ROI is zero
  • digital smell AI Slop
    Andrew Kelley's term for the recognizable stylistic markers of LLM-assisted contributions that abstainers can detect immediately — 'like when a smoker walks into the room'

Arguments

The OpenAI/Microsoft AGI clause is dead. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • OpenAI's 2019 launch language committed to licensing only 'pre-AGI' technologies to Microsoft
  • By late 2024, AGI in the contract was operationalized as a $100B profit milestone — not a capability threshold
  • October 2025 introduced an 'independent expert panel' to verify AGI declarations
  • February 2026 OpenAI publicly affirmed 'AGI definition and processes are unchanged'
  • April 27, 2026 contract restructure makes OpenAI's revenue share to Microsoft 'independent of OpenAI's technology progress' through 2030, with Microsoft's license non-exclusive through 2032
  • The Verge reached the same conclusion in their reporting
Implicit premises:
  • If revenue share is explicitly decoupled from 'technology progress' (the only mechanism by which AGI achievement would change anything), the AGI trigger has no remaining contractual function
  • OpenAI's silence on the AGI clause itself in the April 27 statement, juxtaposed with their February affirmation that it was 'unchanged,' is the diplomatic way of retiring it
Inferences:
  • AGI as a contractual concept never survived contact with commercial reality — every attempted definition (Charter language, profit threshold, expert panel) reveals more about the corporate dynamics than about AGI itself
  • Future commercial agreements pegged to AGI achievement should be treated skeptically — the precedent now is that such clauses get renegotiated out before they can ever fire
DeepSeek V4 brings frontier-adjacent capability at a fraction of the price of US frontier models. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • MIT-licensed, 1M context, MoE architecture: Pro 1.6T/49B active, Flash 284B/13B active
  • Pricing: Flash at $0.14/$0.28 per million tokens input/output; Pro at $1.74/$3.48 — undercuts GPT-5.4 Nano and other frontier models
  • Architectural efficiency gains documented in DeepSeek's paper (FLOPs, KV cache reductions)
  • Self-reported benchmarks place V4-Pro within 3-6 months of state-of-the-art
Implicit premises:
  • Pricing reflects underlying inference cost (not subsidy) — the FLOP reductions support this
  • Open weights plus MIT license means the price floor will fall further once Unsloth and others release quantized variants for local inference
  • Trailing the frontier by 3-6 months is acceptable for the majority of use cases
Inferences:
  • The cost moat that justified frontier-lab pricing is shrinking rapidly; the differentiator must now be something other than raw model quality (UX, tooling, agentic capability, distribution)
  • Local/private inference becomes more viable for serious workloads as consumer hardware approaches the threshold to run quantized Pro models
LLM tooling must shift from prompt/response to messages/typed-parts because model outputs no longer fit the text-in / text-out shape. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The 2023 LLM library was built for text in / text out
  • Models now accept multi-turn message arrays, multimodal attachments, schemas, and tools
  • Models now return mixed output: reasoning, text, tool calls, tool outputs, images, audio — and may execute server-side tools (code interpreter, web search)
  • The old conversation object made it hard to feed in pre-existing conversations (e.g. for chat-completions API emulation)
Implicit premises:
  • An abstraction should faithfully represent what the underlying systems actually do — leaky abstractions force workarounds (e.g. SQLite-only conversation reinflation)
  • Future model capabilities (more modalities, more typed outputs) are easier to add to a typed-parts stream than to a text stream
Inferences:
  • Existing prompt-engineering tools and wrappers built on the prompt/response model will face similar refactors as multimodal/agentic capabilities mature
  • Serializing/deserializing responses (response.to_dict / from_dict) becomes a core feature, since these richer objects can't be reconstructed from a string
A blanket ban on LLM-assisted contributions is rational for projects whose primary review goal is cultivating contributors, not landing code. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • In successful OSS projects, PR review is an investment in the contributor's growth
  • LLMs let a stranger submit a perfect PR that teaches the project nothing about the human behind it
  • Reviewer time is finite — reviewing an LLM PR yields no future contributor
  • If a PR was mostly LLM-written, the maintainer could just fire up their own LLM to solve the problem
Implicit premises:
  • Trust in OSS is accumulated through humans demonstrating judgment over time — LLM-mediated submissions short-circuit that signal
  • Andrew Kelley's claim that LLM 'digital smell' is detectable makes enforcement viable in practice
Inferences:
  • Projects whose review purpose is purely 'land good code' (e.g. monorepo with paid maintainers) have less reason to ban LLM contributions than community-driven OSS
  • There is no universal answer — the policy follows from the project's theory of value

Narratives

  • The AGI clause's seven-year evolution: From the 2018 OpenAI Charter ('highly autonomous systems that outperform humans at most economically valuable work'), through the 2019 Microsoft partnership announcement promising to license only 'pre-AGI technologies,' to the 2024 disclosure that AGI was internally defined as the systems capable of generating $100B in cumulative profit, to the October 2025 introduction of an 'independent expert panel,' to OpenAI's February 27, 2026 affirmation that 'AGI definition and processes are unchanged,' to the April 27, 2026 restructure that explicitly decouples Microsoft's revenue share from 'OpenAI's technology progress.'
    → The chronological structure is the argument — each successive redefinition shows AGI being domesticated from a civilizational threshold into a balance-sheet milestone into a panel decision into an irrelevance
  • Matt Levine's 2023 hypothetical (Willison's 'all-time favorite' commentary): OpenAI achieves benign superintelligence, cuts its investors a check for their capped return, and solves all of humanity's problems — capitalism ends, and nobody needs Microsoft products.
    → Crystallizes why the AGI clause was always a paradox: any AGI capable of triggering the clause would also obsolete the commercial structures the clause was meant to govern. Reinforces the obituary tone.
  • The LLM library's evolution from April 2023 to April 2026: started as a wrapper around text-in/text-out OpenAI calls, accumulated attachments (image/audio/video), then schemas (structured JSON output), then tools (tool calls), then reasoning support, then multimodal output — until the original abstraction had to be retired for a typed-parts stream.
    → Personal-developer-history-as-microcosm: shows in miniature how the AI industry's last three years have stretched every abstraction past its breaking point.
  • The pelican-on-a-bicycle benchmark: Willison's running test where every new model generates an SVG of a pelican riding a bicycle. DeepSeek-V4-Flash produces a respectable pelican with mean expression; DeepSeek-V4-Pro produces a one-winged hairy-backsided pelican that looks 'drawn by a different artist from the bicycle.' Scott Jla's escalation: a horse riding an astronaut riding a pelican riding a bicycle, with ChatGPT Images 2.0 spontaneously adding a road sign 'WHY ARE YOU LIKE THIS.'
    → Vibe-check for model generations; signals that improvements come in unexpected and uneven ways; offers a personality counterweight to the otherwise transactional release reports
  • talkie-1930: Nick Levine, David Duvenaud, and Alec Radford release a 13B 'vintage' language model trained on 260B tokens of pre-1931 English text. When asked to generate an SVG of a pelican on a bicycle, it refuses to acknowledge the request and instead asserts that 'An SVG of a pelican riding a bicycle was generated in 1860... The idea was suggested by the fact that pelicans are sometimes seen fishing on horseback, on the banks of the Rhine.'
    → Demonstrates that 'vegan' models trained on pre-cutoff data behave fundamentally differently — they hallucinate in period style; their training data shapes not just facts but mode of expression. Charming and slightly unsettling.
  • Bun's 4x Zig compile improvement: Anthropic-acquired Bun built parallel semantic analysis and multiple codegen units in their Zig fork — and explicitly will not upstream it to Zig because Zig has banned LLM-authored contributions.
    → Concrete case study of what the Zig LLM ban actually costs the broader Zig ecosystem — a real performance improvement remains stuck downstream. Frames the policy debate with stakes.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What is DeepSeek V4 and how does it compare to existing frontier models?
    Open-weights MoE in two sizes; trails frontier by 3-6 months; undercuts everyone on price; can plausibly run locally on high-end consumer hardware in quantized form
  • ✅ Why can DeepSeek price so aggressively?
    Architectural efficiency — order-of-magnitude reductions in single-token FLOPs and KV cache at long context, especially in Flash
  • ✅ How was AGI defined in the OpenAI/Microsoft contract, and what happened to that definition?
    It evolved from the Charter language to a $100B profit threshold to an expert-panel verification process, and is now functionally retired as of April 27, 2026
  • ✅ Why does LLM 0.32 need such a big refactor?
    Because model I/O has fundamentally changed — multi-turn message arrays as input, typed-parts streams (text, reasoning, tool calls, tool outputs, multimodal) as output
  • ✅ Why is the general public hostile to AI even as ChatGPT usage grows?
    Per Nilay Patel: a 'software brain' worldview that treats everything as automatable has alienated everyone whose work and relationships resist database modeling
  • ✅ Should open source projects ban LLM-assisted contributions?
    Depends on the project's theory of value — for projects investing in contributor growth (contributor poker), a blanket ban is defensible; the practical detectability of LLM contributions makes enforcement viable
  • ⭕ Is it possible to train an LLM exclusively on out-of-copyright / licensed data?
    Base models yes (talkie-1930-13b); but useful chat variants currently require modern LLMs as judges and synthetic conversation partners, contaminating the purity. The talkie team aspires to bootstrap entirely from vintage data eventually.
  • ⭕ What is the strategic implication for OpenAI/Microsoft of the AGI clause being retired?
    Willison reports the facts but does not speculate. The implication — that AGI rhetoric is decoupling from contractual reality, and that the partnership is normalizing into a standard non-exclusive license — is left to the reader.
