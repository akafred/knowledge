AI isn't a tool anymore. It's a collaborator.

Ernie Svenson · email · 2026-04-28 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

AI Employee vs Tool FramingHuman as Orchestrator of AgentsPrompting as Communication
Effective use of AI now requires the same human skill of clear guidance and dialogue that effective delegation to human employees always has, and people who struggle with the latter will struggle with the former.

practical · argumentative — A short persuasive essay aimed at solo/small-firm lawyers, arguing that AI adoption requires the same interpersonal guidance skills as managing human employees. Uses personal anecdote and analogy to advance a practical claim about what skill matters most.

Key terms

  • collaborator AI Employee vs Tool Framing
    An entity that requires ongoing dialogue, context-sharing, and iterative correction — not one-shot commands
  • guidance Prompting as Communication
    The skill of explaining what you want clearly, giving examples, correcting iteratively, and sustaining dialogue — equivalent to good management
  • tool AI Employee vs Tool Framing
    Something you give a single command and it executes — no dialogue, no context, no learning

Arguments

People who can't guide humans won't be able to guide AI either. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • AI now demands the same skills as managing human employees: clear explanation, examples, iterative correction, patience for back-and-forth.
  • Some people have never been good at giving guidance — they're trapped in their own perspective.
  • Those people's employees usually don't last long.
Implicit premises:
  • The skill of guidance is a stable personal trait, not easily acquired.
  • AI's need for guidance is structural, not a temporary limitation that will be engineered away.
Inferences:
  • Since the same skill set is required for both, failure at one predicts failure at the other.
Solo/small-firm lawyers have an advantage in AI adoption. strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • They do everything themselves, so they know their own standards.
  • They know what 'good' looks like.
Implicit premises:
  • Knowing your standards is sufficient preparation for articulating them to AI.
  • Lawyers in larger firms do not know their own standards as well.
Inferences:
  • If you know your standards, you can guide AI — so solos are better positioned.

Narratives

  • Author had Claude analyze his website and lead magnet page. It caught micro-level copy errors, strategic positioning issues, and brand voice drift. When corrected, it updated its understanding and remembered next time.
    → Demonstrates the 'collaborator' thesis through personal experience — shows AI operating beyond tool-level, engaging in iterative dialogue.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What is the right mental model for thinking about AI in 2026?
    Think of AI as a capable new hire / collaborator, not a command-response tool.
  • ✅ What skill matters most for effective AI use?
    The ability to explain clearly what you want and why — the same skill as good delegation.
  • ✅ Who will struggle with AI adoption?
    People who have always been bad at delegating to and guiding human employees.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe employee analogy is pushed without acknowledging where it breaks down. AI doesn't have intrinsic motivation, emotional responses, career goals, or social context — all of which shape human employee management. The skills may overlap but are not identical.
    Read next: Literature on human-AI teaming (e.g., Parasuraman & Riley on automation trust) would clarify where the analogy holds and where it misleads.
  • incompleteThe claim that prompt engineering doesn't matter is stated as a contrast to 'guidance' but doesn't engage with what prompt engineering actually is. Much of prompt engineering IS the skill of clear explanation. The opposition may be a straw man.
    Read next: Consider whether 'guidance' is simply prompt engineering rebranded for a non-technical audience.
  • uninformedNo acknowledgment that AI's need for human guidance may be a temporary state. As models improve, the 'collaborator' framing may itself become obsolete, replaced by more autonomous agents.
    Read next: Follow developments in agentic AI and autonomous task completion to test whether the guidance-skill thesis has a shelf life.
  • incompleteThe piece ends with a sales pitch for the author's Inner Circle community, which undermines the analytical posture. The claim that guidance is 'just a basic human skill' sits in tension with selling a paid program to develop it.
