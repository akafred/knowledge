Experiences with local models for coding

Birgitta Böckeler · rss · 2026-07-08 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Agent HarnessCoding AgentsCognitive SurrenderContext Rot & Context LimitsEvalsFeedback LoopsInference OptimizationLocal AI
Running small, locally-hosted open models for agentic coding is not yet a plug-and-play substitute for frontier models — it remains frustrating and far below frontier capability, and is viable today only for a narrow class of small, well-scoped, often pre-planned tasks, with the choice of task (more than the model or harness) being the dominant factor that determines whether it works.

practical · mixed — a first-person practitioner experience memo (narrative), scaffolded by an expository evaluation rubric (the 'viability funnel'), carrying an argumentative through-line that reaches a hedged viability verdict — Second of a two-part series on Martin Fowler's site (the first memo covered the enabling factors — hardware, model choice, runtime, harness). Reader/RSS metadata attributes it to 'Martin Fowler' but the content and Reader summary confirm the author is Birgitta Böckeler. She is explicit about the anecdotal, small-N, low-tech-stack-diversity nature of her evidence and repeatedly hedges her generalizations.

Key terms

  • agentic coding (vs. auto-complete)
    Her explicit scope: the model works through a coding harness, autonomously reading and changing files via tool calling and multi-turn conversation — not single-line completion. This is the harder task on which she judges local models.
  • viability funnel
    Her ordered 7-stage rubric of increasing demand a setup must survive: (1) fits in RAM, (2) runs at reasonable speed, (3) handles tool calling, (4) builds functionally correct code, (5) sustains a continued conversation / more context, (6) handles a larger or more complex task, (7) code quality is acceptable relative to review effort.
  • small (local) model
    Defined operationally by what fits and runs on her own hardware (M3 Max 48GB, M5 Pro 64GB) — quantized (mostly 4-bit) open models, often Mixture-of-Experts with few active parameters (e.g. qwen3.6-35b-a3b = ~3B active of 35B). 'Small' is relative to frontier cloud models, not an absolute size.
  • manual evals vs. automated evals
    Manual = repeating a few chosen tasks by hand across models/configs to feel the user experience; automated = a vibe-coded one-shot eval harness that runs a task N times for more data, with the agent expected to 'one-shot' the problem. The two modes can disagree.
  • text wall of doom
    Her name for the runaway, repetitive output a small model emits when a conversation is continued past a certain point (e.g. Gemma 4 26B when asked for a follow-up refactor); she notes it may be mitigable by enabling a 'presence penalty'.
  • sensor (for self-correction) Feedback Loops
    A feedback channel the agent can call to check and correct its own output (e.g. a browser it can inspect after editing a chart). In the automated eval she gave the agent a browser as its only sensor, and it never called it; she argues expanding sensor use could enable self-correction and raise viability.

Arguments

Small local models for agentic coding are viable today only for a narrow class of small, well-scoped, often pre-planned tasks, and are not a general substitute for frontier models.
Explicit premises:
  • The choice of task is the biggest single factor determining viability.
  • On more complex tasks the models fall apart, crash on continued context, or must be given up on ('gave up after 11 minutes').
  • Even where they succeed, capability is 'very far away' from what bigger models deliver.
  • The setup is 'not plug-and-play' and requires careful, hands-on work.
Implicit premises:
  • Viability is task-relative, not an absolute model property — so a verdict is only meaningful once the task class is fixed.
  • The reader's job is to calibrate expectations to the task, because the same model swings between success and failure with task, machine, and method.
  • Because published/aggregate benchmarks abstract away the task, they cannot answer the viability question the way concrete, specified tasks can.
Inferences:
  • The 48GB-vs-64GB same-settings quality divergence implies small-model evals may be non-reproducible across hardware, undermining standardized single-machine benchmarking — she flags it as a mystery but does not draw this methodological conclusion.
  • If missing/unused sensors explain many one-shot failures, then reported one-shot 'failure rates' understate achievable viability and conflate model limits with harness gaps — she implies but does not state this.
  • The 'detox' benefit could be turned into a deliberate recommendation (use weaker models as a comprehension/friction tool), but she leaves it as a personal reflection rather than advice.

Narratives

  • Task 1 — asking the models to rename a chart, sort its bars descending, and add cumulative-percentage x-axis labels every 10th bar; Qwen Coder Next 80B MoE one-shot it in 2.5 min then crashed on the next message, Qwen3.6 35B and Gemma 4 31B needed 15 min of back-and-forth on the cumulative percentages, and Gemma 4 26B succeeded fully but then produced the 'text wall of doom'.
    → Concretely demonstrates the funnel's middle stages (functional correctness and continued-conversation limits) and shows that 'success' and 'usable' are different things.
  • Task 2 — adding a horizontal 'Countries' bar chart (top 10 + 'Other') to a single access-log visualisation script; the harnessed agents burned 8-12 minutes and were abandoned ('Gave up after 11 minutes'), yet pasting the whole 450-line file into a plain LM Studio chat with no harness produced a working solution in ~6 minutes.
    → Shows that harness overhead and code-search can hurt more than help for a single-file task, and that a 'go old school' whole-file paste can beat an agentic setup.
  • Re-running the Task 2 automated eval on the 64GB M5 Pro after the 48GB M3 Max — same model, same settings — dropped failures from 5/7 to 1/7, a 'vast difference in quality' she calls a mystery.
    → The standout anomaly: implies that hardware/memory-pressure, not just model choice, governs output quality, quietly destabilizing any single-machine benchmark.
  • The three-phase 'roller coaster' journey — manual evals to feel the UX, then a vibe-coded automated eval setup after Thoughtworks colleagues shared input, then day-to-day use of Qwen3.6 35B MoE on real work and personal tasks (bash/Python scripts, website content, small changes, a game whose complex logic fell apart).
    → Frames evaluation itself as iterative and setup-sensitive, and maps which everyday task types actually work.
  • The 'back to basics / detox' reflection — enjoying the return to weaker capabilities because it lowers the temptation to check out, increases signal about what helps, and restores real review instead of surrendering to strong-model autonomy and paying for it in later rework.
    → Carries the secondary thesis that the constraint of weak models is itself a benefit to comprehension and craft.
  • Colleague Jigar Jani does almost all his coding with Qwen 35B MoE (4BIT) on a 48GB MacBook against a real-world Python/React codebase, improving as he enhances his harness with skills like Graphify and Understand Anything, while insisting code review is essential.
    → A more-successful counter-case that bounds her own pessimism and shifts credit toward harness engineering and human review.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Which kinds of coding tasks can small, locally-run models actually handle for agentic coding right now?
    Small, well-scoped, single-/few-file, often pre-planned changes — plus many bash/Python scripts — work acceptably; tasks needing much code search, large context, cumulative/derived logic, or continued conversation tend to fail or become not-realistically-runnable.
  • ✅ Is local small-model agentic coding viable as a day-to-day practice?
    Not plug-and-play and very far from frontier models, but usable enough that she integrates qwen3.6-35b-a3b for narrow, pre-planned tasks and keeps building intuition about when to reach for it.
  • ⭕ How should one evaluate small local models for coding?
    Partially: her viability funnel plus carefully specified tasks give structure, but manual and automated evals disagreed and she does not resolve which is the truer signal or how to reconcile them.
  • ⭕ Why did the same model with the same settings produce vastly different output quality on the 48GB vs. the 64GB machine?
    Unresolved — explicitly left as a 'mystery'; she does not investigate memory-pressure, runtime, or quantization causes.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe 48GB-vs-64GB same-model, same-settings quality divergence is reported but never investigated. For a rigorous adopter this is the crux: if output quality depends on RAM headroom/memory pressure rather than model settings, then any single-machine small-model benchmark is untrustworthy and non-transferable.
    Read next: Controlled experiments holding model + quantization + settings fixed while varying available RAM headroom; llama.cpp / MLX / LM Studio documentation on memory-pressure, offload, and non-deterministic behavior under constrained RAM; work on eval reproducibility across hardware.
  • incompleteThe manual-vs-automated eval divergence is described but not diagnosed. It is unclear whether manual iterative 'success' merely reflects the human closing the loop through follow-up messages while the automated one-shot exposes the model's real ceiling — i.e. whether the two modes measure different things.
    Read next: Literature comparing one-shot vs. multi-turn agent benchmarks; studies on human-in-the-loop correction inflating apparent capability; self-correction/sensor studies (her own 'sensors for coding agents' work).
  • incompleteNo systematic cost/latency/throughput accounting. There are vivid anecdotes ('gave up after 11 minutes', '~6 minutes', '2.5 minutes') but no tokens/sec, no minutes-per-task distribution, no download-time, electricity, or RAM-opportunity-cost figures — so the practical economics of the setup remain qualitative.
    Read next: Local-inference throughput benchmarks (tok/s by model/quantization/hardware); total-cost-of-ownership analyses for local vs. cloud coding models; latency-vs-quality tradeoff studies for quantized MoE models.
  • incompleteThe apparent tech-stack effect (Bash/Python succeeding more than JavaScript/TypeScript) is asserted as an impression on a tiny, low-diversity dataset (two main JS/TS tasks) with confounds she names but does not control.
    Read next: Larger multi-language evals of the same small models; analysis of training-data composition by language; whether the effect is language difficulty, task difficulty, or harness/tooling maturity.
  • incompleteThe harness's contribution is under-explored and internally in tension: she found swapping OpenCode vs. Pi made little difference, yet Jigar Jani's near-all-local coding depends on continuously enhancing his harness with skills. How much of viability is raw model vs. harness engineering (skills, code-search/understanding tools, self-correction sensors) is left open.
    Read next: SmallCode-style harness-engineering studies for small models; her own harness-engineering framework; controlled comparisons isolating harness features (progressive disclosure, tool routing, skills) at fixed model.
  • incompleteThe reasoning-on/off tradeoff is unexamined. Her default runs with 'reasoning off', yet the failures she reports include 'very long reasoning chains followed by excruciatingly slow attempts to finally edit the file' — the effect of thinking mode on tool-calling reliability, latency, and RAM for small models is never systematically tested.
    Read next: Model documentation on thinking/reasoning modes for tool-calling agents; studies on reasoning-token cost vs. task success for small quantized models; effect of presence penalty and sampling settings on runaway output.
    closed byViability of local models for coding — This memo directly tests reasoning on vs. off in an automated eval and finds reasoning off is faster and performs the same to slightly better — a preliminary empirical answer to the reasoning on/off tradeoff the companion memo leaves unexamined.
