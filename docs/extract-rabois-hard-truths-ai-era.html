Key terms

  • barrels Talent Density
    People who can take an idea from concept to shipped product without needing someone else to manage them — they 'barrel' through the entire process. The constraint on organizational output.
  • ammunition Talent Density
    Talented people who can execute well but need a barrel to direct them. Abundant relative to barrels.
  • tokens (as consumed by CMOs) Token Throughput
    AI inference compute — LLM API calls. Rabois predicts marketing will consume more AI compute than engineering.

Arguments

The PM role is dying because AI eliminates the need for a translation layer between vision and code. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • AI tools (vibe coding, Claude Code, Lovable) allow non-engineers to build software directly
  • The best companies are already operating with fewer PMs or none
Implicit premises:
  • The primary value of PMs was translating business requirements into technical specifications — not strategy, user research, or cross-functional coordination
  • Founders and operators can articulate product vision clearly enough for AI to execute on it
  • The quality of AI-generated code is sufficient for production use
Inferences:
  • If PMs are unnecessary, the entire PM career ladder and organizational structure around it (APM programs, PM-to-VP pipeline) is also obsolete
  • Design and engineering become a single role when AI mediates between intent and artifact
Customer feedback is harmful for consumer product development. strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • Consumers cannot articulate what they want before they see it
  • Great consumer products (by implication: iPhone, Instagram, etc.) were not built by asking customers what to build
Implicit premises:
  • There is a meaningful distinction between consumer and enterprise products in this regard
  • Founder taste and intuition are more reliable signals than user research for consumer products
  • The successes built on founder intuition are representative, not survivorship-biased
Inferences:
  • If customer feedback is harmful for consumer products, then the entire lean-startup / customer-development methodology (Steve Blank, Eric Ries) is wrong for consumer — it only applies to enterprise/B2B
CMOs will consume more AI tokens than engineers. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Marketing involves massive content generation, personalization, and distribution — all tasks AI excels at
  • Engineering use of AI (coding assistants) is valuable but bounded by code quality requirements
Implicit premises:
  • The volume of marketing content is orders of magnitude larger than the volume of code
  • Marketing quality thresholds are lower than engineering quality thresholds, making AI output more directly usable
  • Token consumption is a meaningful proxy for value creation
Inferences:
  • If marketing consumes the most AI, then AI companies should be building for marketers, not developers — the GTM focus of the AI industry is misallocated

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How should companies organize and hire in the AI era?
    Hire barrels (end-to-end owners), eliminate PMs, keep teams small, and let founders drive product directly.
  • ✅ Who benefits most from AI — engineers or other functions?
    Marketing (CMOs) will be the biggest beneficiary and consumer of AI, not engineering.
  • ✅ Should you listen to customers when building products?
    Not for consumer products — founder taste should drive product decisions.
  • ⭕ What does the transition look like for existing organizations with large PM teams?
  • ⭕ How do you maintain quality and avoid blind spots without customer feedback or PM mediation?

Gaps

  • incompleteThe 'don't talk to customers' thesis ignores the distinction between different kinds of customer input: usability testing, behavioral data, Jobs-to-be-Done interviews, and direct feature requests are very different activities with different failure modes. Rabois appears to collapse them all into one category.
    Read next: Teresa Torres's 'Continuous Discovery Habits' for a rigorous framework on which kinds of customer input are valuable and when; Clayton Christensen's Jobs-to-be-Done theory for a middle path between 'ignore customers' and 'build what they ask for.'
  • incompleteThe PM-is-dying thesis lacks a theory of what replaces PM functions beyond spec-writing: prioritization across competing initiatives, stakeholder alignment, go-to-market coordination, and cross-team dependency management. If AI handles translation, who handles these?
    Read next: Shreyas Doshi's writing on PM archetypes and which PM functions are strategic vs. tactical; Marty Cagan's 'Transformed' for a model of empowered product teams that might survive AI compression.
  • incompleteThe barrels framework assumes barrels are born, not made. There is no discussion of how to develop barrels, whether barrel-ness is domain-specific, or how the framework applies when AI itself can become a barrel (autonomous agents shipping end-to-end).
    Read next: Cedric Chin's writing on expertise development and deliberate practice for whether 'barrel' capability is developable; research on autonomous AI agents for whether AI will eventually replace barrels too.
  • uninformedThe claim that CMOs will be the #1 token consumers does not engage with the possibility that AI agents (not humans) will be the primary consumers of tokens — where the distinction between 'marketing tokens' and 'engineering tokens' becomes meaningless because agents operate across functions.
    Read next: Anthropic's and OpenAI's writing on agentic workflows; the emerging literature on AI-to-AI communication and multi-agent systems.
