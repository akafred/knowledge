How to Speak

Patrick Winston / MIT OpenCourseWare · video · 2026-04-10 Reader 🖥️

Effective speaking is a learnable craft governed by knowledge and practice far more than by innate talent; Winston provides a systematic toolkit of heuristics — covering openings, structure, tools, slides, inspiration, job talks, memorability, and closings — so that your ideas receive the recognition they deserve.

practical · lecture / demonstration — A practical how-to lecture on oral communication, delivered as a live demonstration of the techniques it teaches. The lecture is itself an exercise in the principles it advocates, making the medium inseparable from the message.

Key terms

  • Empowerment promise
    An opening statement that tells the audience what they will know or be able to do by the end that they cannot do now — the reason for being there
  • f(K, P, T) — Knowledge, Practice, Talent
    A formula where communication quality is a function of knowledge (dominant), practice (important), and talent (minor). K and P dwarf T.
  • Cycling
    Repeating key ideas multiple times (at least three) because ~20% of the audience is fogged out at any moment
  • Building a fence
    Explicitly distinguishing your idea from similar ones so the audience does not confuse them
  • Empathetic mirroring
    Winston's 'lunatic fringe' hypothesis that mirror neurons fire when watching a speaker physically interact with boards or props, creating a felt, embodied experience that slides cannot produce
  • Winston's Star (5 S's)
    Five elements that make work memorable: Symbol, Slogan, Surprise, Salient idea, Story
  • Hapax legomenon
    A type of slide (or element) so unusual that you can get away with it exactly once per presentation — e.g., a deliberately incomprehensible complexity diagram
  • Contributions (final slide)
    The final slide should list your contributions (what you did), not conclusions (what you found) — it stays visible during Q&A and tells people who you are

Arguments

Speaking skill is learnable and knowledge-dependent, not talent-dependent. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Communication quality = f(K, P, T) where T is small
  • Winston, a non-athlete, was a better skier than Olympic gymnast Mary Lou Retton because he had more K and P
Implicit premises:
  • What holds for skiing (a physical skill) transfers to speaking (a cognitive-physical skill)
  • A single anecdote about one domain is sufficient evidence for a general principle
Inferences:
  • Therefore anyone can become an effective speaker through acquiring knowledge of techniques and practicing them
Blackboards and props are more effective than slides for teaching. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Student surveys consistently say 'more chalk, less PowerPoint'
  • Boards have graphic quality, appropriate speed, and serve as a target for hands
  • Humans have only one language processor — text on slides competes with the speaker's words
Implicit premises:
  • Empathetic mirroring (mirror neurons) creates embodied understanding that static images cannot
  • Student preferences reliably track actual learning effectiveness
Inferences:
  • Therefore use boards for informing/teaching and reserve slides for exposing (conference/job talks)
You should not end a talk by saying 'thank you'. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • 'Thank you' implies the audience stayed out of politeness and wanted to be elsewhere
  • Political speakers (Christie, Clinton) demonstrate effective non-thank-you endings
  • Conventions exist in other domains (Catholic mass, concerts) that signal closure without gratitude
Implicit premises:
  • The final moment of a talk has disproportionate psychological weight
  • Audience perception of the speaker's confidence matters
Inferences:
  • Therefore end with a salute, a benediction, a joke, or a callback — anything that leaves a stronger final impression
For work to be remembered, it needs Symbol, Slogan, Surprise, Salient idea, and Story (Winston's Star). strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • Winston's own arch-learning thesis became famous and accidentally had all five elements
  • The arch was a symbol, 'one shot learning' was a slogan, learning from one example was a surprise, the near miss was a salient idea, and the narrative of how it worked was a story
Implicit premises:
  • One case study (his own thesis) is sufficient to establish a general framework
  • Memorability is a necessary condition for intellectual impact
Inferences:
  • Therefore deliberately engineering all five S-elements increases the chance your work will be recognized

Narratives

  • Mary Lou Retton skiing at Sun Valley — an Olympic gold medalist is a novice skier, while Winston, an ordinary person, is far better because of knowledge and practice.
    → Grounds the f(K,P,T) formula in a vivid, surprising counterexample to the assumption that talent is decisive.
  • The Serbian convent — a nun pulled Winston's hands from behind his back because the gesture was deeply insulting in that culture.
    → Illustrates why hands-behind-back is problematic and why having something to do with your hands (the board) matters.
  • Hedda Gabler and the potbellied stove — Ibsen's prop builds tension throughout the play until the manuscript is burned.
    → Demonstrates the power of props as remembered focal points, borrowing authority from dramatic art.
  • The bicycle wheel and duct tape — reframing a gyroscope problem so that even non-engineers get it right, while mechanical engineers are at coin-flip accuracy.
    → Demonstrates looking at a problem the right way; also a live prop demonstration.
  • Alan Lazarus and the pendulum ball — a physics professor puts his nose against the wall and lets a steel ball swing back, trusting conservation of energy.
    → Shows the memorable power of physical demonstration and personal risk.
  • Terminal A at Logan Airport — a stranger asks Winston to critique his slides; Winston says 'too many slides, too many words' without seeing them.
    → Illustrates the universality of the most common slide crime.
  • Julia Child at the fundraiser — 'Is it fun to be famous?' 'You get used to it.'
    → Motivates caring about packaging: you never get used to being ignored; your ideas deserve good presentation.
  • Bar in San Diego with Delores Etter and Bill Weldon — 'vision' and 'done something' in five minutes.
    → Establishes the five-minute rule for job talks from authoritative sources.
  • Doug Lenat in Austin — 'I always finish with a joke, and that way people think they had fun the whole time.'
    → Demonstrates ending with a joke as a viable closing technique.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Is speaking ability primarily a matter of talent or can it be learned?
    It is overwhelmingly learnable — knowledge and practice dominate talent in the f(K,P,T) formula.
  • ✅ How should you begin a talk?
    With an empowerment promise — tell the audience what they will gain — never with a joke.
  • ✅ What are the core heuristics for maintaining audience comprehension?
    Cycling (repeat 3x), building fences (distinguish your idea), verbal punctuation (landmarks), and asking questions (re-engage).
  • ✅ Which presentation tools are appropriate for which purposes?
    Blackboards for teaching/informing, props for memorability, slides only for exposing ideas in talks.
  • ✅ How do you inspire an audience?
    Express passion about your subject; tell novices they can do it; show experts a problem in a new way.
  • ✅ How do you ensure your work is remembered?
    Engineer five elements: Symbol, Slogan, Surprise, Salient idea, Story (Winston's Star).
  • ✅ How should you end a talk?
    Final slide = contributions. Final words = salute, joke, or benediction — not 'thank you'.
  • ⭕ Does this framework apply equally to written communication?
    Winston mentions writing is the second most important skill but does not address it in this lecture.

Gaps

  • scope limitationThe lecture focuses entirely on in-person, live speaking. It does not address video presentations, remote/virtual talks, podcasts, or any mediated format — despite these being the dominant communication modes in many professional settings.
    Read next: Compare with resources on virtual presentation (e.g., Nancy Duarte, Garr Reynolds) for the digital equivalent.
  • evidence gapThe f(K,P,T) formula and the 20% fog-out rate are presented as facts but without citation. The empathetic mirroring / mirror neuron hypothesis is self-labeled 'lunatic fringe' but treated as explanatory. Winston's Star is derived from a single self-referential case study.
    Read next: Look into research on cognitive load theory (Sweller), dual-coding theory (Paivio), and the replication status of mirror neuron claims for more rigorous grounding.
  • cultural biasThe advice is deeply situated in MIT / American academic culture. The 'don't say thank you' advice, the preference for humor, the informality of anecdotes — these may not transfer to other cultures, industries, or power dynamics (e.g., a junior person presenting to senior executives in Japan).
    Read next: Cross-reference with Erin Meyer's 'The Culture Map' for how communication norms vary across cultures.
  • omissionWinston claims speaking > writing > ideas in determining success but never argues for this ordering or addresses the obvious counterexamples (great writers who are poor speakers, ideas that succeed on merit despite poor presentation).
    Read next: Examine whether this is a rhetorical exaggeration designed to motivate the audience or a genuine empirical claim.
  • implicit premiseThe entire lecture assumes that the goal of speaking is persuasion and memorability — that you want your ideas to 'win.' It does not consider speaking as a tool for collective inquiry, dialogue, or epistemic humility. The framework is fundamentally adversarial/competitive.
    Read next: Compare with Bohm's 'On Dialogue' or Scharmer's 'Theory U' for communication frameworks oriented toward shared understanding rather than persuasion.
