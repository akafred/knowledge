14) Øystein Ulseth pendler mellom å lede og levere

Truls Jørgensen og Audun Fauchald Strand — Heis Podcast · podcast · 2026-04-07 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

The best technology leaders pendle (commute) deliberately between leading and delivering — driven not by career ambition but by the joy of building at the right level of abstraction — because crossing that boundary in both directions is what makes you precise: a hands-on background gives leaders pragmatism about complexity and trade-offs, while a leadership background gives builders the holistic, risk-aware perspective that lets them choose what to build.

practical · mixed — Long-form conversational interview (Norwegian podcast). Primarily a reflective career narrative with embedded practical claims about how to navigate the engineer-manager pendulum, what crossing the IC/leader boundary teaches, what good distributed architecture practice looks like, and how to prepare an organization for AI-assisted development. Practical in the Adlerian sense — most propositions ultimately argue for how to organize one's career and one's architecture function, not just for understanding.

Key terms

  • pendle (verb) / pendel (noun) Hands-On Leadership & the Pendulum
    To deliberately move between leading and delivering across a career — not as indecision, but as a structuring principle. In Ulseth's usage it is bidirectional and ongoing; he has moved from leader of a 50+ unit back to enterprise architect, and even now as sjefsarkitekt he holds both dimensions (bredde og dybde) in one role. Pendling is not alternation of jobs but a stance: never get so far from one side that you lose the perspective it gives.
  • Engineer-Manager Pendulum Hands-On Leadership & the Pendulum
    Fredrik Majors' (CTO Handelkamp) concept invoked by the hosts to name the pattern Ulseth exemplifies: technology leaders deliberately oscillating between IC and leadership work because each role keeps the other honest.
  • drivkraft / bygge-glede (drive / build-joy) Intrinsic Motivation & Making
    The first-principle motivator Ulseth substitutes for 'ambition'. The joy of building something that didn't exist — felt equally when typing a BASIC program at age 10, when writing code, and when sketching a PowerPoint that crisply renders a complex idea. The 'click' when an abstraction lands precisely without losing essence.
  • presisjon i abstraksjonen / å treffe på riktig abstraksjonsnivå Organizational Abstraction Levels
    Audun's (host's) re-statement of what pendling buys you: the ability to pick the right level of abstraction, because you have seen both sides of the equation — what it costs to implement at the low level and what it must mean at the strategic level.
  • bredde og dybde (breadth and depth)
    Ulseth's two-dimensional design of the sjefsarkitekt role. Bredde: framework-building — architecture governance, architect community, conditions under which others make good architecture choices. Dybde: a personal portfolio of initiatives the sjefsarkitekt drives hands-on, in collaboration with others. He explicitly says he needs the dybde portfolio for the role to work for him.
  • sosioteknisk arkitektur (sociotechnical architecture) Sociotechnical Architecture
    Architecture practice that explicitly designs organisation and technology together; treats Conway's law as a force to be deliberately harnessed (design the org for the architecture you want) rather than passively accepted. Ulseth notes this is a new acquaintance for him and Fremtind has adopted it as their target architecture model.
  • fart og flyt (speed and flow) Evolutionary Architecture & Fitness Functions
    The properties a modern architecture should optimise for: teams shipping with minimum friction. Loose coupling and high cohesion are the levers; central architecture boards are now a flow-limiting factor at large scale.
  • konversasjonell arkitektur (conversational architecture) Evolutionary Architecture & Fitness Functions
    Andrew Harmel-Law's model of distributed architecture decision-making: teams are empowered to make architecture choices the way they are empowered for development decisions, on the condition that they consult relevant stakeholders, document, and inform a central body — which then no longer 'approves' decisions but receives them.
  • vibe-coding Vibe Coding
    AI-assisted development in which the developer does not understand the code the tool produces. Ulseth treats this as a distinct, distinguishable mode and is sceptical of it as a sustainable practice.
  • god utviklingskultur (good engineering culture)
    The cluster of practices (continuous delivery is the named example) that Ulseth treats as a precondition for scaling AI-assisted development. Old practices, not new — but AI now provides a much larger incentive to actually live them.

Arguments

Senior technologists should pendle between leading and delivering rather than climb a one-way ladder. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Going from IC to leader teaches you to triage complexity accurately.
  • Going from leader to IC teaches you to choose what to build with holistic, risk-aware, commercial framing.
  • Each direction makes you better at the other (Audun's 'presis i abstraksjonen').
  • Build-joy, not promotion, is what drives sustainable career energy — and build-joy is medium-agnostic, so it can be served at any level.
Implicit premises:
  • The skills going up and the skills coming down are non-fungible — you cannot acquire 'leadership perspective' as an IC by reading about it, and vice versa.
  • Some organisations recognise both tracks as legitimate seniority; in others, pendling would be career-suicide. The argument silently assumes you are in a Storebrand/Fremtind-like environment.
  • The cost of switching directions (re-onboarding, status disruption) is lower than the cost of staying too long on one side.
Inferences:
  • Organisations that want strong senior architects should design role structures (like the bredde/dybde sjefsarkitekt) that let one person hold both dimensions, reducing the need for job-switches to pendle.
  • Career advice that frames sideways or 'down' moves as failures is actively harmful to engineering leadership pipelines.
Modern large IT organisations should adopt distributed (sociotechnical / conversational) architecture decision-making. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Centralised architecture review is now the bottleneck on speed and flow.
  • Centralised approval also creates the illusion of control while real decisions go underground.
  • Conway's law means architecture follows org structure, so distributed delivery teams will produce distributed architectures whether or not the official model permits it.
  • Empowered teams already make development decisions; extending the same empowerment to architecture decisions (with consultation and documentation obligations) is a natural continuation.
Implicit premises:
  • Distributed architects can grow the judgement needed to make these decisions well, given a community of practice and a sjefsarkitekt providing breadth-level framing.
  • Loose coupling and high cohesion are general enough principles to settle most architecture disagreements without central arbitration.
  • The risk of bad local decisions is smaller than the risk of bottleneck/illusion-of-control from central decisions.
Inferences:
  • The sjefsarkitekt role exists to build the conditions (governance, community, principles) under which good distributed decisions become likely — not to make the decisions.
  • Architecture review boards as gating mechanisms should be replaced with informational/consultation forums.
An organisation should establish good engineering culture before scaling AI-assisted development. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • AI-assisted development is currently most valuable in the hands of skilled developers with good engineering culture.
  • The developer remains accountable for the produced code regardless of tool.
  • AI raises cadence, which exposes weak culture faster and at higher cost.
  • Continuous delivery is a well-described practice that AI now provides a much larger incentive to actually live.
Implicit premises:
  • Good engineering culture is identifiable and improvable ex ante — you can tell whether you have it before you bet on AI tools.
  • Vibe-coding (no comprehension of generated code) is not a viable workaround for missing culture; it merely shifts the risk.
Inferences:
  • Organisations rushing AI tools into low-culture environments will see the productivity gains evaporate or invert.
  • The most leveraged AI investment in a regulated industry may be modernising legacy systems, where the cadence/complexity ratio is currently worst.
Architecture practice has been transformed (centralised → distributed; office → multi-location/multi-country) but the architect's core function has not. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • 10–15 years ago, a central architecture team could make all the decisions and shared the same coffee machine.
  • Now, IT organisations are too large and too distributed for that model.
  • The role of the architect now lives inside teams, owning depth in their area over time.
  • AI tools change cadence, not what architecture is for.
Implicit premises:
  • There is a recognisable 'architect function' that survives this transformation — Ulseth never defines it, but treats it as load-bearing.
  • The architect's value is in the act of designing/choosing, not in the institutional locus where the designing happens.
Inferences:
  • Architecture training should de-emphasise board procedures and emphasise embedded craft, community of practice, and decision-documentation.
  • Measurement of architecture function should shift from 'decisions approved' to 'speed and flow enabled'.

Narratives

  • Age 10, the microcomputer and the BASIC manual: Ulseth and a friend type in programs from a manual hoping for Donkey Kong; mostly they get sinuskurver. But the discovery that 'these machines can be made to do things, maybe things other people need' lights the fire that has steered his whole career.
    → Establishes that build-joy precedes any career structure — supports the claim that pendling is steered by an internal compass, not external incentives.
  • The 2022 return: after three years leading the 50+ Architecture & Data unit (later IDA — Insight, Design, Architecture), Ulseth feels he has drifted 'a little too far from what I find most fun'. On the eve of his fourth parental leave he decides he will come back as enterprise architect, not as leader. He returns in November 2022 — the same month ChatGPT launches.
    → Concrete instance of the pendulum swinging down; supports the propositions that pendling is steered by build-joy and that build-joy can rationally override status.
  • The move from Storebrand to Fremtind: 'I felt almost — either I stay here for the rest of my career, which would have been fine, or I leave. I wanted to see another organisation before I legger inn årene.' Now at Fremtind he is sjefsarkitekt working with a new IT strategy and a sociotechnical architecture target.
    → Supports the proposition that planned career rupture is valuable even when no escape is needed.
  • The kitchen-renovation analogy for coupling and cohesion: high coupling = your pipes run through your neighbour's wall, you can't renovate your kitchen without coordinating their bathroom. Low cohesion = the electrician, plumber and carpenter are in different places, you have to coordinate them yourself. Good architecture = one place to turn to that handles it all.
    → Argument-by-analogy that loose coupling and high cohesion are the general levers for speed and flow; serves as the talk's 'explain it to non-architects' moment.
  • The 'fullstack from PowerPoint to JavaScript' self-description: Ulseth describes his Storebrand tenure as pushing the fullstack idea past front/back-end into business strategy as well — he sees PowerPoint and JavaScript as ends of the same craft spectrum.
    → Implicit support for the claim that build-joy is medium-agnostic; foreshadows the later answer about whether sjefsarkitekt PowerPoint can be as satisfying as code.
  • The git/subversion story: at some workplaces the de-facto version control choice was actually political — 'the only allowed thing was Subversion' — and only later did git become permitted. Audun retells this as a case where centralisation produced hidden non-compliance until reality forced the change.
    → Supports the proposition that centralised technical control creates the illusion of conformity while real decisions go underground.
  • The two 'wow' moments: ChatGPT in November 2022 — 'within 30 seconds I understood something important had shifted' — and Claude Code after Christmas 2025, with similar suddenness. Ulseth has been using Cursor and Claude Code privately since launch but pinpoints a discrete capability jump in early 2026.
    → Supports the proposition that important AI capability shifts announce themselves quickly to a practised hand — and grounds his confidence (against the host's hedge) that this one is real.
  • Audun's Finn.no anecdote: 'I noticed I could get bigger things built if I talked more and coded less — that gave me a certain joy when the size of things grew.' Restates the pendulum from a host's experience.
    → Cross-validation by a host of the medium-agnostic build-joy claim.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What drives an experienced technology leader to pendle between leading and delivering?
    Build-joy (drivkraft / bygge-glede), not ambition — the desire to keep making and to keep operating at the right level of abstraction.
  • ✅ What do you learn going up that makes you better when you come down — and vice versa?
    Up: triaging real vs. perceived complexity. Down: holistic perspective, budget/risk realism, pragmatism on trade-offs, commercial value awareness.
  • ✅ How should a senior architecture role be designed in a modern IT organisation?
    Two dimensions: bredde (governance, architect community, conditions for distributed decisions) and dybde (a personal portfolio of deep initiatives the role-holder owns).
  • ⭕ How should distributed architecture decision-making work?
    Hypothesis: conversational architecture (Harmel-Law) — teams empowered, consultation and documentation required, central body receives rather than approves. Ulseth flags he hasn't seen it in practice yet.
  • ✅ What must an organisation have in place before scaling AI-assisted development?
    Good engineering culture (continuous delivery is the named example) and, in regulated industries, strong technology–compliance collaboration. Without engineering culture, build it first.
  • ⭕ How will AI-assisted development evolve?
    Two host-vs-host views: maximalist (Audun) vs. stabilising-mid-point (Truls). Ulseth refuses to over-predict but is high-conviction that the current capability is real and durable.
  • ✅ Is the architect's core function changing?
    No — practice (locus, distribution, cadence) is changing dramatically; the function is the same. (But the 'function' is never defined — see gaps.)
  • ⭕ Why don't more senior technologists pendle if it works so well?
    Ulseth speculates: most people's personality-test ambition profile points up-and-onward, and his does not. He explicitly declines a 'fasitsvar'.

Gaps

  • incompleteScale dependence of pendling. The whole career story plays out inside two large Norwegian financial-services organisations (Storebrand, Fremtind) that recognise technical depth as legitimate seniority. The conversation never tests whether pendling is feasible — or how it changes shape — in small startups (no role to come back to), pure consultancies (the IC vs. leader frame may not apply), public-sector hierarchies (rigid grade structures), or US-style stock-comp tracks where stepping back is also stepping out financially.
    Read next: Pair with Charity Majors' 'Engineer-Manager Pendulum' original essay to see her conditions for it working; pair with Will Larson on staff-engineer archetypes for the IC-track question; pair with Norwegian-public-sector accounts (NAV optimising for fast flow) for the bureaucracy edge case.
  • incompleteThe people who can't pendle. Ulseth's account is sympathetic to both directions but assumes the listener has the option of swinging both ways. It does not address those who are only-leaders (lost technical currency) or only-deliverers (no taste for organisational complexity) — and whether they should be coached, paired, or left in their lane. There is a real possibility that pendling is a personality privilege as much as an organisational one.
    Read next: Camille Fournier on technical-leadership transitions; Cutler on the costs of forcing technical contributors into managerial roles; Allen Holub on accountability and the limits of role-fluidity.
  • incompleteThe architect's function is never defined. The talk asserts that architecture has not changed in its core function, only in practice — but it does not say what the function is. The closest thing to a definition is 'enabling speed and flow via loose coupling and high cohesion', which is more a goal than a function and which collapses architecture into platform engineering in some readings.
    Read next: Vlad Khononov on coupling in distributed systems; Ruth Malan on architecture as 'the design decisions that are hardest to change'; Team Topologies for the sociotechnical framing Ulseth invokes.
  • incompleteBredde/dybde sounds clean but is operationally underspecified. How does a sjefsarkitekt choose which initiatives to take as 'mine'? Ulseth nods that they should be 'both important and interesting' and that the two usually overlap — but the harder cases (important-but-boring, interesting-but-marginal) are not discussed. This is the place where pendling-by-design either works or quietly collapses into one side.
    Read next: Cutler on how leaders allocate attention; deep-dive into 'staff-plus' patterns where senior ICs balance technical and political work.
  • incompleteThe claim that AI raises the incentive to live good engineering culture is asserted but not mechanised. Continuous delivery has been well-described for 20 years and only patchily adopted. Why will AI succeed in changing this where the prior decade's tooling, DORA reports, and platform engineering did not? A causal story is missing.
    Read next: DORA 2025/2026 specifically on AI's interaction with delivery practices; Birgitta Böckeler and Martin Fowler on AI-coding feedback loops; Kent Beck's TCR work as the practice end of the same idea.
  • incompleteNo data on whether pendling actually produces better outcomes. The argument is largely 'I felt better and have heard I do better.' The episode lacks any reference to organisational research on whether engineer-manager pendulum careers correlate with better team or product outcomes — and whether the benefits go to the individual, the team, or the organisation.
    Read next: Pragmatic Engineer / Newcomer on staff-plus role outcomes; LeadDev surveys on engineer-manager transitions; academic work on bidirectional career mobility in knowledge work.
  • uninformedUlseth notes he has not yet seen conversational architecture practiced and that sociotechnical architecture is a new acquaintance — yet these are the frameworks he is now bringing into Fremtind's IT strategy. The talk does not engage seriously with the published critiques of these models (e.g. that Team Topologies under-specifies platform-team funding, or that Harmel-Law's model assumes a maturity teams may not have).
    Read next: Andrew Harmel-Law's own book and the Architecture Decision Records literature; critical reviews of Team Topologies (Manuel Pais et al.).
