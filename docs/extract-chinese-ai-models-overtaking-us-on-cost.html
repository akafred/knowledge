The AI-model market is flipping from US to Chinese dominance in real production usage, and the decisive driver is per-task cost — decisive precisely because the agentic era multiplies calls per task, so distribution consolidates on the cheapest 'good-enough' models faster than any residual US capability lead can matter.

theoretical (expository market analysis) with a practical undertow · market-shift analysis / opinion essay (short LinkedIn post) — On its face descriptive — it explains a market reversal — but it carries an implicit practical directive: stop paying the US 'best model' premium, and assume that in the agentic era cost, not benchmark rank, decides which models get built on. The P.S.S. explicitly anchors the piece in a disruption framework ('good enough and cheap' beats 'best and gated').

Key terms

  • distribution
    installed real-usage share across the developer ecosystem — who actually gets called in production — not marketing reach or channel access
  • routed volume
    token traffic actually dispatched to a model through a marketplace/gateway (OpenRouter) — real production calls, as opposed to downloads, benchmark runs, or press
  • open-weight Open-Weight Models
    models whose weights are downloadable and self-hostable (Qwen, DeepSeek, Zhipu GLM) — deployable on domestic chips and freely forkable
  • for equivalent work
    normalizing cost to a common task/eval so a cross-family price ratio (the ~9x premium) is meaningful

Arguments

The AI-model market is flipping from US to Chinese dominance in real production usage, and per-task cost is the decisive driver. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Usage share on OpenRouter reversed in 12 months (US 70%->30%; Chinese ~1%->60%+; ~18T vs ~5.5T weekly tokens).
  • Chinese models cost a fraction (~1/9) of the premium US model for equivalent work.
  • The chat-to-agents shift multiplies calls per task, so per-call price now dominates spend.
  • Anthropic conceded US models are only several months ahead and that Beijing is winning adoption on cost.
  • ~80% of young open-source-stack AI companies (per a16z) now reach for Chinese models.
Implicit premises:
  • OpenRouter routed volume is a valid proxy for the whole AI-model market.
  • Cost dominates capability once models are 'good enough'.
  • Distribution, once set, is sticky in a way benchmarks never are.
Inferences:
  • If cheap models are good enough and agents multiply call volume, buyers route to the cheapest -> usage share follows price.
  • Once usage share consolidates it locks in via distribution stickiness, so it outruns any residual US capability lead.
US incumbents are strategically mispositioned for this shift — gating capability and abandoning open weights exactly when the market rewards cheap-and-open distribution. strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • OpenAI released its strongest model (Sol) to only 20 government-approved partners.
  • Meta, which defined open weights with Llama, has fallen below 1% of routed volume.
  • Alibaba's Qwen has become the most-forked open model on earth.
Implicit premises:
  • Gated/closed distribution forfeits the routed-volume share that determines the durable lead.
  • Being 'best and gated' loses to 'good enough and cheap' with predictable regularity.
Inferences:
  • Restricting the strongest model to a handful of partners cedes the ecosystem's real usage to whoever is cheap and open.

Narratives

  • Meta, the company that defined open weights with Llama, has collapsed below 1% of routed volume while Alibaba's Qwen became the most-forked open model on earth.
    → A cautionary reversal-of-fortune that dramatizes the thesis: even the open-weight pioneer can be routed around once cheaper, better-distributed open models arrive.
  • Right in the middle of the shift, OpenAI launched Sol, its strongest model, to only 20 government-approved partners.
    → A pointed contrast that sets 'best and gated' (US, exclusive) against 'good enough and cheap' (Chinese, open) — the emotional core of the distribution-beats-benchmarks argument.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Who is winning the AI-model market in real production usage, the US or China?
    China, by routed-volume share — although the US is conceded to retain a slim (several-month) capability lead.
  • ✅ Why are Chinese models winning?
    Price — a ~9x cost gap for equivalent work — made decisive by the agentic shift that multiplies calls per task.
  • ✅ Does capability/benchmark leadership still decide the market?
    No — once models are 'good enough', cost and then distribution stickiness decide; benchmarks do not lock in the way distribution does.
  • ⭕ Is OpenRouter routed volume representative of the whole AI-model market (direct enterprise API contracts, hyperscaler-hosted traffic, first-party consumer apps)?
    The piece never defends the proxy; the entire usage-flip claim is measured on one developer marketplace.

Gaps

  • incompleteOpenRouter-as-whole-market proxy (the load-bearing soft spot). The entire usage-flip claim rests on OpenRouter routed volume — a developer marketplace that excludes direct enterprise API contracts, hyperscaler-hosted traffic, and first-party consumer usage (ChatGPT/Claude apps), where US models may still dominate. The author never defends the proxy, so a 'reversal' on OpenRouter is silently equated with a reversal of the whole market.
    Read next: Triangulate multiple share sources (a16z State of AI, hyperscaler/API disclosures, Poe/enterprise usage reports) and read OpenRouter's own methodology to see what slice of the market it actually represents.
  • incompleteForward-dated / unverifiable specifics. A model named 'Sol' released to 'only 20 government-approved partners' is asserted and then hedged by the author himself ('Sol may release to the public at any time ... it's possible ... it's out already'). Treated as evidence for the gating-vs-distribution contrast despite being unconfirmed.
    Read next: Verify against OpenAI's actual release announcements before relying on the Sol datapoint.
  • incompleteUnsourced quantitative figures. The weekly (18T/5.5T), daily-China (140T, 1000x) token volumes and the eval-suite dollar figures ($4,811 / $3,357 / $1,071 / $544) are stated without a defined 'standard evaluation suite', without a source, and without saying which model of each family was priced — so the ~9x premium is not reproducible.
    Read next: Primary pricing pages plus a specified benchmark-cost methodology (fixed prompt/token workload) to compute a like-for-like cost ratio.
  • incomplete'Distribution is sticky' asserted with no mechanism. The closing maxim carries the weight of the whole 'lead is now durable' claim, but no mechanism is given for why routed-volume share should lock in — and on a router marketplace switching costs are famously low (you change a model string), which is the opposite of stickiness.
    Read next: Switching-cost / moat analysis specific to model marketplaces and gateways; contrast against genuinely sticky layers (fine-tuning, workflow lock-in, owned data).
    closed byYou Outsourced the AI—but You Still Own the Risk — That gap flagged 'distribution is sticky' as asserted with no mechanism and asked for a contrast against genuinely sticky layers (fine-tuning, workflow lock-in, owned data). Trap 3 supplies the mechanism: enterprise AI lock-in is acute because the model's behavior is woven into application logic — every prompt, workflow, safety filter and evaluation metric is calibrated to one provider — so switching is Jenga-like, not the low-cost model-string change a router marketplace implies.
  • incompleteCross-family price comparison leans on 'for equivalent work'. The $-per-eval-suite comparison treats model families as substitutable, but open weights ship without the harness, routing, and tooling that closed APIs bundle (Delangue's engines-vs-cars). If the closed price includes the car and the open price only the engine, the 9x premium is not like-for-like.
    Read next: extract-clem-delangue-engines-vs-cars — the engines-vs-cars category-error argument — and total-cost-of-ownership comparisons that include harness/hosting for open weights.
