Key terms

  • underground bias
    Informal, structural dynamics — sponsorship flowing along lines of familiarity/visibility, subjectively interpreted merit, uneven access to inputs like P&L exposure — that operate beneath formal policy and improved attitudes to reproduce inequality without violating any rule or leaving a formal grievance available.
  • meritocracy
    A contested and interpreted ideal, not a measured fact: merit is 'interpreted rather than simply measured,' and organizations that most loudly proclaim meritocratic values show greater pro-male bias because belief in the system's fairness reduces the impulse to self-scrutinize (the meritocracy paradox).
  • prove-it-again bias
    The documented pattern in which women's competence is presumed absent rather than present, requiring repeated demonstration men are not asked to provide — women evaluated on an established record, men on potential; women's mistakes catalogued, men's absorbed.
  • P&L roles vs. staff functions
    Operational profit-and-loss line roles (the COO/CFO path to CEO) contrasted with staff support functions (HR, finance, legal); the structural bottleneck is that women advance more easily into staff functions while being steered away from the P&L pipeline that leads to the top.
  • glass cliff / savior effect
    The systematic preference for appointing women and underrepresented leaders during organizational crises (glass cliff), followed by their ouster and replacement with traditional white-male 'saviors' (savior effect), so failures get misattributed to demographic identity rather than the pre-existing crisis — a self-reinforcing cycle.

Arguments

Over 60 years overt bias against women leaders declined but reorganized into structural 'underground bias,' and the men/women perception gap widened precisely as surface attitudes improved. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • The judgment gap moved from ~35%/35% (2006) to 35% men / 90% women (2026).
  • The standards gap moved from 68%/32% (2006) to 83%/28% (2026).
  • Only 37% of women vs. 70% of men believe promotion criteria are applied equally; 40% vs. 76% believe their company is a meritocracy.
  • External research: proclaimed-meritocracy organizations show more pro-male bias; the glass cliff and savior effect are robustly documented; women are as willing as men to take stretch/international roles.
Implicit premises:
  • Self-reported perceptions validly measure the underlying system rather than merely the respondents' awareness.
  • Women's higher agreement (90%) is the more accurate read of reality than men's (35%).
  • Declining overt-attitude scores mask, rather than reflect, genuine structural change.
  • The 1965 survey items remain valid, comparable measures across 60 years of workplace change.
Inferences:
  • If overt attitudes improved yet the perception gap widened, the bias must have moved from the visible/attitudinal register into the informal/structural one.
  • From the meritocracy-paradox research: proclaiming fairness causally worsens bias, so surface signals of inclusion can coexist with (and even mask) deteriorating fairness.
The leadership-pipeline gap is structural, not attitudinal. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Attitudes about women in leadership have genuinely improved since 1965, yet executive interviews consistently identify a structural bottleneck.
  • Women are sorted out of operational line roles beginning at the 'broken rung' and remain concentrated in staff functions (31% of SVP line roles; 16% of COO).
  • Men are ~3x more likely to be encouraged toward P&L responsibility and far more likely to receive pathway coaching (~half vs. 15%).
  • Even when female line managers outperform men on objective metrics, they face steeper promotion climbs; women are as willing as men to take stretch roles.
Implicit premises:
  • Behavioral/pipeline outcomes reveal system structure independent of stated attitudes.
  • Objective-metric outperformance by women isolates evaluator bias as the residual cause of the promotion gap.
Inferences:
  • If attitudes improved, willingness is equal, and outperformers still stall, the remaining cause must be structural/informal (access, sponsorship, subjective criteria) rather than preference or competence.

Narratives

  • A fully vetted, board-approved incoming CFO was, two months in, invited to a board dinner but not the board meeting itself — so members who had already approved her could grow 'comfortable.' No policy was violated and no formal grievance was available.
    → The book's clearest exhibit of 'underground bias': a bias operating with zero formal footprint, signaling whose authority requires extra ratification while remaining fully deniable.
  • After a company paused its inclusion efforts for legal reasons, leaders suddenly discussed only 'performance' in promotion conversations; employees who had felt recognized as rising leaders began to doubt they were still being considered.
    → Shows how removing the DEI 'window dressing' exposed the real, informal decision-making patterns underneath — the formal/informal gap made visible.
  • An executive states plainly: 'We don't ask the woman whether she is interested; we simply assume she is not.'
    → Dramatizes the untested-assumption mechanism — organizations that never question their assumptions keep arriving at the same answer because they never asked.
  • A reforming executive shifted his evaluation question from 'Who performed best?' to also asking 'Whose potential are we not fully seeing yet? Where is opportunity flowing, and why?' — after realizing the issue was interpretation, not intent, and that merit was being defined too narrowly as current performance.
    → Models the individual-level remedy and a double-loop reframe: fixing outcomes by questioning the frame ('merit = current performance') rather than blaming intent.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why did the perception gap between men and women widen precisely as surface attitudes improved?
    Because overt, attitudinal bias was replaced by informal 'underground bias' (sponsorship, subjective criteria, uneven P&L access) that formal policy does not reach — amplified by women's heightened awareness and by DEI rollbacks that removed the 'window dressing' and exposed the real decision patterns.
  • ✅ Is the leadership-pipeline gap attitudinal or structural?
    Structural: attitudes have genuinely improved, but a bottleneck in access to operational P&L roles, unequal sponsorship/coaching, and subjective evaluation criteria persists.
  • ✅ Do women lack the ambition or willingness for demanding operational roles?
    No — research finds women as willing as men to take stretch and international assignments; the observed gap reflects the unequal consequences women face when they take risks, plus unasked assumptions, not a difference in willingness.
  • ⭕ Is men's low perception of bias (35%) or women's high perception (90%) the more accurate measure of the actual system?
    The article privileges women's read as revealing structural reality but never independently triangulates the two perceptions against objective within-sample advancement outcomes.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe mechanism for change is under-specified. The article names the tools for redesign (predefined criteria, structured documentation, P&L pipelines, actionable feedback) and asserts the missing ingredient is 'willingness,' but does not operationalize how organizations actually adopt them or how to interrupt the self-reinforcing glass-cliff/savior cycle — the 'why diversity programs fail' problem it diagnoses is left as the reader's.
    Read next: Change-adoption and implementation research (Kotter; 'Fearless Change' patterns), the organizational-behavior literature on de-biasing structured decisions, and HBR's own 'Why Diversity Programs Fail.'
  • incompleteThe headline findings rest on executives' self-reported perceptions, yet the piece reads the widening women/men gap as evidence of worsening structural reality and privileges women's read — without triangulating perception against objective within-sample advancement outcomes, or ruling out that rising awareness (rather than rising bias) drives the divergence.
    Read next: Methods contrasting perception with behavioral/objective measures — audit studies, administrative promotion-record analysis, and work distinguishing bias-in-fact from bias-awareness.
  • incompleteThe sample is 193 U.S. senior executives across unspecified industries plus a few qualitative interviews. Generalizability to other geographies (e.g., Nordic/European labor markets with different parental-leave and board-quota regimes), to organizational levels below the C-suite, and to non-corporate sectors is unaddressed.
    Read next: Cross-national comparative and multi-level studies of leadership advancement, and the interaction of national policy (quotas, leave) with the underground-bias mechanism.
