Predict, Don't Enumerate

Michael Roytman · rss · 2026-06-04 Reader 🖥️

AI-Driven Vulnerability DiscoveryMythos
Now that AI makes vulnerability discovery effectively unbounded, security programs survive only by replacing severity-based enumeration with predictive, context-aware prioritization — and Anthropic's endorsement of EPSS is the external permission CISOs have been waiting for to make that shift official.

practical · argumentative — The piece argues for a concrete shift in CISO practice: replace severity-based enumeration with probabilistic prioritization. It mounts a theoretical case (density of bugs, knowing vs pointing machines) only to motivate specific action items (rewrite SLAs, change board metrics, invest in telemetry, fix compliance dialogue, restaff).

Key terms

  • EPSS (Exploit Prediction Scoring System) AI-Driven Vulnerability Discovery
    A statistical, non-LLM predictive model that, given a CVE and global exploitation signals, returns probability of exploitation in the next 30 days. Treated as the canonical public example of a 'knowing machine'.
  • Pointing machine AI-Driven Vulnerability Discovery
    A system (most current scanners + LLM bug-finders) that enumerates and names possible vulnerabilities without understanding their environmental risk.
  • Knowing machine AI-Driven Vulnerability Discovery
    A predictive system that understands how code behaves in a particular environment and recognizes the context that turns a hazard into a risk.
  • Hazard vs risk AI-Driven Vulnerability Discovery
    Hazard = a flaw that could theoretically be exploited. Risk = a flaw that can be weaponized reliably in this environment, against these controls, through this traffic. Bugs are abundant; weaponizable bugs against a given target are rare.
  • Machine-scale AI-Driven Vulnerability Discovery
    A problem regime where finding counts (hundreds of thousands to millions per month) structurally exceed human remediation capacity, making any human-scaled prioritization scheme self-defeating.
  • Local context
    The organization-specific data — asset inventory, application topology, reachability, deployed controls, on-site telemetry, remediation history — that converts a global probability into an enterprise-specific risk score and that attackers cannot have.
  • Exploitability-weighted exposure over time
    The proposed replacement board metric: aggregate risk weighted by probability of exploitation, tracked longitudinally, paired with predicted-vs-observed exploitation as a calibration line.
  • Mythos Mythos
    Anthropic's forthcoming model, used here as a stand-in for an order-of-magnitude expansion of AI-discovered vulnerabilities over the next 24 months — the forcing function that makes the shift non-optional.
  • CVSS AI-Driven Vulnerability Discovery
    A severity-scoring framework that survives only because of regulatory inertia (PCI, HIPAA, breach-notification rules); easy to grade against, but uncorrelated with real exploitation.
  • Security-data engineer
    A role oriented toward closing the feedback loop between prioritization and observed exploitation; the staffing answer to the bottleneck moving from scanning to deciding/measuring.

Arguments

Severity-only prioritization (CVSS) must be replaced by probabilistic prioritization. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Finding volume is now machine-scale and growing.
  • Vulnerabilities are dense — enumeration cannot terminate.
  • CVSS severity is uncorrelated with actual exploitation.
  • Predictive models (EPSS) exist, are public, and are widely embedded.
Implicit premises:
  • Action threshold should be set at expected-loss reduction, not at theoretical worst-case.
  • Auditors and boards will accept a probabilistic story if a CISO leads with it.
Inferences:
  • Any framework whose primary axis is severity (not probability × exposure) is structurally unfit and should be retired, not patched.
Anthropic's EPSS recommendation legitimizes a shift practitioners have already made. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Frontier labs usually recommend LLMs.
  • Anthropic recommended a non-LLM predictive model for the prioritization step.
  • Auditors and boards have blocked the shift by demanding 'external justification'.
Implicit premises:
  • An AI vendor's recommendation carries more compliance weight in 2026 than a security vendor's.
  • The bottleneck for change was political/institutional rather than technical.
Inferences:
  • CISOs should cite Anthropic's guidance directly in compliance dialogues — the citation itself is part of the intervention.
  • Other frontier labs may follow; expect normative pressure on auditors to update CVSS-anchored controls.
Local context, not global prediction, is the defensive frontier of the next decade. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • EPSS sees the internet but not your environment.
  • Two identical CVEs can have risk profiles orders of magnitude apart depending on exposure and controls.
  • Most organizations already have the input data.
Implicit premises:
  • Attackers, even AI-equipped ones, cannot replicate defender-side telemetry and topology data.
  • Defender-side data integration is feasible without new acquisitions.
Inferences:
  • Vendor offerings that wrap global EPSS without ingesting customer-side context are leaving the asymmetric advantage on the table.
  • The next CISO hire is a data engineer, not another analyst.
LLM-based bug-finding without probabilistic prioritization will make security programs worse, not better. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Pointing machines + faster remediation = more churn and waste.
  • Agent tokens cost money; remediation has breakage cost.
  • Mythos-class models will multiply findings by 10x in 24 months.
Implicit premises:
  • Remediation capacity grows slower than discovery capacity.
  • Cost of unnecessary remediation is non-trivial and includes engineering opportunity cost, not just dollars.
Inferences:
  • Adopting LLM-based bug-finding before installing a local knowing machine is net-negative.
  • There is a window where AI-augmented programs underperform legacy programs that simply ignore the new findings.
The bottleneck in security programs has shifted from detection to decision and calibration. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Findings already exceed capacity; finding more does not help.
  • Feedback loops on predicted vs observed exploitation are mostly absent.
  • Job descriptions still reflect a detection-bottleneck era.
Implicit premises:
  • Prioritization is a learnable, improvable system, not a one-time policy decision.
  • Without measurement, prioritization quality is unobservable and therefore undefendable.
Inferences:
  • Vendor metrics that emphasize detection breadth are now mis-aligned with buyer value.
  • MSSPs/managed-detection firms whose pricing is per-finding face structural margin pressure.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Are software vulnerabilities sparse or dense — i.e., can enumeration plus patching ever finish?
    Dense. AI-driven discovery has empirically settled what Geer could not in 2014.
  • ✅ If enumeration cannot terminate, what is the right action threshold?
    Probability of exploitation × asset exposure, above a threshold tied to team capacity and risk tolerance.
  • ✅ Why has the predictive approach not been adopted despite being technically available since 2021?
    Lack of external authoritative cover (auditors, MRM, boards). Anthropic's recommendation supplies it.
  • ✅ Where is AI's defensive value highest — discovery or prioritization?
    Prioritization, via predictive models grounded in local context; LLMs amplify the discovery problem rather than solve the defensive one.
  • ✅ How should boards measure security program effectiveness?
    Exploitability-weighted exposure over time, paired with a predicted-vs-observed exploitation calibration line.
  • ⭕ How should compliance regimes be updated to permit probabilistic prioritization?
    Author urges CISOs to engage auditors 'now, in writing' but does not propose specific framework language or a model audit response.
  • ⭕ Who owns and updates a local knowing machine — the security team, the platform team, an outside vendor?
    Implies security-data engineers but does not address build-vs-buy, governance, or model-risk-management of the predictive model itself.
  • ⭕ What happens when attackers gain access to defender-side context (insider, compromise, public-sector leaks)?
    Author asserts local context is an asymmetric advantage but does not address its fragility.
