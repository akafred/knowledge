How Platform Engineering Using Golden Bricks Can Enable Fast and Smooth Delivery

Ben Linders · rss · 2026-05-21 source 🖥️

Cognitive LoadDORA & Engineering MetricsGolden PathModularity & CouplingPlatform EngineeringSociotechnical ArchitectureTeam Autonomy
Internal platforms should be run as products that expose composable 'golden bricks' rather than prescriptive 'golden paths,' so that developer teams can self-assemble their own flow of value with low cognitive load while the platform team is held accountable to adoption, experience, and DORA-like outcome metrics.

practical · expository — A conference-talk write-up (Daniel Bryant at GOTO Copenhagen) with a follow-up InfoQ Q&A. Mostly expository reporting of practical prescriptions; the implicit imperative is 'build your internal platform this way.' Not an argument with premises so much as a curated set of recommendations backed by appeals to authority (Fournier, Nowland, DORA, SPACE, DX Core 4).

Key terms

  • Golden brick Golden Path
    A composable, self-service platform capability (a DBaaS, a CD pipeline, an environment-as-a-service, etc.) that teams can pick up and combine to build their own way of working.
  • Golden path Golden Path
    A single prescribed way to do something on the platform. Bryant treats this as the default platform-engineering offering and warns it 'can become a golden cage.'
  • Platform Platform Engineering
    A sociotechnical internal product: self-service APIs, tools, and service knowledge bundled into something developer teams choose to use.
  • Cognitive load Cognitive Load
    The amount of stuff a developer must hold in their head to get code shipped; the thing platforms exist to reduce.
  • Flow of value
    A team's end-to-end ability to deliver business value without being blocked by external dependencies (raising tickets, waiting on DBs, ports). Each team — including the platform team — must 'own' theirs.
  • Product (vs. project)
    An ongoing, customer-facing engagement measured by adoption and satisfaction, with no end date — applied to internal developer tooling.
  • Adoption / Experience / Outcomes
    A three-tier measurement scheme: are teams using it (adoption), is it pleasant and fast (experience, via SPACE / DX Core 4), and does it move the business (outcomes, via DORA-like lead time, deployment frequency, change failure rate).
  • Leaky abstraction Modularity & Coupling
    A platform abstraction whose internals show through, causing developers to depend on implementation details so the platform team can no longer change them.
  • Sociotechnical
    Platforms are simultaneously social (team-to-team relationships, service knowledge, collaboration) and technical (APIs, tools).
  • Separation of concerns (across platform layers)
    Infrastructure orchestration, platform orchestration, and application consumption must each have clear interfaces so layers can evolve independently.

Arguments

Platforms should expose composable golden bricks rather than prescribed golden paths. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • A prescribed single way ('golden path') can become a 'golden cage.'
  • Composable capabilities let teams move quickly while maintaining safety and consistency.
  • Each team must own their flow of value.
Implicit premises:
  • Teams are sufficiently capable to assemble correct, safe systems from primitives without prescription.
  • Safety and consistency can be encoded into the bricks themselves, not into a prescribed sequence.
  • The cost of a wrong assembly by a team is lower than the cost of a wrong prescription by the platform team.
  • The organisation already runs on stream-aligned, autonomous teams (Team Topologies-style).
Inferences:
  • Therefore platform teams should resist the temptation to ship 'the way' and instead ship building blocks plus optional reference compositions.
  • Therefore the unit of platform versioning is the brick, not the path — which has implications for backward-compatibility commitments the article does not draw.
  • Therefore 'paved roads' in the Netflix/Spotify sense are an intermediate, not a target.
Platforms must be run as products, not projects. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Developers are customers.
  • If developers avoid the platform, that is feedback.
  • 'If the platform makes the right thing easy to do, developers will choose to use it.'
Implicit premises:
  • Developers actually have the option not to use the platform (i.e. no mandate, no monopoly).
  • Voluntary uptake reliably tracks platform quality (rather than, say, lock-in or politics).
  • The platform team has the authority and resources to behave like a product team — including saying no to demands.
Inferences:
  • Therefore mandating platform use is self-defeating; it removes the diagnostic signal.
  • Therefore platform teams need PMs, user research, and roadmaps — not just SREs.
Platform value should be measured via adoption, experience, and outcomes (DORA-style). strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Adoption shows whether teams are actually using the platform.
  • Experience captures provisioning time and developer satisfaction (SPACE, DX Core 4).
  • Outcomes connect to business impact via lead time, deployment frequency, change failure rate.
Implicit premises:
  • These three layers compose to a sufficient picture of platform health.
  • DORA metrics, designed for delivery performance, transfer cleanly to platform performance.
  • C-level audiences will accept these metrics as legitimate platform-value evidence.
Inferences:
  • Therefore platforms without instrumented usage telemetry cannot be defended at the C-level.
  • Therefore there is no real defence of a low-adoption-but-strategically-necessary platform — an awkward implication the article does not address.
Platforms must be layered with clear interfaces (infra orch / platform orch / app consumption) to remain evolvable. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Separation of concerns is the critical principle.
  • Mixing concerns makes platforms fragile.
  • Clear interfaces let layers evolve independently.
Implicit premises:
  • The three-layer carve-up Bryant offers is the right carve-up (not, say, two layers or five).
  • Interfaces can be designed stable enough to absorb the changes the underlying tech imposes.
Inferences:
  • Therefore a monolithic 'IDP' that crosses all three layers is anti-pattern by construction.
  • Therefore Conway's law applies — the platform team itself probably needs three sub-teams.

Narratives

  • Bryant's own career arc: classical Java -> SOA -> microservices, with each step ratcheting up his cognitive load until 'it was a lot of stuff to learn to get code shipped' and he wanted 'a bunch of tools I could mix and match.'
    → Origin myth for the bricks idea — frames composability as the developer's own felt need, not an engineering fashion. Implicitly argues 'I am you; I needed this; therefore you need this.'
  • The ticket vignette: 'You're waiting for someone to give you a database or open a port. Your flow of value is disrupted.'
    → Concrete instantiation of the abstract 'flow of value' concept; supplies a vivid failure mode that the brick model claims to dissolve.
  • The 'golden cage' image — a golden path that has become a prison.
    → Metaphor doing argument-work: makes the path-model feel oppressive without having to demonstrate that it actually is. Rhetorical lever for the bricks proposition.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What is the right operating model for an internal developer platform?
    Run it as a product offering composable golden bricks, measured by adoption/experience/outcomes.
  • ✅ How should platforms be measured and justified to executives?
    Adoption (are they using it), Experience (SPACE/DX Core 4), Outcomes (DORA-like).
  • ✅ How should platforms be architecturally structured?
    Three layers with clear interfaces — infra orchestration, platform orchestration, app consumption.
  • ⭕ Under what organisational conditions can the bricks model actually work?
    Not addressed — autonomy, team topology, and decision rights are assumed.
  • ⭕ How does safety/consistency travel through composition when teams freely combine bricks?
    Asserted ('composable capabilities let teams move quickly while maintaining safety and consistency') but the mechanism is never explained.
  • ⭕ What about regulated environments where prescription is non-optional (audit, segregation of duties, data residency)?
    Not mentioned.
  • ⭕ What happens when adoption is low but the platform is strategically necessary (e.g. consolidation, cost, risk)?
    The 'adoption = feedback' frame assumes this case away.
  • ⭕ How do you avoid leaky abstractions while still exposing enough of the brick to be useful?
    Warning issued, no design guidance.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe 'golden path vs golden brick' distinction is rhetorical, not analytical. What actually changes when you move from path to brick? The article never decomposes the shift along the three axes where the shift really lives: (a) governance — who owns correctness, the platform team (path) or the consuming team (brick); (b) composability — who designs the assembly, and against what contract; (c) team autonomy — does the consuming team have the decision-rights, time, and skill to assemble well? Without this decomposition, 'bricks' is a slogan, not a model.
    Read next: Skelton & Pais, Team Topologies (esp. platform team / stream-aligned team interaction modes). Camille Fournier's writing on platform teams. Gregor Hohpe on architecture decision records and IT architecture as conversation. Manuel Pais on the cognitive-load assessment.
  • incompleteThe organisational shift the article requires but does not theorise. Bricks-not-paths only works in an organisation that has already pushed correctness, security review, cost ownership, and incident response down into stream-aligned teams. In an org where the platform team is also the compliance team and the on-call team, bricks-without-a-path means each stream team must reinvent guardrails. The piece treats this organisational redesign as free — but it is the actual hard part.
    Read next: Cutler's TBM essays on institutional overload and accountability. Allen Holub's #NoAccountability work. Conway's Law re-readings (Skelton/Pais). Andrew Clay Shafer / DevOps adoption literature on the politics of platform ownership.
  • incompleteStrawmanning of the golden path. In Spotify's original formulation and most current usage, a golden path is an opinionated default route through optional tooling — not 'the single way to do things.' Bryant collapses 'golden path' into 'golden cage' to set up the brick contrast, which makes the move feel sharper than it is. The real design choice is the ratio: which bricks ship with reference compositions (paths) attached, and which don't.
    Read next: The Spotify Backstage docs on golden paths. The 'paved road' material from Netflix engineering. Honeycomb's writing on opinionated developer platforms.
  • incompleteNo treatment of the leaky-abstraction risk that the brick model amplifies. The more composable the platform, the more surface area developers can latch onto — and the harder it becomes to change the implementation later. Bryant raises leaky abstractions as a warning but never connects them to the brick model, which is precisely where they bite hardest.
    Read next: Spolsky's original 'Law of Leaky Abstractions.' Ousterhout, A Philosophy of Software Design, on deep vs shallow modules — bricks must be deep modules or the abstraction tax is real.
  • incompleteMeasurement loop is one-way. Adoption / Experience / Outcomes is a defensive scheme for justifying the platform upward to C-level, but the article does not ask whether these metrics are good enough to guide platform design decisions downward (which brick to build next, which to deprecate, where the composition contracts are wrong). They measure existence, not direction.
    Read next: Fournier & Nowland on platform-team operating cadence. Cutler on operating-system design and signal-vs-noise in metrics. SPACE paper original (Forsgren et al.).
  • uninformedNo engagement with the actual maturity literature on internal developer platforms — CNCF Platforms Working Group whitepaper, Team Topologies' Thinnest Viable Platform concept, the platform-engineering maturity model from PlatformCon. The piece treats the field as if Bryant is naming things for the first time.
    Read next: CNCF Platforms WG whitepaper (2023+). Team Topologies' TVP material. McKinsey/ThoughtWorks platform engineering reports for the practitioner-economics view.
