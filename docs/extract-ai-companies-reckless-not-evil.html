AI Companies Aren't Evil. But They Are Reckless.

Julie Guirado (Persuasion / Center for Humane Technology) · email · 2026-05-04 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

AI Regulation & Governance (External)AI-Driven Vulnerability DiscoveryMythos
The absence of external AI governance is not a policy gap but a structural failure: a broken incentive system rewards reckless development, and only mandatory independent testing, transparency requirements, and legal liability can realign private incentives with public safety — as has been done with every other consequential technology.

practical · persuasive essay / policy argument — An op-ed making the case for AI regulation. Uses the Anthropic Mythos incident as a concrete hook, then generalizes to a structural argument about incentives and governance. Primarily prescriptive — it diagnoses a problem and proposes a regulatory framework.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why are AI companies building dangerous systems — are they evil?
    Not evil but reckless — they are rationally responding to a broken incentive structure that rewards speed over safety.
  • ✅ Can AI companies govern themselves?
    No — the Mythos incident demonstrates that self-governance fails; Anthropic's private containment was breached.
  • ✅ Is AI too complex or too important to regulate?
    The opposite — complexity and consequence increase the obligation to govern. Historical precedents prove it is possible.
  • ✅ What would meaningful AI regulation look like?
    Independent pre-deployment testing, standardized public risk reporting, whistleblower protections, and legal liability for foreseeable harm.
  • ✅ Do we have historical precedent for regulating transformative technologies without killing innovation?
    Yes — automobiles, aviation, pharmaceuticals, and nuclear energy all operate inside hard-won accountability systems.
  • ⭕ How would US-only regulation interact with international AI competition?
  • ⭕ How can regulatory frameworks keep pace with AI's rapid capability growth?
