Declining America

ongoing by Tim Bray · rss · 2026-05-21 Reader 🖥️

AI Backlash
For non-US professionals of conscience, the current state of the United States — its leader's threats against allied sovereignty and its border surveillance of travelers' speech — is reason enough to decline even valuable in-person professional invitations there.

practical · mixed — Practical: the piece both performs and models an action (declining a US trip) and offers a reusable template ('maybe someone in a similar situation might find it useful'). Form is mixed — partly expository framing, partly an embedded letter that functions as a narrative artifact carrying the argument. Very short (~207 words); most claims are gestural rather than fully argued.

Key terms

  • Declining America
    Double meaning: (1) the act of declining (refusing) invitations to the US, and (2) America itself in decline. The title compresses both — the personal refusal is a response to, and a symptom of, the national decline.
  • matter of principle
    A non-negotiable ethical stance independent of personal cost-benefit calculation; specifically, that visiting a country whose leader has threatened one's own nation is itself complicity-adjacent.
  • disclose my social media output
    US border practice of demanding travelers' social media handles/content for inspection, treated by Bray as a speech-vetting regime that creates real risk for opinionated public writers.
  • unbeliever contributions AI Backlash
    Skeptical, non-boosterish input in AI discussions; positions Bray as someone whose value to AI conversations is precisely his skepticism.

Arguments

I (a Canadian) ought to decline this US invitation. strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • The US leader has repeatedly threatened Canadian sovereignty and disrespected Canadian nationhood.
  • That same leader has followed similar rhetoric against other countries with military action.
  • I feel, on principle, negative about visiting such a country.
Implicit premises:
  • A citizen's choice to physically attend events in a hostile state confers some legitimacy on that state.
  • Personal participation, even for benign professional reasons, is morally entangled with the host nation's conduct.
  • Principles of this sort should override professional opportunity, even attractive opportunity.
Inferences:
  • If many similarly-positioned non-Americans applied the same reasoning, US-hosted professional gatherings would systematically lose their international skeptic-class participants.
  • The cost of the current US administration's conduct is partly paid in lost foreign intellectual contribution.
Even if I could 'work around' the principled objection, prudence forbids the trip. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • US border officers may demand disclosure of social media output.
  • Disclosure carries a significant risk of an extremely negative outcome.
  • I have a family to support and cannot afford that risk.
Implicit premises:
  • My existing online output contains material that would plausibly trigger a negative border outcome (i.e., I am the kind of opinionated writer the system targets).
  • Border enforcement is sufficiently arbitrary that risk cannot be reliably mitigated.
  • Family financial responsibility is a legitimate constraint on professional risk-taking, including political risk.
Inferences:
  • The class of people who self-censor or self-exclude under such a regime is precisely the class of opinionated public writers — i.e., the regime selectively filters speech-rich travelers.
  • Border practice is therefore doing soft censorship of cross-border professional discourse.
This refusal is worth publishing as a template, not just sent privately.
Explicit premises:
  • Others may be in a similar situation.
  • A worked example of a polite, reasoned decline could be useful to them.
Implicit premises:
  • There is a growing population of non-Americans facing the same decision.
  • Norms around declining US travel are unsettled enough that people need scripts.
  • Making the refusal public turns a private choice into a small coordinating signal.
Inferences:
  • Bray treats the moment as a coordination problem among professionals of conscience, not just a personal scheduling decision.

Narratives

  • The invitation-and-decline scene: Bray receives a respected, high-signal AI unconference invite, considers it, and refuses — the refusal letter is reproduced verbatim.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Should a non-American professional accept US-based invitations under the current administration?
    For Bray, no — on combined principled and prudential grounds.
  • ✅ How does one decline such an invitation without burning a valued professional relationship?
    By naming the political reason first, the personal risk second, and reaffirming friendship with the organization.
  • ⭕ What is the right response, as an AI skeptic with standing, to AI venues one cannot attend?
    Not addressed — Bray notes the gathering would have welcomed 'unbeliever contributions' but offers no alternative channel for delivering them.
  • ⭕ Is America actually in decline (the title's other reading)?
    Asserted by the title, never argued in the body.
  • ⭕ What does it cost the host country, and the field, when senior skeptics from abroad self-exclude?
    Unaddressed.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe piece asserts 'decline' in the title but argues only for a personal travel choice. The civilizational thesis — that America is in decline — is gestured at, not defended. A reader sympathetic to the personal decision still gets no argument that this episode is symptomatic of a larger fall rather than a single-administration anomaly.
    Read next: Pair with longer-form diagnoses of US decline (e.g., institutional-decay writers, comparative democratic-backsliding literature) to see whether Bray's tacit framework holds up.
  • incompleteThe implicit premise 'attendance confers legitimacy' is never defended. There is a serious counter-tradition — that engagement with flawed states is precisely how skeptical voices do their work — which Bray does not address.
    Read next: Read against the 'engagement vs. boycott' debate in academic, scientific, and dissident traditions; consider how AI skeptics in particular weigh presence vs. absence.
  • incompleteNo discussion of remote participation. The 21st-century alternative — joining the unconference by video, contributing in writing, sending a paper — is conspicuously absent, even though it would let Bray's 'unbeliever contribution' reach the room without the border risk.
    Read next: Look at how distributed/hybrid professional gatherings handle politically constrained participants; this is a Cutler-adjacent organizational design question.
  • incompleteThe border-risk argument is asserted as 'significant risk of an extremely negative outcome' without evidence or calibration. For a writer this careful, the absence of any base rate or case is notable; the reader is asked to take the probability on trust.
    Read next: Reporting on US border social-media screening practices (EFF, Knight Institute, recent journalism on detentions of writers/academics) for actual frequencies and patterns.
  • incompleteThe piece is silent on the asymmetry it creates: the people most likely to self-exclude are precisely those whose skeptical AI contributions are most needed in US-hosted rooms. Bray notes the gathering wanted 'unbeliever contributions' but does not reckon with what his absence — multiplied — does to the discourse he cares about.
    Read next: Read alongside discussions of self-censorship effects and how skeptic exit reshapes communities (Hirschman's Exit, Voice, and Loyalty is the classic frame).
