The 8 software engineering metrics AI broke

Michael Hill · article · 2026-06-16 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Code Was Never the BottleneckCognitive DebtDeveloper Experience & FlowDORA & Engineering MetricsGoodhart's LawMeasuring AI ImpactTechnical DebtToken Throughput
Software engineering metrics were built on the assumption that human effort and output move in rough proportion, and AI has severed that link by making activity nearly free — so activity/volume proxies (deployment frequency, cycle time, PR and commit volume, lines of code, satisfaction, efficiency/flow, DevEx/DXI) now measure tool adoption rather than engineering quality, while only outcome-based measures (time to recover, escaped defect rate, delivered value) remain trustworthy, because they measure what happened rather than how much happened.

practical · mixed (expository listicle scaffolded on an argumentative core) — Trade journalism built from named-practitioner quotes (a CTO, an EVP of consulting, two senior engineers, a vendor's head of AI/research, a community CEO) rather than data or citation to a study. The per-metric structure looks like a bare checklist but each entry does real argumentative work: state the metric's original validity assumption, show the specific mechanism by which AI severs it, then (for 3 of 11 metrics discussed) argue why that mechanism doesn't apply.

Key terms

  • Tokenmaxxing Token Throughput
    The practice of deliberately maximizing AI token consumption to game a productivity metric rather than to produce better software — presented here as the purest current instance of Goodhart's Law under AI.
  • Goodhart's Law Goodhart's Law
    Reframed as a threshold that AI has crossed from occasional to unavoidable: pre-AI, gaming a metric required deliberate effort; with AI, inflating deployment frequency, cycle time, or PR volume is a side effect of ordinary tool use, not intentional manipulation.
  • DORA metrics DORA & Engineering Metrics
    Split into two evidentiary classes: three (deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate) are declared broken because they track volume/speed; the fourth (time to recover) is declared to survive because it tracks an outcome (recovery from failure) rather than activity.
  • SPACE framework
    Cited as the framework built specifically to go beyond activity/velocity — yet the article argues its own dimensions (satisfaction, activity, efficiency/flow) are equally distorted by AI, undercutting the idea that a 'more human' framework is inherently safer.
  • Escaped defect rate
    Positioned as one of the three metrics AI cannot inflate without consequence, because it measures an outcome (a bug reaching production) rather than an activity, so it directly tracks whether verification is keeping pace with generation speed.

Arguments

Most standard activity/volume engineering metrics (deployment frequency, cycle time, PR and commit volume, lines of code, satisfaction, efficiency/flow, DevEx/DXI) no longer signal engineering health under AI-assisted development. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • These metrics were built on the assumption that human effort and output move in rough proportion
  • AI has made output production nearly free, severing that proportionality
  • Named practitioners report specific mechanisms (tool-adoption masquerading as maturity, defect-escape rising as cycle time falls, satisfaction rising while engagement hollows out) by which each metric's signal breaks
Implicit premises:
  • The proportionality assumption was previously validated well enough by practice, not merely correlational luck, so its severing is a real regression rather than exposing a pre-existing flaw
  • A handful of named-practitioner anecdotes, one per metric, generalize to the industry rather than describing idiosyncratic organizations
Inferences:
  • Generalizes from single-quote mechanism stories to a sweeping claim covering 8 distinct metrics across 2 frameworks plus standalone measures
  • Treats a plausible causal mechanism (AI makes activity cheap) as sufficient to conclude the metrics are now actively misleading, rather than merely noisier
Three metrics — time to recover, escaped defect rate, and outcome/value-delivered measures — remain trustworthy because they measure outcomes rather than activity. strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • Time to recover concerns incident response speed, a problem unrelated to code-generation speed
  • Escaped defect rate concerns bugs that slip past verification into production, an outcome rather than an activity count
  • Outcome measures (feature usage, error-rate change, customer impact) directly track delivered value rather than a proxy for it
Implicit premises:
  • Verification and incident-response processes are not equally accelerated or gamed by AI the way code generation is
  • No adjacent process (e.g., AI-written verbose test suites inflating apparent coverage, or AI-assisted triage reducing recorded recovery time) can indirectly distort these three metrics the way it distorts the other 8
Inferences:
  • Infers durability from the activity-vs-outcome category distinction alone, without independently testing whether AI has in fact left these three untouched
  • Extends the general principle 'AI cannot fake outcomes' to specific metrics without addressing how each is actually measured in practice

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Do standard software engineering metrics still measure what they were designed to measure now that AI accelerates code production?
    No — for 8 of the 11 metrics discussed, activity/volume proxies now measure tool adoption or token spend rather than engineering quality or value.
  • ✅ Which metrics remain reliable signals of engineering health in the AI era?
    Time to recover, escaped defect rate, and outcome/value-delivered measures — because they track results rather than activity.
  • ⭕ How should an organization concretely redesign its dashboard around outcome-based measurement (which metrics, thresholds, or combinations)?
  • ⭕ Are the three recommended 'safe' metrics themselves immune to Goodhart's Law once organizations start explicitly targeting them, or only currently under-targeted?

Gaps

  • illogicalThe article's own framing is internally inconsistent about scope: the intro promises 'ten metrics that are breaking down,' the SPACE section is introduced as covering 'a couple of metrics' but actually covers three, and the title promises '8' broken metrics — which does match the count of metrics explicitly presented as broken (3 DORA + 3 SPACE + 2 standalone), but only by excluding the 3 counter-example metrics from the 'ten' the intro promised. The total metrics actually discussed is 11, not 10, and 'a couple' undercounts by one. This suggests the taxonomy was fitted to a round headline number rather than derived from a principled classification.
    Read next: Compare against the live leaddev.com page for possible post-publication editing (the mismatch is exactly the kind an editor introduces when trimming or adding an example late); no corpus cross-reference needed.
  • uninformedThe central claim that three metrics (time to recover, escaped defect rate, outcome/value measures) 'still hold up' under AI is argued purely by category (outcome vs. activity) and supported by zero data, study, or even anecdote — in sharp contrast to the detailed, named-practitioner mechanism given for each of the 8 metrics declared broken. Nearly every other specific claim in the piece is sourced to quotes from individuals at vendor-adjacent companies (Jellyfish sells engineering-intelligence software; Radixweb is a technology consultancy; Aviator sells PR/merge-automation tooling) rather than to any independent benchmark or controlled study.
    Read next: This corpus's extract-ai-coding-two-kinds-debt.json documents the same general phenomenon (a comfort-metric like satisfaction/NPS rising while something real falls) with an actual RCT (N=52) and an 8-cohort/225-intern longitudinal study — read that piece's evidentiary structure as the bar this article does not meet. Look for DORA/Accelerate's own published research on whether MTTR specifically has been measured, not just asserted, as AI-invariant.
  • incompleteThe article treats Goodhart's Law as a problem specific to activity/volume metrics and implicitly exempts its own three recommended outcome metrics without argument. But each of the three is itself targetable: MTTR can be gamed by redefining what counts as 'recovered,' escaped defect rate by narrowing what counts as 'production' or as a 'defect,' and outcome measures by selectively reporting favorable outcomes. The piece never asks whether outcome metrics are inherently more Goodhart-resistant or merely currently under-targeted because organizations haven't gotten around to gaming them yet.
    Read next: Manheim & Garrabrant's taxonomy of Goodhart's Law variants (regressional, extremal, causal, adversarial) to test whether outcome proxies are categorically different from activity proxies or just earlier in the same targeting cycle.

Closes gaps elsewhere

  • The Future Of Software Engineering · g6 — That extraction flagged a gap: no discussion of the economic incentive structures driving organizations toward AI-era regressions (e.g., short-term productivity metrics rewarding large batch output), citing Goodhart's Law and Jerry Muller's 'Tyranny of Metrics' as the needed reading direction. This article supplies exactly that analysis — naming the mechanism (once a metric like token spend, deployment frequency, or PR volume is targeted, gaming becomes a costless side effect of normal AI tool use rather than requiring deliberate effort) and demonstrating it across DORA, SPACE, and volume metrics. It is a partial closure: it doesn't address the batch-size regression specifically that g6 was raised against, but supplies the general incentive-structure account g6 said was missing.
