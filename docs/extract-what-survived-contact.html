When agents write the code, rigor does not vanish but migrates to the objective and its evaluation; directing an agent is therefore a management act (a hire, not a tool-use) whose one non-delegable duty is owning the objective and the oracle that checks it.

practical · mixed — A retired practitioner's field report from a 'future of software development' retreat, blending narrative reportage of the room with an argumentative reframe of how non-engineers should direct AI coding agents and expository detail on evaluating objectives (BDD, the referenced oracle).

Key terms

  • survived contact
    Which of the author's ideas held up, got argued with, and stayed standing when stress-tested against a room of experts who knew the subject better than he did
  • the spine
    The core finding the author cares most about and keeps arguing: rigor doesn't vanish when the agent writes the code, it migrates upstream into specs and down into tests, types, constraints, and risk-tiering
  • a hire, not a tool
    The claim that when a manager reaches for an LLM instead of routing work to their team, they have added a participant to be managed, not picked up an instrument to operate
  • manage by objective (not by method) Manage by Objective (not by Method)
    Drucker's 1959 principle applied to agents: when the worker is out-known about the specifics, you supply a goal it can test itself against rather than prescribing how to do the work
  • the oracle The Oracle (Referenced Evaluation)
    The evaluation of the objective — the fitness function the keep-the-survivor game runs on — that decides whether generated output is correct, and the one thing the person hiring the model cannot delegate back to it
  • BDD / specification by example
    Stating the objective AS its own executable evaluation: a Given/When/Then scenario that is the behavior written so it runs, rather than a description sitting next to a separate test
  • referenced (not authored) then
    The 'then' of an acceptance scenario computed live from a running reference (e.g. requiring byte-for-byte identity with the existing Rails app) per input, instead of a hand-written, frozen expected result
  • sample of one Product Discovery & Experimentation
    The author's single-project, single-agent, single-retired-developer anecdote whose edges he keeps marking; also turned on the retreat itself, which is a sample too and not a random one
  • software darwinism
    Standing up several candidate implementations at once and keeping the one that survives the oracle, rather than iterating serially, once building is cheaper than deciding what to build

Arguments

The room's 'may non-engineers steer the model?' permission question is the wrong question; the right requirement is that whoever directs the model can manage by objective. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Reaching for an LLM instead of routing work to your team is a hire, not tool-use, and a manager doesn't ask permission to give work to a new participant.
  • Drucker (1959): when the worker knows the specifics better than the manager, you manage by objective, not by method.
  • The non-engineer steering an agent is exactly a manager out-known by the thing they direct, and the slop the room fears is the old danger of managing by method when you should manage by objective.
Implicit premises:
  • Managing by objective is a teachable, hireable skill that does not require first becoming an engineer.
  • The model is reliably out-known about the specifics by whoever directs it, making method-prescription the wrong stance.
Inferences:
  • Therefore you don't forbid the hire; you require the director to own the objective and its evaluation.
The stake the author feared was missing from the model is not actually missing when you manage by objective — it lives in the objective, not the worker. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Hand the model a task and the missing stake is felt: it does what you said without caring whether what you said was worth doing.
  • Hand it a goal it can test itself against and it tries, checks itself, throws out its own bad rocks, and keeps going until the goal is met — the way a stakeholder behaves.
  • The checkable form of the stake is the only form you could ever have relied on anyway.
Implicit premises:
  • Stakeholder-like behavior is defined by goal-directed self-correction, not by inner caring.
  • The objective can encode 'what is worth doing' well enough to substitute for the worker's stake.
Inferences:
  • Therefore manage by task and the absence of stake is real; manage by objective and the objective supplies it.
BDD with a referenced (not authored or frozen) oracle is the strongest way to state an objective, because it is the objective's own evaluation and covers inputs you never enumerated. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • A Given/When/Then scenario is the behavior written so it runs — it is the objective, not a pointer to it — while prose specs can't run, types are inferable, and constraints and risk tiers are downstream.
  • On Roundhouse a running reference (the Rails app) supplied the expected answer, so the acceptance gate required byte-for-byte identity computed fresh per request rather than a frozen list of answers.
  • A referenced oracle isn't a list of answers, it's a way of generating them, so it holds over inputs never written down.
Implicit premises:
  • An oracle that generates answers beats one that stores them.
  • A running reference is available — which holds when rebuilding an existing system but not for genuinely new behavior.
Inferences:
  • Therefore the referenced oracle is the non-delegable asset, checking every request rather than a handful of authored scenarios.

Narratives

  • The author kept his laptop open with Claude coding in the background throughout the retreat's sessions, and when an incredulous participant probed how he was using it he showed live examples straight from the work in progress.
    → Grounds the 'sample of one' in demonstrable practice and lets the ideas be stress-tested with evidence rather than assertion.
  • In the 'bring me a rock' session, the room narrowed exploration-by-elimination from how to explore to who should even be allowed to, surfacing the worry that non-engineers steering the model produce slop.
    → Sets up the reframe that directing a model is a hire, not a permission-gated tool-use.
  • Roundhouse — the author's third rebuild of the same problem — used an acceptance gate that fetched the same URL from Rails and from each generated target and required byte-for-byte identical responses, queried live per request and never frozen.
    → Concrete demonstration of the referenced oracle: the objective evaluated over every input, not a handful of authored scenarios.
  • 'We're regulated; we can't afford the risk' was the exact sentence the author heard about open source nearly thirty years ago, from someone whose call was defensible but who was guarding the wrong artifact — governance never lived in inspecting every line but in governing what the code answered to.
    → Analogical argument that governance relocates rather than disappears, predicting the same relocation for agent-written code.
  • Three people on three continents — matz, Ori Pekelman, and the author — have each been carrying a substantial piece of a system, coordinating through nothing more exotic than issues and pull requests.
    → Offers evidence for the open question of whether large systems can be composed from small teams that never merge into one.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How much of the author's 'sample of one' thesis survived contact with a room that knew the subject better than he did?
    The spine (rigor migrates, doesn't vanish) held and matched the earlier retreat; his ideas were sought out, argued with, and taken seriously, and the near-term engineering forecast had aged fast toward his optimism.
  • ✅ Given that expertise separates a good outcome from slop, should non-engineers be allowed to steer the model at all?
    Wrong question: directing a model is a hire, not a tool-use, so you don't forbid it — you require the director to own the objective and its evaluation (manage by objective).
  • ✅ Is a peer without stake (the model) a diminished peer?
    No — the stake was never missing from the worker; it lives in the objective. Manage by task and its absence is real, but manage by objective and the objective supplies the stake in a checkable form.
  • ✅ How should an objective be evaluated so that manage-by-objective actually works?
    State the objective as its own runnable evaluation (BDD), and where a running reference exists, use a referenced oracle that computes the expected answer fresh per input rather than authoring and freezing answers.
  • ⭕ Once building a thing costs less than deciding whether to build it, why iterate serially — and when do you evolve an implementation versus start over?
    Left open: he floats 'software darwinism' (stand up several candidates, keep the survivor) and notes restarts are cheap when the oracle stays constant and prior code becomes a corpus to consult, but doesn't know where the evolve-vs-restart line falls.
  • ⭕ How short does the list of things only a human can do get as best practices (code review, security audit, testing, docs, triage, postmortems) move to the machine?
    Left open: the register flipped fast and even code review and security audit went early; his bet is that when the list stops shrinking, the oracle is the only thing left on it.
  • ⭕ Can large systems be composed from small ones held by small teams that never merge, coordinating only via issues and pull requests?
    Left open as a line to add to the map, backed only by a three-person, three-continent anecdote.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe whole cross-retreat comparison rests on an acknowledged seam: the author read only the public summary of the earlier retreat, so he cannot separate 'the conversation genuinely grew' from 'the earlier summary simply compressed this out.' The central 'what changed' claim is offered on face value with that limitation showing.
    Read next: Obtain the fuller record (not just the public key-takeaways summary) of the earlier Thoughtworks retreat and re-run the comparison against like-for-like source material.
  • incompleteThe optimistic evidence is uncontrolled, single-team, self-reported metrics (a sample of one), which the author admits carries no conclusions; the piece names the asymmetry but does not offer a path to controlled, comparable evidence for the upside.
    Read next: Look for controlled or quasi-experimental studies of agentic-coding productivity and quality, and for methods that make single-team metrics comparable across teams.
  • incompleteThe referenced-oracle technique is demonstrated only where a running reference implementation already existed (rebuilding Rails behavior in Roundhouse). The piece does not address how to construct a self-generating oracle for genuinely new behavior, where the intent lives only in a human's head and there is nothing to reference.
    Read next: Read on property-based and metamorphic testing, and on deriving executable oracles for green-field behavior where no reference system exists.
  • illogicalThe claim that the model's missing stake 'lives in the objective' assumes the checkable oracle fully captures what the human cared about. It doesn't examine the Goodhart case where a passable oracle diverges from the true, uncheckable intent — the model satisfies the letter of the objective while missing its point.
    Read next: Read on specification gaming, Goodhart's law, and reward hacking in agentic systems, and on how to detect an oracle that has drifted from intent.
  • incompleteThe 'small teams that never merge into one' scaling hypothesis rests on a three-person anecdote and does not address where plain issues-and-PRs coordination breaks down as the number of subsystems, their coupling, or team count grows.
    Read next: Read on coordination cost, Conway's law limits, and the failure modes of loosely federated subsystems at larger scale.
