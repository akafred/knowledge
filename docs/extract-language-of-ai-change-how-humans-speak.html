The Language of AI Could Change How Humans Speak

Bruce Schneier and Ada Palmer · rss · 2026-07-09 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

AI SlopCognitive SurrenderWriting as Thinking
Because large language models are trained on only a narrow, written and scripted slice of human language while excluding the vast majority of unscripted natural speech, humans' growing immersion in AI-generated text will feed back into how we talk, think and see ourselves — flattening, homogenizing and distorting our expression and self-understanding toward the machine's impoverished patterns — unless we find ways to train these models on authentic informal human speech.

theoretical · op-ed / persuasive cultural-criticism essay — Predominantly theoretical — a diagnostic prediction about how LLM-generated language will reshape human speech, thought and self-understanding — with only a gestural practical coda (a call to train models on informal speech). Key terms

  • Large language models (as an impoverished linguistic sample)
    Systems whose grasp of human language is limited to written text and scripted movie/TV dialogue, structurally excluding the unscripted face-to-face and voice conversation that is most of human speech.
  • Unscripted / authentically human speech
    The free-wheeling, meandering, interrupted, emotionally-textured natural conversation that is the vast majority of language and a vital component of culture — and the very thing LLMs never see.
  • Linguistic convergence / homogenization
    The narrowing of human expression — shorter sentences, top-1,000 vocabulary, rigid formulas, curt command register — as people imitate the narrow patterns of AI text, compounded by models training on their own output.
  • Sycophancy
    The design tendency of chatbots to agree with and affirm user statements regardless of merit, entrenching confirmation bias and even worsening psychosis.
  • Selective slice / survivorship bias in the corpus
    The distortion that results when a model (or a historian) generalizes about a society from a non-representative sample of its available communications — over-weighting the written, online, elite or dramatic.
  • Online disinhibition effect
    The documented tendency to be crueler in text online than face to face — the slice of behavior LLMs disproportionately learn from, since flame wars leave an eternal footprint while spoken reconciliation fades.

Arguments

Widespread immersion in AI-generated text will degrade and homogenize human language and, through it, human thought and self-understanding. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • LLMs are trained on a narrow written/scripted slice and miss the majority of natural speech (p1).
  • Humans imitate the linguistic patterns they are immersed in — as texting/social media, the 2022 Siri/Alexa curtness study, and children absorbing new speech patterns show.
  • Machine language is measurably narrower and more rigid than human speech (Coruna study; the formulaic replies) (p4, p5).
  • LLMs increasingly train on their own output, compounding the narrowing (p6).
Implicit premises:
  • Language habits shape cognition and self-perception, not merely surface style (p14).
  • The natural features being lost are valuable, so their erosion is a harm rather than neutral drift (p15).
  • Human exposure to AI text will be large and one-directional enough to move population-level linguistic norms.
Inferences:
  • From 'we imitate what we're immersed in' plus 'we'll be immersed in narrow AI text' -> our language will narrow and rigidify.
  • From 'language shapes thought' plus 'our language narrows' -> our thought and self-understanding narrow and distort.
LLMs will systematically misrepresent human communication — making us seem more quarrelsome and over-weighting topics salient online — because they generalize from a non-representative slice. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Training on a selective slice of a society's communications introduces distortions, as historical survivorship bias shows (Norse sagas, chivalric romances, Cicero) (p10).
  • LLM corpora over-represent written, online and scripted speech (Twitter/X, Bluesky, LinkedIn, Goodreads, police-drama TV) and under-represent natural conversation (p10, p11).
Implicit premises:
  • The distortion in the model's picture will propagate into humans' self-picture via the imitation mechanism of the first argument.
Inferences:
  • Selective corpus -> distorted model output -> (via imitation) distorted human self-understanding.
A better path exists: train models on informal, authentic human speech. strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • The same ingenuity that builds AI models could solve the data problem (p13).
Implicit premises:
  • The distortion is a fixable data-collection problem rather than an intrinsic limit of the technology.
Inferences:
  • If richer speech data can be gathered, models — and thus our linguistic environment — need not be impoverished.

Narratives

  • A 2022 study found children in Siri/Alexa households became curt with humans, barking 'Hey, do X' and expecting obedience, especially toward default-female voices.
    → Empirical anchor for the claim that AI-command habits erode courteousness and train a barking-orders register (p3).
  • A University of Coruna study finding machine language has a narrower sentence-length range (12-20 words) and vocabulary than human speech.
    → Evidence that AI text constricts expression and drops the meanders/interruptions that carry emotion (p4).
  • ChatGPT answering 'I hate Beth!' with a rigid three-part affirmation/invitation formula and 'What's Beth's deal?!' with a multiple-choice-exam bullet list no human would speak.
    → Concrete illustration of the inhuman conversational formulas we may absorb through repeated exposure (p5).
  • Students telling their teachers they use generative AI because they struggle to express what they think — not realizing that writing/speaking is how one realizes what one thinks.
    → Shows how leaning on AI for expression forecloses the thinking that expression normally produces; the model regurgitates unexamined guesses in confident language (p8).
  • Historical survivorship bias — Norse sagas depicting Viking warriors not farmers, chivalric romances erasing medieval republics, and Cicero authoring 10% of surviving Latin and 70% of surviving Roman uses of 'republic'.
    → Analogy grounding the claim that training on a selective slice distorts the picture of a society — and, by extension, LLMs' (and our) picture of ourselves (p10).
  • The online disinhibition effect — venting ferocious online rage, then reconciling warmly in person — where flame wars leave an eternal footprint but spoken reconciliation fades.
    → Explains why LLMs learn humanity at its cruelest and are shaped by online aggression even when trying to avoid it (p9).

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How will widespread use of LLMs affect human language and, through it, human thought and self-understanding?
    It will homogenize and narrow expression (curter, shorter, top-1,000 vocabulary, rigid formulas), entrench sycophantic confirmation bias and impostor syndrome, and — because language is treated as formative — distort how we think and see ourselves.
  • ✅ Why are LLMs linguistically impoverished, and why would they misrepresent us?
    They are trained on a selective slice — written, online, scripted speech — that excludes the majority of natural conversation, and generalizing from that slice systematically distorts the picture (as with historical survivorship bias), compounded by models training on their own output.
  • ⭕ Can this be fixed?
    The authors disclaim a known solution and appeal to ingenuity to train models on informal speech, but concede their one concrete route (recording natural conversation) is infeasible at scale on privacy grounds — leaving the remedy open.
  • ⭕ Does the form of the language we habitually use actually shape our thought and self-perception?
    Assumed throughout as the load-bearing mechanism but never argued or qualified — the essay does not engage whether or how much linguistic form determines cognition.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe essay's central causal step — that habitually adopting AI's linguistic patterns reshapes not just expression but cognition and self-understanding — rests on an unexamined strong form of linguistic relativity. It never engages the long debate over whether or how much linguistic form determines thought, nor distinguishes surface stylistic mimicry from genuine cognitive change.
    Read next: Linguistic-relativity / Sapir-Whorf debate (Lera Boroditsky's experiments versus Steven Pinker's 'The Language Instinct'); psycholinguistics on register/style versus underlying cognition.
  • incompleteThe imitation dynamic is assumed to run one way — humans regressing toward the machine's narrow distribution — but the essay never argues why influence is asymmetrically downward rather than humans continuing to speak naturally offline, code-switching between an AI register and a human register, or reacting against AI style; the magnitude and direction of population-level uptake is extrapolated from a few micro-studies.
    Read next: Sociolinguistics of register and code-switching; language-change and diffusion-of-innovation literature; empirical evidence on whether texting/social media actually degraded offline speech rather than merely adding a register.
  • incompleteThe corpus-bias argument slides, without argument, from 'the model's picture of us is distorted' to 'our self-picture will be distorted,' conflating a representational bias inside the model with a formative effect on humans; the bridge between the misrepresentation argument and the imitation argument is asserted rather than built. And the proposed remedy — train on informal speech — is self-undercut by the authors' own privacy caveat, with no alternative (representative sampling, synthetic data, consented corpora) explored.
    Read next: Model-collapse and synthetic-data literature; representative-sampling versus exhaustive-recording tradeoffs; privacy/consent frameworks for speech-data collection.
