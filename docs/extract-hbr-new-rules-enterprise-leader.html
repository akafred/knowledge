The New Rules for Becoming an Enterprise Leader

HBR.org · podcast transcript · 2026-07-14 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Adaptive Advantage / Adaptive CapacityAgentic AIAI Labor DisruptionCareer DevelopmentDecision-Making PracticesExpert GeneralistLeader Self-AwarenessPsychological Safety
Three converging forces — pervasive personal/organizational use of AI, geopolitical turbulence, and compressed leadership pipelines — have made every one of the seven classic functional-to-enterprise leadership transitions harder and faster than before, so organizations must replace 'ready now' succession planning with a 'ready enough plus support' model that deliberately builds both outer leader skills (strategic thinking, decision architecture, stakeholder navigation) and inner leader skills (polarity tolerance, emotional regulation, real-time self-awareness) earlier in careers, through curated crucible experiences rather than credentialing.

practical · mixed — HBR IdeaCast interview in which hosts Alison Beard and Adi Ignatius draw out Michael Watkins's updated framework (from his HBR article '3 Forces That Are Redefining the Transition From Manager to Leader'). Structurally it alternates between expository framework-presentation (three forces, seven transitions, outer/inner leader skills) and directly prescriptive advice for executives and the organizations developing them — a practical text organized as a decade-later revision of Watkins's own earlier model (The First 90 Days).

Key terms

  • three forces
    The three drivers Watkins identifies as jointly redefining enterprise leadership since his original model: pervasive/agentic AI use, geopolitical turbulence, and compressed leadership pipelines
  • three languages
    What a generalist leader must now speak: business, technology, and specifically the interaction between the two — an addition to the older T-shaped-leadership model, which only required business-function breadth
  • ready enough
    The replacement standard for succession-planning readiness: not certified full readiness for a role, but sufficient readiness combined with a deliberate organizational bet and ongoing support
  • adaptive advantage Adaptive Advantage / Adaptive Capacity
    A form of competitive advantage grounded in the capacity to move and learn faster than the environment and competitors, as opposed to static advantages like IP or manufacturing scale
  • outer leader skills / inner leader skills
    Outer leader skills: recognized, largely traditional competencies (strategic thinking, disciplined decision-making, adaptive influence, stakeholder navigation). Inner leader skills: mental agility across perspectives, tolerance for holding polarities, emotional regulation, and real-time self-awareness — developed with IMD colleagues as the neglected complement to outer skills
  • crucible experience
    A deliberately curated, high-stakes assignment (e.g., a turnaround, or taking a successful business to the next level) designed to test and develop senior leadership capability under real conditions
  • tensional range / tension tolerance
    The capacity to shift perspective between levels (cloud vs. ground, big picture vs. detail, now vs. future) and to hold opposing views simultaneously, treated as a core but currently unmeasured inner-leader capability
  • corporate diplomat
    A leader who applies classical-diplomacy habits of thought — reflexive coalitional thinking, building alliances of convenience around specific issues rather than only with natural allies — to both external (competitor/industry) and internal (senior-stakeholder) relationships

Propositions

  • explicitAI use has become so pervasive and so close to indistinguishable from a leader's own work that leaders must be prepared to use AI tools personally in order to make a successful transition to enterprise leadership.conf: high
  • explicitGeopolitical turbulence (the Ukraine war, US-China-Europe trade-bloc tensions, Middle East conflict) has become a primary factor that leaders must actively orient their businesses around, not a background condition as in the prior period of relative stability.conf: high
    supportsThe Case for Hiring a Chief Resilience Officer · p10 — Both treat disruption/turbulence as a now-permanent condition leaders must structurally orient around, rather than an exceptional event to plan past.
  • explicitFlattened organizations and AI's elimination of entry-level positions are compressing leadership pipelines, giving aspiring leaders less time and experience before they reach senior levels of responsibility.conf: high
    supportsThe loudest warning about AI and jobs yet · p3 — Watkins's compressed-pipeline force names the same entry-level-substitution mechanism at the general-management level that this labor-economics piece documents for the broader workforce.
    extendsWe've Stopped Arguing About Whether AI Changes Software · p8 — Extends the engineering-specific 'disappearing entry-level rung' problem to the general enterprise-leadership pipeline — same mechanism, a different professional ladder.
  • explicitTraditional succession planning's 'ready now' standard should be replaced with a 'ready enough, with support' model, because compressed pipelines force organizations to make talent bets rather than wait for certified readiness.conf: high
    extendsSupporting Your Employees' Career Growth When Everyone Is Overwhelmed · p2 — Both argue that a traditional linear/certification-based model (career ladder; succession readiness) is breaking down under the same AI/uncertainty pressures, from different angles.
  • explicitThe specialist-to-generalist transition now requires fluency in a third 'language' — the interaction between business and technology — not just breadth across traditional business functions as in the older T-shaped-leadership model.conf: high
    supportsSupporting Your Employees' Career Growth When Everyone Is Overwhelmed · p5 — Watkins's specialist-to-generalist transition (deep specialization no longer sufficient) matches this piece's claim that deep specialization is itself now a risk because specialized skills are more automatable.
  • explicitThe integrator's job has shifted from synthesizing team input into a decision to designing the architecture and governance for decision-making across human and AI inputs, including judging which inputs get processed by which form of AI.conf: high
  • explicitLeaders increasingly must act on conclusions produced by AI systems whose reasoning they don't fully understand, which creates new vulnerabilities around measuring risk and assigning accountability when something goes wrong.conf: high
    supportsResearch: Why You Shouldn't Treat AI Agents Like Employees · p6 — Both converge on human accountability as the load-bearing constraint precisely because the AI system itself cannot be held accountable for its conclusions.
  • explicitRising uncertainty is breaking traditional plan-and-execute strategy cycles; in Watkins's own survey research, close to 75% of senior leaders said events were moving faster than their planning cycles could account for.conf: high
  • explicitDurable competitive advantage is shifting from static sources (IP, manufacturing scale) toward adaptive capacity — the ability to move and learn faster than the environment and competitors — which may be the only form of advantage that survives a rapidly moving capability frontier.conf: high
    supportsPresentation: Engineering at AI Speed: Lessons from the First Agentically Accelerated Software Project · p1 — Independently arrives at the same conclusion — that in a fast-moving environment, the capacity to learn/adapt becomes the only durable edge — from a software-delivery-cost argument rather than a leadership-strategy one.
    supportsTravel Through the Lens of AI with Booking.com CEO Glenn Fogel · p1 — Both argue no fixed position protects a company against a fast-moving environment, converging on adaptive capacity as the only durable advantage.
  • explicitStrategic thinking — especially the ability to detect and integrate weak signals — underpins both traditional and adaptive strategy, and organizations should build 'intelligent organizational sensory systems' (Watkins cites Amazon) that distill weak signals into actionable intelligence.conf: medium
  • explicitBecause change now outpaces the traditional bounded transformation cycle (current state → transition → new stable state), organizations should build continual adaptability and resilience directly into the organization rather than treating transformation as episodic.conf: high
  • explicitAdaptive talent, psychological safety, and trust are the essential foundation of an adaptive organization; without them, small teams, human-AI hybrids, governance frameworks, and agile methodologies layered on top will not actually work.conf: high
    supportsPresentation: The Human Scalability Problem: Why Your Teams Don't Scale Like Your Code · p5 — Watkins's claim that psychological safety and trust are the essential foundation of an adaptive organization matches this piece's claim that autonomy is unusable without trust and psychological safety first.
  • explicitUnder high uncertainty, prioritization should shift from committing to a single plan toward developing options, placing selective bets, and avoiding irreversible commitments — a 'two-way door' mindset for setting priorities.conf: high
  • explicitThe diplomat role has widened both externally (building alliances and 'coalitions of convenience' with competitors, e.g. around AI protocols) and internally (building broader trust and consensus among senior stakeholders), because consequential bets under uncertainty require wider support.conf: high
  • explicitStepping up to enterprise leadership requires a deliberate cognitive reorientation in which a leader lets go of the functional discipline that made them successful lower down (e.g., a CFO's financial discipline), because clinging to it becomes a liability at the top.conf: high
  • explicitLeadership development should be split into 'outer leader skills' (strategic thinking, disciplined decision-making, adaptive influence, stakeholder navigation) and 'inner leader skills' (mental agility, polarity tolerance, emotional regulation, real-time self-awareness), and organizations should start building the inner set much earlier in careers.conf: high
  • explicitReal-time self-awareness — awareness of what's happening in the environment as it happens — is a distinct capability from retrospective self-awareness of one's own strengths and vulnerabilities, and is less commonly built or assessed.conf: high
    extendsWhen You Start to Find Employee Requests Irritating · p10 — Watkins's 'real-time self-awareness' (awareness of the unfolding environment) sharpens this piece's therapeutic/countertransference-style self-awareness framing into a specifically enterprise-leadership competency.
  • explicitDeliberately curated 'crucible experiences' (e.g., a turnaround assignment, or taking a successful business to the next level) are a more effective way to test and build senior leadership capability than conventional leadership-development programs.conf: medium
  • explicitExisting assessment tools (e.g., the Hogan Development Survey) can usefully surface emotional-regulation 'derailers,' but no adequate instrument yet exists to assess tensional range or tension tolerance.conf: high
  • explicitOrganizations are increasing the share of externally recruited senior leaders because compressed pipelines reduce the supply of internally 'ready' candidates, even while continuing to invest in internal development — a nuanced rather than either/or shift, partly offset in large multi-divisional firms whose sub-business leaders already function as a built-in bench.conf: medium
  • implicitThe seven named transition labels themselves (specialist→generalist, analyst→integrator, etc.) remain the right skeleton for describing enterprise leadership; only the content and difficulty of each transition has changed since Watkins's original model.conf: medium
  • implicitThe three-forces, seven-transitions framework generalizes across industries, geographies, and organization sizes, even though every illustrative example in the conversation is a large, AI-adopting, multi-divisional global corporation.conf: medium

Arguments

Personal AI fluency is now a baseline requirement for successfully transitioning into enterprise leadership, not a delegable technical concern. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Virtually every leader today works with AI personally and within organizations using AI, increasingly agentic AI (P1)
  • The use of AI technology has become almost indistinguishable from the leader's own work (P1)
Implicit premises:
  • A leader who does not personally use AI tools cannot credibly direct or govern an organization that depends on them
  • Technical fluency at the top is now inseparable from strategic capability, reversing the older assumption that senior leaders can delegate technical depth entirely to specialists
Inferences:
  • Boards evaluating enterprise-leader or CEO candidates should treat personal AI fluency as a baseline competency to screen for, not an IT-department concern
Traditional succession planning's 'ready now' benchmark should be replaced by a 'ready enough, with support' model. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Compressed leadership pipelines reduce the time and experience available before leaders reach senior responsibility (P3)
  • At many organizations, leaders judged 'ready now' are routinely deemed not-ready the moment a real position actually opens, because someone else fits the real-time need better (N2)
Implicit premises:
  • No amount of assessment rigor can manufacture the years of experience that the compressed pipeline has removed
  • An organization that keeps insisting on full 'ready now' certification will simply fail to fill roles, making 'ready enough plus support' the only workable alternative
Inferences:
  • The real scarcity is in the supply of lived experience, not in the assessment method — so improving how readiness is measured cannot substitute for the missing years
Durable competitive advantage is shifting from static assets toward adaptive capacity. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Static advantages (IP, manufacturing scale) remain real in some domains (Watkins concedes this)
  • The capability frontier of AI is receding rapidly in front of leaders, so fixed capability positions erode quickly (P9, and see P10 on strategic thinking)
  • Leaders are increasingly building options and placing reversible bets rather than committing to fixed long-term plans (P13)
Implicit premises:
  • A rapidly moving capability frontier makes any snapshot advantage temporary, which converts the meta-capacity to keep adapting into the only advantage that survives frontier movement
Inferences:
  • Firms that invest in adaptive capacity should outcompete firms defending a current static advantage, all else equal — an inference Watkins does not draw explicitly but that follows from his premises
Organizations should invest in building 'inner leader' capabilities (polarity tolerance, emotional regulation, real-time self-awareness) much earlier in careers, not only at the point of senior transition. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Inner leader work 'has to happen actually a long time... before you end up at the very top' (P16)
  • Current organizations mostly recognize and build 'outer' leader skills, treating inner-leader capacity as something that develops informally or not at all
Implicit premises:
  • Inner-leader capacities take longer to develop than outer/technical competencies, so starting late leaves insufficient time regardless of program quality
  • The absence of good assessment tools for tensional range and tension tolerance (P19) means organizations currently have no reliable way to target or verify this development
Inferences:
  • Without new assessment instruments, an early inner-leader development push will itself be hard to design or verify — the assessment gap Watkins names (P19) undercuts his own prescription for earlier investment
AI's elimination of entry-level roles threatens organizations' own future supply of senior leadership talent.
Explicit premises:
  • AI is increasingly eliminating entry-level positions (P3)
  • Senior-level talent has historically been developed through an apprenticeship of entry-level work
Implicit premises:
  • There is currently no adequate substitute mechanism replacing the developmental function entry-level work used to serve
Inferences:
  • Organizations aggressively automating entry-level work now may be quietly depleting their own future leadership bench — a cost that would only become visible years later, once the compressed cohort reaches senior roles

Narratives

  • Watkins recounts being with senior HR leaders at an unnamed global food-products company the day before the interview, discussing the governance risks of AI-agent networks making decisions with consequences for customers, and how to measure that risk and assign accountability when something goes wrong.
    → Supports P7/A_integrator-accountability — grounds the abstract claim about AI-driven decision vulnerability in a live, current client conversation, and functions as evidence that this is an active unresolved problem for organizations rather than a hypothetical
  • Watkins cites the 'standing joke' at many organizations he's worked with: leaders are deemed 'ready' for a senior role right up until the position actually opens, at which point someone else is suddenly judged better suited in real time.
    → Supports P4 and the argument for replacing 'ready now' succession planning — shows the traditional readiness standard failing in practice, not just in theory
  • Beard invokes Jeff Bezos's one-way-door/two-way-door distinction, and Watkins affirms it as capturing his point about prioritization under uncertainty.
    → Supports P13 — anchors Watkins's 'developing options, placing selective bets, avoiding irreversible commitments' prioritization mindset in a well-known, concrete decision heuristic
  • Watkins recalls his early career as a professor of negotiation at Harvard's Kennedy School, teaching international diplomacy and studying major diplomatic negotiations, where he observed that diplomats reflexively think coalitionally and build 'coalitions of convenience' around specific issues rather than only with natural allies.
    → Supports P14 — supplies the origin and warrant for reframing the widened stakeholder-management role as 'corporate diplomacy' rather than a generic soft skill

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What differentiates a great functional/team leader from someone ready for an enterprise-wide role?
    Enterprise leadership requires holistic, trade-off-making judgment across the whole organization rather than optimizing for one's own team — a distinction now sharpened by the three forces.
  • ✅ Why has the functional-to-enterprise leadership transition gotten harder in the decade since Watkins first wrote about it?
    Three converging forces: pervasive/agentic AI use, geopolitical turbulence, and compressed leadership pipelines.
  • ✅ How should organizations replace traditional succession planning given compressed pipelines?
    Keep the underlying planning work (defining roles, skills, recruiting) but replace the 'ready now' readiness certification with a 'ready enough, plus support' bet-making model.
  • ⭕ How do you develop leaders who can hold polarities and stay self-aware in real time?
    Watkins proposes curated crucible experiences as the development mechanism but explicitly states no adequate assessment instrument yet exists to target or verify this capability.
  • ⭕ Who is accountable when a network of AI agents makes a decision that harms a customer?
    Watkins raises this as a live, unresolved governance question from a recent client engagement but does not offer or gesture toward an answer.
  • ✅ Should organizations look inside or outside for their next generation of senior/CEO leadership given compressed pipelines?
    A nuanced both/and: boards should always compare inside and outside candidates, and large multi-divisional firms already have a built-in bench because sub-business leaders effectively run standalone large businesses.

Gaps

  • incompleteWatkins names 'ready enough' as the replacement for 'ready now' succession planning but gives no criteria for how 'enough' is determined, who decides, or how organizations avoid this becoming exactly the same unreliable, in-the-moment judgment call he criticizes in the old model (his own 'standing joke' anecdote) — just under a more permissive label.
    Read next: Decision-science literature on calibrated judgment under uncertainty (Annie Duke, Thinking in Bets) and the corpus's own structured-empowerment/curated-options work (extract-structured-empowerment-decisions), to test whether 'ready enough' can be operationalized rather than left to gut feel.
  • incompleteWatkins raises the AI-agent accountability problem (governance risk when autonomous AI decisions harm customers) as a live open question from a client engagement 'yesterday,' but the conversation never returns to it with even a first-pass framework for who should be accountable.
    Read next: extract-outsourced-ai-still-own-the-risk's three-layer AI supply-chain accountability model and extract-hbr-chief-resilience-officer's argument that authority/accountability (not tooling or visibility) is the binding constraint on enterprise resilience both offer partial frameworks Watkins doesn't engage with.
  • uninformedThe claim that AI is 'increasingly eliminating entry-level positions' is asserted without data in this conversation — unlike the planning-cycle-speed claim, where Watkins cites his own survey (~75%). The corpus's own labor-economics coverage treats the entry-level/AI relationship as mechanism-unclear (the aggregate productivity data doesn't yet obviously support the displacement story), so Watkins's confident causal framing may be running ahead of the evidence.
    Read next: extract-loudest-warning-ai-jobs and extract-stopped-arguing-whether-ai-changes-software for the primary-source debate on whether and how AI is actually displacing entry-level hiring.
  • incompleteWatkins says organizations need new assessment instruments for 'tensional range' and 'tension tolerance' but offers no design for what such an instrument would measure or how it would avoid the validity problems he himself implies plague existing leadership assessments — the gap is stated as a need, not pursued as a research direction.
    Read next: Organizational-psychology literature on paradox/polarity management (Wendy Smith, Marianne Lewis) and critiques of 360/psychometric assessment validity.
  • incompleteThe three-forces/seven-transitions framework is presented as applying to 'virtually every leader' and organization, but every illustrative example in the conversation (the food-products company, generic references to 'very large organizations') is a large, AI-adopting, multi-divisional global corporation. There is no engagement with how the framework applies, or fails to apply, to smaller organizations, non-profits, public-sector bodies, or firms in AI-cautious or resource-constrained settings.
