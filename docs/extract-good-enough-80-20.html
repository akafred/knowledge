Why "good enough" is the whole point of 80/20

Ernie Svenson · email · 2026-07-03 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Cognitive SurrenderDecision-Making PracticesTaste & JudgmentTrust
The real value of the 80/20 rule is not the input-output ratio but the permission it grants to stop at 'good enough' — the outcome-relative level a job actually demands — because the last increment of effort usually goes unnoticed, so the true discipline is the confidence to quit polishing rather than the diligence to do more.

practical · Expository/argumentative newsletter essay in a coaching, hortatory register, built around one central anecdote (over-customizing a new tool) and a metaphor (excess fat as opposed to nutrition), addressed to lawyers; closes with a product plug (the Inner Circle). — The load-bearing move is reframing the 80/20 rule from an input-output ratio into a psychological permission-to-stop, and diagnosing perfectionism as fear of criticism rather than high standards. 'Good enough' is explicitly outcome/audience-relative ('the level the job demands', 'what the moment called for'), and Svenson does supply a locator for it — cultivated judgment plus an outside read from a mentor or an AI — so this is not a naive 'just stop'. The unexamined premises are that the demanded level is reliably discernible and that the outsourced read (especially a machine one) is trustworthy.

Key terms

  • good enough
    A deliberate target: the quality level the essential part of a job actually demands, fixed by outcome and audience ('what the moment called for'), past which further effort is unnoticed waste — explicitly NOT laziness or cutting corners.
  • the 80/20 rule (Pareto principle)
    Reframed from an input-output ratio ('do the 20% of the work that gets 80% of the result') into a permission and decision to STOP at good enough — 'one tells you where the results come from, the other tells you when to quit.'
  • the last 20%
    The marginal effort past good-enough that 'usually produces something no one will notice' — over-polish, decoration, 'excess fat' that chews up valuable time.
  • perfectionism
    Not high standards but usually fear of criticism in disguise — a confidence problem that insidiously overrides 80/20 and wastes enormous time.
  • confidence
    The cultivated capacity to tell load-bearing parts from decoration and cut with conviction — buildable by experience or borrowable from a mentor or an AI; the real bottleneck to stopping at good enough.

Arguments

You should stop at 'good enough' rather than chase the last 20%, because the marginal effort is usually wasted. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The last 20% of effort usually produces something no one will notice.
  • That extra work does not affect a client matter or win a case; it just chews up valuable time.
  • The point of 80/20 is to decide the level of good-enough the job demands.
Implicit premises:
  • The value of effort is measured by its effect on the outcome (client matter, case, the moment's demand) — a consequentialist, audience-relative standard of quality.
  • Returns to effort actually diminish for the task at hand — the value curve saturates before the extra 20% is spent.
  • You can tell, in advance and reliably, which parts are load-bearing and which are decoration.
Inferences:
  • The argument holds wherever the demand bar is discoverable and value saturates (throughput/commodity work, the tool-config example, routine deliverables).
  • It fails wherever the differentiating value is the un-specifiable last increment (craft, design, prose, brand, high-consequence legal work), because there the 'returns diminish' premise is false and the audience's real standard may be near-perfection.
Over-polishing is usually a confidence problem, so the cure is to build (or borrow) the confidence to stop, not to raise your standards.
Explicit premises:
  • People often over-polish out of fear of being criticized, not because the work needs more.
  • Knowing what to leave out takes confidence.
  • Confidence can be built by experience or borrowed via a mentor or an AI sounding board.
Implicit premises:
  • The outside reader (mentor or AI) has trustworthy taste about where 'good enough' really is.
  • Borrowing someone else's judgment about when you are done builds, rather than substitutes for, your own discernment.
Inferences:
  • If the borrowed reader's taste is itself unverifiable or biased — as it is for an LLM — the cure imports a new, hidden risk; and habitually letting an outside read decide 'when it's done' is exactly the dependency the corpus calls cognitive surrender.

Narratives

  • Getting a shiny new tool and spending hours customizing every setting and color-coding folders to build the 'perfect' system — before using it even once — which affects no client matter and wins no case, just chewing up valuable time; framed as 'excess fat in a meal that's supposed to deliver just nutrition.'
    → The central illustration of the last-20%-as-unnoticed-waste claim, and the clearest case for the outcome-relative standard ('None of it wins a case') (supports p2, p9).
  • The experienced lawyer learning 'which details a judge will actually care about', where the novice keeps everything 'just in case' because they cannot yet tell load-bearing parts from decoration.
    → Illustrates discernment/confidence as a product of experience — the basis for cutting with conviction (supports p6).
  • Handing a draft to an AI at 11 p.m. and asking it to critique your thinking, poke holes in your argument, or tell you where you're adding stuff that doesn't earn its keep — 'a sounding board available... when no one else is', though it 'won't replace a good mentor.'
    → Illustrates borrowing an outside read to locate 'good enough' (supports p7); also the hook for the taste/cognitive-surrender tension — the piece recommends outsourcing precisely the taste judgment the corpus finds AI cannot be trusted with (g1).

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why do people who love the 80/20 rule fail to actually apply it?
    Because perfectionism — often fear of criticism in disguise — insidiously overrides it, so they keep polishing past the point of value.
  • ✅ Isn't 'good enough' just an excuse for laziness or cutting corners?
    No — it is a deliberate, thoughtful, strategic target; skipping the unnoticed last 20% is discipline, not sloth.
  • ✅ How do you know where 'good enough' is, and what to leave out?
    Through experience-built confidence to distinguish load-bearing parts from decoration, accelerated by an outside read from a mentor or an AI.
  • ⭕ When is stopping at good enough the wrong call — when is the last, un-specifiable 20% actually the whole point?
    Not addressed; Svenson asserts the last 20% is generally unnoticed and never marks the taste/craft/high-stakes domains where that increment is precisely the value.
  • ⭕ What are the risks of outsourcing the 'is it done' judgment to an AI?
    Not addressed; he recommends it as a useful sounding board without confronting AI taste's unverifiability, its training-distribution bias, or the dependency it can create.

Gaps

  • incompleteSvenson prescribes outsourcing the 'is it done / what doesn't earn its keep' judgment to a mentor or an AI ('the fastest way to build confidence is to borrow someone else's'), but never addresses that an AI's 'taste' is unverifiable and re-encodes its training-distribution bias (the taste cluster's central empirical finding), nor the cognitive-surrender/dependency risk of borrowed confidence — the exact move the corpus flags as the point at which your own judgment stops forming and the outside read becomes your read. The essay recommends the very outsourcing its neighbours warn against.
    Read next: Read extract-you-cant-unit-test-for-taste (AI 'taste' is untrustworthy and launders anglophone-Wikipedia bias; the doubted Reykjavik=10 score) and the corpus's cognitive-surrender material on 'borrowed confidence'; ask when an outside read cultivates your own discernment versus when it substitutes for it and creates dependence on a non-stationary, biased arbiter.
  • incompleteThe claim that 'the last 20% usually produces something no one will notice' is stated as a near-universal and paired with an outcome-only standard ('wins a case'), but the piece never marks the domains where the last, un-specifiable increment IS the value — craft, design, prose, brand, and high-consequence work (a safety-critical filing, an appellate argument) where refusing to stop is warranted rigor, not fear. Without a boundary, the advice over-generalizes from routine deliverables to work where 'good enough' forfeits the whole point.
    Read next: Read the taste cluster — extract-you-cant-unit-test-for-taste (correctness is not quality) and extract-how-to-write-with-style (Vonnegut's 'have the guts to cut' aims at what illuminates the subject, a taste target, not at good-enough) — plus Ousterhout on design quality; test whether 'good enough' is ever the wrong stopping rule and specify the tasks where it is.
  • incomplete'Good enough for whom' is left under-specified. The standard is audience-relative ('the level the job demands', 'which details a judge will actually care about'), yet Svenson treats the demanding audience as singular and self-evident. He does not address conflicting audiences, or the case where the relevant reader's genuine standard is near-perfection (a regulator, an appellate court, a brand, an adversary looking for the attackable flaw) — which would collapse his effort-versus-value gap and vindicate the 'perfectionist' he diagnoses as merely fearful.
    Read next: Read on satisficing versus maximizing (Herbert Simon) and stakeholder / quality-attribute analysis: who sets the adequacy bar, and what the stopping rule should be when the audience is adversarial or the cost of a missed detail is asymmetric.
