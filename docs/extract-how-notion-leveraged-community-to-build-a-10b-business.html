How Notion leveraged community to build a $10B business | Camille Ricketts

Lenny Rachitsky, Camille Ricketts · podcast · 2026-04-16 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Product Discovery & Experimentation
Companies with freemium, creative products can achieve enterprise-scale growth by investing early in organic community — ambassadors, influencers, champions, and content — rather than premature monetization or paid acquisition, because community creates the ubiquity and legitimacy that de-risks the product for larger buyers.

practical · mixed — Conversational interview blending practitioner narrative (Ricketts's career arc and Notion's community-building story) with argumentative and expository segments where both host and guest advance claims about when and how community-led growth and content marketing work. Key terms

  • community-led growth
    When a product's community creates such ubiquity and name recognition that it de-risks the product for enterprise buyers and enables upmarket expansion — distinct from mere 'having a community'
  • atomic unit of sharing
    A discrete artifact created inside the product that users are intrinsically motivated to share publicly because it expresses something about themselves (e.g., Notion templates, Figma designs, Canva creations)
  • discovery Product Discovery & Experimentation
    A step beyond awareness — when a person has sufficient intent to actively seek out and learn about a product, not merely having heard of it
  • content-market fit Product Discovery & Experimentation
    The content analog of product-market fit: understanding who your audience is, what causes them anxiety or pain, and creating content that serves as a painkiller for those needs — not just producing material adjacent to your product
  • ambassadors
    Non-transactional community members who are genuinely passionate about the product, voluntarily vocal, and supported (but not paid) by the company with early feature access, events, and promotion of their work
  • champions
    The most avid users of Notion inside customer companies, convened in a dedicated Slack for deeper connection with the product team and customer success — distinct from ambassadors who are individual/consumer-oriented

Arguments

Community-led growth is the optimal strategy for freemium, creative products to achieve enterprise scale. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Community creates ubiquity and name recognition
  • Ubiquity de-risks the product choice for enterprise buyers
  • Products with an atomic unit of sharing naturally generate community enthusiasm
  • Notion, Figma, and Canva all followed this pattern successfully
Implicit premises:
  • Enterprise buyers are risk-averse and rely on social proof / prior awareness when evaluating tools
  • Paid acquisition alone cannot create the depth of trust that organic community provides
  • What worked for Notion, Figma, and Canva generalizes to the category of freemium creative tools
Inferences:
  • If your product is freemium and has a shareable creative artifact, community investment will yield enterprise revenue over time
You should not impose ROI metrics on community efforts early on. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Notion did not measure community concretely until ~2022, years after starting
  • They could see organic scale tied to community efforts in geographic expansion and survey data
  • Premature ROI demands can 'cut at the knees' organic community momentum
Implicit premises:
  • Community value accrues non-linearly and is difficult to attribute in early stages
  • The company has enough runway or alternative revenue to tolerate unmeasured community spend
Inferences:
  • Trust the qualitative signals early; formalize measurement only after organic traction is evident
Content must be treated like a product, with content-market fit as the governing framework. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • You must identify your audience's deep needs (promotion, avoiding failure, reducing anxiety)
  • Content should be a painkiller, not a vitamin
  • First Round Review succeeded by giving operators a platform to share failures and practical wisdom
  • Quality requires 8-10 hours of writing per piece; performance correlates with time invested
Implicit premises:
  • Most content marketing fails because it is produced without audience-need analysis
  • The painkiller/vitamin framework from product strategy applies directly to content
Inferences:
  • Invest heavily in fewer, higher-quality pieces targeted at acute audience pain points rather than producing volume
Traditional press still matters and should not be replaced by owned media and influencer channels. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Press provides breadth of reach plus a credibility stamp
  • David Pierce's Wall Street Journal article was Notion's demonstrable 'big break'
  • Companies in the First Round portfolio that got strong press coverage saw measurable differences in discovery, candidate pipeline, and investor interest
Implicit premises:
  • The credibility transfer from established media to a startup is difficult to replicate through owned channels
  • A single well-placed article can have outsized impact relative to its cost
Inferences:
  • Maintain press relationships as a complement to community and content, not as a legacy channel to be abandoned

Narratives

  • Early Notion office culture — apartment-like loft, no shoes, tea on couches, broken HVAC, headlamps instead of overhead lighting, late-night launch preparations
    → Establishes emotional authenticity and the 'familial' quality Ricketts attributes to Notion's early team; sets up the theme that close-knit human bonds drive both internal culture and external community
  • January 3, 2021 outage — massive service disruption on first day back from holidays; Twitter users both complaining and sending supportive messages ('give them a break', 'sending hugs')
    → Demonstrates that Notion's community had become emotionally invested in the company's success — evidence that community-building created genuine loyalty, not just awareness; also illustrates that product resilience is higher than founders fear
  • Creator who made $35,000 in four months selling one Notion template, and consultants who now employ dozens of people
    → Concrete evidence that aligning community members' economic incentives with company growth creates a self-reinforcing ecosystem; shows the scale of the template economy
  • Sitting to Elon Musk's right at Tesla, driving journalists around in the Roadster
    → Credibility-building narrative; also introduces the lesson about being 'incredibly on point with information' and Musk's ability to 'paint an emotional picture of the vision'
  • David Pierce's Wall Street Journal article ('this is the one work life productivity app you'll ever need') as Notion's 'big break' — visible inflection in growth graphs
    → Primary evidence for the argument that traditional press still delivers outsized impact; the single most concrete growth-attribution claim in the entire conversation
  • First Round Review's early days — leveraging partner connections to land big-name interview subjects, which created a flywheel for subsequent subjects
    → Tactical illustration of the cold-start problem in content marketing and how to solve it through network leverage
  • Ambassador induction rituals — 20 people at a time, monthly; new members introducing themselves ('I'm from Venezuela, here's how Notion changed my life')
    → Illustrates the deliberate-slow-growth principle and the emotional quality that small-cohort induction preserves
  • Stripe Atlas as a community play — building a community of founders around startup incorporation/advice rather than around the core payments product
    → Example of community-building that is adjacent to rather than centered on the core product; broadens the applicability of community-led growth beyond product-centric communities
  • Lenny Rachitsky's own origin — guest post in First Round Review drove his first ~500 newsletter subscribers
    → First-person testimony to the discovery power of high-quality content platforms; validates Ricketts's claims about content's impact from the other side

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What is community-led growth and how does it actually work as a growth mechanism?
    Community creates ubiquity and name recognition through ambassadors, influencers, champions, events, and template ecosystems. This ubiquity de-risks the product for enterprise buyers, enabling upmarket expansion from a consumer/prosumer base.
  • ✅ When should a company invest in community-led growth, and when should it not?
    Invest when: freemium product, atomic unit of sharing, organic enthusiasm already visible. Don't prioritize when: long sales cycles, high price points, sales-led culture. A 2×2 matrix (PMF status × enterprise vs consumer) maps to different community forms.
  • ⭕ How do you measure the impact of community efforts?
    Net new website visitors is offered as the leading indicator; influencer campaigns were tightly measurable. But Ricketts explicitly advises against early measurement and acknowledges they didn't measure concretely until years in. The attribution problem is acknowledged but not solved.
  • ✅ How should you design and run an ambassador program?
    Start with ~20 of the most organically vocal users; induct small cohorts (20/month); incentivize with early feature access, AMA sessions with leadership, event subsidies, and promotion of their work; listen to what members want rather than imposing structure; keep the community on a platform they already use (Slack).
  • ✅ What makes content marketing effective?
    Pursue content-market fit: identify audience pain points, create painkiller content, invest 8-10 hours per piece for quality, leverage network connections to land early big-name subjects for credibility flywheel.
  • ✅ Is traditional press/PR still relevant in the age of owned media and direct-to-audience channels?
    Yes — press provides breadth and credibility stamp that owned channels cannot replicate. David Pierce's WSJ article was Notion's demonstrable inflection point. Influencer and owned media are complements, not replacements.
  • ⭕ How much should a founder personally invest in social media presence?
    Ricketts advises following personality and authenticity rather than setting quotas; gives no framework beyond 'lean into what feels natural'. The question is acknowledged but answered with a generality.

Gaps

  • incompleteSurvivorship bias in the case studies: Notion, Figma, Canva, and Stripe are all wildly successful companies. No failed community-led growth attempts are discussed. The conversation does not address companies that invested heavily in community and did not see returns, making it impossible to assess the base rate of success for this strategy.
    Read next: Seek case studies of failed community-led growth efforts to understand failure modes and necessary conditions beyond 'freemium + atomic unit of sharing'.
  • incompleteThe measurement problem is acknowledged but left unresolved. Ricketts advises against early measurement, and the primary metric offered (net new website visitors) has obvious attribution challenges. The advice amounts to 'trust that it's working based on qualitative signals,' which is reasonable for a $10B company with founder buy-in but unhelpful for resource-constrained startups.
    Read next: Look for frameworks that quantify community ROI without premature optimization — e.g., lagging indicators, cohort analysis of community-exposed vs unexposed users.
  • uninformedThe cost side of community-led growth is never discussed. Three full-time community team members, subsidized events (30/month globally), influencer budgets — the total investment is never quantified. Without cost data, the implicit 'just invest in community' advice cannot be evaluated by companies with different resource levels.
    Read next: Seek data on the actual cost structure of running community programs at scale — headcount, event budgets, influencer spend, opportunity cost.
  • incompleteThe 2×2 matrix (PMF × enterprise/consumer) is teased but only two of four quadrants are discussed in any detail (post-PMF consumer = ambassadors/influencers; post-PMF enterprise = champions/consultants). Pre-PMF consumer and the full pre-PMF enterprise quadrant get only cursory treatment (customer advisory boards mentioned briefly).
    Read next: Ricketts mentions she will share the full matrix; seek that artifact or construct the remaining quadrants from other sources.
  • illogicalThere is a tension between 'don't impose metrics early' and 'our KPI was net new website visitors.' If net new visitors was the team's responsibility metric, that IS an early metric. The advice to avoid measurement is somewhat contradicted by the actual practice described.
    Read next: Clarify the timeline: when exactly did Notion start measuring community impact formally, and what qualitative proxies did they use before that?
  • incompleteThe conversation treats community, content, and press as three organic growth levers but does not discuss how they interact or cannibalize each other. Are they additive? Multiplicative? Does over-investment in one reduce returns on others?
    Read next: Look for integrated frameworks that model the interaction between community, content, and earned media as a system rather than independent channels.
