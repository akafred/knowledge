Key terms

  • evals Evals
    Statistical tests applied to agentic application outputs to measure accuracy, fairness, and domain-specific correctness — analogous to but 10x more important than traditional software tests
  • Tiger Team
    A cross-functional team assembled specifically for an AI project, pulling people from engineering, data science, and product roles outside the normal org chart
  • agentic applications Agentic Loop
    Software where an LLM runs in a loop, calling tools and maintaining memory, deployed as a production interface (e.g., a client within a SaaS application)
  • vibe coding Vibe Coding
    Using AI coding assistants (Claude Code, Cursor) for a co-creative development experience — distinct from building agentic applications
  • memory Agent Memory
    A structured compression of a queue of messages — working memory, semantic memory, observational memory
  • non-determinism Non-Deterministic Software
    The property of agentic applications where multiple successful runs can produce different response bodies, unlike traditional software

Arguments

Organizations should invest primarily in domain-specific evals rather than generic benchmarks. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Model providers already train and eval against public benchmarks and datasets.
  • The things important for your application are unique to your organization's area of core competence.
  • The data your organization has is not publicly present in model training data.
Implicit premises:
  • Generic evals provide diminishing returns because model providers are already optimizing for them.
  • The gap between model capability and production readiness is filled by domain-specific quality assurance.
Inferences:
  • Therefore the highest marginal value of eval effort is in proprietary, domain-specific test suites.
Cross-functional Tiger Teams are the right organizational structure for shipping agentic applications. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Data scientists are comfortable with statistical uncertainty but not production software.
  • Software engineers build production systems but aren't trained in statistical thinking.
  • Agentic applications require both skillsets simultaneously.
Implicit premises:
  • Traditional org structures separate these disciplines into different teams or departments.
  • A single person rarely possesses both deep software engineering and data science skills.
  • The speed of AI adoption (3-4x faster than previous waves) means organizations cannot wait for organic skill diffusion.
Inferences:
  • Therefore a cross-functional team structure that pulls from both disciplines is necessary.
Experienced engineers should overcome default skepticism and embrace AI engineering. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • As engineers age, they shift from default enthusiasm to default skepticism.
  • Being good in a new field requires being okay with discomfort and being bad at something initially.
  • There is significant career opportunity in being an early adopter.
Implicit premises:
  • AI engineering is not a passing fad — it is comparable to DevOps and data engineering in staying power.
  • The cost of missing this wave is higher than the cost of temporary discomfort.
Inferences:
  • Therefore the rational strategy is to lean into the discomfort of learning AI engineering.

Narratives

  • Gatsby's evolution from a React framework built to scratch an itch by two friends to a popular open source project requiring community management and commercial pragmatism.
    → Establishes credibility and provides a concrete example of the open source community lifecycle pattern.
  • An HR SaaS platform observed users exporting CSVs and pasting them into ChatGPT, leading the company to build an in-app agent that could answer HR questions using organizational data.
    → Concrete example of the modal use case for agentic applications, illustrating both the privacy problem and the domain knowledge advantage.
  • Mastra's 'Kindergarten' Slack channel where the team shares links and pairs frequently to diffuse AI tool expertise.
    → Illustrates the humility and collaborative culture needed for AI-augmented engineering teams.
  • A CTO of a 150-200 person engineering org personally writing code and leading the agentic application project.
    → Illustrates that AI projects are high-value enough to warrant senior leadership directly contributing, and that cross-functional leadership is critical.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How should organizations structure teams to ship agentic AI applications?
    Use cross-functional Tiger Teams that combine software engineering and data science skills, outside normal org structure.
  • ✅ What makes evals effective for agentic applications?
    Evals built on organization-specific data and domain expertise, created with subject matter experts, measuring accuracy against comprehensive domain-specific question sets.
  • ✅ How should agentic applications be deployed to production?
    Staged rollout using feature flags: beta testers, then 1%, 5%, 10%, 50%, over weeks not days.
  • ✅ How fast is AI engineering being adopted compared to previous technology waves?
    Roughly 3-4x faster than DevOps and data engineering adoption.
  • ⭕ What specific accuracy thresholds should organizations target for agentic applications?
    Acknowledged as context-dependent ('sometimes higher, sometimes lower') but no framework for determining thresholds is offered.
  • ⭕ How do you maintain quality and reliability of AI coding agents used in the development process itself?
    Not addressed — the discussion of evals focuses on product agents, not development workflow agents.
