13) Tobias K. Torrissen om kodekvalitet, konferanser og AI

Truls Jørgensen og Audun Fauchald Strand — Heis Podcast · podcast · 2026-03-16 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Agent HarnessAI as AmplifierArchitecture Decision RecordsCode QualityCraft CommunityPublic-Sector DigitalizationSoftware Design FundamentalsTechnology Leadership (Heis)Vibe Coding
In the age of LLM-generated code, the durable value of an engineering leader is not in writing code but in cultivating the engineering practices, explicit design discipline, and craft community that determine whether a long-lived system can be understood, maintained, and adapted — and code quality, understood as 'obvious over clever,' matters more in this era, not less.

practical · mixed — A conversation that mixes narrative (JavaZone origins, NAV quality reviews, son who vibecodes apps), expository reflection (his three-camp taxonomy of AI coders, his definition of architecture's purpose), and prescriptive practical argument (juniors should learn fundamentals before AI tools; teams should double down on engineering practices in the AI era; KS processes should leave more room for product thinking). The overall thrust is action-guiding: how should an engineering leader, junior, or team behave now that code is cheap to generate.

Key terms

  • kodekvalitet (code quality) Code Quality
    Not classical metrics (cyclomatic complexity, coupling, method length) but: obviousness over cleverness, readability, deletability, fitness-for-purpose for the system's actual mode (long-lived regulated software vs. throwaway). Quality is defined by whether the team — '70 forvaltere' of varying ambition and life-balance — can collectively own and evolve the code over 30 years.
  • obvious, ikke clever Software Design Fundamentals
    A design heuristic: prefer code that is immediately understandable to a new reader over code that is compact, elegant, or sophisticated. Few abstractions, explicit over implicit.
  • tilført vs. essensiell kompleksitet Software Design Fundamentals
    Borrowed Brooks/Moseley-Marks framing: essential complexity is inherent to the domain (you can move it but not destroy it); accidental/added complexity is what design discipline can remove. The job is minimising added complexity so only essential remains.
  • engineering-praksiser
    A bundle: fast feedback loops, observability, frequent delivery, learning-oriented work methodology, TDD, architecture decision records, pair/team design discussion. Practices that produce 'bevissthet rundt hvordan du utvikler software'.
  • harnesse (en språkmodell) Agent Harness
    To wrap and constrain a language model so it produces code of quality matching the team's standards — explicit design context, ADRs, practices, observable feedback. Camp 2 in the taxonomy.
  • vibecoding Vibe Coding
    Used loosely. He distinguishes: (a) his son spying out apps from home for ad revenue — kortsiktig, kjapt, deretter — which he calls his personal meaning; (b) the more serious 'on-to-pi-tenakt' parallel-agentic large-scale development that doesn't engage with code as an abstraction. He hints these are conflated in discourse.
  • fagmiljø / community Craft Community
    A self-organising volunteer-driven craft community (JavaBin, JavaZone, Booster, Kodemaker fagdag) that produces formative encounters and intergenerational transmission of practice. He attributes his career to such encounters.
  • Architecture Decision Records (ADR) Architecture Decision Records
    Less important for sporbarhet than for facilitating a fornuftig prosess where team perspectives are aired. The act of producing them is the value, not the artifact.
  • KS1 / KS2 Public-Sector Digitalization
    Norwegian state-investment quality-assurance gates (concept choice; project pre-realisation plan). He criticises them for over-quantifying targets early and locking in goals that learning will outdate, but works inside them to nudge toward smidig/produktutvikling.
  • voksenopplæring
    What his code-quality reviews actually deliver to client leadership instead of the requested traffic-light verdict: adult education in what their developers know and what tradeoffs they face.
  • goldplating Software Design Fundamentals
    The product-development failure mode where teams over-invest in first-iteration polish because tight user feedback creates over-identification with the user's wants. He has a høne to plukke with product-utviklingsmiljø about this.
  • obvious-rapport / tilrådninger (ikke terningskast)
    His review output: a map of choices made and why, plus directional recommendations (more/less distributed, focus more on technical debt) — explicitly not a score.

Arguments

Engineering practices matter more under AI, not less. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Teams with good practices get a steep productivity step-up from AI (TET/Claude observation).
  • Teams without good practices amplify existing chaos with AI.
  • AI forces tacit design knowledge to become explicit and written.
  • Long-lived systems still need maintainable code over decades.
Implicit premises:
  • Productivity gains under AI distribute unequally across teams; this distribution is determined by pre-existing practice quality.
  • Tacit design intuition does not transfer to LLMs; explicit articulation does.
  • The composition of a 30-year-old codebase is determined more by its early discipline than by its tooling at any moment.
  • An engineering leader's job is to set the discipline floor that determines whether AI is leverage or amplification of dysfunction.
Inferences:
  • Investing in engineering practices is a higher-leverage decision in 2026 than in 2020, even though it looks the same on paper.
  • Organisations that adopted AI coding without underlying practice discipline are sitting on technical debt that will be expensive to reverse.
Microservices should be 'an architecture of last resort'; default to monolithic in-process designs. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Distribution adds complexity that often outweighs the local-simplicity gains.
  • Empirical studies suggest all-in-one-class is best up to ~500 lines; only then do you need additional structure.
  • Vlad Khononov / Balancing Coupling shows local-vs-global complexity tradeoff is the right lens.
  • Teams adopt microservices for fashion rather than for goals that justify the cost.
Implicit premises:
  • Global complexity is harder to perceive than local complexity; therefore biases push teams toward microservices.
  • Most domains do not change fast enough to need distributed flexibility (forvaltningsloven changes rarely).
  • Architecture choices should be derivable from domain volatility, not from team-size or hiring fashion.
Inferences:
  • If you cannot articulate the global-complexity cost of your microservice topology, you have not yet earned it.
  • Many existing microservice systems would deliver better value as well-modularised monoliths.
Code-quality reviews should not produce verdicts but facilitate adult education for leadership. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Developers already know the problems; the review surfaces them officially.
  • Traffic-light verdicts are over-simplified for the real tradeoffs.
  • Bad choices are usually due to prioritisation pressure, not incompetence or unwillingness.
  • Leadership lacks the technical context to interpret detailed findings without translation.
Implicit premises:
  • The bottleneck on improvement is leadership understanding, not developer knowledge.
  • External authority can grant developers permission to surface concerns they already had.
  • Translating between technical and leadership levels is a distinct skill worth charging consulting rates for.
Inferences:
  • An organization that pays for a code-quality review and gets a verdict has been short-changed.
  • If your consultants will not be tabloid with leadership, you are not getting value from them.
Junior developers should learn fundamentals before leaning on AI tooling. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • TDD, software design principles, and architecture are still load-bearing 20 years on.
  • Driving a 'Formula 1' (language model) without a Toyota Corolla (fundamentals) is risky.
  • AI tools demand explicit articulation of design — which juniors cannot do without the fundamentals.
Implicit premises:
  • Without internalised principles, a junior cannot recognise when AI output is wrong.
  • The ability to drive a language model well is bounded above by your own design vocabulary.
  • Communities like JavaZone, Booster, Kodemaker fagdag are still the cheapest way to acquire the vocabulary.
Inferences:
  • Organisations that hire juniors and route them through AI-first workflows are creating a generation that cannot diagnose bad output.
  • The seniors who can review AI work will be in higher demand, not lower.
Software jobs will not disappear despite AI generation. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Only ~20% of system development is programming.
  • Cheaper software grows the market (long-tail bespoke).
  • Maintenance share of total cost grows as creation cost falls.
  • High-stakes systems still require human accountability.
Implicit premises:
  • The 80% of non-programming work is harder to automate than programming itself.
  • Demand for software is highly elastic with respect to price.
  • Accountability cannot be transferred to a model.
Inferences:
  • The professional category may bifurcate: low-stakes vibecoders for throwaway apps; engineering-discipline professionals for regulated long-lived systems.
  • Engineering leadership becomes more, not less, distinctive as a role.
JavaZone's deep success is not its peak attendance but its generational continuity. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Many founder-driven conferences die when founders move on.
  • JavaZone is now on roughly the sixth generation of program committees.
  • The hardest organisational act was getting someone else to take over.
Implicit premises:
  • Community institutions require explicit succession mechanisms.
  • Founders should plan for their own removal as part of building.
  • Conference longevity is a proxy for the health of a craft community.
Inferences:
  • Other Norwegian fagmiljø institutions (JavaBin, Dataforeningen, Booster) should be evaluated on succession not on current attendance.

Narratives

  • Roots Bergen (Recent Object Oriented Trends conference, early 2000s): Torrissen as a young developer has lunch with Alistair Cockburn and Martin Fowler, decides this is what he wants to do, joins the early JavaBin programkomite — eventually becoming program committee lead for JavaZone's first 5-6 years.
    → Implicitly supports the proposition that single conference encounters can be career-defining and the broader claim that fagmiljø is formative substrate.
  • The VAT war story: JavaBin volunteer organisers, as 'pur unge utviklere', personally on the hook when Skatteetaten claimed VAT was due on conference tickets; Sun Microsystems bailed them out.
    → Texture for the amateur-volunteer origin of Norwegian Java community; supports the implicit point that institutions get built by people who don't know they're not allowed to do this.
  • First NAV Sykepenger workshop: developers were surprised the reviewers didn't show up in suits with tape recorders. Torrissen reframes review as 'å sitte rundt et bord og snakke om faget vårt'.
    → Implicitly supports the proposition that the review's value is process, not verdict.
  • His son, freshly out of military service, vibecoding apps from home one per day, hoping ad revenue will arrive; 'arveløs så mange ganger at det funker ikke mer'.
    → Personifies camp 3 of the AI-coding taxonomy and the throwaway-app long tail; defines 'vibecoding' by example.
  • Sykepenger architecture: from coordinated to choreographed event-driven design where each step does not know where it sits in the flow — but forvaltningsloven is stable, so the flexibility may not be needed; loose coupling justified instead by drift/resilience.
    → Concrete example of architecture chosen for legitimate but secondary reasons rather than for fashion; supports the claim that reflection on domain volatility should drive design.
  • December obsession with Advent of Code at Novit/Kold (Kotlin), spending hours not on solving but on making the solution beautiful.
    → Personal acknowledgement that aesthetic craft attachment is real, even as he argues it matters less now — gives the concession emotional weight.
  • Talk at Kode24-dagen the day before recording: a TDD talk that ran half an hour past, on a practice from 2004 still being explained in 2026.
    → Supports the claim that fundamentals are durable across two decades.
  • TET/Telenor's Claude adoption talk: teams with mature engineering practices saw productivity step-ups; teams without saw it fall.
    → Empirical anchor for the engineering-practices-matter-more argument.
  • KOS (millions of lines, pre-distribution): unmaintainable monolith at Norge Tilsynsmyndigheter / Nord. Audun and Torrissen both worked there.
    → Counter-narrative to Torrissen's monolith preference; supports Audun's social-scaling argument for microservices.
  • Frisørkjede that today uses a crappy generic booking system, but in a world of cheap bespoke software would commission a perfect one.
    → Hypothetical illustration of demand-elasticity / long-tail bespoke argument.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What does 'good code quality' mean in 2026?
    Obvious over clever; readable and maintainable by a varied team over decades; fit for the system's mode rather than measured by static metrics.
  • ✅ How should engineering practice change in the AI era?
    Double down on engineering discipline — fast feedback, observability, frequent delivery, explicit design, ADRs. AI raises the marginal value of these, doesn't replace them.
  • ✅ Will developers be unemployed?
    No: programming is ~20% of the work; the market for bespoke software expands; high-stakes work still requires human accountability.
  • ⭕ When are microservices justified?
    Disagreement: Torrissen says rarely (last resort); Audun says regularly for social scaling because in-process boundaries don't hold. The conversation names but does not resolve this.
  • ✅ What should juniors do?
    Learn fundamentals (architecture, TDD, core principles) before relying on LLMs; engage with fagmiljø.
  • ✅ What is the value of an external code-quality review?
    Not a verdict but a facilitated conversation that re-surfaces what developers already know, and translates it for leadership.
  • ⭕ How should the Norwegian KS process serve software better?
    Critique articulated (over-quantification, lock-in to obsolete goals) but reform path is unspecified beyond 'focus more on how to work than on what to achieve'.
  • ⭕ What survives of aesthetic craft when machines write the code?
    Personal mourning acknowledged; 'beautiful code' likely fades as a professional value but the conversation does not work out what should replace aesthetic discipline as a craft anchor.
  • ⭕ When the code-quality review is itself agentic, what happens?
    Joked about. Not engaged.
