9) Nils Brede Moe om AI-paradokset, autonomi, læringskultur og fremtidens team

Truls Jørgensen og Audun Fauchald Strand — Heis Podcast · podcast · 2026-01-19 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

The promised productivity gains from AI in software development will not materialize at the organizational level unless teams deliberately slow down — investing in psychological safety, structured reflection, and joint experimentation — because software development is fundamentally a learning process and AI threatens the learning even as it accelerates the output.

practical · mixed — A research-grounded conversation. Brede Moe presents empirical findings from 27 years of SINTEF research on agile teams, autonomy, distributed work, and AI, but in service of prescriptive guidance to engineering leaders: how to build teams that learn under AI assistance. The hosts (Truls Jørgensen, Audun Fauchald Strand) prompt with practitioner questions; Brede Moe answers with research-shaped principles plus caveats. Argumentative in places (the AI paradox), narrative in places (the bilfabrikk study, the NAV Slack origin), expository on team mechanics.

Key terms

  • AI-paradokset Code Was Never the Bottleneck
    The contradiction between individual perceived productivity gains from AI tools (9 of 10 feel more productive) and the organizational failure of AI initiatives (≈95% show no measurable bottom-line return, citing MIT). The deeper paradox: AI promises speed, but to capture the value at the organizational level, teams must slow down — invest in reflection, experimentation, joint practice. 'Du må gå saktere før du kan få til å løpe.' Months of investment, not weeks.
  • autonomi Team Autonomy
    A team having (a) mandate/authority (myndighet), (b) competence to make good decisions, and (c) the right people present to make them — balanced against the principle that fewer team members is always better. Autonomy in his usage is constitutive of effective teams; it is not the same as individual freedom or org-design decentralization. It implies self-management, joint decision-making, and reflective practice.
  • læringskultur Learning Culture & Slack
    An operational team practice — not a vague value — comprising: scheduled reflection time (e.g. every Friday), explicit show-and-tell sessions, workshops, dedicated Slack channels for sharing, joint discussion of tool use, and time set aside for experimentation. Distinct from individual learning. Requires psychological safety as substrate and slack (organizational room) as resource.
  • psykologisk trygghet Psychological Safety
    The group-level condition where every member feels safe to admit mistakes, ask for feedback, and challenge how the work is done. Critically, it is collective — 'hvis ikke alle føler det, så er du ikke psykologisk trygger til stede.' Single dissonant members can poison it ('giftdråper'). Cites Google's Project Aristotle finding that this was the single factor distinguishing high-performing teams.
  • enkelt- og dobbelkrekklæring (single- and double-loop learning) Learning Culture & Slack
    Single-loop learning = doing the same things better (incremental improvement). Double-loop learning = questioning whether you should be doing those things at all — examining assumptions, work processes, who you work with, whether you have the right problem. The latter is what distinguishes teams that actually learn; it requires psychological safety. (Argyris/Schön provenance, though Brede Moe does not name them.)
  • augmented teams Human as Orchestrator of Agents
    His emerging research frame: teams that work with a digital assistant or AI as a team member, not just as individual tooling. The interesting question is whether AI can let teams be smaller while preserving autonomy, and how juniors are integrated when a data scientist plus an AI agent could replace headcount. Open question, not yet a finding.
  • slakk Learning Culture & Slack
    Organizational room — time and capacity not consumed by delivery pressure — that must exist for learning, reflection, and experimentation to happen. Without slack the ildsjel (champion) burns out. Borrowed from systems thinking / DeMarco.
  • ildsjel Learning Culture & Slack
    An individual within a team who burns for improvement, experimentation, or new tool adoption. Indispensable but insufficient — ildsjeler burn out if the broader culture and structure do not support them.
  • augmentere / augmentet team Human as Orchestrator of Agents
    Same as 'augmented teams' above — a team whose work is shaped by collaboration with AI agents as quasi-members, not merely as individual tools each developer uses privately.

Arguments

AI adoption requires teams to deliberately slow down before they can speed up. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Software development is fundamentally a learning process (Brandolini).
  • AI tools make it easy to skip the colleague conversation and ship code one does not understand.
  • Individual perceived productivity does not translate to organizational outcomes (MIT 95% finding).
  • Teams that schedule reflection and share AI practice outperform those that don't.
  • Building the necessary infrastructure (tests, monitoring, delivery pipelines) and new specification practices takes months.
Implicit premises:
  • Organizational outcomes depend on team-level practice, not individual virtuosity.
  • Learning is the binding constraint on software quality.
  • Tool adoption is an organizational change problem, not a procurement decision.
Inferences:
  • Engineering leaders who measure AI adoption by individual productivity will mis-diagnose what is actually happening.
  • Organizations without continuous delivery infrastructure should expect AI to make their failure modes worse before better.
Team-level discussion and joint experimentation with AI is necessary; leaving AI use to individuals is insufficient. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Seniors and juniors benefit from AI very differently.
  • Good practices for tool use are not discoverable individually; they require joint experimentation.
  • Without team-level reflection, the senior-junior gap widens and juniors lose calibration.
Implicit premises:
  • The team is the appropriate unit of analysis for AI adoption.
  • Calibration of code quality is a socially-mediated skill, not a property of individuals.
  • Junior development pathways depend on observable collegial work — which AI substitutes away.
Inferences:
  • Without intervention, AI will hollow out junior skill formation while making senior work feel more productive.
  • Hiring practices may need to change — possibly fewer juniors per team is not the answer if the team loses its development capacity.
Psychological safety is the foundational substrate for all the practices that allow AI to be safely absorbed. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Without safety, people do not admit they don't understand the AI's output.
  • Without safety, double-loop learning (questioning whether we are doing the right things) is impossible.
  • Project Aristotle showed psychological safety as the single distinguishing factor of high-performing teams.
  • Safety is collective — a single unsafe member poisons it for everyone.
Implicit premises:
  • AI amplifies existing team conditions rather than creating new ones.
  • The risk of AI is socially mediated: it is the inability to surface confusion that is dangerous, not the AI's confusion itself.
Inferences:
  • Investment in psychological safety is a prerequisite for AI investment; the order matters.
  • Teams without safety should expect AI to amplify their dysfunction.
Structured cultural practices (scheduled reflection, show-and-tell, workshops, dedicated channels) outperform reliance on individual champions. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Champions (ildsjeler) burn out under delivery pressure without cultural support.
  • Learning practices need to be scheduled to survive against delivery pressure.
  • Slack (organizational room) must be created — it does not appear by itself.
Implicit premises:
  • Heroic individual energy is non-renewable.
  • Without leadership recognition of slack as a resource, learning is always crowded out.
Inferences:
  • Leaders should explicitly schedule learning time and protect it from delivery pressure.
  • Visible structural commitments (a recurring Friday meeting) signal cultural permission better than statements of value do.
Hybrid meetings should be avoided where possible; when unavoidable, structural interventions (everyone on their own camera, starting the digital meeting before the physical one, asking remote participants first) materially help. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Empirical sub-study finding: remote participants speak later in mixed meetings.
  • Physical meetings begin before and continue after the formal slot — remote participants are structurally excluded from this.
  • Working agreements among colleagues are stronger than rules from management.
Implicit premises:
  • Inclusion in informal conversation, not just formal turn-taking, is what builds psychological safety across distance.
  • The barrier of not seeing faces is asymmetric — it disadvantages the absent.
Inferences:
  • Default-remote meetings may be the cleaner solution where a team has any remote participants.
Augmented teams (with AI agents as quasi-members) is the interesting research frontier, but unanswered questions about team identity, autonomy, and junior development make it premature to prescribe specific structures.
Explicit premises:
  • We know small teams outperform large teams.
  • AI agents could let teams be smaller while still having needed competence.
  • If everyone just works with their own AI, the team disappears as a unit.
Implicit premises:
  • Team-as-unit has intrinsic value distinct from the sum of its members' productivity.
  • Junior development pathways are non-obvious in agent-augmented configurations.
Inferences:
  • Five years of empirical work is needed before confident prescriptions can be given.
  • Organizations rushing to restructure around 'AI as team member' are running ahead of the evidence.

Narratives

  • Brede Moe's career arc: 27 years at SINTEF, started as developer realizing he was not the best, moved to research on team dynamics in software development, did his doctorate on autonomous teams when 'no one thought it was fun' — took 5-8 years before the field caught up.
    → Establishes credibility and signals that he has been ahead of the curve before; subtly suggests current AI-and-teams research will follow the same delayed-recognition pattern.
  • Cross-functional team around vulnerable children referenced from Smidigpodden — doctors, psychologists, physiotherapists who agreed on the goal but still struggled to make the team work.
    → Illustrates that even with shared mission and motivation, cross-functional teamwork is hard; supports the proposition that tverrfaglig teams require deliberate investment.
  • Recent interview with a junior developer who admitted he didn't fully understand the code he had written with AI assistance — he 'should have spent more time understanding it, but I had to ship.'
    → Concrete instantiation of the AI-as-threat-to-learning argument; makes the abstract risk tangible.
  • Audun's own practice: keeping Claude Code on a tight leash with extensive tests and guardrails, claiming it makes him understand the codebase better, not worse.
    → Counter-example that complicates the threat narrative — Brede Moe immediately codes this as a senior practice, reinforcing the senior/junior asymmetry argument.
  • The car factory study (15-20 years ago): one division told to be 20% more productive, another told to 'learn how to use this well.' The learning group won.
    → Empirical anchor for the slow-down-to-speed-up argument; positions current AI exhortations as repeating a known failure pattern.
  • Brede Moe's BPM/SOA dissertation history: spent a year afterwards 'unlearning' the framework once he left the research bubble — admits the industry is fad-driven and that he has been a participant in fads.
    → Epistemic humility — establishes Brede Moe as someone who can distinguish hype from substance because he has fallen for hype before.
  • Pandemic distributed-work research: when COVID hit, his team already had years of data from Ericsson, Spotify, and Norwegian companies on virtual teams — 'lucky' to have findings ready.
    → Demonstrates that the team-level research lens generalizes across contexts; primes credibility for current AI-and-teams claims.
  • NAV Slack origin story: started in 2017 with five people; #general full history visible; when newcomers complained about excessive joking, they tried splitting into 'serious' and 'joke' channels, but developers and designers always migrated to the joke channel and re-merged the 50/50 mix. This happened repeatedly.
    → Illustrates that learning culture is emergent — top-down structural attempts to separate work from play failed because the actual culture is the mix. Implicitly supports the argument that working agreements must come from teams themselves.
  • A colleague set up multiple AI agents to collaborate on a problem and recorded the conversation between them; Brede Moe found it 'both fascinating and very scary.'
    → Concretizes the augmented teams research direction with an example he is wrestling with; openness about not yet knowing what to make of it.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What is the AI paradox, and why do organizational AI initiatives fail despite individual productivity gains?
    Because software development is fundamentally a learning process, and AI accelerates output while threatening the learning. Without team-level practice change, slack, and psychological safety, individual gains do not aggregate to organizational gains.
  • ✅ What does effective team autonomy look like?
    Small teams with mandate, competence, and the right people for decisions; balanced against the principle of having as few people as possible; supported by psychological safety and time for reflection.
  • ✅ How should teams adapt their practices for AI?
    Scheduled reflection (e.g. Friday learning meetings), explicit show-and-tell, workshops, dedicated Slack channels, robust delivery/test/monitoring infrastructure, and new specification practices. Recognize that AI adoption is an organizational change process, not a tool rollout.
  • ⭕ What becomes of teams when AI is in the loop?
    Open research question. Augmented teams might be smaller; AI agents might be quasi-members; but the autonomy implications and especially junior development pathways are unresolved.
  • ✅ How does the senior/junior dynamic change under AI?
    Seniors gain genuine help because they can judge quality and impact. Juniors lack calibration and risk shipping code they cannot explain. The team's calibration-mediating role becomes more important.
  • ⭕ How should AI agents' conversational tone (constant flattery) be managed?
    Raised by Truls; flagged as a concern (substitutes flattery for collegial calibration); not resolved.
  • ✅ What hybrid work setup actually works?
    There is no universal answer; teams must agree among themselves. Hybrid meetings are the worst case; default-digital meeting start helps; mastery on both work and home sides predicts well-being more than ratio does.
  • ✅ Is leadership style relevant to building learning culture?
    Yes — horizontal, peer-oriented leadership ('I think of everyone as horizontal') works better than hierarchical control. Brede Moe demonstrates this in his own group: no fixed RTO rule, but high office attendance because the culture is fun.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe MIT '95% of AI projects show no return' figure is asserted as foundational but never sourced, methodologically interrogated, or compared to base rates for non-AI IT projects (where failure rates are also famously high). The framing relies heavily on this number.
    Read next: Pursue the MIT study directly (likely the MIT NANDA / Sloan AI 'GenAI Divide' report from 2025) and compare against Standish CHAOS-style baselines for IT project failure to establish whether AI is failing more than other IT initiatives, or merely the same.
  • incompleteThe framing is almost entirely about knowledge-work software teams in professional services / large org settings. The argument is silent on: solo developers, small startups without team infrastructure, contracting/outsourcing relationships, non-developer roles where AI is also disrupting work, and contexts where 'team' is not the unit of production.
    Read next: Read Tabarrok / Acemoglu on AI and labor markets in non-team-shaped work; and Beane's Skill Code on apprenticeship in mixed expert/AI environments outside software, to test how portable Brede Moe's prescriptions are.
  • incompleteThe senior/junior asymmetry is identified but the prescription for what to do about it stops at 'build safety so juniors can admit confusion.' There is no concrete model of how junior skill formation actually works in an AI-augmented team — what scaffolding, what kinds of exercises, what the new apprenticeship looks like.
    Read next: Beane's Precision Proactivity (extracted this session) directly addresses this — proactive skill formation under AI assistance. Brede Moe's empirical team lens and Beane's apprenticeship lens together would be more complete than either alone.
  • incompleteBrede Moe's account of læringskultur emphasizes practices internal to a team (reflection, show-and-tell, Slack channels). It is silent on inter-team learning at scale — how lessons from one team's AI experimentation propagate across an organization of dozens or hundreds of teams. This is precisely where the MIT 95% gap might be most operationally relevant.
    Read next: Cutler on institutional overload and operating systems; SINTEF's own work on large-scale agile (Brede Moe has co-authored on this); Lean enterprise / Forsgren-Humble-Kim on horizontal practice diffusion.
    closed by11) AI-spesial med Vilde Opsal og Anders Sveen — Describes a working organization-scale propagation pattern for AI learning across teams: surface an ordinary team that succeeded, run fast and safe Slack channels, workshops, internal blog summaries, and a no-opt-out learning culture.
  • incompleteThe conversation treats AI tooling as relatively homogeneous — Copilot, ChatGPT, Cursor, Claude Code are mentioned as a cluster. But the team-level practices likely differ between chat-based assistants, autonomous agents, and in-IDE pair tools. Brede Moe gestures at this with 'augmented teams' but doesn't yet differentiate.
    Read next: Empirical studies of differential effects by tool class — DORA's 2025 AI findings, Microsoft / GitHub research on Copilot effects, ongoing studies of agentic coding tools.
  • incompleteThe car-factory study (cited as 15-20 years old) is the central empirical anchor for the slow-down-to-speed-up claim, but it is cited without source — the listener cannot evaluate the comparison. Similarly the 9-of-10 figure.
    Read next: Source the original study; even if accurate, ask whether software development resembles auto-manufacturing learning curves enough for the lesson to transfer.
  • incompleteThe argument assumes that team autonomy and team learning are the right units of analysis. It does not engage with alternative views — e.g. that AI might shift the unit back to the individual contractor or fragment work into smaller, more decomposable tasks, in which case the team-level prescription is the wrong response to the wrong question.
    Read next: Read agentic-work literature (Eric Ries, Naval-style 'agency leverage' arguments, Yegge's vibe-coding manifesto) for the opposing view.
  • incompletePsychological safety is treated as foundational and necessary, but the conversation does not address how to actually build it in distributed, hybrid, and AI-augmented configurations — only that it is harder there. This is the most-cited construct in the conversation and the least operationalized.
    Read next: Edmondson's recent work on hybrid safety; Brede Moe's own SINTEF publications on distributed-team trust formation.
    closed byPresentation: The Human Scalability Problem: Why Your Teams Don't Scale Like Your Code — Supplies concrete mechanisms for building the safety Brede Moe treats as foundational but unoperationalized: relationship-first team formation, leaders modeling vulnerability, rituals that persist across changing team composition, and direct handling of fear.
  • uninformedThe hosts and Brede Moe do not engage with the strong critique that AI agents are not yet reliable enough to be modeled as 'team members' in the augmented-team sense — they neither hold state, nor carry context across sessions in the way a colleague does, nor have stakes. The augmented-teams framing risks anthropomorphizing.
    Read next: Read critics like Gary Marcus, Emily Bender, and Murray Shanahan on the gap between LLM behavior and team-member competence.
    closed byResearch: Why You Shouldn't Treat AI Agents Like Employees — Engages the anthropomorphizing critique the podcast skipped, with experimental evidence: framing AI as a team member/employee measurably reduces human accountability and error-catching, supporting the case against modeling agents as colleagues (p1-p3, p7).
