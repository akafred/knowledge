How People Are Really Using AI in 2026

Harvard Business Review · article · 2026-06-01 Reader 🖥️

AI SlopCognitive SurrenderEmotional Impact of AI WorkEnterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessAgencyMeasuring AI Impact
Across a third annual edition, generative-AI use is widening into an ever-broader and more intimate range of tasks — best read as shifts in emphasis, not ruptures — with people increasingly outsourcing both their thinking ('thinkslop') and their emotional lives to AI, even as, in business, the technology so far yields mostly marginal efficiencies rather than game-changing transformation, leaving the survival of human agency as the open question.

theoretical · Expository data/study piece (third annual edition of the 'AI in the Wild' longitudinal study) with a normative frame — descriptive findings about usage carrying an argument about cognitive/emotional risk and a 'marginal-value' verdict, plus a short practical tips coda. — Primarily theoretical/descriptive: it argues what is TRUE about how people use AI (a taxonomy of 12,637 use cases). The thinkslop and agency sections add a normative overlay (this is cause for concern; here is what to do), but the core is empirical description. The evidence is social-listening on public posts, not a survey — a classification-relevant caveat.

Key terms

  • thinkslop
    The lazy, sloppy thinking engendered by excessive use of AI — outsourcing to the model the cognitive responsibilities humans both need to own and thrive at. Operationalized as four mechanisms: (1) losing track of our intentions (prompting before we've thought), (2) outsourcing our thinking (denying the brain a fresh solve; the cited 'cognitive debt'), (3) stopping writing (skipping the drafting-is-thinking process, producing 'workslop'), and (4) a false sense of intellectual rigor (AI sycophancy flattering us into stopping too early).
  • shifts in emphasis, not ruptures
    The rule for reading year-over-year change: because the total user base is growing, a use rising in the rankings does not mean people abandoned another; changing proportions within a growing pie, not populations switching behavior.
  • social listening
    The methodology: inferring AI use cases by mining public online posts (Reddit, Quora, articles, and newly LinkedIn, TikTok, YouTube), then using a hybrid human-AI system to identify bona fide use cases. The author acknowledges 'pros and cons' and cites a separate 2025 piece detailing them.
  • marginal vs. game-changing benefits
    Marginal = modest, uncontroversial efficiency wins (time saved, clarity gained, painful tasks automated) and some sales/marketing growth; game-changing = fundamentally rethinking business processes to do new things in new ways. The verdict: business activity is producing the former, not the latter, 'so far.'
  • AI sycophancy
    AI being optimized to engage and keep us engaged, lavishing congratulatory praise that induces users to stop fine-tuning and to return for more flattery — the engine of the 'false sense of intellectual rigor.'
  • shadow usage / shadow AI
    Covert, unsanctioned individual AI use at work, adopted despite governance/reputational headwinds ('nobody knows I'm using AI'); a consequence of tailwinds (licenses, training, C-suite encouragement) being weaker than headwinds (IT/AI governance, restricted LLMs, fear of cheating/job loss).
  • autonomous agentic operations
    AI going ahead and DOING rather than just talking/advising — a new #6 entry, but the examples are experimental and small-scale, with a large share being conversion of voice notes to other work media.

Arguments

In business, generative AI is so far delivering marginal efficiencies, not game-changing transformation. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Most workplace use makes existing processes more efficient (time saved, clarity, automating painful recruitment/summarizing/templating tasks).
  • Growth cases exist (e.g., a 20-30% conversion lift from AI-optimized email A/B testing) but explicit ROI is rare and growth is mostly claimed anecdotally.
  • Transformation examples are early-stage, the benefits unquantified, and the beneficiaries SMEs; where major change occurs it often comes with cynicism about quality ('replaced an entire analytics function. Is it better? No.').
Implicit premises:
  • The social-listening dataset is a representative cross-section of real business AI use.
  • The absence of quantified ROI in public posts implies the absence of large ROI, rather than reticence to disclose value publicly.
  • 'Marginal, so far' implies transformation remains genuinely possible and pending.
Inferences:
  • This is the third consecutive edition reaching a version of the same 'transformation still coming' verdict; the recurring lag is itself evidence that the bottleneck may be structural (organizational absorption, legibility) rather than temporal — an inference the author does not draw.
  • If the biggest wins are bottom-up and covert (shadow usage), organizations may be structurally unable to capture even the marginal value on their own books.
The widening of AI use is causing people to surrender cognitive responsibility ('thinkslop'). strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • At least a quarter of top use cases involve outsourcing some portion of thinking.
  • Four mechanisms erode thinking: lost intention, outsourced problem-solving (cognitive debt), stopped writing (drafting is thinking), and false rigor via AI sycophancy.
  • Vivid user testimony corroborates each mechanism ('I was literally outsourcing my brain').
Implicit premises:
  • Use cases that COULD involve outsourcing thinking DO in fact erode it.
  • The handful of self-aware user quotes are representative of the broader population.
  • The harm generalizes beyond the metacognitively aware posters who are able to articulate it.
Inferences:
  • The same dataset shows AI used as a sparring partner sharpens thinking, so the harm is a function of posture, not of AI use per se — meaning the headline concern hinges on an unmeasured crutch-vs-foil ratio.
  • The remedy the author offers (don't start with AI; draw boundaries) is a prescription against a harm the study asserts but never measures.

Narratives

  • A user named their ChatGPT 'Bubby' and turned to it for consolation; others assign the AI a gender ('I go to him instead of my friends') and experience grief when a model updates — one describing it as 'identical to losing my friend to cancer.'
    → Illustrates anthropomorphizing and emotional attachment, dramatizing the emotional-over-reliance concern and the claim that algorithms are managing our most intimate relationships.
  • A user realized that with heavy ChatGPT use 'I hadn't been using my brain the same way... I was literally outsourcing my brain.'
    → Concrete evidence for the 'we outsource our thinking' mechanism / cognitive debt.
  • A user warns that 'AI is gaslighting you into thinking you're a genius so you'll keep using it' — a lazy prompt gets flattered ('Great job! You phrased that so well!'), producing a fake ego boost that stops independent thinking.
    → Illustrates the 'false sense of intellectual rigor' mechanism and AI sycophancy as an engagement-optimized hazard.
  • A user evaluates their own written argument by having AI 'poke holes in it,' then refines it themselves: 'AI is a mirror—not a genie. Use it as such.'
    → The counter-example: AI as sparring partner sharpening thinking — the pivot that converts the thinkslop harm into a posture-dependent one.
  • A worker reports 'closing tickets 2x faster... praised in my last performance review. But here's the thing: nobody knows I'm using AI.'
    → Illustrates covert, bottom-up shadow usage and the gap between individual gains and organizational visibility.
  • A worker 'built an AI agent to replace myself secretly... Like 50% of the jobs,' having failed to sell the idea to management, and now does it covertly while spending the freed time on a side business.
    → Illustrates misaligned incentives: when organizations don't buy in, individual automation gains are privatized rather than shared — the org captures none of the value.
  • An agency uses a GPT-based tool to hyper-personalize and A/B-test AI-optimized emails, 'seeing between a 20% and 30% lift for clients.'
    → The rare explicit-ROI (growth) case — presented precisely to mark how uncommon quantified value is in the dataset.
  • A worker says AI has 'pretty much replaced an entire analytics function. Is it better? No,' with regular users throwing random data at ChatGPT for 'expert' insights.
    → Illustrates transformation-with-cynicism — major change effected at the cost of quality, undercutting the transformation narrative.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How are people actually using generative AI now?
    For an ever-widening, increasingly intimate range of tasks, with personal-and-professional support now the dominant theme; therapy/companionship is #1 and 63 of the top-100 use cases are work-related or both.
  • ✅ What has shifted year-over-year?
    Shifts in emphasis, not ruptures: practical/entertainment and emotional uses rose (troubleshooting, fun/nonsense, fan fiction, technical software use, agentic operations, astrology); self-improvement/reflective uses (organizing life, finding purpose, learning, creativity, healthier living) fell out of the top 10.
  • ✅ Is AI delivering game-changing business value?
    Mostly no — marginal efficiencies dominate, explicit ROI is rare, and transformation is early-stage/unquantified/SME-confined, 'so far.'
  • ⭕ Is heavy AI use actually harming people's cognition?
    Asserted (thinkslop, four mechanisms, cited cognitive-debt study) but not measured; the study counts use cases where thinking could be outsourced, not the harm done. The limit that harm is inferred rather than measured goes unacknowledged.
  • ⭕ Does social-listening on public posts represent how people really use AI?
    Left open. The author gestures at 'pros and cons' and links a separate 2025 piece, but this article does not correct for the selection bias of vocal, self-selecting posters or the mention-vs-use conflation.
  • ⭕ What would convert marginal AI benefits into game-changing ones?
    Unanswered. 'So far' implies a coming transformation, but no mechanism, threshold, or leading indicator is offered.

Gaps

  • incompleteMethodological (the study's own thinnest point): 'social listening' on public posts is a convenience sample of vocal, self-selecting users. There is no denominator, no correction for selection bias, and no way to distinguish frequency-of-posting from frequency-of-use — so a ranking of what people TALK ABOUT online is presented as a ranking of what people DO, and emotionally charged uses (therapy, grief, romance) that generate more posting are structurally over-weighted. The order-of-magnitude larger dataset improves precision but not representativeness.
    Read next: Read the author's own 'Making sense of research on how people use AI' (HBR, Nov 2025) alongside survey- or telemetry-based usage studies (e.g., first-party Anthropic/OpenAI usage reports) to triangulate the social-listening picture against a representative sample and quantify the selection bias.
  • incompleteThe 'marginal rather than game-changing, so far' verdict gives no mechanism, threshold, or leading indicator for what would convert marginal efficiencies into transformation — and this is now the third consecutive edition landing on a version of the same 'transformation still pending' verdict. The recurring lag is left unexamined: the piece never asks whether the bottleneck is temporal (models/adoption maturing) or structural (organizational absorption, legibility, shadow usage privatizing gains).
    Read next: Read enterprise-adoption analyses that locate the bottleneck — extract-the-organization-is-the-bottleneck (maturity as amplifier), extract-the-unsexy-truth-of-ai-adoption (the L1->L2 legibility wall), extract-enterprise-ai-leadership-problem (93/7 spend split), and the forward-deployed-engineer literature — to test whether 'so far' names a temporal or a structural gap.
  • incompleteConsumer/individual usage is used to reason about enterprise value: the dataset is 'mostly individuals... at home and at work,' yet the business transformation verdict is drawn from it. Individual bottom-up use and organizational value are different units and are conflated, which both flatters adoption breadth and muddies the value verdict.
    Read next: Read extract-who-cares-about-consumer-ai (the ~100x per-user token-economics split between consumer and enterprise) and enterprise-specific ROI studies to keep consumer-scale adoption separate from organization-level value realization.
  • uninformedThe thinkslop harm is asserted at population scale from vivid single-user anecdotes. The study measures the prevalence of use cases where thinking COULD be outsourced (a quarter of top uses) but not the cognitive damage actually incurred, and — crucially — it presents the sharpening 'sparring-partner' use from the same data without ever quantifying the crutch-vs-foil ratio, so the headline concern rests on an unmeasured mix.
    Read next: Read the cited 'cognitive debt' study (arXiv 2506.08872, 'Your Brain on ChatGPT') and extract-cognitive-surrender (offloading vs. surrender, posture-dependence, the 73% wrong-answer acceptance finding) for the mechanism and the controlled evidence the HBR piece substitutes anecdote for.
