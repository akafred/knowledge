17) Jonas Slørdahl Skjærpe om svinghjuleffekter, liminalfaser og kunsten å ta heis

Truls Jørgensen og Audun Fauchald Strand — Heis Podcast · podcast · 2026-05-18 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

AI SlopLearning Culture & SlackOrganizational Abstraction LevelsPublic-Sector DigitalizationSociotechnical ArchitectureTechnology Diffusion & Transformation CurvesTechnology Leadership (Heis)
Large sociotechnical transformations — whether NAV's digital transformation a decade ago or the current AI shift — work by accumulating slow flywheel momentum that then snaps into a sudden S-curve climb through a high-friction liminal phase, and the leaders who guide organizations through such transitions are those who can 'take the elevator' across organizational floors and disciplines, translating between domain, technology and executive language while staying close enough to the craft that their advice is grounded in something real.

mixed · narrative-argumentative — long-form interview where the guest's career anecdotes (NAV transformation, COVID, going consultant, Silicon Valley trip) carry an argumentative spine about how complex sociotechnical systems change — Hosts Truls Jørgensen and Audun Fauchald Strand interview their former boss Jonas Slørdahl Skjærpe, now partner for technology consulting (Financial Services) at EY, formerly IT-direktør at NAV. The hosts ask short questions; the guest answers at length. Tone is collegial — all three worked together at NAV — so claims are stated with practitioner certainty rather than defended at length. The conversation moves through three loosely chained arcs: (1) the NAV transformation in retrospect, (2) leadership across consultant/leader/professional modes, (3) AI as the next paradigm shift.

Key terms

  • svinghjul / svinghjuleffekt (flywheel / flywheel effect) Technology Diffusion & Transformation Curves
    Slow-building organisational momentum where many capability investments compound silently and then deliver disproportionate returns when an event arrives. Used twice: about NAV's IT capability before COVID, and about transformations more generally — 'energetic processes that happen slowly first, then fast'.
  • liminalfase (liminal phase) Technology Diffusion & Transformation Curves
    The transitional phase between two paradigms — after the old rules are abandoned and before the new ones are established. Characterised by friction, higher risk, and the need to let some old structures go before the new can settle. Jonas attributes the term to social anthropologists he worked with at NAV. He applies it both to NAV's transformation and to the current AI moment 'on almost all fronts'.
  • kunsten å ta heis (the art of taking the elevator) Technology Leadership (Heis)
    The capability of moving fluently across organisational floors (executive, middle, team) and across silos, translating language and concerns between them. Jonas treats it as both a personal joy and a strategically critical capability for transformation, because organisations leak information vertically and horizontally and need traversal specialists. Requires security in your craft, humility about other perspectives, and metaphor/model fluency. Explicitly trainable.
  • S-kurve (S-curve) Technology Diffusion & Transformation Curves
    The adoption/diffusion curve — slow start, steep middle, plateau — invoked to argue that many simultaneous S-curves were rising during the NAV transformation (deploy frequency, share of developers, team formation) and that AI in mid-2026 is still 'very early on the curve'. Distinct from a 'kumulativ' curve.
  • fra opposisjon til posisjon (from opposition to position) Technology Diffusion & Transformation Curves
    The transition phase in transformation when the change agents stop fighting and become the institution that invites others in. 'Fighting' stops working past a certain point; one must occupy the structures and meet people where they are.
  • fagtom zeitgeist (craft-empty zeitgeist)
    A moment when the field is hungry for substantive learning — books circulated, ledergruppe homework, high book-reading activity — because the discipline is collectively re-skilling for a new paradigm. Used to describe the post-Accelerate NAV moment.
  • sosioteknisk arkitektur (sociotechnical architecture) Sociotechnical Architecture
    The integrated design of social/organisational structures together with software architecture. Jonas says discovering he found this kind of system design fun was what made him realise leadership and IT-arkitektur were the same problem in different clothes.
  • trykkoker (pressure cooker)
    A successful transformation acts as a pressure cooker that forces organisational floors and silos closer together because tasks now cross hierarchies and silos simultaneously. People riding the elevator become valuable in this state.
  • retenking av abstraksjonsnivå (rethinking the abstraction level) Organizational Abstraction Levels
    What happens periodically in software: a new technological wave (high-level languages, OS/DB, internet, cloud) raises the level at which humans express intent, changing what skills are needed and how work is organised. Jonas argues generative AI is doing this again, moving from 'high-level instructions' to 'intentions, with instructions written by machines'.
  • AI slop AI Slop
    Low-quality AI-generated output that nobody actually understands or owns. Jonas claims everyone has personal responsibility to ensure what they produce is of quality and that they comprehend it — naming this in a team meeting that morning.
  • læringsprosess vs produksjonsprosess (learning process vs production process) Learning Culture & Slack
    Personal heuristic for AI use: at any given moment you are either learning with AI's help or producing with AI's help, and the two cannot be collapsed. You cannot skip the learning that leads up to the production output, because AI presents convincing-looking output that may be substantively poor.
  • traktor-analogien (the tractor analogy) Technology Diffusion & Transformation Curves
    Tractors arrived in the US around 1900 but did not displace horses until the 1950s because the product was immature, skills were scarce, and farms were too small to justify capital-intensive operation. Jonas uses this to argue that AI's real impact will unfold over decades of restructuring, not in a couple of years.

Arguments

The current AI moment should be analysed as a liminal phase on a paradigm-shift S-curve, not as a discrete tool deployment. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Software has shifted abstraction level periodically (high-level languages, OS/DB, internet, cloud/CD).
  • Generative AI moves us from instructions to intentions written by machines — a fundamental abstraction shift.
  • We are early in the S-curve; complexity ceilings and process work will push back as productivity rises.
  • Like the tractor and the car, full impact requires decades of restructuring of skills, products and organisations.
Implicit premises:
  • Past technological transitions are reliable analogies for the current one (i.e., AI is not categorically different from earlier general-purpose technologies).
  • The right unit of analysis is the sociotechnical system, not the individual productivity delta.
  • Restructuring time is bounded by human and organisational adaptation, not by model capability.
Inferences:
  • Programs that judge AI by short-term per-developer productivity will systematically underestimate it.
  • AI strategy should plan for years of high-friction work in which old structures must visibly let go.
  • Sovereignty / regional capability matters more than productivity benchmarks for European actors.
NAV's COVID performance proves the value of slow capability accumulation rather than crisis-time heroics. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Many transformation S-curves had already crossed their steep section by 2020.
  • Laptops and home-office capability had been deployed shortly before — barely in time.
  • The dagpenger-forskudd episode shows alignment from political top to developers within days.
Implicit premises:
  • Capabilities are visible only when stressed.
  • Luck and preparation are not separable in retrospective analysis.
  • Software-organisational fitness has a long lead time and short observability window.
Inferences:
  • Defending capability investments in normal times requires anticipating crises that may never come (or come late).
  • Post-crisis attempts to 'institutionalise crisis-mode alignment' will mostly fail — Truls notes things 'became maybe a little better, no more'.
Leaders who can 'take the elevator' across organisational floors are the hinge capability for transformation. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Large organisations leak information vertically (across hierarchical levels) and horizontally (across silos).
  • Transformation acts as a 'trykkoker' that forces these gaps closed temporarily.
  • Translating between floors requires a different language at each — content, style, what is respected.
  • Riding the elevator is trainable: craft security, humility, metaphor fluency.
Implicit premises:
  • There is no organisational mechanism that closes these information gaps without specific people doing the translation.
  • The translator role is more valuable than additional specialist depth in transformation phases.
  • Most leaders cannot in fact do this translation, otherwise the role would not be invaluable.
Inferences:
  • Talent strategy in transformation should explicitly identify and protect translators — not just specialists.
  • Coaching programs for senior engineers should include metaphor/model fluency, not just technical depth.
  • Promotion paths that punish elevator-takers for 'not staying in their lane' are systematically destructive.
Healthy AI use requires never collapsing learning and production into a single act. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Models are trained to make you feel smart and not question their output.
  • AI output looks convincing while being substantively poor.
  • Quality control rests with the human, who must understand what they produced.
Implicit premises:
  • There is a meaningful distinction between learning and producing — they are not always entangled for human work.
  • Human craft cannot be skipped to ship craft-quality artefacts.
  • 'AI slop' is a real failure mode that can be avoided by personal discipline.
Inferences:
  • Junior engineers may need explicit learning phases that exclude AI, like the chess world-champion analogy.
  • Teams should distinguish AI-assisted learning sessions from AI-assisted production sessions and not mix them.
  • Quality reviews of AI-produced work must check the producer's understanding, not just the output.
Introversion is compatible with — possibly aligned with — senior technology leadership.
Explicit premises:
  • Substantive discussion gives Jonas energy; small-talk drains it.
  • He composes a team that complements his weaker registers.
  • At very large scale, the 'what do people think of me' load becomes too large to track and must be dropped.
Implicit premises:
  • The leader does not personally need to manufacture social energy — environments can be designed to produce it.
  • Self-knowledge about energy-draining vs energy-giving registers is itself a leadership skill.
  • Scale beyond a threshold actually relieves an introvert leader of small-scale social tax.
Inferences:
  • Selection processes that screen out introverts for senior tech roles are throwing away a viable archetype.
  • Introverts entering leadership should design their team composition explicitly, not assume it will form well.
  • There may be a counter-intuitive scale threshold above which introversion becomes net-easier, not harder.

Narratives

  • First summer job recommendation: Jonas had to recommend either XP or Rational Rose as a process for software development. He picked Rational Rose because 'just wait for the next version, it will be great' — and later realised he was wrong.
    → Frames the speaker as someone who has lived being on the wrong side of a paradigm shift; this licenses the later epistemic humility about AI.
  • Accelerate (the book) became homework for the NAV IT leadership team; Jonas wanted to understand what the research actually said and what the implications were. The hosts recall that DevOps practitioners had been claiming these things for years, but the book gave them research legitimacy.
    → Illustrates the 'fagtom zeitgeist' and shows leaders performing the same learning they were asking their organisations to do.
  • There were moments during the NAV transformation when Jonas felt 'now we are past the point of no return.' He notes it could have gone wrong — and they could 'have ended up in stortinget's kontroll' — but never felt strong personal pressure.
    → Captures the lived experience of the liminal phase: high risk in retrospect, equanimity in the moment.
  • COVID hit NAV with twelve major events at once including a 60x traffic increase ('Norway becoming USA-sized overnight'). Anders Egge drove out laptops alongside scarce toilet paper during the 'dopapir-krise'. Many employees jumped onto Slack on the announcement weekend and worked through.
    → Dramatises both the multi-S-curve nature of the crisis and the cultural readiness that prior investments had built.
  • The dagpenger 'forskudd' story: With 400,000 people permitted overnight, the regulations required individual case-by-case judgment, which was impossible. A 'sharp jurist' realised they could create a 'forskudd' (advance) instead of a new benefit, which propagated rapidly through the political top and reached NAV developers; a working solution shipped by Tuesday.
    → Illustrates strategic alignment from political top to delivery team that Jonas argues is otherwise nearly impossible to engineer.
  • Silicon Valley visit: Jonas met startup CEOs working on AI, visited Stanford, and was struck by both the investment intensity and the 'quite drøye business in det' — minimal guardrails on biology, industry, tech.
    → Sets up the AI section's combination of awe and unease, and the European-sovereignty concern.
  • Tractor analogy: Tractors arrived in the US in the early 1900s with metal wheels (got stuck in soft soil), scarce maintenance skills in rural areas, and farms too small to justify capital expense. Tractors only displaced horses around the 1950s when product, skills and farm sizes had all restructured.
    → Concrete historical case for the implicit premise that general-purpose technologies need decades of structural change before delivering full impact.
  • Kyle Kingsbury's car analogy: a long series on how long it took cars to fully transform US cities into car-preferred infrastructure — decades from the first automobile shock to the fully restructured urban environment.
    → A second analogous case to back the tractor argument; also signals shared reading-stack with the hosts (Aphyr/Jepsen).
  • Gukesh as the new chess world champion after Magnus Carlsen — reportedly the only young player who was not allowed a computer until age 12, and therefore had to learn chess differently before being accelerated by machine training.
    → Carries the argument that early-stage learners may need protected craft-acquisition before AI assistance — without Jonas having to defend the claim abstractly.
  • Law firm policy: some law firms allegedly forbid junior associates from using AI for the first two years until they have basic training. Jonas has 'sympathy' for this approach but acknowledges EY has not chosen it.
    → Adds a parallel sector's experiment with shielded learning, leaving the question open rather than resolved.
  • A team meeting that morning where Jonas pointed out that 'AI slop is a thing' and that personal responsibility for output quality is non-negotiable.
    → Grounds the learning-vs-production rule in a concrete recent leadership act.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How does a large organisation actually transform across paradigms?
    Through accumulated flywheel momentum that crosses many simultaneous S-curves, passing through a liminal phase of high friction where old structures must be released before new ones settle, and through the transition from opposition to position once the change is institutionalised.
  • ✅ What lessons from the NAV transformation generalise?
    Capability built before the crisis is what makes the crisis survivable; crisis-time alignment is easy because the narrative is given; non-crisis leadership requires constructing that narrative every day; opposition-to-position transitions cannot be skipped.
  • ✅ Is leadership a separate discipline from craft?
    No — partner-style work that combines owner/leader/practitioner is energising rather than confusing, provided you explicitly signal which mode you are in so colleagues do not mistake your opinions for decisions.
  • ✅ How should we understand AI's place in software engineering right now?
    As an abstraction shift comparable to high-level languages, OS/DB, the internet and cloud, currently at the very start of its S-curve, in a liminal phase whose full impact will take decades of organisational restructuring to deliver.
  • ⭕ How should organisations handle juniors in the AI era?
    Open question. Jonas has sympathy for shielded learning (chess, law firms) but admits EY has not chosen that path; the implicit answer is that pedagogy must be redesigned but the shape of the redesign is unclear.
  • ✅ What makes someone able to 'take the elevator' across organisational floors?
    Security in your craft, humility about other perspectives, fluency with metaphor and model. Trainable, not innate.
  • ✅ Why does a podcast on this topic exist at all?
    Truls says it openly: the hosts wanted to keep doing the work of translating between organisational floors together after their jobs no longer required it of them.
  • ⭕ Can the conditions of crisis-time alignment be reproduced in normal times?
    Implicitly no — Truls notes that COVID-time alignment did not become a new normal; things 'became maybe a little better, no more'. The hosts and guest do not propose how to engineer alignment outside crisis.
  • ⭕ Is Europe structurally positioned to participate meaningfully in the AI shift?
    Jonas names the gap and the sovereignty concern but offers no path to closing it; the conversation leaves this as an observation, not a plan.
  • ⭕ How do you tell whether you are in a real S-curve or a hype curve that will collapse?
    Truls raises blockchain as the cautionary case; Jonas concedes 'it is not always you know when you are in it.' No reliable heuristic is offered.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe flywheel/S-curve/liminal frame is used to describe both the NAV transformation (which succeeded) and the AI shift (in progress). The conversation never names a transformation that failed at the liminal phase and asks why — so the framework is at risk of being unfalsifiable on its own terms.
    Read next: Read failed transformations through the same frame — e.g., government-IT projects that died in their liminal phase, large agile adoptions that reverted. Cutler's overload work and the 'people who tried Spotify model' literature would expose where the frame breaks. The Geoffrey Moore 'Crossing the Chasm' tradition has more developed accounts of curves that stall.
  • incompleteThe 'learning vs production' rule is stated as a personal heuristic but not connected to any educational theory or empirical evidence. For a leader of consultants asked to scale this practice, there is no model for how the two modes should be partitioned, whether across days, projects, or moods.
    Read next: Donald Schön's reflection-in-action; deliberate-practice literature (Ericsson); Beck's 'tidy first?' framing of preparatory vs delivery work — the tidy/messy split echoes the learning/production split structurally. Worth syntopical pass against Beck's recent 'Starving Genies' work on AI-assisted craft.
  • incompleteThe elevator metaphor is generalised from Jonas's own career and from the show's animating premise. The conversation does not seriously consider organisations where vertical traversal is structurally suppressed (military, regulated finance with information walls, classified environments). The implicit premise that elevators are always available may not hold.
    Read next: Read about organisations with designed information barriers — security classifications, MAR-walled investment banking, healthcare compartmentalisation. James C Scott's 'Seeing Like a State' on legibility. Where the elevator is locked, what substitutes?
  • incompleteThe introvert-leader claim rests on one practitioner's experience and one rhetorical contrast (scale removes small-talk tax). Jonas notes that some colleagues found it hard to distinguish his opinions from his decisions — a cost that may be specifically introvert-shaped (lower social-cue density) but is not investigated.
    Read next: Susan Cain on introvert leadership; the Cutler thread on 'signals that look like decisions' in leadership communication. The implicit problem of 'low-cue leadership at scale' is worth its own extraction.
  • incompleteThe tractor and car analogies argue for slow structural change, but neither is examined for disanalogies. AI compresses learning loops in a way physical machinery cannot. The argument relies on the assumption that adoption is gated by humans, not by capability — which is plausible but not defended.
    Read next: The general-purpose-technology economics literature (Bresnahan & Trajtenberg); David Autor on labour-market restructuring time. Counter-cases: dial-up to broadband transitions, mobile phones — both moved faster than tractors. What predicts speed?
  • uninformedThe Gukesh anecdote is reported confidently ('the only one of the young players not allowed a computer before age 12') but is not actually verified in the conversation. Whether true or apocryphal, it is doing argumentative work it has not earned.
    Read next: Quick fact-check; more importantly, treat the underlying claim about protected early craft as a hypothesis and look at the actual evidence on AI use in early-career learning — programming pedagogy research will move on this in 2026-2028.
  • incompleteThe claim that NAV's success was driven by capability investments is asserted from inside the case by the very people who made those investments. There is no counterfactual — no comparable agency that did not invest similarly and failed similarly. Some of the 'flywheel' may be storytelling.
    Read next: Compare cross-country COVID welfare-IT responses (Germany, Denmark, UK), and Norwegian agencies of similar size that did not transform similarly. The Skatteetaten and Helsedirektoratet experiences would be a natural comparison.
  • illogicalJonas argues both that we are early on the AI S-curve (so 10% gains are not the story) and that complexity will push back on productivity gains as they accumulate. These are compatible but in tension: if the curve climbs steeply because of intent-level abstraction, the complexity ceiling should appear later; if complexity bites soon, the curve is shallower than claimed. The argument is not adjudicated.
    Read next: The Brooks / Mythical Man Month tradition on accidental vs essential complexity; Ousterhout on system design; recent measurements on long-running agent harnesses (Anthropic harness design work) which directly test the complexity-ceiling claim.
