Supporting Your Employees' Career Growth When Everyone Is Overwhelmed

Rebecca Knight · rss · 2026-05 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

AI Labor DisruptionCareer DevelopmentLearning Culture & Slack
When workloads and AI/economic uncertainty crowd out career thinking, managers should reframe development away from long-horizon ladder-climbing toward near-term, broad, learning embedded inside the existing flow of work — and lead it by visibly doing it themselves.

practical · expository — A 'What the Experts Say' HBR advice piece structured around six prescriptive subheadings and a Do/Don't list. It argues for specific managerial actions, not for understanding a phenomenon. Two experts (Helen Tupper of Amazing If; Matthew Bidwell of Wharton) supply the authority; Knight is the synthesizing journalist.

Key terms

  • Career ladder Career Development
    An outdated mental model of linear vertical progression through predictable rungs; treated as something to move away from.
  • Staying wide Career Development
    Cultivating breadth and scenario-based bets rather than deep specialization, because specialization carries automation risk in a fast-changing landscape.
  • Learning in the flow of work Learning Culture & Slack
    Treating real work events (client meetings, presentations, Q&A) as the primary growth venue, rather than scheduled separate 'learning time'.
  • Show your own work
    Visibly modeling learning as a leader — sharing what you read, what you're trying, what failed — so the prescription does not read as 'just words'.
  • Two-year horizon Career Development
    The planning window in which Bidwell claims one can speak with confidence about skills; contrasted with a ten-year horizon where one cannot.
  • Permission to learn
    An explicit managerial grant — both time and license — for an employee to study something not obviously tied to today's job.
  • Lighten their load
    Actively subtracting tasks before adding learning expectations; treated as the manager's prerequisite move.

Arguments

Managers should reframe career development around learning, not ladders. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Careers no longer follow predictable vertical paths.
  • Technology change makes long-term plans unreliable.
  • Good learners create opportunities; non-learners get left behind.
Implicit premises:
  • The organization will not provide a substitute structure for the ladder; the employee's own learning capacity must.
  • Adaptability is morally on the employee even when the volatility is created by employers and markets.
Inferences:
  • If learnability is now the primary career asset, employers who don't measure or reward it are mismanaging human capital.
  • Workers' bargaining power shifts toward the most legibly adaptable, away from the most deeply skilled.
Plan near-term, stay wide long-term. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • We can predict skill needs over two years but not ten.
  • Specialization carries automation risk; breadth hedges it.
Implicit premises:
  • Two-year-stable skills (strategy, customer understanding, organizational reading) are themselves not automatable — an unargued claim.
  • Individuals can usefully 'think in scenarios' without the data or tooling that strategic planners use.
Inferences:
  • Generalists are now the lower-variance career bet, reversing decades of 'find your niche' advice.
Embed learning in the existing week rather than scheduling it separately. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Dedicated learning days are wasted because people worry about backlog.
  • Every week contains stretch opportunities (client events, presentations) that already exist.
  • Reflection after the event is what converts the experience to learning.
Implicit premises:
  • The volume of work is fixed and not negotiable upward; therefore embedding is the only option.
  • The manager has discretion to reassign visible work (opening a meeting, leading Q&A) — which assumes a fairly autonomous middle manager.
Inferences:
  • If learning must be embedded because separate time is impossible, then overload is treated as a permanent climate, not a problem to be solved.
Managers must visibly model their own learning ('show your own work'). strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Teams take behavioral cues from leaders.
  • Words without modeled behavior lack credibility.
  • Honesty about failed experiments builds trust.
Implicit premises:
  • Credibility — not capacity — is the binding constraint on learning cultures.
  • Visibility of effort can substitute for changing the conditions of work.
Inferences:
  • The 'corner-office noodling with Claude while setting impossible targets' line concedes that the system is producing impossible targets — but proposes only individual transparency in response.

Narratives

  • The project manager who wants to move into customer insights — Tupper recommends pushing them toward quantitative research, business model innovation, and companies bringing products to market, beyond the formal qualification.
    → Illustrates 'make it personal' and 'stay wide' — that good development plans license learning beyond the obvious next-role requirements.
  • The team member who wants to stretch into a broader business role — already has a client event on her calendar; the manager has her open the meeting, lead the Q&A, and debrief the client.
    → Concretizes 'build learning into the week, not onto it' — that the calendar already contains the growth opportunities.
  • The product-management aspirant whose enduring core skills (strategy, customer, organization) outlast the changing definition of what a PM does.
    → Supports the claim that two-year skill horizons are knowable while ten-year ones are not.
  • The leader who schedules a learning day for the team and watches everyone spend it worrying about the backlog at their desks.
    → Negative case for separate-time learning; supports the embedded-learning prescription.
  • Bidwell's 'corner-office noodling with Claude while setting impossible targets' image.
    → Implicitly concedes that current target-setting is unreasonable, while the prescription that follows is only about leader honesty, not target reduction.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How do you make career development feel relevant in a moment dominated by short-term pressure?
    Reframe around near-term, learnable skills, embedded in current work; drop ten-year ladder talk.
  • ✅ How do you find time for these conversations when your own plate is just as full?
    Per-person mapping conversations, plus embedding learning in existing meetings — not adding new time blocks.
  • ✅ How should employees position themselves against the risk of AI-driven obsolescence?
    Stay broad; think in scenarios; bet on enduring skills (strategy, customer, organization).
  • ⭕ Is the overload itself a manageable problem or a fixed climate?
    Not addressed. The only intervention on workload is one sentence ('lighten their load') with no structural prescription.
  • ⭕ Who owns career development when the organizational system has stopped offering structured paths?
    Implicitly: the employee owns it, the manager coaches it, the organization is absent. Never argued explicitly.
  • ⭕ What if the targets being set are themselves the source of the overload?
    Acknowledged in the 'impossible targets' aside but treated as a context for leader honesty, not a thing to fix.

Gaps

  • incompleteTreats overload as a permanent atmospheric condition rather than a designed outcome. The article tells overwhelmed managers how to coach overwhelmed reports about growth, but never asks why aggregate work has expanded or whether it should be cut. The one-sentence 'lighten their load' is the closest it comes — it is not developed and not staffed with examples.
    Read next: John Cutler's 'institutionalized overload' (TBM 412 and related) treats overload as a systems problem with feedback loops and organizational levers — read this against Knight's piece to see what's missing when the manager is the only actor in the frame.
  • incompleteThe organization as a unit of analysis is absent. No discussion of role architecture, promotion criteria, learning budgets, internal mobility programs, or HR systems. The implicit model is: each manager improvises a development culture inside their span of control, regardless of structure.
    Read next: Org-design literature on internal labor markets and skill architectures (e.g., Cappelli on talent on demand) — or Cutler on org operating systems — would supply the missing layer.
  • incompleteThe claim that breadth ('stay wide') hedges automation risk while specialization concentrates it is asserted, not argued, and runs against historical evidence that deep expertise often commands a premium during disruption. Which skills genuinely survive — and on what evidence — is not engaged.
    Read next: Empirical work on AI exposure by occupation/skill (Eloundou et al., Brynjolfsson et al.) — and any pushback arguing that depth is now scarcer and more valuable.
    closed byItchy Brain (Still Burning: Michael Grinich & Kent Beck on AI adoption, enterprise developer tools) — Supplies the requested pushback on the breadth-hedges-automation thesis: Grinich argues the profession normalizes toward medicine/law-style deep specialization with longer apprenticeships, while flagging the opposing barrier-dropping trend as unresolved.
  • incompleteRisk and accountability are silently transferred to the individual. Career uncertainty caused by employer decisions (layoffs, restructurings, AI deployment) becomes a personal learnability problem. There is no symmetric obligation on the employer.
    Read next: Critical management scholarship on the 'enterprising self' and responsibilization (e.g., du Gay, Fleming) — and any HBR-adjacent counterpoint arguing for employer-side commitments.
  • illogical'Show your own work' is positioned as the bridge between asking impossible things and maintaining credibility. But if the targets are genuinely impossible (Bidwell's own framing), no amount of leader transparency repairs that; transparency without target reduction is theater. The argument elides the difference.
    Read next: Anything on psychological safety as a precondition for honest upward escalation (Edmondson) — to see what 'showing your own work' would need to do to actually change the work, not just narrate it.
  • uninformedNo acknowledgment that 'permission to learn' presumes a manager with real slack and political cover to grant time for non-job-essential study. In overloaded, deliverable-tracked teams, that permission is often not in the manager's actual gift — the article assumes managerial discretion that may not exist.
    Read next: Cutler on power and space in organizations; flow accounting / slack theory (Tom DeMarco).
