The growing AI backlash

Gary Marcus · newsletter · 2026-05-04 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

AI BacklashAI Bubble DebateAI Regulation & Governance (External)AI SlopLLM Limitations
Generative AI has been a net negative for society outside a few domains like coding, the backlash was predictable and is now accelerating toward political salience, and the industry bears primary responsibility for privatizing gains while externalizing harms.

practical · polemic/opinion — A short, persuasive opinion piece marshalling anecdotal evidence (tweets, news links, personal predictions) into a thesis about public sentiment turning against generative AI. Primarily argumentative with light evidentiary support.

Key terms

  • net negative AI Backlash
    The aggregate societal impact of generative AI, weighed across all domains, is harmful — the harms outweigh the benefits
  • slop AI Slop
    Low-quality AI-generated content flooding the internet and degrading information ecosystems
  • QuitGPT AI Backlash
    A grassroots movement of people abandoning ChatGPT and similar tools as a form of protest
  • over-leveraged AI Bubble Debate
    Data center buildout that exceeds actual demand or justified return, creating economic and environmental risk

Arguments

Implicit premises:

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why is public sentiment turning against AI?
    Because GenAI has been a net negative outside coding — harms are real, visible, and personally felt.
  • ✅ Is the backlash justified?
    Yes — harms outweigh benefits and were predictable.
  • ✅ Could the downsides have been foreseen?
    Yes — predicted by Marcus (2023), AI ethicists, and OpenAI itself (2019).
  • ⭕ Will AI backlash become politically significant?
    Predicted to be a major factor in 2028 elections, claimed to be 'on track'.

Gaps

  • evidentiaryNo quantitative evidence is offered for any of the enumerated harms. The 'net negative' claim rests entirely on an unweighted list of negatives versus two positives.
  • analyticalThe exemption of coding (and brainstorming) from the 'net negative' verdict is asserted without criteria for what makes a domain qualify as net-positive. No framework for how to weigh domain-specific benefits against cross-cutting harms.
  • dialecticalNo engagement with counterarguments: productivity gains in knowledge work, medical applications, accessibility tools, scientific research acceleration. The piece treats these as non-existent rather than as insufficient to offset harms.
  • predictiveThe political prediction (2028 elections) lacks any mechanism story — how does diffuse frustration convert into voting behavior or policy change?
  • constructiveThe closing hope that 'some future form of AI might be great' is unconnected to any analysis of what would make future AI different from current GenAI.
