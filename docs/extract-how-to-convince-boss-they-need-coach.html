How to Convince Your Boss They Need a Coach

Marlo Lyons · rss · 2026-03-27 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Decision-Making PracticesAgencyManaging Up & Influence
The article argues that subordinates can influence senior executives to engage an executive coach by reframing coaching as a strategic tool rather than a remedial intervention, using the executive's own stated pain points as entry points, and carefully managing the power dynamics so the decision feels self-originated.

practical · prescriptive article (how-to) — A practitioner-oriented HBR article offering a five-step tactical playbook for subordinates who want their boss to hire an executive coach. Written from the perspective of a career coach drawing on client cases.

Step 5: Offer a Time-Bound ExperimentLowers commitment cost with a short trial (e.g., eight sessions).

Key terms

  • executive coaching
    A confidential, one-on-one relationship with an external professional who serves as a strategic thought partner, free of internal politics.
  • blind spots Decision-Making Practices
    Leadership behaviors or patterns invisible to the leader due to insulation from candid feedback at senior levels.
  • identity protection / ego barrier
    A leader's attachment to the self-narrative 'I got here on my own terms' which makes coaching feel like a threat to identity.
  • pain points Managing Up & Influence
    Frustrations the executive has explicitly voiced about business challenges (alignment, board pressure, transformation stalls).
  • agency Agency
    The executive's sense that pursuing coaching is their own autonomous decision, not externally imposed.

Arguments

Subordinates can successfully influence senior executives to engage a coach without career risk if they use strategic framing. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Senior leaders receive diminishing candid feedback as they rise.
  • Direct suggestion of coaching triggers defensiveness due to power dynamics.
  • Reframing coaching around the executive's own pain points bypasses defensiveness.
  • Social proof from elite CEOs normalizes coaching.
  • Time-bound experiments lower commitment barriers.
  • Choosing the right messenger distributes political risk.
Implicit premises:
  • The subordinate has sufficient observational access to diagnose the leader's barriers and pain points.
  • The executive's self-interest in solving business problems is stronger than their identity protection when properly framed.
  • The subordinate has access to credible third-party messengers (HR, board members).
Inferences:
  • By matching the approach to the specific barrier type, the subordinate avoids backfire scenarios.
  • By restoring the executive's sense of agency, the subordinate transforms an imposed suggestion into a self-directed choice.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How can a subordinate suggest coaching to a senior executive without it seeming like criticism or insubordination?
    By anchoring the suggestion in the executive's own stated pain points, normalizing coaching as elite practice, restoring agency, choosing the right messenger, and offering a time-bound trial.
  • ✅ What are the main barriers to executive coaching adoption?
    Three categories: ego/identity protection, misconceptions about coaching as remedial, and time/overload.
  • ✅ When should a subordinate stop trying to influence upward on coaching?
    When signals appear: increasing irritation, exclusion from conversations, or deflective comments.
