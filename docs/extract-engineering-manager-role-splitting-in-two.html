The engineering manager role is splitting in two

Stephane Moreau · article · 2026-06-09 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

AI Labor DisruptionCareer DevelopmentCoordination CostHands-On Leadership & the PendulumHierarchy & Org ChartsInformal LeadershipOrganizational LegibilitySmall Teams & CompressionTechnical Leadership Track (IC / Staff-Plus Ladder)
The engineering-manager role is fracturing into two divergent futures — the code-adjacent tech lead manager and the non-technical multi-team EM — and because AI-shrunk teams are driving the split as an unplanned byproduct of flattening, engineers on the management path must diagnose which future their company is building and prepare accordingly rather than aiming at a single 'EM' destination.

practical · argumentative advice article (career strategy) — Descriptive in its trend analysis (why the EM role is fracturing) but its telos is action: the piece exists to tell engineers on the management path to diagnose which future their company is building and prepare accordingly. Prescriptive, not merely explanatory.

Key terms

  • Tech lead manager (TLM)
    The code-adjacent future of the EM role: a senior engineer who sets technical direction, still writes code every day, and carries only the lighter managerial load (1:1s, career conversations, project coordination) of a three-to-four-person team. Historically unstable per Larson, but rendered viable once shrinking teams shrink the managerial load.
  • Multi-team EM (the 'EM of 2030')
    The other future: an EM who manages many small teams across multiple product areas at high ratios trending toward Meta's 50-to-1, doing almost entirely non-technical work (cross-domain 1:1s, performance management, coordination, upward reporting) with shallow depth on any one team.
  • The glue (of the traditional team)
    The coordination, planning, and cohesion work a dedicated EM provided to a ~7-person team — the value that AI-empowered PMs (prototyping directly) plus one or two guardrail engineers dissolve, leaving 'what is the EM for?' as the open question.
  • Span of control / employee-to-manager ratio
    The axis along which the EM role splits: the research-favored ~7-to-1 versus Meta's extreme 50-to-1; a rising ratio past 10-to-1 is read as a signal a company is heading toward the multi-team EM model.
  • Flattening Organizational Legibility
    The reduction of management layers now underway — but happening as an unplanned byproduct of cost-driven layoffs and reorganizations rather than deliberate organizational design, which is why the future EM role is genuinely undecided.
  • EM level tiers (Associate EM / EM / Senior EM)
    A proposed restructuring where EM levels are defined by how many teams they oversee (AEM: 1-2 teams; EM: 3-5; Senior EM: more, or manages AEMs/EMs) — one of three mechanisms companies use to grow engineers into multi-team managers.

Arguments

Engineers on the management path should stop treating 'becoming an EM' as one destination and instead prepare for whichever of the two divergent EM futures their company is actually building. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • AI-augmented small teams and PM-driven prototyping dissolve the traditional team's glue, so the ~7-person-team EM role is being questioned.
  • Two divergent answers are emerging: the code-adjacent TLM and the widened, non-technical multi-team EM.
  • The two paths require opposite preparation (deepen technical judgment vs. practice domain-transferable people skills).
  • Most companies have not deliberately chosen, so the destination is genuinely uncertain.
Implicit premises:
  • The coordination/glue work that justified the traditional EM is being automated or devalued, not merely redistributed (load-bearing).
  • An org will settle toward one pole or the other; there is no stable third role to prepare for.
  • The reader's future belongs to the 'management path' — the pure technical-track / IC-leadership ladder is not treated as an option.
Inferences:
  • If preparation diverges and the outcome is company-dependent, a single generic 'become an EM' plan is dominated by a diagnose-then-prepare strategy under uncertainty.
The tech lead manager, historically judged unstable, becomes a viable model once AI shrinks teams. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Larson's argument that TLMs are unstable was based on traditional team sizes where both the technical and managerial jobs were full-time.
  • If a team shrinks to three engineers, the managerial load shrinks with it, so the attention-split becomes manageable.
Implicit premises:
  • Managerial load scales roughly with team size, so a three-person team's people-work is genuinely light.
  • AI-augmented small teams will be productive enough that companies actively choose them.
Inferences:
  • Therefore the attention-split that made the TLM unstable is small enough at tiny team sizes that one person can hold both jobs.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ If teams become that small, what is the engineering manager actually doing?
    Two divergent answers: the tech lead manager (stays close to the code, light management of a tiny team) or the multi-team EM (widens across many teams, near-entirely non-technical).
  • ✅ Which of these futures is my own company building toward?
    Offers a diagnostic — team-to-manager ratio trend, the profile of the last person promoted to EM, and whether leadership talks 'tech leads / player-coaches' — while conceding the answers will probably not point cleanly in one direction.
  • ✅ How does an engineer whose focus was technical delivery transition to being a pure people-manager of five teams?
    Three emerging possibilities: the role gets filled by professional managers from adjacent fields, companies build selective L&D programs, or companies create tiered EM levels (AEM/EM/Senior EM) by number of teams. Tentative, not settled.
  • ⭕ What about the engineer who wants technical leadership without the management path at all — the staff/principal/architect IC ladder who leads through technical judgment with no direct reports?
    Never asked. The piece scopes itself to 'engineers hoping to grow following the management path,' and even its code-adjacent option (the TLM) carries 1:1s and career conversations — it is still a manager. The pure technical-leadership ladder is silently collapsed into 'management' or omitted.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe two-way split erases the technical-track / IC-leadership ladder. The article scopes itself to 'the management path,' and even its code-adjacent option, the TLM, is defined by managerial load (1:1s, career conversations, project coordination) — i.e., it is still a manager. The staff → principal → distinguished ladder, where one leads through technical judgment and influence with no personnel responsibility ('leadership, not management'), is collapsed into 'management' or left out. For a reader on exactly that ladder, the dichotomy is a false binary: there is a third future — the technical leader who is neither a people-manager nor a coder-with-reports.
    Read next: Read against 'Developing Your Leadership Skills toward Principal Engineering' (the whole IC-leadership-ladder case), heis-12 (a principal engineer orchestrating ~40 architects with no personnel authority, forced into a non-coercive leadership style), and 'kill your org chart' (staff/principal engineers holding project leadership) — all of which name the technical-leadership track this piece omits.
  • incompleteThe binary is asserted, not shown to be exhaustive. The citations (Meta, Amazon, Gartner, Faros) establish that flattening is happening, not that it resolves into exactly these two EM archetypes rather than a spectrum, durable hybrids, or outright role dissolution. No evidence is offered that organizations must pick one; the intermediate case (a TLM who also carries some cross-team scope) is excluded by construction rather than by argument.
    Read next: Read against 'AI Won't Save the Kingdoms We Built' (flattening as politics and cost, with coordination burden re-emerging in a new shape rather than vanishing) and 'Your AI strategy has a trust problem' (flat high-autonomy pods as a third structural option), which suggest the fork is one framing among several.
  • incompleteThe TLM-viability claim rests on managerial load scaling down cleanly with team size, which is untested. Career conversations, performance management, hiring, and conflict do not obviously shrink linearly with headcount — a three-person team can still generate a full-time person-problem — so the attention-split Larson warned about may not actually become manageable just because the team is small.
    Read next: Seek span-of-control research on the composition (not just the count) of managerial load, and practitioner accounts of small-team TLMs, to test whether load really tracks headcount.
