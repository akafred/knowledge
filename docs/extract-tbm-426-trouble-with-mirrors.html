TBM 426: The Trouble With Mirrors

John Cutler from The Beautiful Mess · email · 2026-06-16 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Agent Governance (Internal)Hierarchy & Org ChartsMental Models & UnderstandingOrganizational LegibilityPsychological SafetyTrust
Visualizing how an organization works and 'embracing transparency' cannot by themselves improve it, because organizational truth is never a single neutral reflection but is positional, framed, and continuously negotiated among parties who each have something at stake in what gets seen — so the real need is not to be shown the mirror but to be mirrored: to have one's perspective recognized and held with care.

theoretical · Personal reflective essay (Substack newsletter) — a first-person revision of the author's own prior belief. — Predominantly theoretical: the backbone is a thesis about the nature of organizational truth — that it is positional, framed, and negotiated rather than a single objective fact to be reflected. It closes with a short practical/normative coda (question whether truth will be met with stewardship; shift from 'mirrors' to 'mirroring'), so it has a practical turn, but the essay is a reframing of how something IS, not a how-to.

Key terms

  • mirror Agent Governance (Internal)
    Any device or practice that reflects organizational reality back to a group — dashboards, visualization, transparency, 'radical honesty', 'clear-eyed' assessments — treated by its advocates as neutral truth-telling. Cutler's point is that no such mirror is neutral: every mirror has a frame, and the frame is a choice.
  • mirroring
    The relational act of helping someone feel their perspective is recognized, understood, and held with care without being weaponized against them — engaging some version of reality together. The counterpart and successor to 'mirrors': showing the truth versus helping people feel seen.
  • to be mirrored
    What people in organizations actually want: to have their experience recognized, their perspective matter, and some sense of control — to feel seen by someone they trust, rather than shown an abstract 'truth' that will be held against them.
  • negotiated truth
    In a collective there is no single truth to reveal; what gets foregrounded, backgrounded, acted on, or left alone is constantly negotiated based on power, incentive, timing, capacity, and a hundred other things — not on courage or cowardice. The 'truth' is not hidden or distorted; it is negotiated because it has to be.
  • transparency / radical honesty
    Often a performance and a bid for power rather than a pure good: whoever champions 'honesty' or 'clarity' gets to decide what counts as honest, what counts as political, and who is being 'difficult'. Candor is cheap for those already insulated from its consequences.
  • positional
    The belief in organizational 'mirrors' belongs to a specific organizational stratum — the middle layer of consultants, coaches, agile practitioners, and team leads, close enough to see the mess and caring enough to name it, but lacking the power or the cynicism to just play the game. It is a worldview, not universal wisdom.
  • the view from nowhere
    Borrowed from Thomas Nagel: the impossible objective standpoint. There is no neutral position from which an organization can be seen 'as it really is'; every reflection comes from a position, through a frame.
  • stewardship
    Whether surfaced truth is held with care and accountability, versus simply left 'out there, exposed, with no one accountable for what happens next'. The condition that should be checked before a mirror is held up.

Arguments

Visualization and transparency alone cannot reliably improve a team or organization. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Mirrors are not neutral; every mirror has a frame that is a choice (p2).
  • Seeing something clearly does not mean one is ready, safe, aligned, or empowered to act on it (p4).
  • Effective looking requires both high psychological safety and shared goals, not just safety (p6).
  • Organizational truth is negotiated among many parties, not simply revealed (p7, p8).
Implicit premises:
  • The binding constraint on organizational change is political and relational (motivation, safety, alignment, power, stewardship), not the availability of information.
  • Visualization/transparency programs implicitly assume the opposite — that information is the binding constraint, i.e. that if people could just see clearly, they would act.
Inferences:
  • If information is not the binding constraint, then supplying more and clearer information (better mirrors) leaves the true constraints untouched; therefore improved visibility alone cannot produce change.
The belief in organizational 'mirrors' is a positional worldview, and transparency advocacy is often a covert exercise of power. strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • The belief is concentrated in a specific stratum — the middle: consultants, coaches, agile practitioners, team leads (p9).
  • People at the top don't need the mirror (they arrange the lighting); those at the bottom have internalized that looking changes nothing (p9).
  • Whoever champions 'honesty' or 'clarity' gets to define what counts as honest, political, or 'difficult' (p10).
Implicit premises:
  • Neutral-sounding epistemic ideals (transparency, honesty) can encode and advance the interests of those who advocate them.
  • Venkatesh Rao's Gervais Principle stratification (Sociopaths / Clueless / Losers) maps validly onto real organizations.
Inferences:
  • If a belief is held mainly by one stratum and serves the definitional power of its advocates, then it is not universal wisdom but a positioned interest.
The right move is to shift from showing people the truth ('mirrors') to helping people feel seen and exploring reality together ('mirroring').
Explicit premises:
  • What people want is to be mirrored — recognized, understood, held with care (p11).
  • Truth should only be surfaced where it will be met with stewardship and accountability (p12).
  • Following Peter Block, the point is weaving and strengthening the fabric of community, not individual transformation.
Implicit premises:
  • Collective movement depends on relationship and shared meaning-making more than on accurate representation (p15).
  • Feeling seen and having a sense of control are prerequisites to people engaging with uncomfortable realities.
Inferences:
  • Since people resist un-stewarded truth and fundamentally want to be seen, an approach built on connection and joint exploration will succeed where impersonal transparency fails.

Narratives

  • Earlier in his career Cutler was 'smitten' by the idea that if teams could 'just' visualize how they work and embrace transparency, almost any team could improve — a belief kin to 'if we could just talk it out'. Numerous run-ins with 'reality' and 'the real world' revised but never fully dislodged it.
    → Establishes ethos and humility and frames the entire essay as a hard-won revision of the author's own naive belief — implicitly supporting p1 (the belief is seductive) and lending credibility to the critique that follows.
  • On the morning of his birthday, Cutler looks in the mirror and does not want to see the gray hair; he knows he 'should' want to open the messy closet to 'get real' with the mess, but he doesn't open it.
    → An analogical proof that even a simple one-party mirror is avoided — grounding p5 (we avoid seeing uncomfortable truths) and p4 (seeing does not equal acting).
  • A company whose honest odds of success were 'basically a coin flip', with a dozen substitutable competitors and nothing terribly differentiated, where it was better for everyone to 'add some frosting and sparkle to the mirror' until the job market improved.
    → Illustrates that obscuring the truth can be collectively rational, not merely cowardly — supporting p8 (truth is negotiated by incentive and timing) and p3 (truth is managed / weaponized).
  • Companies are like the funny room full of contorted mirrors: we shift slightly to look VERY BIG, then VERY SMALL, sometimes see 1,000 fractal images of ourselves, sometimes stare into the abyss.
    → A vivid image for the many-mirrors / distorted-perception claim — supporting p7 (there is no single mirror; reflections are partial and angled).
  • Venkatesh Rao's Gervais Principle: the people who want to hold up mirrors to the organization are the Clueless middle who genuinely believe the org is what it says it is; the Sociopaths at the top already know what's behind the mirror because they built it; the Losers at the bottom stopped looking long ago.
    → A borrowed framework marshaled to argue the mirror-belief is positional and stratified — supporting p9 (belief in mirrors is the worldview of the middle) and p10.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why doesn't visualizing how a team works and embracing transparency reliably make it improve?
    Because organizational truth is not a single neutral reflection but positional, framed, and negotiated among multiple parties with stakes — and because seeing something clearly does not make people ready, safe, aligned, or empowered to act on it.
  • ✅ What do people in organizations actually want, if not the truth reflected back at them?
    To be mirrored — to have their perspective recognized, understood, and held with care by someone they trust, which can look like the opposite of transparency.
  • ✅ Whose belief is 'organizational truth', and who benefits from calls for transparency?
    It is the worldview of a middle organizational stratum (consultants, coaches, agile practitioners, team leads); the top don't need the mirror and the bottom have stopped looking, and transparency advocacy is often a bid for definitional power.
  • ⭕ Does mirroring actually deliver the operational improvements that mirrors were supposed to produce, or does it trade truth-seeking for comfort?
    Left open. Cutler pivots to relationship and being-seen but does not show how feeling seen surfaces and resolves the hard elephants — the coin-flip odds, the org-chart vestiges — that the mirror was meant to confront.

Gaps

  • incompleteCutler shows that mirrors alone don't produce change and pivots to 'mirroring', but never shows how mirroring actually surfaces and resolves the hard, uncomfortable realities (the coin-flip odds, the org-chart vestiges) that mirrors were meant to confront. Without that, 'helping people feel seen' risks becoming a sophisticated way to avoid the elephants — comfort substituting for change, and the resolution quietly undermining the very problem it set out to solve.
    Read next: Read Amy Edmondson on psychological safety as enabling candor (not merely comfort); Kegan & Lahey, Immunity to Change, on how meaning-making translates into action; and Peter Block's Community in full, alongside Cutler's own operating-systems / legibility work (TBM 414, the visibility guide) for the mechanism from being-seen to actually confronting the hard things.
  • incompleteThe relationship between mirroring and accountability is left unresolved. Cutler insists surfaced truth must be met with 'stewardship' and asks 'who is accountable for what happens next', yet the mirroring resolution emphasizes feeling seen and NOT being 'held against' people — potentially in tension with the accountability that acting on uncomfortable truths requires.
    Read next: Read on accountability cultures (Allen Holub) and the distinction between blame and accountability; Cutler's own writing on directly-responsible individuals; and restorative-vs-punitive framings of organizational feedback.
  • incompleteThe cynical, power-analytic read of transparency advocates ('actually about power'; candor is cheap for the already-won) is asserted without evidence and, as Cutler half-concedes ('I realize all of this sounds negative'), risks proving too much: by the same logic his own mirroring prescription is equally positional and equally a bid to define what good relating looks like.
    Read next: Read Langdon Winner, 'Do Artifacts Have Politics?', and critical-management studies for the rigorous version of the power-in-ideals claim, and test whether the argument self-undermines when turned on the author's own prescription.
  • incompleteNo account of mechanism or scale. Cutler's clearest example of mirroring is a two-party coaching dyad (you, the coach, the scale), but his own solo-vs-collective distinction makes the collective the hard case. How a leader practically moves a whole team or organization from mirrors to mirroring — how many people 'draw the map side by side' at once — is not addressed.
    Read next: Read facilitation and large-group-intervention literature (Open Space, liberating structures) and Peter Block's Community for community-scale practices that operationalize joint meaning-making beyond the dyad.
