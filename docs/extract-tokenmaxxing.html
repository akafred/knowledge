Agentics / Tech Things: Tokenmaxxing is dead, long live tokenmaxxing

theahura · article · 2026-06-27 source 🖥️

Agentic LoopAI Unit EconomicsEnterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessLong-Running & Managed AgentsOpen-Weight ModelsToken ThroughputUsage Limits & Rationing
Tokenmaxxing — deliberately spending large amounts of AI inference tokens — was born as a purposeful blunt-force tactic to force AI adoption past resistant employees and died once vendors withdrew subsidies and raised prices, but it will be reborn on genuine-ROI footing because a new 'compounding correctness' regime (more tokens now reliably yield better results, making unsupervised 24/7 agent loops worth running) plus cheap open models run in loops make maximal token spend economically rational rather than mere incentive-gaming.

mixed (theoretical-explanatory/predictive with a thin practical-advisory layer) · argumentative opinion essay (contrarian) plus an appended 'Tech Things' news roundup — The dominant mode is explanatory and predictive — why deliberate token burn rose, why it died, why it returns — not a how-to. The practical payload (use generalist platforms, run cheap open models, avoid provider lock-in, don't build brittle single-task agents) is real but thin and partly self-promotional (the author runs the Nori/Agentics AI-adoption community). Snarky register; the argument is serious. Scope is strictly inference / agentic-loop token spend, NOT training or RL compute.

Key terms

  • tokenmaxxing Token Throughput
    Deliberately maximizing AI inference-token consumption as a policy or strategy — spending (or encouraging spend of) large sums on tokens as an end or a forcing function, not merely as a byproduct of doing work. The author splits it two ways: temporally (v1 = subsidy-era incentive-gaming to force adoption, now dead; v2 = ROI-driven maximal spend justified by compounding correctness, being reborn) and by target ('tokens for your developers' via Claude Code + loops, vs 'tokens for your pipelines' via brittle custom agents).
  • compounding correctness
    The claimed current regime in which the more tokens you spend iterating on a task, the more likely a good outcome — errors get corrected rather than accumulating. Explicitly about inference-time / agentic-loop spend (running an agent longer, restarting the prompt), not training or RL.
  • compounding error
    The prior regime: small model errors and hallucinations 'take on a life of their own' over long autonomous runs and become irreversibly embedded, so running agents longer produces worse work and there is no point spending more tokens.
  • loop (Ralph Wiggum loop)
    Run an agent to the end of its turn, then automatically restart the same prompt; split a heavy spec into parts and solve it over iterations with no human supervision. Old (since ~last July) but formerly hard to tune; now 'easy' because compounding correctness lets even sloppy prompting improve each iteration.
  • software factory / dark factory
    A codebase that autonomously writes, reviews, tests and fixes code from a human-supplied spec with no supervision (spec in -> app out). The 'dark factory' is the lights-out extreme.

Arguments

The first wave of tokenmaxxing is dead. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Aggregate token spend rose as the adoption-forcing policies succeeded (everyone now at least uses Cursor in the sidebar).
  • OpenAI and Anthropic, going public, throttled subscriptions and raised API prices, so token subsidies are vanishing.
  • With incentives gone and cost up, teams are rolling back unlimited-token-spend policies.
Implicit premises:
  • Businesses spend only for ROI (the opening premise).
  • The first wave was justified only by the adoption-forcing incentive plus the subsidy, not by intrinsic per-task ROI.
Inferences:
  • Not drawn: the tactic cannibalized its own economic basis — tokenmaxxing's SUCCESS at forcing adoption drove aggregate demand up, which (with IPO pressure) drove the subsidy withdrawal and price hikes that killed it. The author implies each link but never names the self-defeating feedback loop.
Tokenmaxxing will be reborn long-term, on genuine-ROI footing. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • We've entered a 'compounding correctness' regime where more token spend generally yields better results.
  • Loops make unsupervised long-running spend easy and are the current focus of excitement.
  • The AISI/Mythos security runs showed no diminishing returns across tested token budgets ('pay more to win').
  • Cutting-edge teams are already building 24/7 agent infrastructure; the latent incentive awaits diffusion.
Implicit premises:
  • Compounding correctness is real and general, not confined to security exploit discovery.
  • 'More tokens -> better output' translates into net-positive ROI (value exceeds the now-unsubsidized cost).
  • The trend within the tested budget range extrapolates to open-ended spend.
Inferences:
  • Not drawn: 'no diminishing returns in the tested range' does not establish that no ceiling exists.
  • Not drawn: the author's own commercial interest aligns with the pro-spend conclusion.
  • Not drawn: if compounding correctness is real, it further erodes frontier-lab pricing power — reinforcing the very cost pressure that killed v1 and pushing all value to open models.
Open-model platforms are the real winners of the tokenmaxxing rebirth. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Cost-per-iteration math: a ~5x-cheaper model at 1.05x/iter run 5x more beats a 1.1x/iter model.
  • Frontier-lab token burn will never survive CFO scrutiny.
  • Open models (GLM 5.2) are approaching frontier quality at a fraction of the price and sometimes beat GPT-5.5 on benchmarks.
Implicit premises:
  • Per-iteration quality ratios roughly hold and iterations are independently additive toward a better outcome.
  • Loops (repeatable, restartable spend) are the dominant consumption mode.
Inferences:
  • Consumers should avoid provider lock-in by adopting tools that sit on top of all major players (stated as advice).
Pipeline-style tokenmaxxing (custom single-task agents) is genuine waste, unlike developer-side tokenmaxxing. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Relying on an agent for a fixed task is more expensive than deterministic workflow automation.
  • These agents were never as correct as a deterministic pipeline, so none took off.
  • Adding checker-agents-on-checker-agents multiplies token cost without fixing accuracy.
  • Generalist platforms skinned per task now do it better than a bespoke agent.
Implicit premises:
  • Correctness is the binding requirement for these data-processing tasks.
  • A deterministic implementation is available for them.
Inferences:
  • Not integrated: whether compounding correctness (which supposedly rescues loops) also rescues these pipelines, or whether the generalist-platform shift moots custom pipelines regardless — the two rebirth stories run in parallel and are never reconciled.

Narratives

  • Meta tied performance evaluations to per-person token usage; employees gamed it by having two agents talk to each other all day to inflate token counts.
    → Canonical example of tokenmaxxing-as-incentive-gaming; grounds the claim that it was deliberate and that a consumption metric predictably gets gamed (Goodhart).
  • AISI budgeted 100M tokens per Mythos attempt (~$12,500 each, ~$125k for ten runs); none of the models showed diminishing returns across the tested budgets — security reduces to a 'proof of work' where you win by paying more, not by being clever.
    → The single quantified data point for 'more tokens = better'; carries the entire 'compounding correctness' claim — but only in the security-exploit-discovery domain.
  • Boris Cherny, creator of Claude Code, said 'loops' on stage and everyone freaked out — though loops (the 'Ralph Wiggum loop') have existed since ~last July.
    → Illustrates that the rebirth vehicle is not new capability but newly-easy execution; supports the 'loops are the operational face of tokenmaxxing v2' point.
  • If Claude gives 1.1x improvement per iteration and GLM 5.2 gives 1.05x at ~5x less cost, you run the cheaper loop 5x more and end up better; GLM 5.2 (~$1.4/$4 per M) undercuts Opus 4.X ($5/$25) and even beats Haiku 4.5.
    → Concrete cost-per-outcome economics that make open models 'the real winners'; the essay's most data-anchored argument.
  • A buyer asks a consultant to 'build me an agent'; the consultant essentially writes a skill file and charges $2m.
    → Illustrates market arbitrage / buyer ignorance in pipeline tokenmaxxing — value extracted from not knowing generalist agents already suffice.
  • StrongDM's software factory pushes engineers toward spending $1000 in tokens per day; the author's own factory spends ~$600/month.
    → Hype-vs-reality anchor — the author polices an inflated claim with his own real number while conceding a 'kernel of truth.' Exactly the rigor a skeptical reader values.
  • Executives saw custom-agent building, decided 'oh, this is just a more flexible Zapier workflow,' and demanded non-deterministic 'agentic' pipelines for one-off tasks (e.g. 'I need data labeling, so I'll build a data-labeling agent') instead of deterministic code.
    → Names the category error behind pipeline tokenmaxxing — using probabilistic agents where deterministic automation is cheaper and more correct.
  • Roundup: GPT-5.6 (Sol/Terra/Luna) and Mythos are being released only via opaque government-gated 'trusted partner' processes; Cerebras serves ~750 tok/s.
    → Context notes — government picking winners/losers, and the aside that very fast inference could revert async agent operation back to synchronous use. Loosely coupled to the main argument.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Was tokenmaxxing an accident by dumb executives, or deliberate?
    Deliberate — a purposeful blunt-force change-management tactic to force AI adoption past resistant senior staff.
  • ✅ Is tokenmaxxing dead?
    The first (subsidy-era, incentive-gaming) wave is dead; a second, ROI-driven wave is coming — 'dead, but we haven't seen the last of it.'
  • ✅ What changed to make maximal token spend worthwhile again?
    A shift from 'compounding error' to 'compounding correctness' plus easy loops and cheap open models — though WHY the shift happened (the technical mechanism) is left unexplained.
  • ✅ Who captures the value of the tokenmaxxing rebirth?
    Open-model platforms win on cost-per-outcome; the frontier labs (whose price hikes caused the death) lose under CFO scrutiny.
  • ⭕ Does developer-side tokenmaxxing actually deliver net-positive ROI (value > now-unsubsidized cost)?
    Never quantified — asserted as 'ostensibly' good. Cost is measured precisely; value is only adjectival.
  • ⭕ Is 'compounding correctness' an established general fact or a vendor/consultant-convenient narrative the author has a stake in?
    Not examined; the author sells AI adoption and never discloses that the pro-spend thesis benefits him.
  • ⭕ If inference gets very fast (Cerebras ~750 tok/s), do we revert from async agents to synchronous operation?
    Raised as an open possibility in the roundup, with a demo pointer (chatjimmy.ai), but not argued.

Gaps

  • incompleteNo general evidence for 'compounding correctness.' The single data point (AISI/Mythos: no diminishing returns across tested budgets) is narrow security exploit-discovery, where raw compute plausibly substitutes for correctness in a way it may not for open-ended coding loops. The regime-shift claim ('spending more tokens generally results in better results') is generalized from that one benchmark and from vibes.
    Read next: Look for benchmark data on whether iterative agent loops monotonically improve on general coding/knowledge tasks versus plateau or degrade; read the test-time / inference-time compute scaling literature (o-series scaling curves) and any controlled loop-vs-single-shot comparisons.
  • incompleteCost is quantified, value is asserted. Every number in the piece is a cost (token prices, $12.5k/attempt, $600/month, $1000/day); the productivity or quality VALUE that would justify maximal spend is never measured, only hedged ('ostensibly'). The essay therefore cannot tell marginal from transformative benefit.
    Read next: Read HBR's 'How People Are Really Using AI in 2026' / the 'workslop'/'thinkslop' findings (marginal-not-game-changing benefits) and controlled productivity studies; ask whether the dev-loop value curve is transformative or merely marginal against unsubsidized cost.
  • incompleteNo mechanism for the regime shift. The author asserts 'That's no longer true. We've entered a different regime' but never explains what technically flipped compounding error into compounding correctness — better base models? harness-level self-verification? evals in the loop? RL-from-verifiable-rewards? The rebirth's causal engine is a black box.
    Read next: Read on agent self-verification and eval-in-the-loop harness design, RLVR / verifier-based training, and what actually reduces error accumulation in long runs; distinguish model-capability gains from harness/scaffolding gains.
  • incompleteVendor-vs-user value and the author's own incentive alignment are unexamined. 'More tokens = better' is exactly the narrative that benefits whoever sells tokens or AI-adoption services — and the author runs such a service, soliciting business throughout ('reach out, we can help!'). Who bears the cost (users, post-subsidy) versus who captures the value (vendors, consultants) is never separated, and the conflict is undisclosed.
    Read next: Read critically for supply-push vs demand-pull: who profits from maximal token consumption; compare against sources arguing 'most effective users are not the heaviest users'; watch for the token-seller framing in any pro-spend argument.
  • incompleteThe two-kinds distinction is not integrated with the rebirth. The author says compounding correctness rescues developer loops but that pipeline agents are waste; he never reconciles whether compounding correctness would also rescue pipelines, or whether the generalist-platform shift moots custom pipelines regardless. The two 'this too shall pass' stories run in parallel without a unifying account.
    Read next: Trace whether reliability gains (compounding correctness) and consolidation gains (generalist platforms replacing bespoke agents) are the same phenomenon or two; read on why deterministic pipelines still beat agents for fixed high-accuracy tasks.
