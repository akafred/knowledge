No, AI Did Not Kill Agile

Andrea Laforgia · email · 2026-04-00 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Agile & XPCode Was Never the BottleneckProduct Discovery & ExperimentationSpec-Driven Development
AI accelerates building software but does not reduce the epistemic uncertainty about what to build, so the iterative learning loop at the core of Agile becomes more important, not less, when coding is nearly free.

practical · argumentative — The text argues that practitioners and decision-makers should continue (and intensify) iterative, learning-driven development rather than adopt spec-first AI workflows. It is practical because the ultimate claim is 'you should keep iterating' — a prescription for action.

Key terms

  • knowledge problem Code Was Never the Bottleneck
    The irreducible uncertainty about what software to build — requirements that cannot be known in advance and must be discovered through user interaction with working software
  • speed problem Code Was Never the Bottleneck
    The constraint of how fast code can be turned from idea into working software — the problem AI actually solves
  • semantic diffusion Agile & XP
    Fowler's concept: how Agile's original meaning was diluted as it spread, leading to 'faux-agile' — ceremonies without the adaptive principles
  • sedimentary spec Spec-Driven Development
    A specification document that accumulates layers of decisions over time, burying wrong assumptions under refinements so they are never re-examined
  • validated learning Agile & XP
    Ries's concept: a rigorous method for demonstrating progress under extreme uncertainty by building, measuring, and learning from real user behavior

Arguments

AI does not make Agile obsolete because Agile addresses a problem (knowledge/requirements uncertainty) that AI does not solve. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The 'AI killed Agile' claim is based on AI's coding speed advantage
  • Agile was created to address requirements uncertainty, not coding speed
  • 40-60% of software failures originate in requirements, not implementation speed
  • Only about 20% of features deliver their intended positive impact
Implicit premises:
  • A methodology should be judged by its relevance to the problem it was designed to solve, not by how it interacts with tools for a different problem
  • Requirements uncertainty is a permanent feature of software development, not a temporary limitation of current technology
Inferences:
  • If requirements uncertainty is permanent and irreducible, then no amount of technological acceleration will ever make iterative discovery obsolete
  • The proportion of failures attributable to requirements (vs. implementation) will likely increase as AI makes implementation cheaper and faster
Cheaper building should lead to more iteration, not less. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • AI collapses the Build phase from weeks to hours
  • The Build-Measure-Learn loop is the mechanism for reducing uncertainty
  • Kent Beck recommends experimenting with approaches previously deemed too expensive
Implicit premises:
  • When a constraint is relaxed, rational actors exploit the freed capacity rather than abandoning the activity
  • The Build-Measure-Learn loop's value was always limited primarily by the cost of the Build phase
Inferences:
  • Teams that use AI to eliminate iteration rather than increase it are making a choice driven by organizational preference, not by logic
Spec-driven AI development recreates waterfall's failure mode. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Comprehensive specs accumulate assumptions that go unexamined ('sedimentary')
  • Winston Royce himself warned the waterfall pattern he described was 'risky and invites failure'
  • The FBI Virtual Case File failed due to specs that couldn't keep up with organizational learning
  • Healthcare.gov failed despite massive investment because of frozen requirements
Implicit premises:
  • The medium of the specification (natural language prompt vs. formal document) does not change the fundamental dynamic of prediction vs. discovery
  • Historical patterns of failure from specification-first approaches will repeat in AI-assisted contexts because the root cause (epistemic, not technological) is unchanged
Inferences:
  • AI-generated code from wrong specs will fail faster and more expensively because the speed of generation removes the natural brake that gave teams time to notice they were building the wrong thing
The 'AI killed Agile' narrative is politically motivated — it serves management's desire for control over an uncertain process. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Agile became bureaucratic partly because management wanted predictability
  • The spec-first pitch appeals to stakeholders who want to define requirements and hand off execution
  • Cockburn's four imperatives (Collaborate, Deliver, Reflect, Improve) each check the tendency to plan in isolation
Implicit premises:
  • Organizational power structures reliably favor approaches that centralize decision-making, regardless of whether those approaches work
  • Technology narratives are often adopted or rejected based on political convenience rather than technical merit
Inferences:
  • The debate about AI and Agile may be irresolvable on technical grounds because the real disagreement is about organizational power, not methodology

Narratives

  • The Snowbird meeting (February 2001): seventeen practitioners from different methodologies united against heavyweight processes
    → Establishes Agile's original purpose as addressing uncertainty, not speed — grounding the author's reframing in historical fact
  • The FBI Virtual Case File: $170 million project killed by specifications that couldn't keep up with organizational learning
    → Demonstrates that spec-first failure is not hypothetical — it has destroyed major projects, and the root cause (specification rigidity) is exactly what AI-spec workflows would reproduce
  • Healthcare.gov: $2.1 billion spent, six users on launch day, months building against frozen wrong requirements
    → The most dramatic failure case — shows that even massive resources cannot compensate for refusing to learn incrementally
  • Winston Royce's 1970 paper: the 'inventor' of waterfall actually warned it would fail and recommended iterating at least twice
    → Rhetorical judo — uses the authority of waterfall's originator against those advocating spec-first AI development
  • The performative sprint planning meeting, the status-report standup, the retrospective that changed nothing
    → Concedes the legitimate frustration behind the 'AI killed Agile' argument, building credibility before dismantling the conclusion

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Has AI made Agile obsolete?
    No — AI solves the speed problem but Agile addresses the knowledge problem, which AI does not touch. Agile's iterative learning loop is more relevant, not less, when building is nearly free.
  • ✅ Can rapid spec iteration with AI serve as a substitute for Agile iteration?
    Only if the spec is treated as a disposable hypothesis. When the spec becomes a comprehensive, version-controlled artifact, it recreates waterfall's failure mode regardless of iteration speed.
  • ✅ Why are so many people receptive to the 'AI killed Agile' narrative?
    Two reasons: legitimate frustration with faux-agile (ceremonies without principles), and the political appeal of returning to top-down specification for management.
  • ⭕ How should Agile practices change in light of AI capabilities?
    The author says Agile practices 'should evolve' and quotes the Manifesto's commitment to uncovering better ways, but offers no specific prescriptions for what evolved practices look like.
  • ⭕ What happens to the role of software engineers when building becomes nearly free?
    The author implies the bottleneck shifts to Measure and Learn, but does not explore what this means for engineering roles, skills, or team structures.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe author argues that Measure and Learn remain expensive and irreducibly human, but does not explore whether AI might also transform these phases — e.g., AI-driven user behavior analysis, automated A/B testing at scale, or AI-assisted synthesis of qualitative feedback. The claim that learning is irreducibly human is asserted, not defended.
    Read next: Literature on AI-augmented product discovery, automated experimentation platforms (e.g., Eppo, Statsig), and whether AI can reduce the cost of the Measure/Learn phases
  • incompleteThe article never engages with domains where requirements genuinely are well-understood upfront (e.g., regulatory compliance, protocol implementations, mathematical computations). The knowledge-problem framing may be less applicable in these contexts, where spec-first AI development might be entirely appropriate.
    Read next: Cynefin framework (Dave Snowden) for distinguishing simple/complicated/complex/chaotic domains; literature on when waterfall-like approaches are actually appropriate
  • incompleteThe author prescribes more iteration but does not address what 'iteration' looks like when AI can generate full applications in minutes. Traditional sprint-based iteration may indeed be too slow as a container — the article dismisses the ceremony but doesn't propose what replaces it.
    Read next: Kent Beck's recent work on AI-era development practices; continuous deployment and feature flagging literature; vibe coding workflows
  • incompleteThe political analysis of Section 5 identifies management's desire for control but does not explore whether AI might enable new forms of collaboration that transcend the old management-vs-engineering power dynamic (e.g., non-technical stakeholders directly prototyping with AI, collapsing the specification-to-implementation gap).
    Read next: Literature on no-code/low-code democratization; end-user development; how AI changes the relationship between domain experts and software creation
