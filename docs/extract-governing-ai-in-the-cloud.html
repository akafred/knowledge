Governing AI in the Cloud: A Practical Guide for Architects

Dave Ward · rss · 2026-06-15 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Agent Governance (Internal)Agent SecurityAI Regulation & Governance (External)Enterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessGolden PathGuardrailsPlatform EngineeringPolicy-as-Code
Architects should regain control of ungoverned ('shadow') AI not by trying to police the models themselves but by building a staged, automated control plane in the cloud infrastructure — discover every AI touchpoint, classify data at the moment of creation, and enforce access at the data path through IAM and policy-as-code — while making the compliant path the easiest one so teams adopt governance instead of routing around it.

practical · Prescriptive practitioner guide (InfoQ engineering article, part of the 'Securing the AI Stack' series) — a staged, rules-and-mechanisms how-to for cloud/security architects. — Adler practical work: it tells the architect what to do and how, structured as a sequential implementation path (discover, classify, enforce, make-easy, operate) with concrete code and cloud-service recipes. Its recommendations are load-bearing on a distinctive architectural stance about WHERE control should sit — the data path, not the model.

Key terms

  • Shadow AI
    The unapproved and unknown AI integrations already running in an organization's estate — consumer chatbots, IDE copilots, a weekend LangChain PoC that drifted into a customer journey, self-hosted models — that widen the attack surface; the governance problem is framed not as whether AI is present but as how many instances you cannot see.
  • Classify at creation / classification at write time
    Applying sensitivity and AIApproved classification tags to every object at the instant it is created — event-driven and synchronous (e.g. an S3 PutObject trigger invoking Comprehend, tagging or quarantining within seconds) — so no data ever sits untagged; contrasted with overnight batch scanning that leaves an exposure window.
  • IAM-based enforcement / block the data path
    Turning passive classification tags into active, deny-by-default access controls that decide which identity may reach which data — controlling the data path rather than the AI models themselves, on the explicit premise that modern ML architectures are too distributed and ephemeral to control at the model level.
  • Policy-as-code Policy-as-Code
    Governance rules expressed as executable, version-controlled, testable code (OPA/Rego, AWS Cedar, HashiCorp Sentinel; Kyverno/OPA-Gatekeeper for admission control) that evaluate contextual conditions IAM cannot express — data age, model-registration status, environment, break-glass — at runtime, per request, and are unit-tested in CI/CD like application code.
  • Model registry
    A single source of truth for every production model — tracking training-data lineage, security-scan results, approval chains, monitoring config and incident history — realized as Kubernetes CRDs that admission controllers read to enforce governance at runtime; adoption depends on it delivering operational value (deploys, rollback, monitoring), not on being a compliance checkbox.
  • AI governance (as delivery-pipeline discipline) AI Regulation & Governance (External)
    The engineering discipline of making safe AI deployment the default path — visibility plus classification plus enforcement plus policy-as-code plus telemetry woven into normal delivery — run as an internal product for engineering customers, rather than an after-the-fact audit checkpoint.

Arguments

Enforcement should sit at the data path (IAM plus policy-as-code), not at the AI models themselves. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Modern ML architectures involve multiple services, containers and ephemeral compute, making model-level controls impractical.
  • Every object can be classified with tags at creation, and IAM/OPA can gate access to it per request.
  • Deny-by-default IAM with explicit deny, VPC-endpoint binding and org-level SCPs makes the data path a hard chokepoint that even privileged users cannot bypass.
Implicit premises:
  • Controlling which data reaches a model is a sufficient proxy for governing AI risk — the risky thing about AI is primarily the data it ingests, not the model's behavior or outputs.
  • The sensitive data flows worth governing are mediated by cloud storage and IAM-controlled paths, rather than by direct prompts, browser sessions, unmanaged endpoints or already-trained model weights.
  • Inference-time exfiltration and prompt injection are either out of scope or handled elsewhere.
Inferences:
  • Therefore place the control where identity, network and policy can be enforced deterministically — the data path — and let the models remain uncontrolled black boxes downstream.
Governance must make the compliant path the easiest path (golden path / governance-as-product), or teams will route around it. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Security controls fail when they become obstacles to productivity.
  • Developers under deadline pressure choose the simplest path, using personal accounts and shadow IT when the secure path adds friction.
  • Wrapping compliance in default tooling (a SecureS3Client that tags and encrypts transparently) and grading approvals by risk makes the secure path the fastest path.
Implicit premises:
  • Developer behavior, not control strength, is the binding constraint on real-world governance — controls that people bypass provide no protection.
  • The organization actually has the platform capacity to build and maintain golden-path tooling and keep it good enough to win on convenience.
Inferences:
  • Therefore invest in developer experience, paved-road tooling and risk-based approvals so the secure option is also the lazy option.
Governance must start with multi-source discovery, because you cannot govern what you cannot see. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Most organizations do not know how many AI integrations are already running.
  • CASB, service mesh and API-gateway signals each reveal a different slice of AI usage and each has blind spots.
  • Visibility is not the same as governance, but it is the precondition for it.
Implicit premises:
  • Consequential AI usage leaves an observable signal in infrastructure telemetry — CASB, mesh or gateway.
  • The inventory can be kept current as new AI touchpoints land, not just captured once.
Inferences:
  • Therefore sweep with CASB, mesh and gateway, centralize into a SIEM, and build one dashboard answering 'where are we actually using AI, and which paths are approved'.

    → Serves as documented evidence that the widened AI attack surface is already being exploited, grounding the urgency of the guide in real incidents rather than hypotheticals.
  • 'Given a choice between a complex secure path and a simple insecure path, developers under pressure will choose simplicity... The secure option becomes the lazy option, and lazy wins every time.'
    → Crystallizes the golden-path thesis into a memorable principle about developer psychology — the reason governance must compete on convenience rather than mandate.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How do I find the AI running in my estate that I do not know about?
    Multi-source discovery — CASB sweeps for public-provider calls, service-mesh telemetry for self-hosted in-cluster models, and API-gateway logs as a choke point — centralized into a SIEM and one dashboard that answers where AI is used and which paths are approved.
  • ✅ Where should the control point for AI governance sit?
    At the data path — IAM plus policy-as-code gating which identity may reach which classified data — rather than at the models, which are too distributed and ephemeral to control directly.
  • ✅ How do I stop sensitive data from reaching AI models?
    Classify data at creation (sensitivity + AIApproved tags), then enforce with deny-by-default IAM, AIApproved gating, VPC endpoints and SCPs, backed by policy-as-code for contextual rules.
  • ✅ How do I get teams to follow governance instead of routing around it?
    Make the secure path the easiest path — golden-path tooling that tags and encrypts by default, risk-based graduated approvals, a model registry that delivers operational value, and governance run as an internal product.
  • ✅ How do I keep governance from becoming a delivery bottleneck?
    Graduated risk-based approvals (auto / auto-with-scan / human), policy-as-code tested in CI/CD, and treating governance violations as production incidents rather than quarterly audit findings.

The residual shadow AI that evades all three signals (personal-device, off-corporate-network browser sessions) is never addressed, leaving the largest slice of shadow AI outside the map.

  • incompleteThe 'block the data path, not the model' thesis governs what data reaches a model but leaves inference-time and output-side risk uncontrolled. The article concedes that models 'can exfiltrate entire databases through inference APIs if improperly secured' and never returns to it — prompt injection, model-output filtering, and egress through inference sit outside the data-path frame yet are where much AI-specific risk lives.
    Read next: Read alongside agent-security / lethal-trifecta and runtime output/egress controls to cover inference-time and behavioral risk.
  • incompleteThe EU AI Act and data-residency/sovereignty dimension is absent. Classification tags list a 'GDPR,HIPAA' ComplianceScope and customer data gets a 90-day retention window (a right-to-be-forgotten nod), but the article never engages EU AI Act obligations (risk classification, human oversight, conformity assessment, post-market monitoring) or where AI processing physically occurs and how data crosses borders — the deciding questions for a regulated European telco or bank.

  • incompleteThe scheme is AWS-centric — Macie, Comprehend, S3 object tagging, IAM condition keys, SCPs, VPC endpoints — with only passing mention of Azure Purview and Google DLP. The article asserts the pattern is provider-agnostic but never shows the load-bearing enforcement primitives (tag-conditioned deny, SCP-equivalent org guardrails, VPC-endpoint gating) on Azure or GCP, where a European enterprise avoiding US-hyperscaler lock-in may actually sit.

  • incompleteThe organizational thesis — 'technology is the easy part; the hard part is security, engineering and product sharing ownership' — is asserted in the closing but given no mechanism. How ownership is actually split, who arbitrates the leak-versus-block tuning of OPA rules, and how the cross-functional operating model is stood up are all left open, despite the article naming this as the genuinely hard problem.
