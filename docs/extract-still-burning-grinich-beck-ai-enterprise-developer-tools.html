We are not in another technology hype wave but in a step-function transformation: the marginal cost of producing software is collapsing toward zero, large incumbents are adopting AI faster than any prior shift, and the whole ecosystem — not just the AI labs — is accelerating; in this 'Red Queen' regime, profit and productivity may not visibly improve because every competitor is running at the new tempo, so the only sane response is to paddle straight into the wave by sustaining the maker's compulsion (the 'itchy brain'), reshaping engineering leadership toward hands-on impact, and accepting that software engineering as a profession will resemble medicine or law more than the unique 2010s template that minted six-figure undergrads.

mixed · mixed — A long-form podcast conversation between an experienced craft thinker (Beck) and an enterprise-infrastructure founder (Grinich). Practical in its diagnoses (what AI is doing to engineering management, what 'enterprise ready' means, how big companies are adopting) and theoretical in its framing (the Red Queen evolutionary analogy, the marginal cost of software approaching zero, software engineering as a historically anomalous profession). Form is mixed: expository (WorkOS's vantage point, Grinich's biography), argumentative (why this AI moment is a step function, not just acceleration), and narrative (the Himalayan climber-Sherpa story, Beck's Gusto payroll example, Grinich's 18-month founder gap-year, Beck's reflection on JUnit and scaling). The conversation does not have a single explicit thesis; the joint thesis is constructed across both speakers.

Key terms

  • Red Queen (evolutionary theory) Industrialization of AI
    Grinich's framing device, drawn from evolutionary biology via Alice in Wonderland: every species is already at its survival limit; everyone runs as fast as they can just to stay in place. Applied to AI adoption, it means the whole ecosystem will accelerate together, so profit and productivity gains may be invisible at the company level — falling behind means falling away.
  • marginal cost of (creating) software → zero Near-Zero-Cost Code
    Grinich's claim that the cost of writing code has 'gone essentially to zero' with coding agents. Specifically the marginal cost of producing one more unit of software output, not the total cost of operating software systems. Beck immediately problematizes this by pointing to operation cost, error rate, and downstream business cost.
  • itchy brain Emotional Impact of AI Work
    Beck's name for the felt phenomenon: an idea or thought lodges in your head and the discomfort does not abate until you have made the thing in the world. Beck frames it as 'a bit more selfish' than Grinich's connect-with-people version; Grinich calls it 'a compulsion,' shared by long-distance founders as 'the same kind of mental disorder.' The durable version of the maker drive — opposed to external-validation chasing, which burns out.
  • picks and shovels
    Beck's gold-rush framing for WorkOS's position: by selling infrastructure to everyone in the AI gold rush, Grinich gets to see what everybody is actually building, not just what gets press. Used to credential the across-the-ecosystem vantage claim.
  • enterprise ready Enterprise AI Adoption & Readiness
    The bundle of capabilities a SaaS product needs to sell to large customers: SSO/SAML authentication, SCIM provisioning, access control, role-based permissions, audit logging. 'Not the fun stuff to build,' but required to grow up-market. The gap between 'running system' and 'mature fully running system.'
  • explore / expand / extract (3 X's) Explore / Expand / Extract
    Beck's phase model: explore for product-market fit; expand as fast as possible once found; extract capital from what you've built to feed the next thing. Each phase has incompatible skill demands and tradeoffs. People retcon the expand phase into either inevitability or near-disaster.
  • industrialization phase (of AI) Industrialization of AI
    Grinich's analogy: every culture going through industrialization burns coal, pollutes rivers, runs without baselines, then cleans up. We are in the dirty-transformation phase of AI — token budgets blown with no priors, money spent without optimization — and that is appropriate. Optimization comes after the baseline.
  • step function shift (vs. acceleration) Industrialization of AI
    Grinich's claim that people are misreading the moment: they think AI is making things faster, when in fact the regime has changed. Acceleration is more of the same; step function means a different surface entirely. The 'goal for all these companies should be to get to the other side of the change as fast as you can.'
  • agency Agency
    Grinich's framing — the realization (he places his own around age 18–19) that the world is malleable and that the people who change things 'just worked really, really, really hard.' He notes the term has become popular and uses it as the link between his geek interest (going deep) and his impact orientation.
  • geek Intrinsic Motivation & Making
    Beck cites a definition (attributed to a writer whose name the auto-caption garbles as 'Jeep Hills' — likely Jerry Pournelle, Paul Graham, or similar; unverifiable here): someone who is highly creative, highly technical, and highly desirous of being both. Used to credential Grinich.
  • white-collar labor disruption AI Labor Disruption
    Grinich's reading of what AI fundamentally is — not 'a big technology wave' like crypto or VR, but a labor disruption that will reshape how work and jobs function. He credits Amodei and agrees with him. The reason the change will be enormous and also why people are missing it.

Arguments

The whole software ecosystem (not just AI labs) is genuinely accelerating, and this is a regime change rather than a hype cycle. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • WorkOS sees revenue and growth patterns across hundreds of SaaS/AI companies and observes broad acceleration.
  • Code agents compress timelines (12-week projects to 20-minute runs).
  • AI models make products more human (natural-language interfaces beating SQL etc.), which alone would be a major technology shift.
  • Large incumbents are adopting faster than ever, in part because COVID built their transformation muscle.
  • Massive capital is flowing into compute, energy, and data centers — comparable historically only to railroads or wartime mobilization.
Implicit premises:
  • Each of these forces multiplies the others rather than substituting.
  • Vendor revenue is a reasonable proxy for ecosystem-wide adoption.
Inferences:
  • Multiple independent forces compounding implies step-function transformation, not linear improvement.
  • Comparable historical mobilizations (railroads, wartime) produced regime changes, not hype cycles.
Profit is the wrong measure of AI's impact; speed is the right measure. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Measuring AI's profit effect would require a counterfactual where only one company uses AI.
  • Every competitor is adopting AI (universal adoption).
  • By the Red Queen evolutionary logic, when everyone runs faster, no one outpaces the pack on absolute fitness — all the gains go to staying in the race.
Implicit premises:
  • Companies that fail to adopt drop out, so the comparison set self-selects.
  • Faster experimentation and exploration translate to harder-to-measure but real competitive advantages.
Inferences:
  • If profit gains are invisible in equilibrium, then the surviving signal is tempo: experiments per unit time, time-to-new-market.
Software engineering as a profession will normalize toward medicine, law, and traditional engineering — longer training, more specialization, smaller in-house teams, higher pay per specialist. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Other knowledge-work professions universally require multi-year apprenticeship (residency, clerkship, architecture school's additional five years).
  • Software was uniquely a four-year undergrad → six-figure L3 pipeline, driven by enormous demand and value-creation.
  • With marginal code-production cost approaching zero, that demand profile changes.
Implicit premises:
  • Education systems and labor markets will route around the absence of in-the-job apprenticeship by adding pre-job training (the historical pattern in other professions).
  • The skills that survive are domain-specialized, judgment-heavy operation of large-scale systems — not generic coding.
Inferences:
  • If demand for generic coding falls and demand for judgment-and-specialization rises, the profession's structure must shift to match the other knowledge professions.
The maker-compulsion ('itchy brain') is the only durable founder motivation; external-validation motivations burn out. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Grinich's most miserable period was the 18 months between companies when he had no thing to chase.
  • Founders Grinich has observed who are driven by external validation 'get cooked' over time.
  • Beck names the same phenomenon ('itchy brain') from his own experience — the idea won't leave until you make the thing.
Implicit premises:
  • The Himalayan Sherpa's 'look at the step in front of you' generalizes: process-oriented motivation outlasts summit-oriented motivation.
  • Long-horizon endurance is what determines who is still building decades in.
Inferences:
  • If maker-compulsion is the durable substrate, then institutions/conditions that protect attention and the ability to make things are non-negotiable infrastructure.
Hands-on, customer-driving engineering leadership is replacing the pure people-manager. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Across customers, Grinich sees hands-off delegation-style managers losing ground.
  • CTOs at big companies are returning to code or picking up AI coding tools.
  • Companies are reorganizing around 'driving forward customer usage and value' rather than around psychological-safety process.
Implicit premises:
  • What makes leaders effective in a fast-moving regime is direct contact with the value-producing surface, not orchestration of others doing it.
  • AI tools restore individual leverage to senior leaders, making hands-on contribution feasible again at scale.
Inferences:
  • When the technology floor of individual leverage rises, the optimal leadership style shifts from delegation to demonstration.
The correct posture toward the AI transformation is to head straight into it (paddle into the wave), not sideways or stationary. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The kayaking analogy: the stable position when a wave hits is bow-into-wave with active paddling.
  • The transformation is real (the step-function claim).
  • It will be a shock, but 'we'll make it through' — Grinich is constitutionally an optimist.
Implicit premises:
  • Inaction is not a neutral choice; the wave moves the ecosystem whether or not you engage.
  • Companies and individuals that engage early gain orientation that compounds.
Inferences:
  • Therefore: lean in; don't try to wait it out; the dangerous postures are paralysis and partial commitment.
