We've Stopped Arguing About Whether AI Changes Software

The debate over whether agentic AI transforms software engineering is settled; the transformation is a shift in the center of gravity of the work — from writing code to specifying, verifying, and curating knowledge.

mixed · mixed — A first-person retreat reflection: reportage of what the author heard (expository/narrative) advancing several theses about how software work is changing, and closing with prescriptive 'what to do Monday' guidance (practical). It honestly stages an unresolved counter-argument rather than only asserting.

Key terms

  • center of gravity
    The locus of where the essential, valuable software work happens; the claim is that it is moving away from writing code toward specifying, verifying, and curating knowledge
  • spec
    The specification the agent executes against; in the author's framing the spec, not the code, is now 'the point' and is where a developer's taste lives
  • verifiable enough Verifiability (as the Quality Bar)
    The new quality bar: whether you can confirm the artifact does what it is supposed to, at which point the form of the artifact (code or not) stops mattering
  • ephemeral (code)
    Code treated as a disposable byproduct the agent regenerates from the spec on demand, rather than a durable hand-maintained artifact
  • the code is the better spec
    The counter-position (which the author concedes is hard to wave off) that code, being unambiguous, executable, and deterministic, is the truest specification, and prose specs are the fuzzy thing that should be regenerated
  • Engineering (capital E)
    The deterministic discipline of building things that do not surprise you — same input, same output, every time — which is still required precisely because agents are non-deterministic
  • the why
    The tacit rationale behind a decision — why it was done this way, what was tried and failed — which the author calls the single most valuable knowledge to capture because the code already records the what
  • PageRank for agents
    The author's shorthand for an as-yet-undiscovered mechanism for weighting and ranking knowledge sources for agent retrieval, analogous to how PageRank ranked web pages
  • structural moat / flywheel
    A compounding adoption advantage — organizations ahead use agents to accumulate knowledge, skills, and velocity, pulling ahead faster — that can harden into a permanent gap laggards never close

Arguments

The highest-leverage human work is now the spec, verification, and knowledge capture rather than writing code. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • The work is moving from writing code to specifying, verifying, and curating knowledge; the agent executes against the spec.
  • The new quality bar is verifiability, so the form of the artifact stops mattering once it can be verified.
  • Knowledge — the tacit 'why' — is the real scarce bottleneck, and agents starve for exactly that context.
Implicit premises:
  • A good spec plus a capable agent reliably produces the working artifact.
  • Verification of agent output can in principle be made tractable even though it is currently unsolved.
  • Tacit organizational knowledge can be captured and fed to agents at scale.
Inferences:
  • Therefore developers should shift their effort up the stack to the spec, to how they verify correctness, and to writing down the why.
Deterministic systems still require disciplined capital-E Engineering even in an agentic world. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Agents are wonderfully, terrifyingly non-deterministic.
  • Some systems (a payment system, a submarine reactor) must produce the same output every time with no variance.
  • We still need the ability to change (agile) and the resilience to change (the devops 'ilities').
Implicit premises:
  • A probabilistic generator cannot by itself guarantee deterministic behavior of the systems it produces.
  • The cost of surprise in safety- or money-critical systems is unacceptable.
Inferences:
  • Therefore human engineering discipline must wrap, constrain, and verify the probabilistic agents, and matters more, not less, as agents write the code.
The adoption gap between leaders and laggards may become a permanent structural moat rather than a closable lag. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Organizations that are ahead use agents to accumulate knowledge, skills, and velocity, which lets them pull ahead faster — a flywheel.
  • An organization of thousands of engineers is already doing spec-driven development at scale in production.
  • Firms that operationalize agentic development early get a valuation premium that decays and eventually flips to a penalty for not having it.
Implicit premises:
  • The flywheel's compounding rate exceeds any laggard's achievable catch-up rate.
  • The accumulated advantages (tacit knowledge, velocity) cannot simply be bought or copied late.
Inferences:
  • Therefore the lead becomes structural and, for the slowest, potentially insurmountable.

Narratives

  • The author attended the sold-out Future of Software Development Retreat Europe (hosted by Thoughtworks and Martin Fowler at the Kempinski Palace in Engelberg, Switzerland), run under Chatham House Rule, and writes only his own interpretation of what he heard.
    → Establishes the setting and the epistemic frame — this is one participant's honest take, not reported consensus — which licenses the strong theses while signaling humility.
  • Reading the reports of the February gathering of many of the same people, the conversation was aspirational — what agentic development might look like. This time, everybody in the room was doing it, shipping to production rather than showing slides.
    → Concrete before/after contrast that grounds the claim that the whether-debate is over.
  • Six engineers rebuilt the Amazon Bedrock engine in 76 days — work originally scoped at roughly 40 engineers for a full year — using AWS's agentic AI-DLC method and Kiro; the author calls it 'not a productivity bump' but 'a different physics.'
    → Public-knowledge proof point offered as evidence that agentic development is a change in kind, not degree.
  • The author skipped the security session (it ran opposite his own), but some of the most senior, hardest-to-spook people walked out saying they were terrified; he reasons secondhand that the attack surface has moved to spec, prompts, skills, knowledge layer, and supply chain.
    → Dramatizes the security threat through the reaction of unflappable experts, and models reasoning from that reaction rather than from specifics he did not hear.
  • One attendee described an organization of thousands of engineers already doing spec-driven development at scale — not a pilot, not a lighthouse team — while much of the industry still debates whether any of it is real.
    → Turns the compounding-gap theory into a present, concrete fact and raises the stakes from lag to structural moat.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Is the question of whether agentic AI changes software engineering still open?
    No — it is settled; the room had stopped arguing about whether and moved entirely to how.
  • ⭕ Is code now an ephemeral byproduct regenerated from the spec, or still the artifact that matters?
    Genuinely unresolved; brilliant people are split. The author leans spec-as-center but concedes the deterministic 'code is the better spec' argument is hard to wave off and does not expect resolution soon.
  • ⭕ How do you know the software is doing what it is supposed to do?
    Named as the frontier — 'testing, but bigger than testing' — but presented as the hard problem nobody has fully solved.
  • ⭕ Where do the senior engineers of 2035 come from if agents eat the entry-level rung?
    Open. The room floated apprentice models, pairing, and rotation, but nobody — including the author — had a clean answer.
  • ⭕ What is the retrieval/ranking mechanism that lets agents find the right knowledge ('PageRank for agents')?
    Unsolved and framed as a wide-open frontier; graphs as index-and-memory are promising (roughly as accurate as vector search, faster and cheaper).
  • ✅ What should an engineer or leader actually do now?
    Stop obsessing over code; obsess over the spec (where taste now lives), verification (how you know it is right), and knowledge (write down the why and make agents help); and if you manage people, figure out how to grow the next generation.
