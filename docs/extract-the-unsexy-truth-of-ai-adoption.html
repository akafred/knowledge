#2: The Unsexy Truth of AI Adoption

Turing Post (Ksenia Se) · email · 2026-04-04 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Enterprise AI Adoption & ReadinessOrganizational Legibility
AI adoption in organizations is not a technology selection problem but an organizational legibility problem: companies must make their tacit knowledge explicit, verifiable, and structurally trustworthy before AI can compound, and the hardest transition — making the organization legible to itself — is consistently underestimated and skipped by companies that rush toward autonomous AI capabilities.

practical · mixed — Practical: argues organizations must build through specific maturity levels in a defined order. Expository in form (framework + case studies), but the framework is prescriptive. The case studies are the strongest part — two detailed examples of L1→L2 work. The rest of the framework (L2-L5) is described but not fully elaborated in the accessible text.

Key terms

  • stack of dependencies Enterprise AI Adoption & Readiness
    AI maturity is not a linear sequence of optional stages but a set of capabilities where each layer must be stable before the next can be built. You cannot skip L2 to get to L4 — the L4 capability will collapse. This is the core metaphor that distinguishes the article's framework from standard maturity models.
  • legible (to a machine) Organizational Legibility
    An organization is legible when it can describe its own work in a form that a machine can act on: explicit rules, documented exceptions, named conventions, defined approval chains — the actual processes, not the policy document version. Distinct from 'visible to management': legibility requires precision machines can work with.
  • pilot purgatory Enterprise AI Adoption & Readiness
    Organizations that keep starting AI pilots which work in isolation but never compound or connect. Caused by skipping L2: each pilot requires the knowledge that L2 makes explicit, so they remain brittle and personal — when the employee leaves, the workflow leaves with them.
  • tacit knowledge Organizational Legibility
    Organizational knowledge that lives in people's heads, in informal practice, or in relationships — the actual rules for processing an invoice, the real approval chain (not the org chart version). Not captured anywhere, not describable in a form machines can act on. The primary blocker at L0-L1.
  • bus factor Organizational Legibility
    Used directly: the risk when critical knowledge lives in one person. Making work legible reduces bus factor. The construction firm case (224 commits of logic in one developer's head) is the exemplar.

Arguments

The L1→L2 transition requires organizational self-description, not better AI tools strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Companies at L1 look more advanced than they are — AI use is personal, brittle, undocumented
  • When the employee leaves, the AI workflow leaves with them
  • AI tools cannot absorb the ambiguity that humans have been absorbing implicitly
Implicit premises:
  • AI requires deterministic, machine-actionable inputs — it cannot infer unstated rules the way human workers learn them over time through socialization
  • The problem is not tool capability but input quality; even perfect AI tools fail on undocumented processes
Inferences:
  • Organizations that believe they have an AI problem may actually have a knowledge management problem
  • The consulting and procurement cycles that focus on 'AI strategy' and 'tool selection' systematically miss the actual blocker
Making organizational processes explicit reveals and disrupts informal power structures that depend on opacity strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Project managers at the construction firm were moving money between cost code buckets to manage client expectations — concealing bad news until better news was available
  • Knowledge is 'corrupted' because it's under the table — hoarded for protective reasons
Implicit premises:
  • Organizational opacity is not random — it tends to concentrate where power is being exercised informally or where accountability is being evaded
  • The incentive to maintain opacity is proportional to the vulnerability that legibility creates
Inferences:
  • The organizational politics of L1→L2 are as difficult as the technical work — maybe more so
  • Resistance to AI adoption at L1→L2 may be less about 'change fatigue' than about rational protection of informal power positions
  • This directly echoes Cutler's TBM 414: the legibility problem IS the legitimacy problem — who has the right to make this knowledge explicit, and on whose behalf?

Narratives

  • Bookkeeping company: Food service company processing dozens of invoices weekly from different suppliers. Wanted AI to automate data entry. Discovered 6 weeks of process documentation work needed before AI could start — because rules about fuel service fees, bottle deposits, weight-based vs. unit pricing, and invoice versioning had never been made explicit. Once the system forced clarity, supplier games (slipping fees through ambiguity) decreased.
    → Primary evidence for L1→L2 hardness and for the secondary benefit of legibility (reducing games played in the dark). Also reveals what AI actually requires: not just OCR capability, but explicit business rules.
  • Construction firm: 224 commits of logic in one developer's head; cost code mappings undocumented ('Plumbing' vs. '15.1 PLUMBING'); project managers moving money between buckets to manage client expectations across project phases. When the developer became unavailable, the entire system broke.
    → Evidence for both the technical brittleness of tacit knowledge AND the political dimension: opacity is sometimes strategic. Project managers were using the unmapped cost codes as cover for expectation management (delaying bad news). Legibility would have removed that cover.
  • Demo to leader: 'A product leader watches a demo of an agent completing a multi-step workflow... The demo is real. The capability exists. The immediate response: we need this. Then the company looks inward and the picture is different.'
    → Illustrates the gap between what companies want (autonomous agents) and what they have (undocumented processes). Supports the 'pilot purgatory' diagnosis.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What does the path to AI maturity actually look like?
    A stack of capabilities (L0-L5), not a timeline. Different departments at different levels simultaneously. The transitions between levels are the key analysis unit.
  • ✅ Why do AI pilots fail to compound?
    Companies skip the L2 foundation — they run pilots on undocumented processes, producing personal/brittle workflows that can't scale or connect
  • ✅ What actually happens at the L1→L2 transition?
    Six weeks of process documentation, data cleaning, exception mapping, and rule articulation — work that doesn't look like AI but enables everything above
  • ⭕ What are the organizational politics of making work legible?
  • ⭕ What happens at L2-L5?
