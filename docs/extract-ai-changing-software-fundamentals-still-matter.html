AI Is Changing Software Engineering. The Fundamentals Still Matter.

AI is transforming who produces, consumes, and maintains software, but the organisations moving fastest succeed by doubling down on engineering fundamentals — right-sized boundaries, modular abstractions, clear interfaces, and operational discipline — as the foundation that lets humans and AI build together safely and sustainably.

practical · mixed — A field report summarising the European Future of Software Engineering (FOSE) retreat, written under the Chatham House Rule. Primarily expository (a synthesis of themes from many sessions and hallway conversations) but woven through with argumentative advocacy — 'support don't restrict', 'APIs first', and 'fundamentals still matter'.

Key terms

  • better boundaries Right-Sizing Boundaries & Primitives
    Correctly-scoped separations — between human teams, code modules, and agent access/ecosystems — that organisations invest in to move fast and safely with AI
  • primitives
    Right-sized building blocks (a human team, a code chunk, an agent's scope) that are most impactful when sized for use, composition, and orchestration
  • right-sizing / chunking context to the right size
    The repeated discipline of sizing units and context to the appropriate granularity so both humans and LLMs can act efficiently and effectively
  • operational harnesses
    Support systems built from deterministic, scoped, and tightly role-controlled actions that can operate any deployed software and its operational and infrastructure dependencies at scale
  • vibe to value
    A tentatively-remembered attendee phrase for turning vibe-coded exploration into safe, coherent, iterable value across human, non-engineer, deterministic, and agentic consumers
  • gold rush mentality
    The prevailing event mood of focusing on doing more with AI while under-investing in the maintenance burden that follows
  • slow is smooth and smooth is fast
    The principle that up-front investment in the right primitives yields greater downstream speed and opportunity with AI

Arguments

Good engineering fundamentals — right-sized boundaries, modular abstractions, clear interfaces, and operational discipline — matter more in the AI era, not less. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Investing in right-sized primitives unlocks the biggest opportunities when working with AI.
  • Refactoring a single-file codebase into modular files improved LLM token usage and solution quality.
  • Operations automate best when actions are deterministic, scoped, and role-controlled.
  • Even non-engineer users now depend on high-quality MCP/API access to their tools.
Implicit premises:
  • AI amplifies both the payoff of good structure and the cost of bad structure, turning good practice into load-bearing infrastructure.
  • Automated and agentic consumers are even less tolerant of poor boundaries than human consumers are.
Inferences:
  • Because structure now determines whether AI can work effectively and safely, fundamentals become the foundation that lets humans and AI build together sustainably.
Organisations should support and govern non-engineer software creation rather than try to restrict it. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • The question is no longer if non-engineers will build software but how much.
  • Shadow IT (now vibe-coded apps) is already happening and cannot be avoided.
  • Easy PaaS and cloud a decade ago put similar pressure and could not constrain builders.
Implicit premises:
  • Attempts to prevent democratised creation produce ungoverned shadow IT, which is riskier than supported, well-boundaried creation.
  • Tooling can be designed to be safely and coherently consumed by humans, non-engineers, and both deterministic and agentic automation.
Inferences:
  • Therefore platform, SRE, and operational teams should provide safe, efficient, API-first tooling for the new consumers rather than pretend they can be blocked.
Fully automating remediation by removing humans from the loop can hamper long-term harness and product improvement. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • AI lacks an embodied mind and human-style learning.
  • Removing humans from the loop also removes organisational and product learning.
  • AI's clear wins are in detection, context provision, and suggestion, whereas remediation needs intentional decisions.
Implicit premises:
  • Organisational and product learning is a necessary input to improving harnesses and products over time.
  • The short-term efficiency of removing humans can be outweighed by the long-term loss of learning.
Inferences:
  • So automated remediation should be an intentional, context-specific choice rather than a default end state.

Narratives

  • The author joined almost 70 people at the European Future of Software Engineering (FOSE) retreat hosted by Thoughtworks, operating under the Chatham House Rule, and offers this as her personal summary alongside links to other attendees' reflections.
    → Establishes the provenance and authority of the observations as a synthesis of a diverse, invitation-style gathering rather than a single opinion
  • In a hallway-track moment, an attendee refactored a large single-file codebase into more modular functions and files, then observed that token usage and solution quality improved substantially as file sizes became smaller and more targeted.
    → Serves as concrete 'proof' for the claim that modularity and right-sizing directly improve AI effectiveness
  • A decade ago, easy-to-deploy PaaS and cloud environments couldn't be constrained and forced organisations to either support the new tools or face shadow IT; the same pattern is recurring now with vibe-coded apps.
    → Historical analogy grounding the 'support, don't restrict' argument for the new wave of non-engineer builders
  • The day-2 'they build it, you run it' session opened by focusing on AI SRE and self-healing agents, but many participants cautioned that automated remediation still needs intentional decisions.
    → Dramatises the gold-rush-versus-maintenance tension and the human-in-the-loop caution about lost learning

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What distinguishes the organisations moving fastest with AI?
    Investment in better boundaries and right-sized primitives (for use, composition, and orchestration), not better models or more autonomous agents.
  • ✅ Will non-engineers build software, and how should engineering teams respond?
    Increasingly yes; platform, SRE, and operational teams should support and govern it via API-first, MCP-accessible tooling rather than try to prevent it.
  • ✅ Should day-2 operations be handed to self-healing agents?
    Detection, context, and suggestion are safe wins, but automated remediation should be an intentional decision because removing humans removes organisational learning.
  • ✅ What stays constant as AI transforms software engineering?
    Good engineering fundamentals — well-designed abstractions, clear interfaces, modular systems, and operational discipline — become the enabling foundation.
