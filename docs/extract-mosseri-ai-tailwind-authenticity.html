Adam Mosseri: AI is a tailwind for authenticity

Adam Mosseri (guest); Lenny Rachitsky (host) · podcast · 2026-07-11 Reader 🖥️

Because AI makes creation nearly free and floods the world with synthetic content, the scarce and durable human advantages become taste, judgment and authenticity — which Mosseri argues is a tailwind rather than a headwind for Instagram (audiences will seek out real people and creators, not less), and which in turn reshapes how work is organized: teams compress into small generalist 'pods' led by a new 'product staff' role, while hiring rewards curiosity, grit and a willingness to try over deep specialization.

practical · mixed — A long-form podcast conversation (Lenny's Podcast) between host Lenny Rachitsky and Adam Mosseri (Head of Instagram). Primarily practical: it argues how product builders and leaders should orient themselves and organize teams in an AI era. It mixes argumentative segments (taste/judgment/authenticity as the durable human edge, AI content as a tailwind, small generalist pods), expository segments (how Instagram's recommender actually works, why chronological feeds fail at scale, token-budget management), and narrative segments (Facebook Home failure, reels-built-on-stories mistake, the 2009 Newsfeed redesign and 2022 feed-test backlash, his kids' screen-time policy and a 10-year-old vibe coding with Claude Code). Key terms

  • product staff
    An evolution of the PM into a generalist who, leveraging new AI tools, can also do parts of what a designer, data scientist and researcher used to do — the human core of a small 'pod', with senior specialists pulled in only when the work demands it.
  • taste Taste & Judgment
    The instinct for what is good and what should be built — the faculty Mosseri thinks is 'much more difficult to imagine being automated away', which makes him 'long on designers' even as generalists encroach on design.
  • authenticity as a tailwind
    The claim that an abundance of synthetic/AI content increases (not decreases) demand for creativity, authenticity and 'the person behind the content', so AI content helps Instagram — the largest creator platform — rather than hurting it.
  • judgment Taste & Judgment
    Human decision-making around strategy and what to build under constraints — 'you're not asking an AI to come up with a strategy'; the leader defines success, sets constraints and gives feedback, so directing AI 'looks more like management'.
  • curator (vs. visionary)
    The best product leaders are less prolific idea-machines than curators — of people, ideas, technologies and strategies — who create an environment where great ideas bubble up and are chosen, and who select for team chemistry (trust and rapport), 'more art than science'.
  • reverse centaur
    Cory Doctorow's image (invoked by Lenny) of a horse head on a human body — the AI in charge and the human doing its bidding — the failure mode to avoid, contrasted with the centaur where the human stays in control; used to argue people will not want AI to hand them the strategy.

Arguments

Taste, judgment and the ability to decide what to build are where durable human value concentrates as AI absorbs more of the product-development lifecycle. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • It is becoming much easier to build things, so the scarce work is figuring out what to build
  • Taste is much more difficult to imagine being automated away
  • AI cannot originate a good strategy on its own; at best it gives feedback within bounds a human sets
  • Directing AI on strategy resembles management — defining success, setting constraints, giving feedback
Implicit premises:
  • When one input to production (code/creation) becomes abundant and cheap, value flows to the complementary scarce input (judgment about what and why)
  • Taste and strategic judgment are not reducible to the pattern-completion AI is good at
Inferences:
  • Hiring and team design should be reorganized around taste, judgment and steering rather than around executional throughput
  • Designers and other taste-bearers are undervalued relative to their future importance ('long on designers')
The rise of AI content is a tailwind, not a headwind, for Instagram relative to other platforms. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • AI makes synthetic content abundant, and more content means potentially more attention for an attention/advertising business
  • In a world of abundant synthetic content, people will seek out creativity, authenticity and people more, not less
  • Power has been shifting from institutions to individuals, and Instagram is the largest creator platform
  • Instagram can handle AI content the normal way — remove unsafe, rank interesting vs. not — and label provenance rather than filter by tool
Implicit premises:
  • Scarcity raises value: as authentic human content becomes comparatively scarce, demand for it rises
  • Instagram's existing investment in creators and 'the person behind the content' positions it to capture that demand better than competitors
Inferences:
  • The right strategic response is to keep investing in individual creators and in provenance/identity signals, not to ban AI content
  • Deceptive AI spam accounts (not AI content per se) are the real problem to crack down on
Small generalist 'pods' led by a 'product staff' outperform the old specialist-heavy team. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • New AI tools let a generalist product staff do parts of design, data science and research that previously required dedicated specialists
  • Fewer people to coordinate means faster movement and less design-by-committee
  • You can still pull in a senior or more creative specialist exactly when the work demands it
Implicit premises:
  • Coordination cost, not raw headcount, is the binding constraint on team effectiveness at scale
  • The specialization lost by shrinking the core is recoverable on demand and worth the coordination savings
Inferences:
  • Companies should compress team cores and build teams around the needs of the work
  • But they must deliberately grow tomorrow's senior specialists or the pull-in-on-demand model eventually starves
Hire and cultivate curiosity and a willingness to try things over deep specialization. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • No one really knows what the world looks like in a couple of years, so predictions should be held loosely
  • Learning is gated by willingness to make mistakes and be corrected (the learn-a-language / 'willing to sound like an idiot' analogy)
  • The baseline traits of grit, fast learning and self-awareness let a person get good at almost anything
Implicit premises:
  • Adaptability compounds under high uncertainty, whereas narrow expertise depreciates as tools change the job
  • Curiosity and experimentation are at least partly cultivable, not purely innate
Inferences:
  • Weight adaptability and range over demonstrated specialist skill, especially across the next 10-20 years and multiple industries
  • There is still a real, valuable role for a few genius specialists — it is the age of the generalist, not the end of the specialist

Narratives

  • Facebook Home — Mosseri's first project as a PM, a fork of Android at the OS level plus HTC hardware — was a spectacular failure, but he learned more in that year-and-a-half than any other year, and he is glad he brought it to an end: sometimes the best thing you can do is execute a no-market-fit idea well enough to decide whether it was a good idea in the first place.
    → Illustrates learning-from-failure and the discipline of killing an idea; supports the 'fail corner' theme and the value of judgment about what to build (and what to stop).
  • The first version of Reels (circa 2019) was built on top of Stories because Stories had the most momentum, but Stories was a weak foundation (low readthrough, most reels never seen). By the time the standalone Reels launched (summer 2020) TikTok had exploded during the pandemic; Instagram was 'out of position'. Mosseri, a designer by instinct trying to extend existing primitives rather than add new ones, was wrong — a big fork in the road for the business over eight years.
    → Illustrates the cost of building on the wrong foundation and over-extending existing primitives; a concrete case where taste/judgment about what to build had enormous strategic stakes.
  • At the March 2009 Facebook Newsfeed redesign that Mosseri (a 25-year-old IC designer) shipped, the very first comment was derogatory and bigoted and specifically about the design. Devastated, he later reframed it: rearranging millions of people's 'desk' without warning or explanation would reasonably make anyone furious — so he puts backlash in perspective and steps away (time with kids, outside).
    → Illustrates how he metabolizes public criticism and builds empathy for users; supports the leadership-under-scrutiny and accessibility themes.
  • The 2022 'protest' over the video-viewer feed redesign was really four things conflated — a 4%-of-iOS test never meant to ship, leaning into Reels, leaning into recommendations, and creators upset about declining reach — amplified by big-name creators publicly slapping Instagram and press coverage echoing back. The takeaway was not that they shouldn't have tested, but that they were pushing a little too fast and that at Instagram's scale you must have a comms strategy ('not if it leaks, when it leaks') before you even know you want to launch.
    → Illustrates that experiments at massive scale are inherently risky and require a pre-planned communication strategy; supports the trade-offs and leadership-at-scale themes.
  • Mosseri's screen-time policy for his three kids (10, 8, 6) centers on boundaries: no social media yet, earned iPad time, parent-approved apps. The exception he is experimenting with is vibe coding with his 10-year-old — who built a 19-level eight-bit platformer with its own themes, monsters and item store using Claude Code — because he wants his kids to be digitally and AI literate (learning to make things, not just consume) without a free-for-all.
    → A personal illustration of both the boundary/education philosophy and how accessible building has become — a three-fingered-typing 10-year-old shipping a game in a couple of hours.
  • The 'your algorithm' feature: Instagram maps everything a user has interacted with into an embedding space (a map), then has an LLM describe a region of that map in words (e.g. 'deep pourover coffee snobbery'), letting users see and adjust the topics the system thinks they like — an attempt to give people agency back as feeds are taken over by recommendations.
    → Illustrates the claim that recommenders run on illegible embeddings and that LLMs now make them describable, plus the move to give users agency.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What does the canonical product team look like in 2026, and what is 'product staff'?
    A shift from a 'baker's dozen' of specialists to small 'pods' of 4-6 generalist engineers plus a 'product staff' (an evolved PM who absorbs parts of design/data-science/research via AI tools), with senior specialists pulled in only when the work demands.
  • ✅ Which adjacent roles (data science, user research, design) are most at risk, and should people shift?
    Anxiety is everywhere and functional lines will keep blurring, but functions won't vanish — they'll be reshaped and fewer; Mosseri is actually 'long on designers' because they carry taste, and expects strong ICs to move fluidly across crafts.
  • ✅ What traits are trending up and down in hiring in an AI world?
    Up: curiosity and willingness to try/put yourself out there (on top of the baseline grit, fast learning, self-awareness). Down: large-organizational leadership, because there will be fewer very large teams to manage.
  • ✅ Where will human brains continue to be most valuable as AI eats the product-development lifecycle?
    Taste, judgment (especially around strategy), vision and strategy — AI is not asked to originate strategy; steering it resembles management (define success, set constraints, give feedback).
  • ✅ How should you manage token spend and budgets for engineers and product teams?
    Treat it like any resource (GPUs, opex, payroll); no leaderboards for token spend; shut down low-value 'token incinerators'; expect caps proportional to trust in ROI-positive use as burn rate approaches salary, even as per-unit cost falls.
  • ✅ Is the rise of AI content a headwind or a tailwind for Instagram?
    A tailwind (but a challenge): abundant synthetic content will make people seek out authenticity and people more, and Instagram is the largest creator platform — though it cannot yet rank AI content well and detection may degrade.
  • ✅ Should Instagram filter AI content, and how should it treat AI vs. human content/accounts?
    Don't filter by tool — judge content by its point of view and creator; label provenance (possibly labeling camera-captured content long-term) and surface who posted it, while cracking down on deceptive AI spam accounts.
  • ✅ What does the Instagram algorithm actually 'know' about users?
    Less than people assume: it runs on illegible embedding vectors, not semantic interests; LLMs now let the system describe those embeddings, powering 'see and adjust your algorithm' features.
  • ⭕ Should you outsource a given workflow to AI just because you can?
    Mosseri says no — some cases are win-win, in others the risk outweighs the benefit and humans must stay in control (the reverse-centaur danger) — but he offers no criteria for telling which is which.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe tailwind thesis rests on the empirical claim that audiences will reliably prefer authentic/human content over polished synthetic content at scale, but no mechanism or evidence is offered. Mosseri simultaneously concedes Instagram is 'not very good at ranking AI content yet' and that as models improve it 'might lose the ability to detect' AI content — so the very provenance-labeling and ranking that the strategy depends on may be unworkable, leaving the tailwind under-defended.
    Read next: Empirical work on engagement with labeled AI vs. human content and parasocial/authenticity preferences; the AI-slop discourse on how telltale synthetic output degrades information ecosystems; whether 'the person behind the content' is a durable ranking signal.
  • incompleteThe 'small pods + product staff' model is drawn entirely from Meta/Instagram — a huge, well-resourced company with a deep bench of senior specialists to pull in on demand. Its viability for startups or teams without that bench is unexamined, and whether generalist product staff can sustain deep specialization, design consistency and accessibility over time is asserted-away ('you still pull in a specialist when you need one') rather than shown.
    Read next: Organizational-design literature on the specialist-vs-generalist trade-off; empirical outcomes from companies that eliminated specialist roles; the same specialization-loss failure mode raised (and left open) in the Catherine Wu / Claude Code discussion.
  • incompleteTaste and judgment are presented as durable human advantages 'much more difficult to imagine being automated away', but Mosseri gives no account of why they resist automation, and the opposing view — that taste is an in-group-reaction prediction loop AI can also learn to close — is never engaged.
    Read next: The argument (from the Schoening/Lenny conversation) that taste resembles backpropagation and may not be an enduring human advantage; theories of taste as learnable feedback vs. innate faculty.
  • incompleteMosseri says some workflows are win-win to hand to AI while for others 'the risk outweighs the benefits', and warns of the reverse-centaur failure mode, but offers no criteria for deciding which workflows to outsource — leaving the central practical question of human-vs-AI division of labor unresolved.
    Read next: Risk-tiering frameworks for AI-assisted work; human-in-the-loop decision design; the cognitive-surrender literature on when delegation crosses into loss of control.
