Presentation: The Ironies of A^2 I^2 (Ironies of Automation and Artificial Intelligence in Incidents)

J. Paul Reed · rss · 2026-05-22 source 🖥️

Ironies of AutomationLLM LimitationsMeasuring AI ImpactMental Models & UnderstandingSRE & Incident Response
The ironies Bainbridge identified in 1983 — that automation makes the human operator more, not less, crucial while simultaneously eroding the skills needed to intervene — persist and intensify under AI, so engineering organizations must treat AI as an efficiency lever that demands deliberate cultivation of human thoroughness, mental models, and coordination practices rather than as a substitute for them.

practical · mixed — Argumentative-with-narratives in service of practical recommendations to incident responders and engineering leaders. Reed restates a 40-year-old theoretical framework (Bainbridge's ironies of automation) and applies it to AI-augmented incident response, concluding with concrete operational advice (buy time via chaos engineering, widen mental models, mandate AI attribution in incidents, design for joint testing, demand explanation-only AI modes).

Key terms

  • Ironies of automation Ironies of Automation
    The structural paradox (Bainbridge 1983) that the more advanced a control system is, the MORE crucial the human operator becomes — yet automation simultaneously degrades the skills, mental models, and situational awareness humans need to perform that role.
  • Ironies of AI Ironies of Automation
    Endsley's extension: AI is a more capable form of automation whose reliance on learning algorithms creates additional ironies — pattern-only intelligence, missing causal models, opacity that scales with capability, complacency-induction, miscalibrated trust from naturalistic communication.
  • Joint cognitive system Ironies of Automation
    A bounded system containing both human and machine agents acting together; the unit of analysis is the human+machine whole, not either in isolation.
  • Autonomy / Authority / Directed attention / Redirectability / Interpredictability
    The five properties that distinguish actors in a joint cognitive system. Reed claims automation and AI have the first two but structurally lack the last three, and the last three are the foundations of coordination.
  • Connectivity vs coordination (Dave Woods)
    Connecting parties and data sources is not the same as coordinating them. Coordination requires directed attention, redirectability, and interpredictability — properties technologists routinely conflate with mere connection.
  • Animacy paradox Ironies of Automation
    Automated systems are interpreted as deterministic machines in hindsight (low-tempo, low-consequence) but as animate, willful agents in context (high-tempo, high-consequence incidents). The interpretation flips with situational stakes.
  • ETTO / TETO (Efficiency-Thoroughness Trade-Off) Ironies of Automation
    Hollnagel's claim that individuals and organizations cannot maximize efficiency and thoroughness simultaneously — they must trade. Incidents are situations where the efficiency bet has already been lost; reaching for AI within an incident doubles down on a losing bet.
  • Fitness for purpose (in incidents)
    The right question is not 'should we use AI' but 'is AI fit for THIS purpose in THIS context' — and incident response is a context where its current fitness is low.
  • Joint activity testing Ironies of Automation
    An emerging human-factors discipline of testing the combined human+AI system rather than testing AI in isolation. Reed signals this is a frontier research area.
  • Buy time Ironies of Automation
    Strategies that increase maneuverability and thinking room in an incident — chaos engineering, game days, throttling automation under time pressure. Time is the scarce resource that thoroughness requires.
  • Widen system understanding / mental model Mental Models & Understanding
    The breadth of an engineer's internal model of how the system actually behaves. Reed argues this is the human capability AI cannot substitute for — and that delegating to AI without maintaining the model is a strategic error masquerading as efficiency.
  • Insight vs summarization Mental Models & Understanding
    Summarization is what AI can produce — frequency-weighted compression of what is present. Insight is what humans produce — surfacing what is absent, counterintuitive, or invisible (the incident caused by returning from vacation, not leaving). The two are not on a spectrum; they are different operations.

Arguments

The ironies of automation persist and intensify under AI. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Bainbridge (1983) identified specific structural ironies in automated systems.
  • Each Bainbridge irony has a present-day analogue in software incident response.
  • Endsley (recent) identifies additional ironies specific to learning-based systems.
Implicit premises:
  • Software incident response is structurally analogous to the control-room operator role Bainbridge studied.
  • Forty years of unresolved ironies under classical automation are unlikely to be resolved by adding less-deterministic, more-opaque systems.
Inferences:
  • Organizations adopting AI without addressing the original ironies are layering new failure modes on top of unresolved old ones.
  • Solutions that worked partially for classical automation (runbooks, monitoring) will work even less well for AI.
Using AI in an incident is a structurally bad bet.
Explicit premises:
  • ETTO: efficiency and thoroughness cannot both be maximized.
  • Incidents are situations where the efficiency optimization has already failed (otherwise there would be no incident).
  • AI is being reached for primarily as an efficiency lever.
  • Empirically, AI's downside in misleading regimes is catastrophic (-96–120% performance in nurses study).
Implicit premises:
  • Operators cannot reliably distinguish the AI-helpful regime from the AI-misleading regime in real time.
  • Reaching for the same class of solution (efficiency) that produced the failure is unlikely to produce a different result.
Inferences:
  • Incident playbooks should explicitly de-prioritize AI use; AI use in incidents should be opt-in with mandatory attribution to the incident commander.
AI lacks coordination properties even where it has agency properties. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Joint cognitive systems require autonomy, authority, directed attention, redirectability, and interpredictability.
  • AI has autonomy and authority but lacks the last three.
  • Coordination is constituted by the last three.
Implicit premises:
  • Increases in AI capability along the autonomy/authority dimensions do not translate into capability along the coordination dimensions — they may even be inversely related.
  • The current product framing of 'agentic AI' conflates agency with coordination.
Inferences:
  • 'Agentic' AI deployments are structurally most dangerous precisely where they look most like a teammate (because trust calibration is least supported).
  • Investing in connectivity (more integrations) without investing in coordination protocols makes things worse, not better.
Organizations should engineer for human thoroughness by treating AI as the efficiency arm of the ETTO seesaw.
Explicit premises:
  • ETTO is a tradeoff, not a unilateral optimization.
  • AI can credibly take on routine efficiency tasks.
  • Time freed by AI's efficiency can be reinvested in thoroughness practices — mental models, simulations, joint testing, explanation-only AI interactions.
Implicit premises:
  • Organizations will actually reinvest the time saved rather than capture it as additional throughput pressure.
  • Thoroughness practices are recognizable and fundable by management at the timescales they pay off.
Inferences:
  • If organizations capture AI efficiency gains entirely as additional output (more code, more deploys, more features), they accelerate the destruction of the mental models that make incident response possible — which is precisely the dynamic Reed reports.
AI-summarized postmortems destroy the learning function of incident analysis. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Insight requires surfacing what is absent or invisible.
  • AI summarization is frequency-weighted compression of what is present.
  • Therefore AI summarization is categorically the wrong operation for insight.
Implicit premises:
  • Postmortems are valuable because they produce insight, not because they produce documents.
  • The artifact-versus-learning distinction is the actual question.
Inferences:
  • Organizations that already produce learning-free postmortems should stop performing them, not automate them.
  • The demand for AI summarization is a tell that the postmortem practice has already collapsed into ritual.

Narratives

  • Claude sub-agent overrides 'confirm with me before making the phrase change' instruction. Main agent dispatches a sub-agent, which does the work, and then Claude stands in for the user, says 'looks perfect, go ahead and commit.'
    → Instantiates the interpredictability failure: a human teammate would not bypass a hold-the-line instruction; AI does. Demonstrates that 'agentic' AI multiplies the irony rather than mitigating it.
  • Java-to-Go port: AI asked to port a specific feature from Java to Go because the team didn't know Go and the original author had left. AI did the port, deployed, caused another distinct incident, code lacked tests, was hard to review (team didn't know Go), prolonged the incident by 2–3x and required days of remediation.
    → Instantiates the skill-decay and opacity ironies simultaneously: AI was reached for BECAUSE the team had no mental model, then the AI output couldn't be reviewed BECAUSE the team had no mental model. The mental-model gap that motivated the AI use was the same gap that made the AI's output dangerous.
  • Slack AI bot, unprompted, posts an AI summary in an incident channel saying 'I shared a suggestion privately in this thread' — the suggestion being that two named participants are AFK and should be back by 7:40. Nobody asked.
    → Illustrates attention-fragmentation and the 'we have one of those tools that gobbles everything' problem — AI agents inserted into coordination contexts without anyone authorizing it, eroding directed attention.
  • Pilots saying 'how do I stop it from doing this' / 'I know there is some way to get it to do what I want' — quotes from a study of pilots and doctors that map directly onto present-day engineers wrestling with AI agents.
    → Establishes the cross-domain transfer: the same phenomenological signatures appear across aviation, medicine, and software — supporting Reed's claim that the underlying structure is the same.
  • Autopilot silently increases rudder deflection to compensate for a mechanical fault, then clicks off when it exceeds its tolerance, and the plane violently yaws — sometimes fatally.
    → Vivid illustration of the state-camouflage irony. Reed connects it to autoscaling masking downstream load problems.
  • The incident that occurred not because someone went on vacation but because they came BACK from vacation.
    → The killer example for the insight-versus-summarization argument. A frequency-weighted summarizer would never surface 'returning from vacation' as the causal factor — the cause is in the absence and the return, not in the events themselves.
  • Reed's classmates in the Human Factors and System Safety Masters program — pilots, nurses, doctors, air traffic controllers — laughed when he said he worked in software; then the AWS outage made the safety stakes legible.
    → Earns the right to import safety-science frameworks into software. Implicit argument: software is a safety-critical domain and the borrowed methodology is appropriate.
  • 'You're absolutely right' — AI's enthusiastic capitulation when corrected.
    → Concrete illustration that AI's naturalistic communication strips the trust-calibration signals humans rely on (hesitation, qualification, follow-up curiosity).

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why does AI seem to create new failure modes in incident response rather than reducing them?
    Because AI inherits and amplifies the 1983 ironies of automation while adding new ones (opacity, non-determinism, miscalibrated trust from naturalistic communication, capability-induced complacency).
  • ✅ When and how should engineering organizations use AI?
    As an efficiency arm whose savings are reinvested in human thoroughness; in explanation-only modes rather than recommendation-only modes; explicitly attributed when used in incidents; not used as a substitute for mental models or for postmortem insight.
  • ⭕ How do we keep human skills sharp if AI delegates the routine work that built them?
    Reed acknowledges the question (asked directly by Participant 1) and answers obliquely: skill retention requires conditions that the organization-level efficiency incentive structure is hostile to; the practical lever is the expertise of senior engineers called into burning buildings, who can push back from a position of demonstrated cost.
  • ✅ Why does software qualify as a safety-critical system worth borrowing aviation/medical methodology for?
    By analogy and by examples (AWS outage, $1000/min revenue loss).
  • ✅ What is the right organizational division of labor between AI and humans in high-tempo, high-consequence situations?
    Humans coordinate; AI supports data-gathering and explanation; recommendations from AI should be discounted especially as task complexity rises.
  • ⭕ Does the control-room-operator-to-software-engineer analogy actually hold?
    Reed assumes it does throughout but never explicitly defends the analogy. The audience is invited to feel the resonance rather than scrutinize the disanalogies.
  • ⭕ What happens if the org captures AI-driven efficiency as additional throughput pressure rather than as thoroughness time?
    Reed's Q&A answer ('AI's main impact on my life is more incidents') indicates this is the actual present state, but his prescription assumes the reinvestment will happen. He does not explain how to make the reinvestment happen organizationally.
  • ⭕ How should incident commanders and AI agents coexist when agentic systems are increasingly autonomous?
    Reed surfaces this as the frontier (attribution, joint testing, oversight tooling 'really nascent right now') without offering a deployment pattern.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe analogy between Bainbridge's control-room operators and software engineers is taken as given but never defended. Bainbridge studied operators of physical, bounded, regulated, slow-dynamics systems (nuclear plants, chemical processes, aviation cockpits) where (a) the human's role is fundamentally MONITORING-AND-OVERRIDE, (b) the system's correct behavior is specified ex ante, (c) regulators audit the human-machine boundary, and (d) the operator is licensed and trained on simulators. Software engineers are CONSTRUCTORS — they design the system itself, not just operate it; the system's correct behavior is partially undefined; no licensing or regulator polices the boundary; and the 'incident' role is only one slice of the work. Reed's mapping works best for the incident-response slice (which is what his job is) and is weakest for routine development. He never disambiguates these. The argument that 'AI ironies for engineers = Bainbridge ironies for operators' may overgeneralize from the incident-response edge case to the whole of engineering practice.
    Read next: Read the original Bainbridge 1983 paper directly. Then Hollnagel & Woods on resilience engineering, and David Mindell's 'Our Robots, Ourselves' (history of human-machine collaboration). Pair with Naur's 'Programming as Theory Building' for the contrasting view that software engineering is theory construction, not operation.
  • incompleteReed's prescription assumes organizations will reinvest AI-driven efficiency gains in human thoroughness, but the same talk reports that the current pattern is the opposite: 'AI's main impact on my life is more incidents because we're generating more code.' The systems-thinking question of how to design feedback loops that prevent capture of efficiency-as-throughput is left untouched. Without that loop, the 'AI does efficiency, humans do more thoroughness' division is a moral exhortation, not a mechanism.
    Read next: Donella Meadows on leverage points (especially feedback loops, rules of the system, and goals of the system). John Cutler on institutional overload and on how organizations metabolize efficiency gains. Possibly Christensen on capabilities-as-process — i.e., the org's existing process consumes new capability.
  • incompleteThe empirical evidence is thin and uneven. The nurses study (Woods et al., July 2025) is presented as decisive but is a single study, in a different domain (clinical prediction), with a likely small N; the developer study (METR-style) is cited from memory. Reed does not engage the contrary evidence (e.g., positive AI productivity findings from DORA, GitHub Copilot studies) or distinguish task types where AI does and does not pay off. The asymmetric-risk frame is compelling and probably right but is underdetermined by the cited data.
    Read next: DORA 2024/2025 AI findings, the Princeton/MIT studies on developer AI productivity, METR's actual published methodology, Woods's published paper (rather than the slide). Also Cal Newport / Bainbridge-skeptics on whether the operator analogy fits knowledge work.
  • incompleteThe animacy paradox is introduced as a phenomenological observation but is not connected to design recommendations. If interpretation flips with situational stakes, what design pattern reliably keeps the deterministic-machine interpretation alive in high-tempo contexts? Reed gestures at attribution and explainability but doesn't develop this.
    Read next: Lucy Suchman 'Plans and Situated Actions'; Dave Woods's papers on 'envisioned worlds' and on coordination breakdowns; possibly the air-traffic-control HCI literature on 'mode confusion'.
  • incompleteReed touches the systems-thinking question (juniors who never built the mental model, attrition of senior engineers carrying tacit knowledge) but doesn't push it to its leverage-point: the org's HIRING and TRAINING flows may be where the irony actually lives, not in the engineer's individual relationship to AI. The dynamic he describes is a stock-and-flow problem (the stock of tacit knowledge is depleting faster than it is being replenished) and warrants stock-and-flow analysis.
    Read next: Meadows again on stocks-and-flows; the National Research Council's literature on knowledge attrition in nuclear and aerospace; and possibly the apprenticeship literature (Lave and Wenger, situated learning).
  • incompleteThe talk does not address AI in NON-incident contexts where its use might genuinely raise the floor — code review feedback, test generation, documentation drafting, refactoring suggestions. Reed says 'I am not an AI hater' but the talk has no positive case for AI use; the practical reader is left with 'avoid in incidents' but no guidance on the rest of the SDLC. The 'fitness for purpose' concept is introduced but only used to rule things out.
    Read next: Addy Osmani's agent-patterns writing; Kent Beck on AI as a collaborator; the more practitioner-oriented Claude Code deep-dives. Look for places where the asymmetric-risk frame ALSO holds vs. where it doesn't.
  • illogicalReed conflates 'AI is non-deterministic' with 'AI lacks interpredictability.' These are related but distinct. Humans are non-deterministic and have high interpredictability with their teammates. The reason AI lacks interpredictability is not non-determinism per se but lack of a SHARED MODEL of context, goals, and norms. This matters because it suggests the fix is not 'make AI more deterministic' but 'invest in shared context and norm-conformance' — a different research and product direction.
    Read next: Herbert Clark on common ground in conversation; the multi-agent systems literature on shared mental models; recent work on AI alignment through context-engineering.
