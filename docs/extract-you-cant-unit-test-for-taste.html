You can't unit test for taste

Karl Tryggvason · article · 2026-06-22 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Agentic EngineeringCode QualityEvalsFeedback LoopsHuman-in-the-LoopMeasuring AI ImpactTaste & Judgment
In software you can assert correctness with red/green tests, but taste — judging what output is good, relevant and well-proportioned where there is no ground truth — cannot be unit-tested, so it stays an irreducibly human, iterative judgment even when AI does the production work.

Theoretical at its core — the titular payload is a claim about what is the case (taste has no ground truth and cannot be unit-tested) — delivered through a practical, first-person build narrative whose action-guidance (ground the LLM, keep a human judging, prefer attributable sources) is secondary and mostly implicit. · Mixed: a personal project retrospective / case study used as the narrative vehicle for an argumentative software-craft thesis; essayistic, with three footnotes. — The In the Long Run points-of-interest pipeline is the concrete vehicle; the load-bearing content is the argument that correctness is assertable while taste is not, plus the reframing of AI from 'the feature' to one augmenting tool. Weight the reading toward the craft/judgment thesis, not the running-app mechanics.

Key terms

  • Taste Taste & Judgment
    The subjective judgment of what output is good, relevant, interesting and well-proportioned, for which there is no ground truth and no automated (red/green) test.
  • Correctness (vs. readability / quality)
    Factual accuracy checkable against a ground truth (does the data tally, is the fact right); he explicitly ranks it above readability, reverting better-reading LLM text to attributable Wikipedia.
  • Ground truth / red-green unit test
    A knowable, binary reference answer that lets you assert pass/fail; present for code, absent for 'which point of interest is interesting'.
  • 'The LLM lies but it does have taste'
    The LLM cannot be trusted for factual generation (it fabricates) yet carries useful subjective judgment 'latent in its weights' for ranking — so it is relegated from writing but promoted to scoring.
  • Augmentation ('AI is a new tool in a bigger toolbox') Measuring AI Impact
    AI positioned as one signal among traditional data-processing mainstays, not the foundation of the feature — 'AI solves this' becomes 'AI nicely augments other traditional approaches'.

Arguments

Taste cannot be unit-tested or automatically verified, so it remains an irreducibly human judgment exercised through iteration and manual override. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • There is no ground truth for what counts as an interesting point of interest — the judgment is subjective.
  • Code features 'either work or don't' and usually have an objective measure; messy real-world data does not.
  • Optimizing one route or parameter degrades others, so there is no single objective to test against.
Implicit premises:
  • Unit tests require a knowable, stable, binary correct answer (a ground truth) to assert against.
  • Taste is precisely judgment about quality where no such ground truth exists and reasonable answers differ.
  • Therefore anything lacking a ground truth is by construction outside the reach of assertion-based (red/green) testing.
Inferences:
  • The same asymmetry governs AI code generation: agents can pass tests (ground-truth domains) while producing tasteless design (elegance, simplicity, restraint have no red/green check) — a green build is not evidence of good design.
  • As AI makes producing test-passing output cheap, the scarce, differentiating human contribution shifts to the taste and judgment that no eval can fully certify.
AI belongs as one tool augmenting traditional, deterministic approaches — sited where its errors are cheap (subjective ranking) — not as the correctness-critical foundation of a feature. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The LLM hallucinated on factual generation (populations, geography, namesakes).
  • Reverting to deterministic, attributable Wikipedia data was better for correctness.
  • The LLM's genuine, retained value was narrow: subjective ratings, not authoritative text.
Implicit premises:
  • A component that cannot be trusted for correctness should not be load-bearing where correctness matters.
  • A tool's role should be assigned by where it is reliable, not by where it is impressive.
Inferences:
  • This generalizes to a design heuristic for AI systems: site the LLM where mistakes are cheap or subjective and keep deterministic, attributable sources authoritative wherever correctness is checkable.

Narratives

  • The In the Long Run points-of-interest pipeline: GeoNames dumps to Parquet/DuckDB filtering, route matching with Shapely/Pyproj, Wikipedia/Wikidata plus a Haiku rating for enrichment, then per-route parameter tuning, shipped as V1.
    → The concrete vehicle and body of evidence for the whole thesis; it enacts the correctness-vs-taste asymmetry (supports p1, p8, p12).
  • A naive first-pass filter pulled in the Australian rural locality 'Stonehenge' but not the famous prehistoric megalith; a sanity check caught it and let him tweak the filtering.
    → Shows a green-looking pipeline can be silently wrong and that human judgment is needed to catch it (supports p12, p11).
  • Haiku classified Central Park in Decatur, Illinois as its famous Manhattan namesake, inflated town populations and made mountains larger than reality — even after grounding.
    → Evidence that the LLM 'lies' on facts, motivating the correctness-over-readability revert to Wikipedia (supports p3, p2).
  • On densely populated routes the point-of-interest map collapsed into a population map, showing every town, village and hamlet, forcing per-route parameters and a geographic spread filter.
    → The central demonstration that a correct-by-the-numbers result can be tasteless, requiring judgment and restraint to fix (supports p12, p13, p8).
  • Reykjavik scoring a 10 from the LLM, which he openly doubts: 'is it more significant than Chicago or LA? How about Vatnajokull? I'm not sure.'
    → The human interrogating, but unable to verify, the model's 'taste' — delegated subjective scoring is itself an unverifiable judgment (supports p16, p4).
  • Claude Code wanting to write the implementation first and only afterwards produce the spec for approval — a shortcut he deliberately reined in.
    → Evidence that agents shortcut the agreed process and must be actively held to it (supports p9).
  • The 'we' vs 'I' pronoun dilemma when writing up work done with an AI agent that at times felt like being mentored by a senior.
    → Raises accountability: 'we' as an accountability dodge, 'I' as overclaiming (supports p10).

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How can you build a global points-of-interest pipeline from open data with AI assistance?
    Built and shipped V1: GeoNames plus Parquet/DuckDB plus Wikipedia/Wikidata notoriety signals plus a narrowly-scoped LLM rating, with deterministic processing doing the heavy lifting.
  • ✅ Where should the LLM sit in the pipeline given that it hallucinates?
    Relegated from factual writing to subjective ranking, grounded in location/admin metadata and batched for cost; AI ends up augmenting rather than founding the feature.
  • ⭕ How do you evaluate or verify quality when there is no ground truth for the output?
    Left open — 'this will require more iteration and manual overrides,' hoping for community feedback; he names that evals/frameworks exist but does not build or explore them.
  • ⭕ Would cheaper or local models change the cost-quality tradeoff of the LLM step?
    Flagged as a future experiment ('I'd want to experiment with local or cheaper models here').
  • ⭕ Where does taste come from, and how is it developed or transmitted?
    He exercises pre-existing taste throughout (spotting the population-map problem, the anglophone bias, the suspect Reykjavik score) but never asks where it comes from or how someone without it would build or inherit it.
  • ⭕ If AI-generated code passes tests but has no taste for design, what certifies design quality as AI writes more code?
    He treats code as the 'easily verifiable domain' and turns the taste lens only onto data and text, never back onto the code itself.

Gaps

  • incompleteHe asserts taste cannot be tested but never addresses how taste is developed or transmitted. He brings pre-existing taste to every judgment (the population-map problem, the anglophone-Wikipedia bias, the suspect Reykjavik=10) yet treats it as a given human endowment — silent on how a novice, a team, or the next generation would acquire or inherit it, especially once AI removes the on-ramp tasks that used to build it.
    Read next: Read Polanyi on tacit knowledge and Dreyfus on skill acquisition (novice-to-expert); pair with the corpus's junior-apprenticeship thread (heis-09, heis-12, ki-utvikling-og-folelser) and Beck 'Tidy First?' / Ousterhout on design judgment as cultivated rather than innate. Ask: does taste transmit at all when AI removes the practice that used to form it?
  • incompleteHe confines 'you can't unit test for taste' to messy data and treats code as the safely 'verifiable domain,' but never turns the lens onto AI-generated code itself, where tests pass yet design taste — simplicity, deep modules, restraint — has no red/green check. As agents generate more test-passing code, the untestable-taste problem migrates into the codebase, not away from it.
    Read next: Read Ousterhout 'A Philosophy of Software Design,' Beck 'Tidy First?,' and Hickey 'Simple Made Easy' on design quality as unassertable; cross with corpus docs agents-dont-know-what-good-looks-like (behavioral vs capability verification) and cognitive-surrender (surface-correctness signals). Test the claim: is a green build ever evidence of good design?
  • incompleteHe delegates the 'subjective' rating to the LLM's latent weights and finds it useful, but never examines whether that latent 'taste' is genuine judgment or averaged internet popularity — i.e., whether outsourcing scoring to a model launders the very anglophone-Wikipedia bias he diagnosed a section earlier into a 'subjective score' he can no longer see or audit.
    Read next: Read on models as reflections of their training distribution (Bender et al.) and on popularity-as-proxy-for-quality critiques; probe the LLM ratings against non-anglophone sources to see whether the 'taste' is judgment or a re-encoding of who edits Wikipedia.
  • incompleteBetween the red/green unit test and pure manual taste sits a graded-evaluation middle path — evals, rubric grading, LLM-as-judge — which he names ('I'm sure there are frameworks and evals') but does not develop or attempt. The essay's verification story therefore skips the very tooling that partially addresses its central problem.
    Read next: Read the evals literature (LLM-as-judge, rubric-based and reference-free evaluation) and the corpus's evals concept; ask how far graded, probabilistic evaluation can substitute for taste before genuine human judgment is still required — and precisely where it cannot.
