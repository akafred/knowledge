When Nothing Happens

Fish Food for Thought · email · 2026-07-08 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Whether a leader's harsh behavior is judged as abuse or as excellence turns almost entirely on whether the numbers are good rather than on the behavior itself, so tolerating a high performer's bad conduct is not a moral judgment about their character but an unadmitted bet on their continued performance — a bet that reverses the moment results fade, not when the behavior escalates — and leaders should name and confront that bet today instead of letting it become the scandal they must later explain.

mixed (primarily practical) · narrative / mixed — Roughly 90% of the piece is expository and analytical — it describes a mechanism (how organizations judge a leader's harsh behavior), which is theoretical work concerned with what is true. But its telos is normative: it culminates in a section literally titled 'What to Do About It' and hands the reader an exercise (write down a name; ask the counterfactual). By Adler's test — does it teach what to do — it is a practical piece resting on a descriptive framework. Formally it is a narrative essay that opens with a scene (Jobs at MobileMe), then interleaves a cited framework (Bies) and three matched case studies (Wachner, Knight, Jobs) before turning to direct second-person address.

Key terms

  • When nothing happens / nothing happening
    The title term for the load-bearing event of the essay: an instance of bad behavior that produces no consequence — no board session, no HR investigation, no exit. The absence of a response is not neutral; each non-consequence silently ratifies the behavior and shifts the organization's standard.
  • We punish behavior in context / the machine / the frame
    The central claim device: behavior carries no fixed moral charge; observers assign its meaning through contextual filters, chief among them results. 'The leader didn't shift. The frame did.' The author repeatedly calls the whole apparatus 'the machine' to signal it is a mechanism, not a moral choice.
  • Normalization of deviance Trust
    Borrowed from Diane Vaughan's Challenger analysis: institutions tolerate not single transgressions but accumulating ones; each deviation that produces no catastrophe is absorbed into the new standard until the organization has operated outside its original parameters for years and calls it normal. Applied to a person: 'By 1997, it was the standard.'
  • Three filters (Bies)
    The specific contextual factors that decide whether harsh behavior reads as abuse or motivation: (1) whether the leader develops people into top performers, (2) whether subordinates trust the leader's motives (trust as a 'translator'), (3) the explanation the leader offers for the behavior. 'The most volatile element in that context is whether the leader is still winning.'
  • Abuse vs. motivation / abusive leader vs. master motivator
    The binary verdict rendered on identical conduct — 'in the eye of the beholder.' The same yelling is 'standard setting' when the team wins and 'bullying' when it loses.
  • Cover / the numbers are good
    Good results function as moral cover that reclassifies misconduct as excellence; 'What changed was the cover the numbers were providing.' Impunity ends when the cover collapses (results fall) or is pierced (visibility spikes), not when the behavior changes.
  • The headline you have not written yet / the verdict is on you
    The closing reframe: the gap between how you judge a high performer's behavior today and how you would judge it if their numbers dropped is a latent scandal (the unwritten headline), and it exposes that your tolerance is a bet on performance — making the observer, not the leader, the true subject of the verdict.

Arguments

Tolerating a high performer's bad behavior is an unadmitted, reversible bet on their continued performance, not a judgment about their character. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Organizations punish behavior in context, and the dominant context is whether the numbers are good.
  • Three contextual filters (top-performer development, trust in motives, the offered explanation) — not the behavior — determine whether harsh conduct reads as abuse or motivation.
  • Across Wachner, Knight, and Jobs the behavior stayed constant while the verdict tracked results and visibility ('Same behavior. Different verdict.').
  • Normalization of deviance: each non-consequence absorbs the deviation into the standard until the deviation is the standard.
Implicit premises:
  • Observers' moral judgments of conduct are causally downstream of outcomes rather than of the acts themselves.
  • Results are the proxy the organization actually substitutes for a fixed behavioral standard.
  • What happened to these famous cases generalizes to ordinary organizations and to the individual reader.
  • Harsh behavior and good results are causally separable — the behavior is treated as constant while results vary independently.
Inferences:
  • If the verdict tracks results and results can turn, then today's lenient verdict is contingent and reversible — functionally a bet on performance.
  • If you would act on the behavior the instant the numbers dropped, you already judge it unacceptable and are merely deferring that judgment behind performance.
Impunity for a harmful high performer ends because the cover changes, not because the conduct does. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Wachner's management style did not soften; a single loss year converted fifteen years of publicly reported brutality into fiduciary evidence.
  • Knight's behavior did not escalate; a leaked practice video and a faded winning record, not worse conduct, produced the firing.
  • Jobs' patterns were catalogued unchanged from 1983 to 2010, yet the verdict only ever moved with the share price and his critics' survival.
Implicit premises:
  • Visibility and results are the two mutable variables in the frame, while the behavior is effectively a constant.
  • Boards, licensees, and regulators act on cover-state, not on ethics, until forced.
Inferences:
  • Since the trigger is a change in cover rather than in conduct, predicting when accountability arrives means watching results and exposure, not the behavior.

Narratives

  • Steve Jobs assembles the entire MobileMe team in Apple's Town Hall auditorium in July 2008 after the cloud service breaks in public, opens with 'Can anyone tell me what MobileMe is supposed to do?', follows a clean answer with 'So why the f--- doesn't it do that?', tells them they should hate each other, names a new executive on the spot, and disbands the team — with no board, HR, or organizational consequence, and the team leaving more committed to fixing the product.
    → The cold-open hook and, later, the limit case. It implicitly supports the propositions that extraordinary results upgrade harsh behavior into legend rather than merely tolerating it, and that sustained success removes any internal critic who could check the leader.
  • Linda Wachner runs Warnaco with openly reported brutality (telling a new executive to start firing people to be taken seriously; keeping an executive waiting three days before a two-minute dismissal) for fifteen years while the stock climbs — and nobody acts. Then in 2000 the company loses $338M, the stock falls 87%, Calvin Klein suddenly calls her 'a cancer on the value and integrity of' the brand, the SEC opens an inventory-misstatement probe, and in 2001 the board fires her without severance.
    → The fast-flip case. It implicitly supports the propositions that the same behavior yields a different verdict once the numbers turn, and that yesterday's 'tough boss' anecdotes become today's fiduciary evidence — 'Same behavior. Different verdict.'
  • Bobby Knight coaches Indiana for 29 years — three NCAA titles, 662 wins — while repeatedly violent (grabbing a player's jersey in 1975, throwing a chair across the floor in 1985, head-butting a freshman in 1994, choking a player in practice in 1997). Each incident draws diminishing institutional response until, in 2000, a leaked CNN/SI practice video and a 'zero tolerance' policy — against a team no longer contending — lead to his firing over a minor arm-grab.
    → The slow-version case that carries Vaughan's normalization of deviance. It implicitly supports the propositions that institutions absorb accumulating transgressions until the deviation is the standard, and that what ends impunity is a change in visibility (and faded results), not an escalation in behavior.
  • Diane Vaughan's account of how NASA — with no shortage of intelligence, expertise, or safety procedures — came to launch the Challenger: each deviation from the standard that produced no catastrophe was quietly absorbed into the new normal, so the organization operated outside its original safety parameters for years while calling it normal.
    → The borrowed conceptual engine rather than a leadership anecdote. It implicitly supplies and legitimizes the 'normalization of deviance' proposition that the Knight case (and the reader's own organization) is then read through.
  • The reader's own ordinary versions of the machine: the high-performing engineer whose 1:1s leave people in tears but whose technical judgment is irreplaceable; the salesperson who hits 140% of quota and openly disrespects legal; the executive whose strategy is brilliant and whose treatment of reports is corrosive — each producing no consequence because the results are good, each non-consequence teaching the organization the standard has moved.
    → The transfer device ('The Uncomfortable Mirror'). It implicitly supports the proposition that the mechanism is general, not a property of famous tyrants, and primes the closing prescription.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why does a leader's bad behavior so often go unpunished inside an organization?
    Because organizations judge behavior in context, not on its own, and good results supply the cover that reclassifies harsh conduct as excellence; the non-consequence itself ratifies the behavior as the new standard.
  • ✅ Why did nothing ever stop leaders like Jobs, Wachner, or Knight despite the behavior being public and constant?
    Because the verdict tracked their results and visibility, not their conduct; while they were winning, the three filters (performance, trust, explanation) were pinned in their favor and no critic could confront both the person and the numbers.
  • ✅ What actually changes when the impunity finally ends?
    The cover changes — results collapse (Wachner) or visibility spikes (Knight) — while the behavior stays the same: 'The behavior had not escalated. The cover had collapsed.'
  • ✅ What should a leader do about a tolerated high performer whose conduct is harmful?
    Run the counterfactual — name the person you would act on if their numbers dropped 20% — and recognize that current tolerance is a bet on performance, not a character judgment; the verdict is really on the observer.
  • ⭕ How do you distinguish behavior that genuinely produces high performance from behavior that is simply abusive but co-occurs with success?
    The essay collapses 'harsh' and 'abusive,' invokes the 'master motivator' reading only to dismiss it as beholder-relative, and never asks whether the standards sometimes cause the results — which would change the normative verdict. The causal question is left unaddressed.

Gaps

  • incompleteSelection and survivorship bias in the evidence: all three cases are famous winners who eventually lost, so the pattern is fitted to confirming instances. The essay never examines high performers whose harsh behavior was checked WHILE they were still winning, or abusers who failed early and were never prosecuted — so the claim that results are effectively the only variable is illustrated, not tested against disconfirming cases or base rates.
    Read next: Comparative and base-rate evidence on when abusive-but-successful leaders are and are not sanctioned; cases of accountability arriving during a winning streak.
  • incompleteThe causal relationship between harsh standards and good results is asserted away rather than examined. The whole argument depends on treating behavior as constant while results vary independently ('the behavior stayed the same'), but it never engages the possibility it raises and drops — the 'master motivator' reading — that in some cases the harshness partly produces the performance, which would move the normative verdict from 'unadmitted bet' toward 'defensible trade-off.'
    Read next: Empirical literature on abusive supervision and performance — whether harsh leadership causally improves or degrades team outcomes, and under what conditions.
  • incompleteThe prescription stops at self-diagnosis. It tells the reader to write down a name and ask the harder question, but never says what to DO with the answer: whether and how to act on a tolerated high performer before the numbers force it, how to weigh their irreplaceable contribution against the harm, or how to break normalization of deviance without simply waiting for results to fade.
    Read next: Practical methods for holding high-performing-but-harmful people accountable while retaining their value; interventions that reset an eroded behavioral standard.
  • incompleteThe account is written entirely from the observer's chair. The people harmed — the tearful 1:1s, the disrespected legal team, the choked player — appear only as instruments of the argument, with no attention to their voice, exit, or redress, and no account of remedy for the harm the machine normalizes.
    Read next: Literature on organizational voice, whistleblowing, and abusive supervision from the target's perspective; how harmed parties surface or escape the normalized standard.
