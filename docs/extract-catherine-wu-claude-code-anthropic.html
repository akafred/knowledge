Key terms

  • product taste Taste & Judgment
    The ability to decide what to build, how to build it delightfully, and which of thousands of requests are worth pursuing — includes judging model output quality
  • AGI pilled Riding Model Improvements
    The degree to which you build your product assuming future superintelligent models vs. optimizing for current model capabilities
  • research preview
    A branded early release mechanism that reduces commitment — features ship in 1-2 weeks with explicit framing that they may not persist
  • harness Agent Harness
    The product scaffolding (prompts, tools, UI) built around the model to elicit maximum capability from its current level
  • golden path Golden Path
    The optimal interaction pattern that aligns with the model's strengths and compensates for its weaknesses
  • thinking words
    The verbs displayed in the UI when Claude is processing (e.g., 'manifesting')
  • the model will eat your harness for breakfast Riding Model Improvements
    As models improve, product scaffolding built to compensate for model weaknesses becomes unnecessary and should be removed

Arguments

PMs need to radically compress the time from idea to shipped product rather than focusing on multi-quarter alignment. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • AI has accelerated engineering velocity dramatically
  • Model capabilities improve every few months, invalidating long-term plans
  • Feature timelines have compressed from 6 months to days
Implicit premises:
  • The competitive landscape punishes slow shipping
  • User expectations now include rapid iteration
  • Traditional PM coordination overhead is a net negative when iteration is cheap
Inferences:
  • The PM who spends months aligning roadmaps is destroying value
  • Research preview as a shipping mechanism is a rational response to this speed imperative
Product taste is the most durable and valuable skill as AI makes code cheap to produce. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Code is becoming much cheaper to write
  • Tens of thousands of GitHub issues request every feature imaginable
  • The hard part is deciding what to build and the right UX
Implicit premises:
  • Execution bottlenecks are shifting from implementation to decision-making
  • Taste cannot easily be automated or replicated by models
Inferences:
  • Engineering background helps with prioritization (knowing difficulty) but is not the enduring differentiator
  • Any background can develop taste, so the skill is not locked to a specific career path
Each new model generation should trigger a systematic review and removal of product scaffolding. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Earlier models needed a to-do list tool to complete all call sites in a refactor
  • Opus 4+ naturally uses the to-do list without prompting
  • The team reads the entire system prompt with each model launch and removes unnecessary sections
Implicit premises:
  • Accumulated scaffolding creates product complexity that harms new users
  • Keeping unnecessary scaffolding wastes tokens and adds confusion
Inferences:
  • The product should converge toward simplicity over time as models improve
  • Features that were essential crutches become optional niceties or should be deprecated
Anthropic's unified safety mission is a competitive advantage because it accelerates decision-making. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • When two priorities compete, the team asks which is more important for Anthropic's mission
  • Everyone stands behind the resulting decision
  • Teams willingly sacrifice their own KRs for Anthropic's broader goals
Implicit premises:
  • Most companies lack a single unifying mission strong enough to resolve priority conflicts
  • Decision-making speed compounds over thousands of decisions into a large organizational advantage
Inferences:
  • The mission functions as an organizational coordination mechanism, not just a values statement
  • This explains part of how a late-starting, less-funded company overtook incumbents
Automations must reach 100% reliability to provide real value; 95% is insufficient. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • A 95% automation still requires human monitoring and intervention
  • Building the automation is often slower than doing the task yourself initially
Implicit premises:
  • The value of automation is in freeing cognitive load, which only happens at 100%
  • A 95% automation creates a false sense of security and new failure modes
Inferences:
  • The last 5% of automation reliability is where the majority of the user value lives
  • Most people abandon automations prematurely and never capture the real benefit

Narratives

  • An engineer noticed that early Claude Code would start a 20-call-site refactor but only complete 5. Sid on the team added a to-do list tool inspired by VS Code's 'find all references' list. This forced Claude to track and complete all sites. With Opus 4+, the model naturally uses the to-do list without prompting, making the crutch optional.
    → Illustrates how product scaffolding compensates for model weaknesses and becomes unnecessary as models improve
  • Cat Wu used Co-work to prepare a slide deck for the Code with Claude conference. She connected Google Drive, Slack, and other sources, gave Co-work a prompt describing the narrative she wanted, and it worked for an hour — searching Twitter, internal launch channels, and demo channels — producing a 20-page deck in Anthropic's design system. She reviewed it the next morning, gave one round of feedback (too wordy), and had a near-final product.
    → Demonstrates Co-work's capability for non-code knowledge work and the PM's role as decision-maker on top of AI-generated options
  • A sales team member built a custom web app that pulls from Salesforce, Gong, and internal notes to auto-generate tailored customer decks. It customizes slides based on the customer's deployment (Bedrock, Vertex, Claude for Enterprise), their concerns (code review, HIPAA compliance), and removes irrelevant slides — reducing 20-30 minutes of manual work to seconds.
    → Illustrates the 'surge in personalized work software' enabled by Claude Code lowering the barrier to building custom apps
  • Applied AI team members use Co-work to prepare for 5-10 customer meetings per day. The night before, they ask Co-work to summarize upcoming meetings, pull customer history, surface action items from past meetings, and research answers to open questions — producing a dossier they review before each call.
    → Illustrates Co-work adoption beyond engineering, in a technical go-to-market role
  • The Claude Code source code leaked because a human working with Claude to write a PR made an error that passed through two layers of human review. Anthropic kept the person on the team, treated it as a process failure, and hardened safeguards.
    → Illustrates Anthropic's blameless culture and process-improvement orientation
  • Code review was attempted multiple times with earlier models but accuracy was insufficient. Only with Opus 4.5/4.6 and Sonnet 4.6 did the team feel confident enough that multiple code review agents could traverse the entire codebase and synthesize real issues — to the point where the engineering team now relies on it before merging PRs.
    → Illustrates the strategy of building products at the edge of current capability and waiting for models to catch up

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How does Anthropic's product team ship so fast?
    Clear goals, research preview as a low-commitment launch mechanism, tight engineering-marketing-docs process, and a culture that empowers any individual to go from idea to shipped product in under a week.
  • ✅ What is the future of the PM role in an AI-native world?
    Roles are merging. Product taste — deciding what to build and judging quality — is the durable skill. Engineering background helps short-term for difficulty estimation. The PM who can wear many hats and has low ego about role boundaries will thrive.
  • ✅ How should product teams handle the tension between building for current model capabilities vs. future AGI-level models?
    Be 'the right amount of AGI pilled' — build harnesses that elicit maximum value from current models while prototyping features that don't fully work yet. With each model upgrade, systematically remove scaffolding and test new capabilities.
  • ✅ Why has Anthropic succeeded despite starting behind OpenAI?
    Two factors: (1) a unifying safety mission that accelerates cross-org decision-making and enables teams to sacrifice local goals for company goals, and (2) intense organizational focus.
  • ✅ When should you use Claude Code vs. Desktop vs. Mobile vs. Co-work?
    Claude Code CLI for most powerful coding features; Desktop for front-end work with visual preview and a graphical interface; Mobile for kicking off tasks on the go; Co-work for non-code outputs (slides, docs, Slack, email).
  • ✅ What are the costs of shipping this fast?
    Product consistency suffers — overlapping features, new users confused about best paths, users feel pressure to check Twitter daily for updates. Acknowledged as a real cost they're trying to mitigate (e.g., /powerup onboarding).
  • ✅ How should knowledge workers adapt to AI tools?
    Automate repetitive tasks, invest in getting automations to 100% reliability, build tools you actually use daily rather than one-off prototypes, and avoid over-customization.
