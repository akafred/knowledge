Why Effective Leaders Get Branded as Problems

Luis Velasquez · article · 2026-05-07 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Organizations systematically misdiagnose leadership friction by defaulting to behavioral explanations — the 'evaluation trap' — when the actual source is more often historical reputation, strength overextension, or systemic constraints, and this misdiagnosis causes them to neutralize their most effective leaders.

practical · diagnostic-prescriptive — An executive coaching practitioner's diagnostic framework for leadership friction. Structured as problem identification (the evaluation trap), a four-part taxonomy of friction sources, and a prescriptive diagnostic protocol. Draws on coaching case studies and references psychology research on attribution bias.

Key terms

  • evaluation trap
    The systematic organizational tendency to default to behavioral explanations for leadership friction, misdiagnosing context-driven problems as individual deficits
  • organizational drift
    When an organization relies on outdated narratives and historical reputation rather than current evidence to evaluate a leader
  • overextension of identity
    A leader's core strength becomes a constraint when used beyond its appropriate context — the capability that worked at Level N becomes insufficient or counterproductive at Level N+1
  • system as blocker
    When organizational culture, incentives, structures, or resources actively prevent a leader from executing what is asked of them, making the leader appear problematic

Propositions

  • explicitOrganizations default to behavioral explanations for leadership friction because behavior is the most visible signal and competency models reinforce behavioral framing.
  • explicitAt the senior level, true skill deficits are the minority cause of leadership friction, yet they are the cause organizations default to most often.
  • explicitOnce a leader is incorrectly labeled, every subsequent organizational decision reinforces that label, creating a self-fulfilling cycle.
  • explicitMisdiagnosis causes organizations to select for comfortable alignment over uncomfortable friction, systematically weakening leadership quality.
  • explicitSubjective leadership assessments are more vulnerable to bias than objective performance measures, yet organizations rely heavily on subjective assessments for leadership evaluation.
  • implicitThe same observable behavior can signal four fundamentally different underlying problems, making surface-level behavioral diagnosis unreliable.
  • implicitLeaders who create productive friction are disproportionately valuable and disproportionately at risk of being misdiagnosed.

Arguments

The evaluation trap is a strategic risk, not merely an HR process flaw.
Behavior change alone cannot fix historical reputation problems.
Overextension of identity requires expanding range, not suppressing strengths.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why do effective leaders get branded as problems?
    Because organizations default to behavioral explanations (the evaluation trap), over-attributing friction to individuals while underweighting context.
  • ✅ What are the distinct sources of leadership friction beyond individual behavior?
    Four sources: true skill deficit, historical reputation (organizational drift), overextension of identity, and system as blocker.
  • ✅ How can organizations distinguish between a true skill gap, outdated reputation, strength overextension, and systemic constraints?
    A four-step diagnostic protocol: ground in specific examples, validate recency, distinguish gap from overextension, and reset the conversation with the leader.
  • ✅ What diagnostic steps can prevent misdiagnosis of leadership friction?
    Focus on specific recent examples, validate recency of feedback data, distinguish skill gaps from strength overuse, and explicitly frame the conversation around which source is primary.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe four-source taxonomy is presented as exhaustive but no argument is made for completeness. Interpersonal dynamics (e.g., genuine personality clashes, political conflicts, or power struggles) are not addressed as a distinct category and do not fit neatly into the four sources.
  • incompleteThe article provides no guidance for the common case where multiple friction sources are simultaneously primary. The diagnostic protocol treats them as distinguishable, but the simple heuristic at the end (consistent/recent = skill gap, outdated = drift, strong performance + friction = overextension, environment blocks change = system) does not address compound cases.
  • uninformedThe claim that true skill deficits are the 'minority of situations at the senior level' rests entirely on the author's coaching experience. No data, frequency estimates, or external research is cited to support this base-rate claim, which is load-bearing for the entire argument.
  • illogicalThere is a circularity risk in the diagnostic protocol: the article argues that organizations default to blaming the individual, then provides a framework where three of four sources exonerate the individual. The framework may encode the author's coaching bias (clients are usually right, systems are usually wrong) as a structural feature rather than testing it.
