How to Build a Superteam That Keeps Getting Better

Harvard Business Review · article · 2026-05-01 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Agile & XPFeedback LoopsFounder ModeGiving Feedback (Performance & Coaching)Hands-On Leadership & the PendulumLeader RestraintLearning Culture & SlackProduct Discovery & ExperimentationPsychological SafetyTrust
In periods of rapid change, teams win not because they start with the best plans or the most talent but because their leaders build a continuous-improvement culture — one that makes experimentation, curiosity, early problem-surfacing, hands-on involvement, non-threatening feedback, and growth-oriented investment in people the daily default — so the team's rate of learning, not its starting position, determines whether it becomes a 'superteam.'

practical · mixed (narrative + expository + argumentative) — A prescriptive HBR piece adapted from Ron Friedman's book 'Superteams': it opens and closes with a sustained narrative (the Oklahoma City Thunder), states a survey-grounded thesis, then organizes seven numbered leadership practices, each argued via a survey statistic plus one or two corroborating organizational anecdotes.

Key terms

  • Superteam
    Operationalized purely by self-report: a team whose members rate it 10/10 on both effectiveness and comparison to industry peers. Substantively, in this piece, the label is anchored to the third of three shared strengths — teams that are 'constantly building new skills and improving over time' — distinguishing it from a merely good team that has strong current talent or a strong current plan but is not accelerating.
  • Continuous improvement culture
    Not a single practice but the composite of seven leader behaviors (experimentation, admitted uncertainty, surfaced blockers, hands-on involvement, non-threatening feedback, unconditional growth investment, shared meaning) that together keep a team's rate of learning high regardless of its starting talent or plan.
  • Founder Mode Founder Mode
    Borrowed from Paul Graham/Brian Chesky: leaders staying personally involved in the work rather than delegating and stepping back, deployed here approvingly as evidence for practice 4 ('roll up your sleeves').
  • Involvement vs. micromanagement
    Distinguished by intent and impact: involvement builds team members' capacity by working shoulder to shoulder; micromanagement hovers, corrects, and takes over, leaving people dependent. Asserted as the operational line for practice 4, but never given a concrete behavioral test.

Arguments

Teams win in periods of rapid change through a continuous-improvement culture their leaders build, not through superior starting plans or talent. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • A survey of 6,000+ knowledge workers finds 'superteams' share three strengths, the third being continuous skill-building.
  • Across seven behavioral dimensions (experimentation, admitted uncertainty, surfaced blockers, hands-on involvement, non-threatening feedback, unconditional growth investment, shared meaning), leaders of superteams differ from average-team leaders by large, consistent percentages.
  • The Oklahoma City Thunder achieved two separate rapid rebuilds (2008-2012 and 2022-2025) via deliberate, repeated willingness to abandon working formulas (trading All-Stars in their prime, unconventional lineups) rather than by starting with superior talent or a fixed plan.
Implicit premises:
  • Self-rated 10/10 team effectiveness plus industry-comparison validly identifies genuinely high-performing teams, and this generalizes across the wide range of industries surveyed.
  • LOAD-BEARING: the direction of causation runs leader-behavior -> team-learning -> performance, not the reverse — yet a team that is already succeeding may simply have more slack to admit uncertainty, ask questions, experiment, and take risks, and all seven differentiating behaviors are self-reported by the same respondents who rated their own team's performance.
  • The Thunder is a representative illustration of the thesis rather than a survivorship-biased success story selected because it worked; no comparably resourced team that experimented as boldly and failed is discussed.
  • The thesis that talent and plans matter less than learning rate is compatible with (rather than in tension with) the article's own examples, several of which (Meta, Netflix, Amazon, LinkedIn, the Thunder's later roster including Shai Gilgeous-Alexander) are also unusually talent-dense organizations.
Inferences:
  • From 'these behaviors distinguish self-rated top teams from average teams' plus 'these are learning-oriented behaviors' it infers 'learning rate is the differentiator' — a correlational-to-causal leap common to the survey research program this piece draws on.
  • From repeated Thunder rebuilds it generalizes a single organization's case to a portable, cross-domain leadership formula ('any team can cultivate them, whether in basketball, business, or beyond').
Leaders should stay personally, hands-on involved in the work rather than purely delegating and overseeing. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Sam Presti built his reputation doing scouting work others avoided, and continues to do so despite having a full department for it.
  • Superteam leaders are more likely than average-team leaders to jump in and contribute to the work itself rather than only handing off tasks and overseeing.
  • Brian Chesky's reimmersion in Airbnb's product and customer experience, popularized as 'Founder Mode,' is credited with the company's post-pandemic rally.
  • A Journal of Organizational Behavior study found managers who work alongside their teams report more energy and effectiveness; those who manage from a distance report more stress and exhaustion.
Implicit premises:
  • Involvement and micromanagement are reliably distinguishable in practice by 'intent and impact' alone, though the article supplies no test for applying that distinction in the moment.
  • The cited study's finding about manager energy/stress generalizes to team-level performance outcomes, not only to the manager's own wellbeing.
  • An NBA general manager's personal scouting and a startup founder's product reimmersion during an existential crisis generalize to the ordinary people-manager's 'rolling up sleeves,' despite very different contexts, stakes, and skill requirements.
Inferences:
  • From 'superteam leaders are more involved' plus 'involved managers report less burnout' it infers 'involvement causes better team outcomes,' though the cited research measures manager stress and energy, not team performance.

Narratives

  • The Oklahoma City Thunder's rebuild from a 58-loss season in 2022 to NBA champion in 2025, and its earlier 2008-2012 rebuild to the Finals after relocating from Seattle — both achieved with unusually young rosters, willingness to trade away All-Stars in their prime, and unconventional lineups (two centers, no center, three point guards).
    → The anchoring case for the entire article: recurs at the open, and once within each of the seven practices (Presti's scouting, Daigneault's coaching philosophy, the memorial ritual), functioning as the illustrative spine that makes the survey statistics feel concrete.
  • Brian Chesky's post-pandemic reimmersion in Airbnb's product decisions after realizing 'hire smart people and step aside' had backfired, popularized by Paul Graham as 'Founder Mode.'
    → Corroborates practice 4 (roll up your sleeves) with a named, well-known business turnaround, generalizing the Thunder's hands-on GM pattern to the corporate world.
  • Adobe's replacement of annual performance reviews with short, informal check-ins, reporting 80,000 saved work hours, a 34% drop in voluntary turnover, and an eight-point rise in employees saying feedback helps them perform.
    → Supplies a named-company, quantified outcome for practice 5's feedback claims, the article's most concrete non-Thunder evidence.
  • Haberman, a Minneapolis marketing agency, gives employees $1,000 and three paid days off yearly for personal passions unrelated to work.
    → Illustrates practice 6's 'growth without payoff' claim with a small-company policy example, paired with founder Sarah Haberman's own rationale.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What do the best (fastest-improving) teams do differently?
    Seven specific leader behaviors: more experimentation, contagious curiosity, surfacing blockers, hands-on involvement, supportive feedback, growth investment without guaranteed payoff, and leading with meaning.
  • ⭕ Does close connection/relatedness, protected focus/attention, or continuous-improvement/learning best explain superteam performance?
    Not engaged as a competing-theories question at all; the article simply develops the third of the three strengths its own survey identified (learning/improvement) without acknowledging that the same underlying research also identifies the other two (efficient time/energy/attention use; members improving one another) as co-equal strengths, each independently expanded elsewhere.
  • ✅ Is hands-on ('Founder Mode') leadership productive or counterproductive?
    Productive: presented uncritically as a superteam trait, backed by the Presti/Chesky narratives and the cited JOB study.
  • ⭕ Does the survey's self-report design (10/10 self-rating, self-reported leader behaviors) risk halo/common-method bias?
    Not addressed anywhere in the piece.

Gaps

  • incompleteSame self-report / common-method-bias structure as this corpus's sibling superteam pieces: 'superteam' status is defined entirely by members' own 10/10 rating, and all seven behavioral differentiators (experimentation, curiosity, surfacing problems, involvement, feedback quality, growth support, meaning) are ALSO self-reported by those same members. The correlation could be a halo effect (teams that feel good about themselves report more positive everything on every dimension) rather than evidence that these specific leader behaviors cause learning or performance.
    Read next: Objective or third-party performance measures paired with longitudinal or experimental designs; this corpus's own critique of the identical design flaw in the companion HBR pieces on connection and on protected focus, both drawn from the same or a sibling 6,000-worker survey.
  • uninformedCitations are underspecified throughout: the Journal of Organizational Behavior study on manager involvement gives no sample size, effect size, or definition of 'manage from a distance'; the Frontiers in Psychology moonlighting study names a sample size (324) but no effect size or engagement instrument; every leader-behavior percentage (33%, 43%, 53%, 56%, 59%, 90%+) is reported without a confidence interval, base rate, or description of how 'average team' leaders were sampled.
    Read next: The original Journal of Organizational Behavior and Frontiers in Psychology papers directly; Ron Friedman's book 'Superteams' (Simon & Schuster, 2026), from which this article is adapted, for full survey methodology.
  • illogicalSurvivorship bias in case selection: every illustrative organization (Thunder, Meta, Netflix, Amazon, Spotify, LinkedIn, Microsoft, Airbnb, Adobe, Estée Lauder, HubSpot, Keap, Basecamp, Haberman) is a documented success story for the practice it illustrates. No case of a team that experimented boldly and failed, admitted uncertainty and lost credibility, or had a hands-on leader who tipped into micromanagement is discussed, so each practice's downside risk and failure rate are invisible by construction.
    Read next: Base-rate and survivorship-bias literature (e.g., Mlodinow, Taleb) applied to management case studies; accounts of experimentation cultures or hands-on leadership that did not pay off.
  • incompleteThe article repackages well-established learning-organization and psychological-safety research (Edmondson's psychological safety construct, Senge's learning organizations, Argyris's work on defensive routines and double-loop learning) under the 'superteam'/'continuous improvement' brand, adding survey percentages and a sports narrative, without citing any of these lineages by name or engaging what, if anything, is mechanistically new versus a repackaging with fresh statistics.
    Read next: Edmondson's 'The Fearless Organization'; Senge's 'The Fifth Discipline'; Argyris on double-loop learning and defensive routines — the clear intellectual ancestors of practices 2, 3, and 5 that this piece never names.
  • incompleteThe involvement-vs-micromanagement line ('comes down to intent and impact') is asserted as the safeguard against practice 4 curdling into harmful over-involvement, but no behavioral test, warning sign, or transition point is given for a leader (or reader) to self-diagnose which side of the line they are on — precisely the ambiguity that makes 'Founder Mode' controversial elsewhere.
    Read next: Situational-leadership and delegation literature (Hersey-Blanchard) for readiness-based criteria; critiques of 'Founder Mode' that specify when hands-on involvement curdles into harmful control.

Closes gaps elsewhere

  • The Best Leaders Embrace the Role of Supporting Character · g1 — That gap flags that the critiqued piece never distinguishes contexts where self-centered/hands-on leadership might be appropriate from where it is harmful. This document supplies the missing distinguishing criterion directly: 'intent and impact' — hands-on involvement that builds team members' capacity (Presti, Chesky) versus micromanagement that hovers, corrects, and takes over, leaving people dependent. This doesn't resolve the underlying contradiction between the two pieces, but it gives the behavioral line the gap says is missing.
  • The Office Amenity That Actually Improves Teamwork · g2 — That gap asks for a syntopical comparison of 'competing single-factor theories of team effectiveness' (protected focus vs. social connection vs. psychological safety). This document reveals the underlying integrated three-factor superteams framework — efficient time/energy/attention use, members improving one another, continuous skill-building — from the same research program of which office-amenity's focus-driver and 5-things' connection-driver are each an excerpt of one facet. The apparent rivalry dissolves: these are complementary components of one model, not competing explanations, though none of the three articles acknowledges the other two facets.
