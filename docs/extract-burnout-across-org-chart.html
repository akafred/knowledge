Burnout Looks Different Across the Org Chart. Watch for These Signs.

Daisy Auger-Dominguez · article · 2026-04 source 🖥️

Burnout & Recovery
Burnout is not a uniform condition but a systemic design failure that manifests differently at each organizational level -- as invisible overload for early-career employees, structural compression for managers, moral injury for executives, and identity collapse for founders -- and therefore requires level-specific structural interventions rather than generic wellness programs.

practical · prescriptive management article — A practitioner framework article that taxonomizes burnout by org-chart level and offers targeted interventions. Draws on the author's CPO experience and selected research, but is fundamentally a persuasion piece aimed at leaders, not a research report.

Key terms

  • design failure Burnout & Recovery
    Burnout caused by how work, decisions, and incentives are structured -- not by individual lack of resilience.
  • moral injury Burnout & Recovery
    Executive-level burnout caused by repeatedly acting against one's values under organizational pressure; borrowed from Jonathan Shay's combat-veteran research.
  • responsibility without authority
    The structural condition of managers who bear accountability for outcomes but lack decision-making power or resources to control them.
  • identity collapse Burnout & Recovery
    Founder/nonprofit burnout where the leader's personal worth becomes indistinguishable from organizational survival.
  • invisible labor / invisible overload
    The unrecognized cognitive work early-career employees do to decode norms, expectations, and unspoken rules.
  • structural compression
    The squeeze on middle managers from competing demands above and below with insufficient authority to resolve them.

Arguments

Burnout requires level-specific structural interventions, not universal wellness programs. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Burnout manifests differently at each organizational level.
  • Generic fixes apply broad solutions to deeply specific problems.
  • The drivers differ: ambiguity (junior), compression (manager), moral injury (executive), identity collapse (founder).
Implicit premises:
  • Organizations currently default to one-size-fits-all burnout responses.
  • Leaders have the power and responsibility to redesign systems rather than merely support individuals.
Inferences:
  • If different levels have different drivers, a single intervention cannot address all of them.
  • Therefore interventions must be designed per level and target structural causes.
Burnout is a design failure, not a personal failure. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Capable, committed people are exhausting themselves.
  • Poor workflows create constant urgency.
  • Misaligned incentives normalize exhaustion.
  • Burnout persists despite individual effort.
Implicit premises:
  • If individually competent people systematically fail, the system is at fault rather than the individuals.
  • Organizations have a duty to design sustainable work, not merely to hire resilient people.
Inferences:
  • When capable individuals burn out across an organization, the common factor is the system, not the people.
Executive burnout is a form of moral injury analogous to what combat veterans experience. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Executives are asked to act against their values.
  • Jonathan Shay's research shows prolonged value conflict leads to disengagement and cynicism.
  • Workplace research shows similar patterns of role conflict and value misalignment.
Implicit premises:
  • The moral-injury framework from military psychology transfers meaningfully to corporate executive contexts.
  • Executive 'composure' masks the same psychological dynamics that appear in trauma research.
Inferences:
  • The analogy implies executives may need trauma-informed support, not just peer coaching.

Narratives

  • A nonprofit leader who said 'If I rest, I worry no one will carry the mission.'

  • Author's two decades as Chief People Officer witnessing burnout patterns during growth, crisis, and transformation.
    → Establishes practitioner authority and frames the taxonomy as empirically grounded in lived observation.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Is burnout a single condition with a single solution?
    No. Burnout varies by organizational level; it requires differentiated, structural responses.
  • ✅ How does burnout manifest at each level of the org chart?
    Four distinct patterns: invisible overload (junior), compression (manager), moral injury (executive), identity collapse (founder/nonprofit).
  • ✅ What can leaders do to address burnout structurally?
    Level-specific interventions plus two cross-cutting categories: system design fixes and structural/incentive fixes.
  • ⭕ How do we detect burnout before it becomes crisis?
    The article lists signs per level but does not offer a measurement or early-warning system.

Gaps

  • evidentialThe four-level taxonomy is presented as experiential observation, not validated by comparative research. No data is offered on whether these categories are exhaustive or whether burnout in, say, individual contributors at mid-career differs from managers.
    Read next: Organizational psychology literature on burnout typologies (e.g., Maslach Burnout Inventory segmented by role level).
  • implicit premiseThe moral-injury analogy (Shay's combat-veteran research applied to executives) assumes transferability across vastly different contexts. Corporate value conflict, while real, operates under different stakes, voluntariness, and exit options than military service.
    Read next: Critique of moral-injury concept inflation; Shay's own boundary conditions for the framework.
  • scopeThe article focuses entirely on white-collar, knowledge-worker organizations. Burnout in frontline, manual, or gig-economy work -- where control is even lower -- is not addressed.
    Read next: Burnout research in healthcare, service, and gig-economy workers for comparison.
  • mechanismThe article asserts that burnout is a 'design failure' but does not explain how organizations should diagnose which design elements are failing, nor how to prioritize among the many recommended interventions.
    Read next: Organizational diagnostics frameworks; systems-thinking approaches to workplace redesign.
  • counterfactualNo acknowledgment that some burnout may genuinely stem from individual factors (clinical depression, personality traits, life circumstances) even within well-designed systems. The framing is entirely structural.
    Read next: Clinical psychology literature on individual vulnerability factors in burnout; interaction models.
