Communicating with Confidence When You're Under Pressure

HBR.org · podcast transcript · 2026-05-07 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

Decision-Making PracticesLeader Self-Awareness
Effective communication under pressure requires shifting focus from your internal state (fatigue, stress, emotional resistance) to the outcome you want to create and the impact on your audience — and this shift is enabled by self-awareness practices (checking in before you speak), structural discipline (anchoring to purpose, being concise), and treating self-care as something that happens outside the communication moment so you can prioritize the other person during it.

practical · conversation/coaching dialogue — Podcast transcript from HBR On Leadership (Episode 160), originally aired on Women at Work. A moderated conversation between hosts Amy Bernstein and Amy Gallo and leadership coach Muriel Wilkins, structured around audience questions. Primarily prescriptive — offers actionable techniques for communicating under stress, fatigue, and emotional difficulty.

Key terms

  • go/no-go decision Decision-Making Practices
    The conscious pre-communication check-in to determine whether you are in a fit state to engage effectively, including managing the follow-up conversation
  • anchor / anchoring Leader Self-Awareness
    A focal point (the other person's voice, your purpose, the meeting's agenda) that keeps you grounded and prevents emotional drift during communication
  • holding the tension Leader Self-Awareness
    The defining act of leadership: simultaneously holding contradictory demands (the message vs. your values, the organization's needs vs. people's feelings) without collapsing to one side
  • doing it with ease vs. taking the easy way out
    Taking the easy way out = choosing the externally easier action (email instead of face-to-face) to avoid discomfort. Doing it with ease = achieving inner calm through preparation and acceptance so you can face the harder external action
  • the story
    The narrative you construct about how others will react to your message, built from past experiences, which amplifies emotional resistance and delays action

Propositions

  • explicitThe most important communication skill is listening to understand, not listening to respond, because it prevents reactivity.
  • explicitBefore any important communication, you must check in with yourself about your emotional and physical state — most people skip this step entirely.
  • explicitEach person has different emotional states that degrade their communication; you must know your own specific thresholds.
  • explicitCommunication readiness includes not just delivering the message but having energy for the full conversation that follows.
  • explicitWhen you cannot defer, anchor in the outcome and the 'why' rather than how you feel — take it outside of yourself.
  • explicitYou cannot anchor in outcome perpetually without self-care; doing so leads to burnout.
  • explicitThe outcome has two dimensions: the task/content outcome and the relational outcome (how you want them to feel about you).
  • explicitConfidence is not a feeling to wait for but a state you prepare for — 'don't wait till you're on the field to warm up.'
  • explicitLess is more for appearing confident: focus on key messages, be concise, stay structured.
  • explicitNot everything requires a response; choosing not to respond is often a boundary on yourself, not the other person.
  • explicitImpatience arises from the gap between where you want to be (point B) and where you are (point A); getting upset about the gap doesn't close it.
  • explicitTo communicate effectively you must meet people where they are, not run 10 miles ahead and expect them to hear you.
  • explicitEmotional resistance to delivering a message comes from the accumulation of stories about how recipients will react, built from past experience.
  • explicitWhen delivering a message you don't believe in, you can hold the 'and' — deliver the message while expressing your own values through tone and compassion.
  • explicitLeadership is fundamentally about holding tension; the role itself is the tension.
  • explicitWhether to be transparent about your stress/fatigue should depend on impact on the audience, not on whether it makes you feel better.
  • explicitSelf-care should happen outside communication moments, not through avoidance within them (e.g., choosing email over face-to-face to dodge discomfort).
  • explicitThe greater the stretch and aspiration placed on people, the more radical the appreciation needs to be.
  • explicitTo authentically express appreciation, start with authentic intent; if the intent is inauthentic, any expression will 'smell' inauthentic.

Arguments

Prioritizing listening over speaking is the most important communication practice, especially under pressure.
Pre-communication self-assessment is the first and most neglected step in communicating under pressure.
When communication is unavoidable, anchoring in purpose and outcome — rather than internal state — is the primary coping mechanism, with the caveat that it must be intermittent.
Apparent confidence under pressure is a product of pre-work and structural technique, not an innate state.
Effective persuasion under pressure means accepting the audience's current position and working from there, which requires managing your own frustration first.
To cut through emotional resistance, recognize the story you're telling yourself, separate it from the purpose, and if needed, use a 'phone-a-friend' reality check.
A leader can deliver a message they disagree with without compromising integrity by holding the 'and' — the organizational message and their own values simultaneously.
During high-stakes communication, the leader's job is to prioritize the other person's experience; self-regulation and recovery happen before and after, not during.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ How do you know if you're in the right frame of mind to deliver an important message?
    Check in with yourself — identify emotions, energy, preparedness. Know your personal thresholds. Include follow-up conversation energy in the assessment.
  • ✅ How do you communicate that you need time/space when your team is ready to engage?
    Simply ask — check in with them rather than assuming they must proceed now. The worst case is they say no, which is what you expected anyway.
  • ✅ How do you rise to the moment when you must communicate and you're depleted?
    Anchor in the outcome and the 'why' — shift focus from how you feel to what you're trying to achieve, including both task and relational outcomes.
  • ✅ How do you appear confident and stable when you're not feeling it?
    Prepare before the moment, focus on key messages, be concise (less is more), ask questions to buy pauses, stay structured, bring conversation back to purpose.
  • ✅ How do you handle impatience when your audience isn't getting your message?
    Recognize impatience as frustration at the gap between A and B. Meet people where they are. Step back to assumptions, reframe, or ask what they're hearing.
  • ✅ How do you cut through emotional resistance to sending a difficult message?
    Recognize the accumulated stories driving the resistance, separate story from purpose, focus on the outcome, and phone a friend for a reality check.
  • ✅ How do you deliver a message you don't believe in?
    Try to negotiate the message. If that fails, voice it in your own words aligned with your values — hold the 'and' of message plus compassion. If it violates core values, draw the line.
  • ✅ When is it appropriate to be transparent about your stress or fatigue?
    It depends on audience impact — will it humanize you or destabilize them? Make a conscious choice based on their needs, not yours.
  • ✅ When is it acceptable to use email instead of face-to-face for difficult messages?
    Only when the exchange is genuinely transactional. For relational or high-impact messages, face-to-face honors the person; do self-care outside the communication moment.
  • ✅ How do you express genuine appreciation and gratitude as a leader?
    Start with authentic intent, then find an expression style comfortable for you. The greater the stretch on people, the more radical the appreciation needs to be.

Gaps

  • incompleteThe conversation offers no framework for organizational or structural conditions that make communication under pressure chronic — all advice is individualistic (self-awareness, self-care, preparation). There is no discussion of reducing the systemic conditions (overwork, meeting overload, unrealistic demands) that create the pressure in the first place.
  • incompleteThe distinction between 'doing it with ease' and 'taking the easy way out' is presented as a clear binary, but no guidance is offered for the gray zone — situations where face-to-face is marginally better but the leader is genuinely at risk of harmful reactivity. The advice to do self-care 'outside the communication moment' assumes sufficient recovery time exists.
  • uninformedNo reference to research on cognitive depletion, ego depletion, or decision fatigue — the conversation relies entirely on experiential wisdom and coaching anecdotes. The claim that you can reliably override fatigue by anchoring in purpose contradicts some findings on cognitive resource limits.
