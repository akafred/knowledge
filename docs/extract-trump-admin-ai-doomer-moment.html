The Trump administration's AI doomer moment

Casey Newton · rss · 2026-05-05 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

AI BacklashAI Regulation & Governance (External)AI-Driven Vulnerability DiscoveryMythosProject GlasswingSoftware Supply-Chain Security
The Trump administration, having spent over a year dismissing AI safety concerns as fearmongering and regulatory capture, has been forced by the demonstrable national-security risks of Anthropic's Mythos model to quietly revive the very pre-release government review regime it mocked and dismantled under Biden — revealing that accelerationist AI policy collapses when confronted with actual frontier-model capabilities.

practical · political analysis / opinion column — tracks the reversal of the Trump administration's AI policy from deregulatory accelerationism to safety-conscious oversight — Genre: tech-policy journalism with editorial commentary. Narrates a policy U-turn and interprets its causes and likely consequences.

Key terms

  • doomer industrial complex AI Backlash
    David Sacks's pejorative label for AI safety advocates, researchers, and organizations whom he accused of fearmongering to achieve regulatory capture.
  • Mythos Mythos
    Anthropic's latest frontier LLM, currently in limited preview, whose cybersecurity exploit capabilities triggered the administration's policy reversal.
  • pre-release government review AI Regulation & Governance (External)
    A regulatory mechanism requiring AI companies to submit frontier models for government safety evaluation before wide release — the Biden-era approach that Trump revoked and is now reviving.
  • supply chain risk Software Supply-Chain Security
    A legal designation the Trump administration applied to Anthropic after it refused to amend its Pentagon contract to allow 'all lawful use' — used as a punitive tool that now conflicts with the same administration's desire to use Anthropic's technology.
  • Center for AI Standards and Innovation AI Regulation & Governance (External)
    The renamed US AI Safety Institute under Commerce, which will handle the pre-release model reviews — functionally the same body the administration rebranded to signal its anti-safety stance.

Arguments

The administration's accelerationist AI policy has failed on its own terms. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • The administration's position was that AI safety fears were overblown fearmongering.
  • A concrete frontier model (Mythos) has demonstrated capabilities that even the administration acknowledges pose national security risks.
  • The administration is now reviving the exact pre-release review mechanism it dismantled.
Implicit premises:
  • If your policy was correct, you would not need to adopt the policy of your opponents.
Inferences:
  • The reversal itself is the evidence of failure — the administration's actions refute its own prior rhetoric.
The Trump administration holds an internally contradictory position on Anthropic. strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • One set of officials designated Anthropic a 'supply chain risk' and is working to phase out its models over six months.
  • Another set of officials is working to expand agency access to Anthropic's Mythos model throughout government.
Implicit premises:
  • A coherent policy cannot simultaneously exclude and depend on the same supplier.
Inferences:
  • The contradiction reveals that the supply chain risk designation was punitive rather than analytical — it was about the Pentagon contract dispute, not actual risk assessment.
Pre-release government review of AI models is becoming the bipartisan default regardless of which party holds power. strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • Biden introduced pre-release review via executive order.
  • Trump revoked it, then revived a functionally equivalent mechanism.
  • All major AI companies (Google, Microsoft, xAI) have already agreed to comply.
Implicit premises:
  • When both opposing administrations converge on the same policy, the policy is driven by structural necessity rather than ideology.
Inferences:
  • Pre-release review is likely a durable feature of AI governance regardless of future political shifts.

Narratives

  • The administration began with confident dismissal of AI safety ('hand-wringing'), actively dismantled Biden-era safeguards, lobbied against state regulations, and then was forced by Mythos's capabilities to reverse course and adopt the very framework it had mocked — a classical hubris-to-humility arc.
    → Provides the central dramatic structure of the column: overconfidence meets reality, making the policy reversal legible as a story rather than just a policy change.
  • Despite political hostility, the institutional infrastructure for AI safety (the Safety Institute, pre-release review processes, export controls) proved more durable than the political rhetoric against it — renamed and rhetorically disowned but not destroyed, now quietly re-empowered.
    → Suggests that safety institutions have a structural resilience that survives political cycles, supporting the implicit argument that pre-release review is a bipartisan default.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ What caused the Trump administration to reverse its anti-safety AI policy?
    Anthropic's Mythos model demonstrated cybersecurity exploit capabilities that pose national security risks, forcing the administration to acknowledge that safety concerns were substantive.
  • ✅ How does the new pre-release review regime compare to Biden's?
    Functionally similar — companies submit models for government evaluation before wide release, handled by the same institution (renamed). The mechanism is nearly identical.
  • ✅ What is the administration's position on Anthropic, and why is it contradictory?
    One faction designated Anthropic a supply chain risk; another is expanding Mythos access throughout government. The contradiction stems from the punitive designation colliding with genuine need for the technology.
  • ⭕ What are the likely downstream effects of this reversal on state regulation and export controls?
    State regulation moratorium less likely to pass; export controls may tighten with Sacks's departure. But outcomes remain uncertain.
  • ⭕ What risks does a Trump-administered AI licensing regime pose?
    Newton raises the possibility of censorship — denying releases to 'woke' models or pressuring companies politically — but does not assess how likely this is.
  • ⭕ Do other frontier models besides Mythos pose similar national security risks?
    Not addressed — the article focuses exclusively on Mythos as the catalyst.
