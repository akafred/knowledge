Sebastian Mallaby on AI Safety and the Race for Superintelligence

Yascha Mounk (Persuasion) · email · 2026-04-02 source ↗ Reader 🖥️

The race to develop potentially catastrophic AI is not destiny but a collective action problem with known solutions — prohibiting open-source frontier models, mandating alignment investment, and creating multilateral governance — though the near-term obstacle is political (Trump's zero-sum worldview) rather than technical or intellectual.

Key terms

  • P(doom) AI Alignment & Safety
    The probability that AI development leads to catastrophic outcome — defined on a spectrum from humanity's death to AI domination. Mallaby treats it as a Rorschach test for temperamental pessimism rather than a calculable probability. He rejects Yudkowsky's ~100% estimate as 'ridiculously high.'
  • open weight / open source (AI) Open-Weight Models
    Models whose weights are publicly downloadable and runnable locally — removing the creator's ability to shut down misuse. Mallaby uses 'open source' and 'open weight' somewhat interchangeably, though open weight is more precise for the specific risk (ability to run locally without going through a controlled API).
  • alignment research AI Alignment & Safety
    Technical and engineering work to make AI systems act in accordance with human values and intentions. Mallaby treats it as an underfunded public good that requires government subsidy — not purely a private incentive problem.
  • CERN model AI Regulation & Governance (External)
    Demis Hassabis's proposal for a multilateral AI governance body — analogous to the Centre for European Nuclear Research — that would oversee AI development, propose and enforce policies, or set policies while national governments enforce them. Mallaby endorses this as the appropriate institutional framework.
  • Brussels effect AI Regulation & Governance (External)
    The EU's historically demonstrated ability to set global product standards by virtue of its market size, forcing companies that want EU access to comply globally. Mallaby argues this effect fails for AI because the EU has no frontier AI companies, making EU regulation easy to ignore.
  • schizophrenic
    Mallaby's term for the pattern of AI developers who simultaneously believe their technology is potentially dangerous and advance it anyway. He argues this is not pathology but an enlarged version of universal human risk-taking behavior.
  • FDA equivalent AI Regulation & Governance (External)
    A proposed pre-release assessment body for AI models that would red-team, evaluate, and approve models before they reach consumers — analogous to how the Food and Drug Administration approves drugs for safety before market release. Does not exist anywhere currently.

Arguments

Open-source frontier AI models should be prohibited strength: medium
Explicit premises:
  • The Mexico cyber attack demonstrates that closed-API models can be shut down once misuse is detected — open-weight cannot
  • RAND war games model rolling infrastructure attacks by unidentified attackers using AI tools that cannot be stopped
  • Bioweapon development via open-weight AI is undetectable and unstoppable
Implicit premises:
  • The ability to shut down a misused system mid-attack is a necessary safety feature, not a nice-to-have
  • The privacy and cost benefits of running models locally do not outweigh catastrophic misuse risk at the frontier level
  • Competitive pressure to open-source (to compensate for not being at the frontier) is a market failure that cannot self-correct
Inferences:
  • The attacker in the Mexico case could have used an open-weight alternative — Mallaby doesn't address this counterfactual, which weakens the argument
  • The regulation would need to reach Chinese labs to be effective — Mallaby argues this is possible but doesn't explain the enforcement mechanism
  • Academic/smaller models might remain open-source; only frontier models are the target — this distinction needs precise definition
AI governance through international agreement is possible, with China more willing than assumed strength: low
Explicit premises:
  • China's state restricted Open Claw when it posed safety concerns — demonstrating regulatory willingness
  • Chinese AI researchers at Tsinghua were warning against Open Claw on alignment grounds, not just political safety grounds
  • The UK AI Security Institute demonstrated that technical alignment governance works (discovered and closed a universal jailbreak)
  • Nuclear arms control succeeded between adversaries; the precedent exists for AI
Implicit premises:
  • China's interest in controlling AI (given surveillance capitalism and political control) aligns with safety governance interests
  • Middle powers can create diplomatic momentum without US participation under Trump
  • The adversarial framing of the US-China AI race is overstated — there are convergent safety interests
Inferences:
  • The current US political obstacle (Trump's zero-sum worldview) is temporary; governance frameworks can be built now to implement later
  • The UK is better positioned than the EU to lead because of DeepMind + AI Security Institute credibility
Human-caused AI doom (through granting defensive autonomy) is more proximate than spontaneous AI rebellion strength: high
Explicit premises:
  • Hinton's argument: giving AI systems cyber-defense capabilities necessarily grants self-preservation instincts
  • Self-preservation instinct + superior intelligence = the combination that produces domination
Implicit premises:
  • The decision to grant AI defensive autonomy is a deliberate human choice, not an emergent AI behavior
  • This means the key decision point is policy-addressable (regulate autonomous defensive AI deployment) rather than a matter of unknowable AI internal states
Inferences:
  • The most proximate doom pathway is not science fiction (spontaneous AI rebellion) but engineering (humans build defensive autonomous systems for military/cyber purposes)
  • This reframes the governance target: not abstract 'alignment' but specific prohibitions on autonomous defensive AI systems — closer to arms control than to AI ethics

Narratives

  • Founding histories: DeepMind founded at safety lecture; OpenAI founded to be safer than DeepMind; Anthropic founded as 'splinter group' that thought OpenAI not safe enough; Elon Musk with Grok proclaimed existential terror. Each lab begins as the 'safer' alternative.
    → Demonstrates that the schizophrenia is structural — baked into the competitive dynamic of the industry, not individual character flaws. Supports: 'enlarged version of all humans accepting technological risk'
  • Mexico cyber attack: A group used Claude (closed API) to hack electoral records at mass scale. Once Anthropic discovered the attack, they shut it down. Some combination with OpenAI also involved.
    → Primary case study for closed-API safety advantage over open-weight. Supports: 'open source is ridiculously dangerous' argument.
  • RAND Institute war games: Rolling waves of attacks on Western infrastructure by unidentified attacker — no clean water, no electricity, no attribution. The scenario where you can't shut down the attack because you don't control the tool.
    → Illustrates the nightmare scenario that open-weight AI enables. Supports: open source prohibition argument.
  • UK AI Security Institute: Discovered a phrase that unlocked any leading LLM to bypass safety measures. Disclosed to labs. Labs fixed it. This is governance working.
    → Demonstrates that technical alignment governance is possible and effective in practice — not just theoretical. Supports: governance-is-possible argument.
  • China and Open Claw: Tsinghua professors warned against installing it; lines of ordinary Chinese outside Tencent headquarters wanting engineers to install it; Chinese government ultimately weighed in to restrict it. Framed as alignment risk (agent accessing your data), not political censorship.
    → Counter-narrative to 'China will never cooperate on AI safety.' Shows Chinese authorities applying the same risk logic Mallaby is arguing for. Supports: governance-is-possible argument.
  • Geoffrey Hinton kitchen conversation: Two hours in Toronto. Hinton's argument that giving AI cyber-defense capabilities grants self-preservation instincts, erasing the safety assumption that AI won't have incentive to dominate.
    → The intellectual turning point in Mallaby's own P(doom) journey. Makes doom risk more proximate (military AI policy choice) and less fantastical (spontaneous robot uprising). The most intellectually significant moment in the interview.
  • Jeffrey Sachs analogy: Sachs would always emphasize how deeply poor and dysfunctional developing countries were, then argue that lots of aid could fix it. Both sides of the argument were wrong. Mallaby applies this to Yudkowsky: exaggerates the certainty of doom, then overestimates policy's ability to prevent it.
    → Rhetorical device to position Mallaby between Yudkowsky's extreme pessimism and techno-optimism's complacency. Supports: moderate P(doom) position.

Questions addressed

  • ✅ Why do AI developers simultaneously advance and fear potentially dangerous technology?
    It's an enlarged version of universal human risk-acceptance — we all trade off technological risk for technological benefit. Not pathological, not unique to AI.
  • ✅ How high is P(doom)?
    Moderate — analytically concerning (the Hinton argument is serious); temperamentally resistible for Mallaby. Yudkowsky's 100% is dismissed as temperament masquerading as analysis.
  • ✅ Should open-source frontier AI be prohibited?
    Yes, for frontier models — with carve-out for academic/smaller models. Enforcement would require international cooperation including China.
  • ✅ Can China be a partner in AI governance?
    More than assumed — alignment-risk concern is present among Chinese researchers and has begun to influence Chinese government policy (Open Claw).
  • ✅ What would a real AI governance framework contain?
    Three elements: no open-source for frontier models; alignment investment mandate (tax/subsidy); FDA-equivalent pre-release assessment body. CERN-style multilateral body for coordination.
  • ✅ Does the Brussels effect work for AI?
    No — the EU lacks frontier AI players, so the threat of market exclusion doesn't bite the way it does for car manufacturing standards.
  • ✅ What is the most credible pathway to doom?
    Not spontaneous AI rebellion but human decision to grant AI defensive autonomy — which necessarily creates self-preservation instincts. A policy-addressable risk.

Gaps

  • incompleteMallaby's open-source prohibition argument has a critical counterfactual gap: the Mexico case demonstrates that closed-API models can be shut down, but the attacker could have used an open-weight model instead. The argument assumes that closed APIs are the only available alternative, which may not hold as open-weight models proliferate. Mallaby acknowledges that powerful open-weight models 'circulate already' but doesn't resolve this.
    Read next: Research on whether capability differences between frontier closed and open-weight models are large enough to matter for attack scenarios; Stanford HAI's AI Index on capability gaps
  • incompleteMallaby endorses CERN-model governance and notes that US cooperation requires a new administration, but provides no mechanism for bridging the gap. The proposal assumes governance is intellectually solved and politically delayed — but the enforcement mechanism (how do you ensure Chinese labs comply?) is not examined.
    Read next: Arms control literature on verification mechanisms; IAEA safeguards as model; Dafoe's 'AI Governance: A Research Agenda'
  • uninformedMallaby doesn't engage with the critique that 'AI safety' discourse itself may serve corporate interests — as a way to delay open regulation (safety research as a substitute for binding rules), generate credibility for incumbent labs, and frame existential risk in ways that crowd out more immediate harms. This critique is prominent in AI ethics literature and among researchers like Timnit Gebru.
    Read next: Gebru et al.'s 'Stochastic Parrots'; Bender's work on framing; Whittaker's 'The Steep Cost of Capture'
  • incompleteThe interview is a podcast transcript — the dialogue form allows Mounk to push back but not to synthesize. Several threads are opened and not closed: the precise mechanism of the alignment tax; whether Chinese political safety and alignment safety are actually converging or just using similar words; what 'frontier model' means precisely for open-source prohibition purposes.
    Read next: Mallaby's book 'The Infinity Machine' on DeepMind presumably develops these arguments more carefully
  • illogicalThe 'Three Mile Island' analogy implies that a catastrophic AI incident will produce useful regulation. But Three Mile Island produced nuclear regulation in part because the harm was localized and attributable. A rolling infrastructure attack by an open-weight model — Mallaby's nightmare scenario — might not be attributable in the way necessary to drive political response. The analogy may not transfer.
    Read next: Perrow's 'Normal Accidents' on how catastrophes produce (and fail to produce) regulatory change; March, Sproull & Tamuz on learning from near-failures
